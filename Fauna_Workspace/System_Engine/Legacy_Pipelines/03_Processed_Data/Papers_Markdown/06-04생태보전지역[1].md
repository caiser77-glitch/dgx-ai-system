Foreword 
 
Due to the variety of topographical features and climate conditions, Korea 
possesses extremely diverse habitat types, including coastal wetlands, estuaries, 
paddy fields and woodlands, which provide nesting and feeding grounds for 
migratory birds and carnivorous predators.  However, these habitats have 
become deteriorated and fragmented as a result of extensive development and 
road construction; therefore, many wildlife species are faced with extinction. 
Fortunately, in recent years with an increase of public awareness and global 
cooperation for the maintenance of biological diversity, efforts to preserve and 
restore habitats for endangered and threatened species have been gradually 
activated.  Protected areas, such as the Ecosystem Conservation Areas, were 
increasingly designated to protect wildlife habitats.  In 2004, the Korean 
government enacted the Wildlife Protection Act to pursue the comprehensive 
implementation measures concerning wildlife species and their habitats.  
Under the Act, Wildlife Protection Areas and other wildlife management 
measures were established to protect and manage wildlife habitats. 
Habitat models are especially useful when delineating appropriate habitat 
for protection areas (e.g., Wildlife Protection Areas).  Regrettably, very few 
habitat models have been developed to date, and little is known about the 
habitat requirements of wildlife species in Korea; therefore, this study is timely 
and is needed because its results provide the procedures to evaluate and 
determine suitable habitats for wildlife.  Due to the integrated approaches 
based on the current status of Geographical Information Systems data and 
ecological knowledge, natural resource managers and policy-makers 
responsible for designating protected areas of wildlife habitats and finding 
suitable locations to build wildlife overpasses should be interested in the 
results and suggestions, described in this study.

Abstract 
 
Korea supports a diverse range of habitats, including coastal wetlands, 
estuaries, paddy fields and woodlands, which act as nesting and feeding 
grounds for migratory birds and carnivorous predators.  However, many 
wildlife species are facing extinction as a consequence of habitat loss and 
fragmentation from extensive socio-economic development projects and road 
construction.  Ecologists and natural resource managers believe that habitat 
requirements of wildlife species can be explored with the habitat model at the 
multiple scales, and the exploration is effectively used in wildlife management 
measures. 
The objective of this study was to delineate habitat suitability maps derived 
from habitat models to protect and manage wildlife habitats in Korea.  Four 
major steps toward building a wildlife habitat model and habitat suitability 
maps were used in this study: (1) review of habitat management strategies 
implemented in Korea for protecting wildlife species and their habitats, (2) 
designation of protected areas to preserve wildlife habitats by delineating 
habitat suitability maps, (3) development of habitat model to select proper 
locations of eco-bridges to connect fragmented habitats, and (4) habitat model 
applied in stream restoration on the watershed scale. 
All procedures were integrated to develop GIS-based habitat models for 
wildlife in Korea, with particular consideration for spatially-explicit habitat 
variables derived from the National Geographic Information System (NGIS) 
and the Environmental Information System (EIS).  The spatial patterns of 
environmental variables (i.e., hydrology, soil moisture, water turbidity and 
elevation) were delineated with NGIS and EIS, and the GIS-based habitat 
model was developed.  Although the Korean government has conducted 3 
nationwide surveys to identify population dynamics of wildlife species, only 
the National Survey of the Natural Environment was used to conduct a GIS 
dataset for wildlife species in Korea. 
Habitat suitability models for wild boar (Sus scrofa), leopard cat 
(Prionailurus bengalensis), and Eurasian otter (Lutra lutra) were developed as 
case studies to examine whether the developing procedures of the habitat 
models are realistic under the current situation on the available knowledge on 
ecological characteristics and data available.  Then, these habitat models were 


 
used to delineate habitat suitability maps, which is useful in implementing 
wildlife management strategies.  In data-poor situations, expert opinion and 
literature review are clearly effective approaches to identify life requisites and 
habitat variables of the wildlife in Korea.  The results show that qualification 
and evaluation of habitat model are important to implement wildlife habitat 
management, such as location selection of eco-bridges (i.e., wildlife overpasses), 
designation of wildlife protection areas, and riparian habitat management. 
Habitat models delineate suitable habitats for deciding boundaries of 
protected areas (e.g., Wildlife Protection Areas).  However, only a few habitat 
models have been developed up to now, and little is known about the habitat 
requirements of wildlife species in Korea.  In this regard, the results of this 
study will be used to provide spatially explicit information on wildlife habitats 
for target species, which is effective when selecting candidate locations of 
wildlife overpasses, determining preliminary boundaries of wildlife protection 
area, and implementing riparian habitat management measures to protect 
wildlife habitats in riparian zones.  Like the United States and other advanced 
countries, endangered species are selected as target species for developing 
habitat models.  In addition, habitat suitability models for endemic species 
were developed as an indicator of environmental quality across the country.  
This study will help the policy-makers and wildlife managers in the 
development of habitat suitability models and delineation of habitat suitability 
maps.

Chapter 1. Introduction 
 
1. Background and Objective 
 
Korea is located in northeast Asia, with the southern half of the Korean 
peninsula bordering the East Sea and the Yellow Sea.  The peninsula shares its 
northern border with China and Russia (Figure 1). A variety of topographical 
features characterizes the Korean peninsula, including coastal wetlands and 
estuaries, small offshore islands, and temperate forests, which cover 
three-quarters of the land surface.  The climate is characterized by marked 
seasonal changes in rainfall and temperature. This wide variety of topographic 
and climatic conditions has created extremely diverse habitat types, resulting 
in abundant wildlife species and ecological systems (Ministry of Environment 
[MOE] 2004). 
In particular, coastal wetlands and small islands along the western coast of 
Korea provide breeding, staging, and wintering sites for migratory birds, 
including essential habitats for globally threatened species (e.g., Yellow-billed 
Spoonbill, Red-crowned Crane).  Large carnivores, such as the Siberian tiger, 
have historically used mountainous landscapes to forage and secure shelter 
from severe weather.  Because vast areas of the country serve as breeding, 
resting, and wintering sites for many wildlife species, including internationally 
threatened species, preserving the habitat is vital to the promotion of biological 
diversity on a national, as well as on a global, scale (Republic of Korea [ROK] 
1997). 
However, during the last four decades, many development-oriented 
land-use and management measures have been implemented.  Because of

during the last 40 years. 
As natural wildlife habitats continue to disappear at an alarming rate,

many species of fauna and flora in Korea are threatened with extinction (Won

protected areas for wildlife habitat is a traditional management strategy for 
conserving suitable habitats for wildlife species.  Suitable natural habitats 
for wildlife species can be delineated through the application of Geographic 
Information Systems (GIS) and remote sensing technology based on habitat 
suitability index (HSI) models (Donovan et al. 1987; Mano et al. 2001; Hong et 
al. 2004). 
 
 
To date, few HSI models have been developed, and little is known about 
the habitat requirements of wildlife species in Korea (Rho 2004).  Habitat 
models, including HSI models, are necessary to determine which areas 
represent suitable habitats for wildlife species.  HSI models are used in 
wildlife management to assess the habitat suitability of unobserved areas, 
and are particularly useful for delineating protected areas for wildlife species 
and determining appropriate locations at which to build wildlife overpasses 
in fragmented landscapes.  However, the habitat requirements of only a few 
wildlife species in Korea, such as wild boar (Kim et al. 1998; Seo and Park 
2000; Park and Lee 2003), Spot-billed Ducks (Yoo 1998), Eurasian otter (Han

riparian habitat management strategies from a watershed perspective.  
 
2. Materials and Methods 
 
Governmental documents, including laws and regulations, were used to 
evaluate the current status of wildlife management.  Scientific papers, 
annual reports, and research notes related to wildlife species and their 
habitats were obtained and classified into those pertaining to wild fauna and 
flora.  We used these materials to develop and assess habitat models.  
Recently developed spatially explicit habitat models have become popular 
with advances in GIS and remote sensing technology (Wu and Smeins 2000; 
Store and Jokimaki 2003). 
GIS datasets, such as the National Geographic Information System (NGIS) 
and Environmental Information System (EIS), are effective for developing 
habitat models through their delineation of abiotic characteristics, such as soil, 
topography, climate, and other environmental factors affecting the habitat 
quality of individual organisms.  Satellite images and aerial photographs are 
also used in the development of habitat models.  In contrast to 
environmental variables, biotic variables, such as the number of species and 
their geographic location, are obtained through field observations, 
monitoring programs, and surveys, both at national and local scales.  These 
spatial distributions, abundances, and seasonal variations in wildlife species 
are important in habitat model development and model evaluation. 
Habitat models have received attention as management tools to 
understand the habitat relationships of organisms, to evaluate habitat quality,

and to develop habitat management strategies (Verner et al. 1986).  Here, 
habitat models were developed a priori based on expert knowledge and by 
successive approximation.  This approach requires the time-consuming 
processes of acquiring expert knowledge on the life requisites of the wildlife 
species of concern and transforming expert opinions into computer- readable 
language.  This is recognized as an effective tool for wildlife habitat 
management. 
To develop habitat models for each case study, preliminary habitat 
evaluation procedures were proposed based on literature reviews and expert 
opinion.  First, a habitat model was developed for wild boar based on 
literature reviews; the model was then assessed by a five-member panel of 
experts in landscape ecology, plant ecology, wildlife management, and 
natural resources policy.  For leopard cat and Eurasian otters, some 
information on habitat requirements was acquired from habitat uses and 
models from other countries.  Given the available GIS data and related 
studies on the habitat models of each species, HSI models and habitat 
evaluation procedures were applied in each chapter to suggest effective 
management strategies for wildlife habitats in Korea. 
To suggest wildlife management strategies based on spatially explicit 
habitat models, we used a target-species approach.  Although the use of the 
concepts of keystone, umbrella (Fleishman et al. 2000), and focal species 
(Lambeck 1997) has been questioned (Simberloff 1998), we believe that a 
target species approach provides a strong foundation on which to implement 
habitat management strategies. The target species approach was applied in 
this study by identifying threatening processes responsible for species 
decline and selecting a suite of species where each component species is 
considered most sensitive to the threatening processes (Lambeck 1997).  In 
theory, the needs of the target species collectively represent a variety of 
landscape characteristics that will encompass the needs of many other 
species.

1. Current status of wildlife and habitat in Korea 
 
Very little information is available on the current status of wildlife species 
and their habitats in Korea.  Although the total number of species is 
expected to be around 100,000, only 29,916 species have been recorded in 
Korea (Ministry of Environment [MOE] 2005c).  Aside from vertebrate and 
flowering plant species, the species of other taxonomic groups are not well 
known or documented.  In total, 8,271 species of plants have been reported, 
of which 4,662 are high plant species.  Among the 1,528 species of 
vertebrates recorded, 905 are fishes, 43 are amphibians or reptiles, 457 are 
birds, and 123 are mammals (Table 1).  The number of wildlife species is 
relatively low compared to other countries with similar bio-geographic 
features; this difference is probably caused by a lack of field experts and 
nationwide surveys.  Additional species can be identified and added to the 
list of wildlife species in Korea through systematic surveys and wildlife 
research.

Habitat loss and fragmentation, as well as over-hunting, invasion of alien 
species, and environmental contamination have driven wild animals and 
plants to extinction or endangerment.  Wildlife species are estimated to 
become extinct at a rate perhaps as high as 0.5% per year, mainly because of 
habitat loss and fragmentation (e.g., Vitousek et al. 1997).  In Korea, the 
Siberian tiger and Siberian leopard are regarded as extinct, and the wolf, 
Crested Shelduck (Tadorna cristata) and sitka deer are no longer observed 
regularly.  The Amur goral, Eurasian otter, and Eurasian flying squirrel are 
regarded as critically endangered (Republic of Korea [ROK] 1997; Won and 
Smith 1999; Yoo 1999). 
In Korea, habitats can be divided into five major types: forest, riparian and 
lagoon, wetland, coastal and marine, and farmland (ROK 1997).  Forest cover 
occupies two-thirds of the country; thus, Korea hosts a variety of vegetation 
and abundant wildlife species.  However, forests are dominated by trees 
planted since the 1960s, and the major tree species are coniferous.  
Broad-leaved deciduous forests consisting of oaks, maples, and hornbeams are 
distributed throughout the central area of Korea, whereas broad-leaved 
evergreen forests dominate the southern area and coastal regions. 
In the last few decades, wildlife habitats have been continuously lost and 
fragmented as a result of urbanization and industrialization.  For example, 
forests have been declining at a rate of 0.1% and mud flats at a rate of 1.4% per 
year during the last three decades (MOE 2005a).  In addition, human activities, 
such as clearing of forests for human settlements, agricultural activities, 
over-use of pesticides and fertilizers, and reclamation of tidal flats, have 
contributed to the loss and fragmentation of wildlife habitat, resulting in a 
decrease in biological diversity (MOE 2001). 
About 221 species of wild fauna and flora are currently classified as 
endangered in Korea, and capture, killing, and trade of these species are 
strictly prohibited by the Wildlife Protection Act.  Fewer protected areas have 
been designated in Korea than in other countries of the Organization for 
Economic Co-operation and Development (OECD) for the preservation of 
endangered species and their habitats (MOE 2005b).  To designate additional 
protected areas, precise identification of the current status and spatial 
distribution of wildlife species are required through surveys and monitoring 
programs of wildlife habitats.  The Korean government now implements

2. Wildlife habitat conservation and management 
 
The Korean government has developed various wildlife management 
strategies, ranging from establishment of the Convention on Biological 
Diversity (CBD) in 1992 to the specification of endangered species and 
protected areas (ROK 1997).  Moreover, in 2004, the government enacted the 
Wildlife Protection Act to systematically conserve wildlife species and their 
habitats.  Recent rapid expansion of human activity has resulted in the 
continuing degradation of wildlife habitat and loss of biological diversity in 
Korea.  Because the need to prevent species and habitat extinction is 
increasing, policy-makers and wildlife managers have been implementing 
various wildlife management measures. 
Various measures and strategies have been applied for corporate economic 
growth while maintaining biological diversity.  For example, protected areas 
for wildlife habitat were designated based on within-site analyses; however, 
this process has limited effectiveness in preserving wildlife habitat.  In general, 
the protected areas are too small to provide for all habitat requirements of 
species, such as foraging and escaping. Nature conservation strategies, 
including habitat preservation, have changed from site-oriented protection to 
the conservation of ecological networks within a wider landscape.  The 
concept of ecological networks was formally introduced in Korea in 1995 to 
protect and improve ecosystems (Organization for Economic Co-operation and 
Development [OECD] 1997). 
Although Korea has moved toward modern nature conservation based on 
landscape ecological principles, it still lacks scientific information on landscape 
structure and function as a basis for spatial conservation strategies.  Natural 
disturbances, as well as human activities, have changed the conditions of 
wildlife habitat in many areas, sometimes critically deteriorating exceptionally

suitable habitat of preferred wildlife species. 
Recently, many habitats have been lost or fragmented by large-scale 
development projects nationwide.  Wildlife species have become extinct or 
critically endangered, reducing biodiversity.  A decrease in biological 
diversity is one of the most serious issues among environmental problems, 
because recovery of biological diversity is nearly impossible once it has 
disappeared.  Future generations will live more indigent lives with decrease 
in biological diversity.  The MOE has designated 221 species as endangered to 
prevent the extinction of wildlife (MOE 2005b).  The government has also 
established wildlife protection areas to conserve and enhance the habitats of 
endangered species. 
Protected areas for wildlife conservation in Korea are classified into two 
categories: national protected areas and international protected areas.  The 
preservation of wildlife species and their habitats is a subject of common global 
concern, and contributions at the global level lead to increased protection.  
The designation of internationally protected areas has been established by 
international organizations that cooperate with each country to manage 
wildlife habitats.  Examples of such protected areas in Korea are Biosphere 
Reserves (UNESCO), Ramsar Wetlands, and Migratory Waterbird Networks 
for cranes, shorebirds, and waterfowl in the Asia-Pacific region. 
National protected areas of wildlife habitat in Korea include Ecosystem 
Conservation Areas, Wildlife Protection Areas, Genetic Resource Protection 
Areas, and Natural Monument Protected Areas.  According to the Wildlife 
Protection Act, which was enacted in February 2005, the MOE designates

3. Nationwide survey programs related to species and habitats 
 
To protect and manage wildlife habitat, spatial distributions of target 
species, population changes, and habitat environments should first be 
identified.  The abundance and spatial distribution of wildlife species and 
their habitats in Korea were determined nationwide by three major survey 
programs; 1) the National Survey of the Natural Environment, 2) the Wildlife

Population Census, and 3) the Winter Migratory Bird Census.  Since 2001, the 
MOE has conducted a new survey program, which collects information on the 
species abundance and spatial distribution of 221 endangered species in Korea. 
All surveys have been performed by experts of each taxonomic group (MOE 
2005b). 
The National Survey of the Natural Environment (NSNE) was conducted 
under the Nature Conservation Act and is used for land-use planning and 
habitat management by developing Ecological Suitability Maps using the 
survey results (MOE 2005b).  To develop habitat models, physical 
characteristics, such as gradients that determine the habitat distribution of 
plants, soil moisture content, rainfall, stream width, stream order, stream 
depth, and velocity of water flow should be determined, and these data can be 
collected from the NGIS, EIS, water resources systems, satellite images, and 
aerial photographs. 
The first NSNE was conducted by the MOE during the period from 1986 to 
1991 to investigate natural environments across the country.  This survey 
investigated the existing circumstances of national terrestrial, aquatic, and 
coastal ecosystems.  Moreover, it developed the ‘Degree of Green Naturality’ 
map and the ‘Actual Vegetation Map’.  The NSNE revealed limitations 
regarding the effectiveness of investigations lacking field experts and 
well-designed survey methods.  Thus, a more scientific approach was used in 
the second NSNE.  Survey districts across the country were delineated based 
on terrestrial ecosystem, stream networks, and administrative boundaries.  In 
addition, wildlife survey guidelines were developed for each taxonomic group 
to produce systematic investigations. 
The ‘Ecological Suitability Map’ was developed using the NSNE data to 
evaluate wildlife habitat and to determine the spatial distribution of wildlife by 
providing observed geographic locations of wildlife and endangered species.  
However, although the NSNE focused on biological characteristics, such as the 
number of species, it did not collect information on the habitat characteristics 
of the observed wildlife species; therefore, a habitat-related survey is required 
to develop habitat suitability models. 
Data regarding the abundance and distribution of species and population 
dynamics are collected in two formats: a spatial format (i.e., maps, coordinates), 
and an aspatial format (i.e., inventories, reports).  Aspatial data include 
reports, books and newspaper articles about endangered species, whereas

1. Overview 
 
According to the landscape ecological perspective, to effectively conserve 
wildlife species and their habitats sufficiently large areas should be designated 
as legally protected (Diamond 1975; Forman 1995).  Increasing numbers of 
wildlife species are facing extinction in Korea, because of habitat loss and 
fragmentation caused by industrialization and urbanization.  Our current 
understanding of which areas are suitable for wildlife species of interest and 
their habitat requirements is extremely poor.  Thus, the criteria to delineate 
protected areas are poorly documented, and many local residents argue for the 
arbitrary establishment of protected areas. 
The delineation of protected areas has long been a focus of ecological 
research, but the potential of protected areas as wildlife habitat can be limited 
by their size, location, and the economic and social costs required to support 
them (Chape et al. 2003).  In Korea, many protected areas, especially those for 
mammals and birds, are extremely small, isolated fragments in a matrix of 
human-dominated, modified landscapes.  At present, too little ecological 
knowledge is used to determine the location, size, and shape of protected 
(Ministry of Environment [MOE] 2001). 
In this chapter, we aimed to develop a habitat suitability map for the 
leopard cat (Prionailurus bengalensis) to determine the best locations for 
protected areas.  A habitat suitability map provides information on the 
appropriate size and boundary of protected areas.  We also describe the 
habitat components that should occur in wildlife protection areas.  The 
current protected areas for wildlife habitat across Korea were then assessed by 
comparison to the habitat suitability map. 
The current understanding of leopard cat biology and ecological processes 
in temperate landscapes, such as temperate deciduous forests and evergreen 
forests in Korea, is extremely poor.  Many small cats are globally endangered 
(Baillie et al. 2004), and large cats (e.g., tigers, leopards), which are top 
predators, are now extinct in Korea (Won and Smith 1999).  The overall aim of 
this chapter is to enhance our understanding of the habitat requirements of the 


14   GIS-based Wildlife Habitat Management Strategies in Korea 
leopard cat and to determine which areas are required as protected areas for 
this species.  Because leopard cat survival may depend on a high abundance 
of rodents, which are pest species for humans and agriculture, it is important 
to designate suitable habitat as wildlife protected areas to preserve this species.  
Management practices used to preserve and improve leopard cat habitat other 
than the use of wildlife protected areas will be described.  
The specific objectives of this chapter were to determine the abundance, 
spatial distribution, and habitat features of the leopard cat in the study area; to 
investigate the relationship between habitat characteristics and the life 
requisites of the leopard cat to develop a habitat suitability index (HSI) model; 
to evaluate a habitat suitability map for this species compared to its actual 
locations; and to examine the gaps in suitable leopard cat habitat that are not 
currently designated as existing protected areas. 
 
2. Selection of target species: Leopard cat 
 
Habitat loss and deterioration are considered as significant factors 
threatening leopard cat (Prionailurus bengalensis) populations in most countries 
where the species is endangered (Grassman et al. 2005).  To manage the 
habitats of leopard cat, it is necessary to designate suitable habitats as legal 
protected areas.  A habitat suitability map is generated based on a habitat 
model, which consists of habitat features required by the leopard cat.  At 
present, little information is available on the relationship between the habitat 
characteristics and the needs of leopard cat. 
Direct observation and field surveys should be conducted to develop 
wildlife-habitat models.  Clearly, direct measures of habitat use are required, 
but this is difficult because leopard cat is primarily nocturnal, unobstrusive 
and trap-shy, with extensive ranges.  In Korea, knowledge on the numbers of 
leopard cat is not sufficient to develop and assess a habitat model for practical 
use (Yoon et al. 2002).  Presently, leopard cat has been heavily persecuted and 
the Convention on International Trade in Endangered Species (CITES) holds 
the leopard cat on Appendix II, strictly regulating international commerce.  
Leopard cat has been placed on Appendix I which prohibit all international 
trade in products of that subspecies. 
In order to conserve and manage their habitats, there is a general need for 
quantitative information on the spatial distribution of leopard cat in Korea.

Land-use/land-cover (LULC) composition is a % cover of each land-use/ 
land-cover type.  LULC is classified into 3 categories: 1) natural land cover 
type, 2) semi-natural land cover type, and 3) urban land cover type.  In

grassland, it is easy to find food for the leopard cat, and provides much cover 
resources for ecological functions, such as roosting, resting, and mating sites.

A natural land cover type is most suited for the leopard cat among these three 
categories (Anon 1966).  This natural land cover type has a habitat suitability 
value of 0.8.  Semi-natural land cover, which consists of rice paddy fields, 
orchards, wetlands, and pastureland, has a moderate suitability value 0.4.  An 
urbanized land cover type, such as residential, industrial, public facilities and 
commercial area, is dominated by humans, and many human activities occur 
in the area.  The only one predator of the leopard cat is humans (Yoon et al. 
2002).  Thus, such landscapes dominated by human activity are not suitable 
for leopard cat, and habitat suitability of this area was assigned 0. 
A higher edge density means that there are many borders where various 
land cover types or various vegetation types meet.  If any area has a high edge 
density, there exists many vegetation types and land cover types.  This area 
can provide a variety of foods and places to live.  Thus, as edge density 
increases, habitat suitability increases.  If there is no border (e.g., edge density 
is 0), that area is not very suitable as a habitat. 
Leopard cat is ranked as a top predator in terrestrial ecosystem in Korea.  
As they do not need hiding places, the animals prefer open spaces for their 
habitats (Yoon et al. 2002).  Open spaces are favorable when searching and 
capturing prey animals for a top predator.  Thus, open spaces, such as 
mountain tops or high ridges, can be good habitat place for leopard cat.  If 
there are many ridges, then habitat suitability increases.  In contrast, if the 
percentage of ridges is low, then habitat suitability decreases. 
Water, needless to say, is a critical element regarding wildlife habitats, and 
distance to water is important factor for evaluating habitat suitability.  Habitat 
suitability is higher when the accessibility to water is easier.  The area closer 
to the water, the water edge is regarded as the most suitable place for a leopard 
cat, and has the suitability index of 1.  As distance to the water increases, 
habitat suitability decreases, and becomes less suitable if the distance to the 
water is greater than 1,000-m.  Daily movements of the leopard cat are around 
1,000-m (Rabinowitz 1990).  Furthermore, leopard cat is a good swimmer.  
Many of the animals inhabit offshore area, and water conditions affect 
reproduction.  Thus, closer to the water, the habitat suitability increases.

cover of each land-use/land-cover type.  In this chapter, land-use/land-cover

5. Management implications for wildlife protection area 
 
The wildlife management implications related to protected areas are more 
important in the Gangwon Province than in other provinces, because Gangwon 
has a vast amount of wildlife habitat.  Our results showed that protected areas 
should be expanded in Gangwon to protect all of the suitable habitats for 
leopard cat.  The leopard cat is the highest predator in Korea; therefore, its 
habitat requirements should support the habitat requirements of subordinate 
species (Yoon et al. 2002). 
Because of time constraints, we only generated a habitat suitability index 
(HSI) map for the leopard cat; no other species were used to delineate 
proposed protected areas.  The habitat suitability map for this single target 
species provides only a partial picture to guide investment in the expansion of 
protected areas in Gangwon Province because it is not an accurate guide of all 
conservation requirements.  To implement a more effective delineation of 
protected areas, other wildlife species, such as raptors and amphibians, should 
be included in a suite of target species.  Consequently, habitat suitability 
maps for other wildlife species will be developed and combined to determine 
the most important habitat for protected areas. 
Habitat suitability maps show the area of a region divided into cells that are 
scored by assigning a grade of habitat suitability, from least suitable to most 
suitable, for a particular wildlife species; these maps also indicate seasonal 
variation in habitat suitability (O’Neil et al. 1988).  Although we found that 
Gangwon has a large amount of suitable habitat, only relatively small areas are 
highly suitable for leopard cat.  Thus, additional habitat suitability maps for 
other wildlife species are needed to identify a comprehensive set of 
alternatives in the priority of wildlife habitat for protection. 
We used an overall habitat suitability map for the leopard cat; however, 
habitat suitability maps can be classified into various habitat components, such 
as areas used for foraging, cover, and reproduction.  Each component is 
closely related to the life cycle of the target species.  Therefore, habitat 
suitability maps of each component can be used to protect wildlife habitat that 


Chapter 3 Wildlife Protection Areas   25 
supports species at specific stages of the life cycle.  HSI models can be 
developed for a particular life stage of wildlife species; therefore, habitat 
suitability maps represent the habitat suitability for each life stage.  
The potential abundance of wildlife species is obtained from the HSI model 
and is used to define favorable or unfavorable areas for the species.  A 
distribution map of the potential abundance represents the extent of 
occurrence, and therefore, the area is adequate for the protection of each 
wildlife species.  For example, an area in which the probability of a species’ 
presence is four times higher than the probability of its absence may be 
considered favorable for that species and may therefore be designated as a 
protected area.  Moreover, to manage wildlife protection areas, highly 
suitable habitat from the suitability map should be designated as core areas of 
the protected habitats, and neighboring areas should be considered buffer 
areas in the protected area system. 
The areas highlighted in this analysis are urgent priorities for the expansion 
of wildlife protected areas that comprehensively protect vertebrate species, but 
they do not include all areas that are required for the conservation of all 
taxonomic groups.  Conversely, if a given area has not been highlighted as a 
priority (i.e., the most suitable area), it does not mean that it should not be 
considered for further protection.  Only the leopard cat was used to delineate 
protected areas here, and even though the species is one of the highest 
carnivore predators in Korea, this habitat analysis cannot claim to represent all 
habitat suitability for diverse wildlife species.  For example, we did not 
consider habitat requirements of birds and amphibians.  As habitat maps for 
additional species are created, the integrated habitat maps will allow the 
consideration of a broader range of wildlife species in regional gap analyses. 
Even though the results appeared coherent and the habitat models 
performed well during validation in this study, the results should be 
interpreted with caution.  The habitat models did not achieve a complete fit, 
and the use of linear relationships alone may be insufficient to characterize the 
response of wildlife species to environmental habitat variables.  This is 
frequently cited as problematic in the use of HSI models at the landscape scale, 
but it could also pose major caveats for the local-scale predictions of the model.   
The habitat suitability map for the leopard cat was created at the landscape 
scale at a 1×1-km resolution.  The suitability map represents the potential 
distribution of species abundance, generated by relationships between 
environmental habitat variables and species occurrences.  Given the paucity 


26   GIS-based Wildlife Habitat Management Strategies in Korea 
of biotic characteristics and insufficient knowledge of the habitat 
requirements of leopard cat, the habitat suitability maps at the landscape 
scale can only be used for a preliminary delineation of protected areas. 
As more and better data become available, future assessments of habitat 
suitability maps will include not only additional species, but also more 
refined information on the habitat requirements of those species.  In 
addition, long-term viability and temporal variation can be included to 
ensure more refined spatio-temporal suitability of protected areas.  Better 
data on protected areas will include information on the management type 
and its effectiveness to enable improved evaluation of which species are 
likely to be adequately protected in which areas. 
Massive efforts to establish protected areas over the last few decades have 
already contributed substantially to the conservation of wildlife habitat in 
Korea.  However, the national protected areas still fall short of minimally 
protecting even the leopard cat, the best-known and highest carnivore 
predator in this country.  If the provincial government of Gangwon is to 
conserve its living biodiversity heritage, a greatly increased and strategically 
placed investment in establishing new protected areas must be made as soon 
as possible.  This gap analysis is a first attempt to establish a framework to 
identify urgent priorities for protected areas.

1. Overview 
 
Habitat fragmentation is the separation of previously continuous blocks of 
suitable habitat into two or more disconnected pieces (Forman 1995).  
Habitat fragmentation caused by linear disturbances, such as roads, railways, 
and waterways, has been cited as one of the most important threats to 
wildlife conservation (Sherwood et al. 2002; Forman et al. 2003; Lee et al. 
2004).  Many management measures have been implemented to mitigate the 
negative effects of linear disturbances on wildlife habitat.  In particular, to 
connect 
fragmented 
habitats, 
eco-bridges 
(i.e., 
wildlife 
overpasses, 
underpasses, and road-level crossings) are increasingly being considered by 
wildlife managers and transportation planners globally, including in Korea 
(Clevenger and Waltho 2000; Ahn et al. 2004). 
Economic growth has led to the construction and expansion of road and 
railway systems in Korea, and most activities related to transportation 
systems have been conducted without much consideration for environmental 
effects of roads on wildlife habitats.  However, with increasing numbers of 
wildlife-vehicle accidents and population declines in wildlife species, the 
Korean government has recognized the negative effects of road systems on 
wildlife (Lee et al. 2004).  The negative effects of roads on wildlife habitat 
are numerous and include both the direct effects of habitat loss and the 
indirect effects of habitat fragmentation and deterioration (Forman and 
Alexander 1998).  The habitat changes related to road construction are 
particularly important for mammals that have relatively large home ranges, 
causing isolation from resources and potential mates by roads, individual 
mortality by collision with vehicles (Reed et al. 1975; Slater 2002), and 
restricted movement (Andrews 1990; Peterson et al. 2003). 
Recently, eco-bridges have been constructed in many countries to connect 
two or more separated blocks of suitable habitat across areas of unsuitable 
habitat.  In Korea, about 50 eco-bridges of various types have been built 


28   GIS-based Wildlife Habitat Management Strategies in Korea 
during the last decade to reduce the number of wildlife-vehicle collisions.  
However, road-related mortality of wildlife continues to increase, and 
monitoring of eco-bridges has indicated that some are not in suitable 
locations or were not effectively designed to facilitate animal movement (Ahn 
et al. 2004).  Wildlife-vehicle collisions may become more serious in Korea in 
the future as the traffic volume and number of roads increase substantially.  
Therefore, the selection of suitable location for eco-bridges is important, 
because the high cost of construction limits their installation to relatively few 
sites (McGuire and Morrall 2000). 
Eco-bridges, including wildlife overpasses, were originally constructed for 
vertebrate species to avoid inbreeding caused by habitat fragmentation (Dr. 
C-M Won, National Institute of Environmental Research, pers. comm.).  
Inbreeding leads to a loss of genetic diversity, resulting in weak or sterile 
offspring.  Particularly, mammal species require relatively large home range 
areas to meet their life requirements for food and mates.  Therefore, 
mammals are the first consideration in constructing a wildlife overpass.  The 
strategy for wildlife overpass construction should be to connect separated 
populations in fragmented landscapes to promote the genetic diversity of 
wildlife species. 
Spatially explicit habitat models can be used to delineate habitat 
suitability maps, which can then be used to predict the most suitable 
locations for wildlife eco-bridges.  Candidate locations can be identified 
using a spatial analysis of the habitat suitability maps and road systems to 
determine the locations with the highest collision probability.  To our 
knowledge, few studies that have determined suitable locations for 
eco-bridges have been published (Ahn et al. 2004); most previous eco-bridge 
studies were conducted at a fine scale (e.g., Lee et al. 2002).  However, 
national strategies to preserve wildlife species in fragmented landscapes 
should be implemented at the landscape scale.

and used to delineate a coarse-scale habitat suitability map representing

2. Selection of target species: Wild boar 
 
Which species is the best target species for conserving and managing 
wildlife overpasses?  There is no single answer to the question, because 
topographical features and wildlife population dynamics nearby wildlife 
overpasses vary from place to place in Korea.  The idea of managing wildlife 
habitats through target species is attractive, but the selection of target species 
is a challenge for scientists and wildlife managers.  Wildlife underpasses 
and overpasses are selected for construction depending on the topographical 
condition and species of greatest concern (Lee et al. 2002).  Contrary to 
wildlife underpasses, which are constructed mainly for fishes, amphibians 
and reptiles, most wildlife overpasses are built for maintaining the 
movement of mammals and bird species (Ahn et al. 2004).  In general, 
waterbirds are likely selected as target species in riparian regions, whereas 
carnivores and raptors are primarily considered as target species in 
mountainous regions. 
So far, most eco-bridges were built for helping the movements of 
mammals and amphibian in Korea (Ahn et al. 2004).  Roe deer, wild boar, 
Asiatic black bear and raccoon dog are one of the most important mammal 
species concerned in the construction and management of wildlife overpasses.  
The effect of habitat fragmentation caused by road construction is higher in 
the generalist mammal species such as wild boar (Sus scrofa; Virgos 2002) and 
roe deer (Capreolus capreolus; Telleria and Virgos 1997).  These large- and 
medium-sized mammal species require relatively large home range for their 
survival and move more distances than small-sized animals do.  Vehicle 
collisions with these large-sized mammals result in relatively huge damage to 
humans and their properties.  Nevertheless, the determination of wildlife 
overpass locations for mammal species to reduce mammal-vehicle collisions 
is poorly documented despite their practical concerns. 
The wild boar was selected as the target species for this case study to 
determine wildlife overpass location based on habitat suitability maps.  This 
animal is one of the most common large-sized mammals and sometimes use 
wildlife overpasses in Korea to satisfy their habitat requirements in 


30   GIS-based Wildlife Habitat Management Strategies in Korea 
fragmented landscapes caused by highways and railroads.  The predator of 
the wild boar (i.e., Siberian tiger, leopard, etc.) has been extinct in Korea.  
For same time, the boar population is now stable and even increasing 
nowadays (Yoo et al. 2004).  The wild boar has relatively little difficulty in 
finding mates in fragmented landscapes.  In this chapter, the wild boar was 
chosen as the target species because this animal is one of the most common 
large-sized mammals in Korea.  Therefore, the death and injury costs of wild 
boar-vehicle collisions are high with its body weight.  Moreover, habitat 
requirements of the wild boar are well documented.  The wild boar is very 
useful in developing the habitat suitability model, which can be used to 
delineate habitat suitability maps to determine the most likely locations for 
wild boar-vehicle collision.

3. Habitat model development and evaluation for wild boar 
 
A. Habitat use 
Usually, home ranges, diets, and habitat uses are used to develop habitat 
models of wildlife species (U.S. Fish and Wildlife Service 1980a; 1980b; 1981).  
In this chapter, habitat uses were primarily studied by analyzing existing 
published documents and questioning expert opinion through the Delphi 
method.  Contrary to the other mammal species, ecology and habitat uses of 
the wild boar have been well documented in Korea, especially food habits, 
movements and habitat requirements (Kim 1994; Kim et al. 1998; Park and 
Lee 2003).  Habitat uses of the species were used to effectively develop 
habitat models, which have also been developed globally based on food, 
cover, water, and reproduction requirements.  For example, U.S. Fish and 
Wildlife Service have developed the habitat models for ≥ 150 wildlife species 
(U.S. Fish and Wildlife Service 1980a; 1980b; 1981).  In this study, winter 
foods, water, cover and reproduction requirements were selected as major 
components for the life requisites of the wild boar model. 
The habitat quality was measured for each site by assessing the 
relationship between environmental habitat variables and suitability indices 
(U.S. Fish and Wildlife Service 1981).  The size of each site was determined 
by considering the daily movement and home range of the species.  A 


Chapter 4 Wildlife Overpasses    33 
moving window approach was used to evaluate habitat quality based on 
habitat variables among various sites (Riitters et al. 1997).  The form of the 
moving window was circular, having a 3-km radius, representing an 
estimated home range of 1,100-3,500 ha (Saunders and McLeod 1999).  The 
circular moving window was moved systematically across study site in 
500-m steps.  The moving steps represented daily distance that wild boars 
traveled, estimated to be 0.5-2.5km (Grzimek 1972). 
Even though wild boar have different feeding habits and movement 
patterns between wintering and non-wintering seasons, habitat uses were 
described based on the entire year because they generally inhabit the same 
areas during all seasons.  Identification of life requisites and associated 
habitat variables are one of most important steps in model development.  
The life requisites of wild boar fall into three categories (i.e., food, cover and 
reproduction), which were used to identify habitat variables. 
Food requirements, especially in the winter, were highly correlated with 
acorn production in the fall season.  Therefore, oak tree older than 20-years 
composes one of the most important factors in food requirements (Yoo 1999). 
Oak trees provide acorns, leaves, and seedlings, which are primary diets of 
wild boar in the winter.  During the winter season of falling snow, it is very 
difficult for the animals to find the root of plants, forbs, and seeds in areas 
facing northern and western slopes (Mr. D-H Lee, Geum River Basin 
Environmental Office of the Ministry of Environment, pers. comm.).  
However, falling snows easily melt with the relatively high temperature in 
the southern and eastern slopes.  Therefore, south- and east-facing slopes 
are considered as one of important factors affecting wild boar habitat quality 
for the winter food component. 
Escaping and resting sites are located preferentially in areas with (1) 
abundant plant cover, (2) deciduous forests in natural areas, and (3) far from 
trails (Park and Lee 2003; Fernandez-llario 2004).  In this study, only ‘the 
distance to roads’ variable was used to assess cover requirements for the wild 
boar, because the other two variables related to plant cover and deciduous 
forest are probably correlated to the composition of oak trees, which already 
has selected as a habitat variable for the food component.  Roads are just 
one of the many factors that have degraded the wild boar habitat.  And 
obviously, the wild boar population is in decline as a result of human 
activities.  The animals have strong wariness, and prefer the places where 
human activities do not heavily dominate.  Park and Lee (2003) suggested 


34   GIS-based Wildlife Habitat Management Strategies in Korea 
that wild boar use the area where > 310-m apart from paved road.  The 
presence of water is also essential for wallowing, and oak trees with their 
relatively dense understory are necessary for bedding and sheltering (Merrigi 
and Saachi 2000).  We selected the ‘distance to roads’ as well as ‘water 
accessibility’ as habitat variables to represent cover requirements. 
The age and density of tree were selected as habitat variables to represent 
reproduction requirements for the wild boar, because nesting sites for 
reproduction components are usually located near old-aged forests 
(Fernandex-Llario 2004).  Woody habitats, especially the habitats of late 
successional plants such as broad-leaved mature woodlands and mixed 
woodlands, provide high quality of food and shelter for the wild boar 
(Meriggi and Sacchi 2000).  This is also true for the reproduction 
requirements, because matured hardwoods provide abundant food of high 
nutrition (i.e., insects) and secure places for rearing piglets. 
In summary, wild boar frequently occurs in areas of mature hardwoods 
especially around oak trees in the southern and eastern slopes (Yoo 1999).  
Acorns and beech mast, which represent the most significant food sources, 
are easily found in the area (Sjarmidi et al. 1992; Woo 1993; Yoon et al. 2003).  
The animals prefer to live near fresh water and relatively remote areas, away 
from roads and trails in Korea (Park and Lee 2003).

B. Habitat model development 
Expert experience and knowledge as well as literature reviews are used to 
quantify the relationship between habitat variables and suitability indices of 
the wild boar.  For each environmental habitat variable, a suitability curve is 
generated to assign a habitat suitability index across the study area.  In the 
following sections, the definition of suitability curves for individual 
environmental variables and their combinations is explained. 
The oak tree is an essential factor affecting food resources for the wild boar 
by providing acorns, green leaves throughout the year.  A specific amount of 
food resources are required for wild boar to survive and grow (Sjarmidi et al. 
1992).  If there are no oak trees, it means rare foods, and wild boar tends not 
to use the place for feeding.  Thus this area results in a very low suitability 
index, in the aspect of the food component for the wild boar.  As oak trees 
increase, the suitability index for the food component increases.  A percentage 
of 30 of oak canopy is assumed to be sufficient as a food resources for wild 
boar; therefore, if oak canopy reaches the 30%, habitat suitability for the food 
component is considered to be optimal with a suitability index of 1. 
Aspect is another important variable affecting food resources.  In the 
winter, much snow falls in the Gangwon Province of Korea.  Snow prevents 
the wild boar from finding the food resources, such as plant roots.  Northern 
or western slopes are particularly hard to melt, so these areas are assumed to 
be unsuitable for winter habitat.  However, it is easy to find winter foods such 
as plant roots and earthworms which live in soil near woody plants because 
the snow melts easily with the warmer weather in the southern or eastern 
slopes.  If the composition of southern or eastern slopes in unit area is zero, 
the area is not suitable for wild boar and its habitat suitability is zero.  As the 
composition of southern and eastern slopes increases, habitat suitability also 
increases.  For example, all areas covered with southern and eastern slopes

5. Management implications for wildlife overpasses 
 
As traffic volume and road construction increases, more wildlife-vehicle 
collisions will occur.  Wildlife overpasses are essential measures to reduce 
wildlife-vehicle collisions and connect natural habitat for wildlife.  However, 
many wildlife overpasses already constructed in Korea have failed to 
perform these functions, primarily because of insufficient monitoring of the 
current abundance and distribution of wildlife species and their movements, 
inappropriate selection of target species, and the unsuitable placement of 
wildlife overpasses (Ahn et al. 2004).  To date, few habitat models have been 
developed, so no suitability maps have been used in developing wildlife 
overpass management strategies.  This chapter described how to select 
target species and suitable locations for wildlife overpasses based on habitat 
models and ecological characteristics of species.  The results clearly indicate 
that habitat suitability maps derived from habitat suitability models provide 
useful information for the selection of suitable locations for eco-bridges. 
A. Selection of target species 
The selection of target species, sites for wildlife overpasses, and distances 
between adjacent neighboring wildlife overpasses are considered in the 
management strategies for wildlife overpasses.  These selections are usually 
conducted at the landscape scale.  Another approach to enhance habitat 
quality in fragmented habitat is to design the length and width, floristic 
composition, and micro-topographic features of wildlife overpasses at a fine 
scale to facilitate the safe movement of wildlife species.  The use of

landscape-scale habitat suitability model encourages the mitigation of 
wildlife-vehicle collisions by selecting suitable locations and reliable designs 
for wildlife overpasses, resulting in the conservation of wildlife species. 
Target species should be selected to delineate habitat suitability maps 
derived from habitat suitability model using direct observations and field 
surveys, road-kill data, and interviews with local residents and wildlife 
managers.  Because the cost of construction for wildlife overpasses is much 
higher than that for wildlife underpasses or road-level crossings, the use of 
large mammals, such as wild boar, would be beneficial for encompassing the 
movement of smaller species, i.e., small mammals, birds, and reptiles.  For 
example, it would not be realistic to consider the construction of wildlife 
overpasses for amphibian alone.  At least a 1-year field survey is necessary 
to identify target species.  For wildlife overpasses, one to several large 
mammal species is selected as target species by wildlife managers and 
transportation planners to identify suitable locations. 
B. Site selection for wildlife overpasses 
The identification of suitable locations for wildlife overpasses is an 
important step in reducing the number of wildlife-vehicle collisions.  In this 
chapter, the wild boar was selected as the target species; therefore, suitable 
locations of wildlife overpasses are determined based on the habitat 
suitability for this species.  Spatially explicit habitat models and suitability 
maps are used to determine the location of wildlife overpasses.  This study 
demonstrated that habitat models and suitability maps are useful in selecting 
suitable locations for wildlife overpasses.  Wildlife managers and 
transportation planners need to co-operate to develop spatially explicit 
habitat models and delineate habitat suitability maps to select candidate 
locations for wildlife overpasses.  In addition, records of the locations of 
animals killed on roads aid in selecting appropriate locations (Clevenger and 
Waltho 2005).  More detailed information on how to acquire wildlife-vehicle 
collisions data is described in section D. 
C. Scale issues in wildlife overpass management 
Like other wildlife, the wild boar likely interacts with its habitat at several 
spatial scales.  For this reason, different habitat features used in habitat 


44   GIS-based Wildlife Habitat Management Strategies in Korea 
models may be relevant in determining the location of wildlife overpasses at 
multiple scales.  However, the spatial scale used depends on the 
information available at that time.  Because our study was conducted at a 
broad scale, the results were used to assess the general large-scale pattern of 
wildlife habitat.  Habitat suitability maps derived from this work will never 
provide detailed fine-scale information. 
Habitat suitability maps will be used to implement management strategies 
in an attempt to reduce the number of animal-vehicle collisions.  This 
broad-scale analysis should be aimed primarily at national mapping of the 
spatial distributions of species; improving the precision should be conducted 
by measuring wildlife-habitat relationships at a fine scale.  Wildlife 
managers and transportation planners must acquire more fine-scale habitat 
variables, such as microclimate, roadside vegetation, dead and downed logs, 
depth of surface water, and many other habitat features related to the 
movement and life prerequisites of the target species.  The inclusion of these 
variables into habitat models will improve the accuracy of habitat suitability 
maps. 
Construction of eco-bridges at suitable locations is not enough to 
effectively reduce animal-vehicle collisions.  Based on habitat suitability 
maps, we can identify candidate locations for linkages between suitable 
habitats at the broad scale.  Efforts should also be made to improve and 
maintain wildlife movements within corridors by expanding vegetative 
clearings along roadsides and reducing obstacles to movement based on the 
results of fine-scale field surveys.  Such management measures also affect 
the overall quality of the connectivity within a landscape for target species 
such as the wild boar.  Habitat suitability for reproduction and foraging will 
also be available from habitat suitability maps to indicate the daily and 
seasonal movement patterns of wildlife. 
In addition to combining habitat suitability maps at a broad scale and 
ecological characteristics at a fine scale, the particular structure of wildlife 
overpasses will also facilitate wildlife movement.  Wildlife overpass 
structures must be well designed, and other auxiliary facilities (i.e., fences, 
mirrors) should be constructed to funnel animals onto the overpasses, based 
on habitat suitability maps.  For example, wildlife overpasses for wild boar 
should be ≥ 70m wide with artificial ponds to provide drinking water, as well 
as fences and mirrors adjacent to the overpasses.

D. Future monitoring and research 
In Korea, including the provinces of Gangwon and Jeonnam, the number 
of wildlife-vehicle collisions has been increasing according to road-kill data 
from the Korea Highway Corporation.  To identify species of road-kill and 
locations of wildlife-vehicle collisions, the Korea Highway Corporation has 
conducted a road-kill survey of 23 highways (total length: 2,778km) since 
1998.  However, this survey has been conducted with a focus on road safety; 
thus, the main species of concern have been medium and large mammals, 
whereas information on other species, such as amphibians, reptiles, and 
insects, is rarely collected in the survey.  In some cases, the survey has not 
included fragmented habitats, even highly suitable habitats that are 
separated by secondary roads, because the survey is conducted only on major 
highways. 
To use road-kill data effectively in the selection of target species and sites 
for wildlife overpasses, the MOE must design a road-kill survey plan that 
encompasses all wildlife species, from invertebrates to amphibians, birds, 
and mammals.  Geographic locations of road-kill should be obtained using 
GPS to determine the spatial aggregation of wildlife-vehicle collisions. If 
possible, all paved roads with two or more lanes should be included in the 
proposed road-kill survey, because these roads are often adjacent to suitable 
habitat, but rank low in the amount of investment to reduce their effects on 
habitat fragmentation. 
Our results showed that habitat models and habitat suitability maps can 
be developed for wildlife, particularly target species, in developing wildlife 
overpasses.  The habitat suitability maps discussed in this chapter will be 
more effective if spatially explicit road-kill data are used to identify 
candidate locations for wildlife overpasses.  Currently, few attempts have 
been made to relate spatially explicit road-kill numbers with the habitat 
suitability map of target species associated with wildlife-vehicle collisions. 
Even though many wildlife species (e.g., Chinese water deer, raccoon dog, 
Eurasian badger) are killed on roads across Korea, habitat models have been 
developed for only a few species (e.g., wild boar, Korean hare, and 
Ring-necked Pheasant).  More efforts are necessary to develop habitat 
models for additional wildlife species.  Moreover, existing habitat models 
should be modified to enhance their reliability.

1. Overview 
 
Since the early 1960s, stream environments in Korea have been degraded 
because of rapid industrialization and urbanization.  Stream degradation 
has been caused by single-purpose channel work for flood control, the 
conversion of riparian habitats into urban land uses, and water pollution.  
Management practices, such as riverbank stabilization and channel 
straightening, have long been implemented while neglecting the ecological 
functions of rivers and riparian zones.  As a result, many rivers and streams 
have lost their ecological functions, such as providing habitats for wildlife 
and fisheries, water recharging, and supporting recreational activities such as 
angling and bird watching.  Currently, about 90% of all major streams 
(National Rivers and Class 1 Regional Rivers, total length of 4,100km) and 
60% of secondary streams (Class 2 Regional Rivers, total length of 26,000km) 
have been channelized (Woo et al. 2003), resulting in a major loss of riparian 
habitat for waterbirds, fishes, amphibians, reptiles, and aquatic mammals. 
The political and social value of preserving riparian ecosystems was 
emphasized in the early 1990s by a serious water pollution accident involving 
phenol on the Nakdong River in Korea.  The Korean government, as well as 
civil society and non-governmental organizations, have increasingly 
recognized the value of protection and preservation of riparian ecological 
functions, and the concept of ‘Close-to-Nature River Improvement’ was 
introduced as a management practice.  The Ministry of Environment (MOE) 
of the Republic of Korea established the ‘Comprehensive Measures on the 
Provision of Clean Water’ in 1993 for the protection of public health and the 
creation of pleasant water environments.  Moreover, the ‘Stream 
Environment Restoration Initiative’ has been conducted since 1998 by the 
Ministry of Construction and Transportation (MOCT), and seven streams, 
such as the O-san and Kyung-an, were selected for pilot projects. 
More recently, riparian habitat management strategies have promoted 
conservation and creation of habitat at the watershed scale, rather than at the

Riparian habitat management from a watershed perspective is a new 
paradigm for protecting and managing riparian habitat for fishes, 
amphibians, reptiles, waterbirds, and mammals.  Riparian habitat is 
overwhelmingly affected by non-point sources of polluted runoff from 
agricultural lands, residential areas, and highways within a watershed.  
Contrary to the conventional stream-focused approach, the new paradigm 
requires watershed-related variables, such as the composition of land-use 
types in the watershed, as essential factors affecting riparian ecosystems.  
Little research on riparian habitat management from a watershed perspective 
has been reported.  Thus, it is now necessary to include watershed-related 
variables in the development of riparian habitat models.  The objectives of 
this chapter were to examine habitat features related to riparian habitat 
management at the watershed scale; to evaluate watersheds based on the 
habitat suitability of wildlife living in the stream and riparian zone; and to 
determine which watersheds are suited for protection or restoration 
measures. 
 
2. Selection of target species: Eurasian otter 
 
As described above, most rivers and streams in Korea had degraded their 
ecological values and functions (i.e., wildlife habitats) by water pollution, 
over-stock, removal of instream and riparian vegetation, dredging, channel 
straightening and stream bank modification.  To prevent further riparian 
habitat degradation and to restore ecological functions of degraded stream, it 
is necessary to determine habitat features required to meet the needs of 
wildlife species in concern.  So far many degraded streams have been 
restored to their previous state, and in some case, fish and waterbird have 
been reintroduced.  For example, Cheongyecheon creek, which has long 
been used as a sewage system, was newly restored in Seoul, Korea. 
Recently, stream restoration was aimed to promote biological diversity

and ecological restoration.  To effectively conserve and manage riparian 
habitats, we should first designate which species are to be considered for the 
development of habitat model.  Habitat suitability model may not 
necessarily involve all species present in a river.  However, restoration of 
riparian ecosystem to simultaneously preserve all species will likely prove to 
be difficult, given the disproportion of wildlife species in riparian areas.  
Although many stream restoration projects have been conducted to create 
riparian habitats across the country, to date none of riparian habitat were 
successfully restored, especially for top vertebrate predator (i.e., Eurasian 
otter). 
It is effective in wildlife habitat conservation to find representative species 
for the wildlife species inhabited in instream and riparian zones.  Ideally, 
the habitat requirements of the target species should encapsulate the 
demands of other wildlife species in need of protection, so called umbrella 
effect (Simberloff 1998).  Habitat suitability model for the representative 
species is developed to identify the key environmental variables affecting 
species abundance and spatial distribution.  For the conservation of wildlife 
species and their habitats, we should begin with the selection of 
representative species as target species for the development of habitat 
suitability model.  A study may not necessarily involve all species present in 
a river.  The selection of single target species is usually influenced by the 
goals of riparian management in the area of interest and to some degree on 
the availability of feasible data and knowledge on suitability curves in the 
habitat suitability model.  The single target species approach was employed 
to identify key habitat features needed for preserving wildlife species of 
concern, and to implement management strategies on the riparian habitats. 
The Eurasian otter (Lutra lutra) was selected as a target species to 
effectively achieve the aim of the stream restoration project, because the otter 
species is a good indicator to represent health of a riparian ecosystem (Kruuk 
1995; Han 1997).  In Korea, the Eurasian otter is top predator in coastal and 
riparian habitats.  The species has relatively large home range and requires 
a variety of habitat features, such as pool, island, point-bar, rock, wetland 
and riparian forest.  Even though all individuals belong to the same species, 
otters have different characteristics between riparian areas and coastal areas.  
In this chapter, we focused to develop habitat suitability model for the otter 
in the riparian areas.  Besides being a top predator in the riparian area, the 
otter species is one of the most endangered wildlife whose population is

3. Watershed-based habitat suitability model for Eurasian otter 
A. Habitat use 
Otters are extremely shy animals, and may tend to act solitary in the sea or 
fresh water (Kruuk 1995).  Eurasian otters live along rivers, marshes, small 
streams, ditches, and lakes (Joo 2002).  The size of the home range depends 
on the quality of habitat, food resources and life stages/sex of the species (Dr. 
S-Y Han, IUCN/SSC otter specialist, pers. comm.).  Typically a home range 
for the species includes clean freshwater for food, and secure shelter i.e., 
riparian vegetation, rock structures and others.  Home range may extend 
from as little as 1.1km when providing enough fish for food resources along 
the coastline, and 40-km along the river when the species move seasonally for 
food and shelter (National Rivers Authority [NRA] 1993). 
In general, habitat use of the Eurasian otter has been conducted by using 
signs (footprints or spraints).  The effective evidence of the otters’ presence 
is the broad, five-toed footprints or droppings or ‘spraints’.  Territorial 
markings are left on prominent rocks or on tree roots along the edge of water.  
Spraints help describe Eurasian otters’ habitat characteristics (Manson and 
MacDonald 1986; Cho et al. 2005).  In lakes, reed beds are frequently used as 
feeding and resting sites for the Eurasian otter.  Along the rivers, the species 
show preferences for places where fish can be easily caught, under banks and 
near the riffles (Kruuk 1995; Han 1997; Lee and Son 2003; Jeong and Jo 2004). 
Suitable habitats supporting otters have relatively more bank-side natural 
vegetation, a greater density of trees, and more potential security sites for 
which vegetation, tree and rock appeared particularly important.  In the 
coastal region of Korea, otter habitat use was strongly correlated to riparian

vegetation, dam or river construction and human interferences such as % 
residential area, commercial and public facilities (Cho et al. 2005).  In the 
freshwater region of Korea, the Eurasian otter prefer areas with relatively 
more bankside natural vegetation, a greater density of trees, and more 
potential security sites for which vegetation, tree and rock appeared 
particularly important (Won et al. 2002; Lee and Son 2003; Min et al. 2004). 
B. Habitat model development 
The habitat conditions were assessed using qualitative habitat evaluation 
procedures (HEP; US Fish and Wildlife Service 1980b).  We selected five 
habitat variables for the Eurasian otter, based on habitat studies of the species 
(Kruuk 1995; Han 1997; Won 1998; Seo et al. 2001; Joo 2002; Jeong and Jo 2004). 
Habitat variables used in the model development are classified into two 
categories: 1) 100-m buffered area from the stream, and 2) habitat 
characteristics of watersheds.  The percent cover of water, human population 
density, and % urbanized land-use represent the quality of watershed, whereas 
the % woody cover and composition of steep slope within the 100-m buffered 
area from the stream represent the habitat quality of the riparian zone.  All 
habitat parameters were converted to suitability indices ranging from 0.0 
(completely unsuitable) to 1.0 (most suitable).  The habitat quality was 
assessed by a sum of scores obtained from the five variables and were 
categorized into five levels: unsuitable (0.0-0.3), less suitable (0.3-0.45), mediate 
suitable (0.45-0.55), good habitat (0.55-0.7), and excellent habitat (0.75-1.0). 
Eurasian otters usually inhabit clean rivers, seas, lakes, and wetlands, where 
food resources are plentiful and unpolluted resting sites (Dubuc et al. 1990; 
Kruuk 1995; Han 1997).  Therefore, the percent cover of water is an extremely 
important factor affecting the habitat of otters.  An increase in the percent 
cover of water at each site increases the habitat suitability.  For example, if % 
water reaches 1,000m2/ha, then the habitat suitability index becomes a value of 
1.0. 
Eurasian otters prefer areas where rocks and riparian vegetation exist.  In 
general, they use rocks or fallen trees as hiding-places, and select nesting 
places within willow plants in the middle of water (Dr. S-Y Han, IUCN/SSC 
otter specialist, pers. comm.).  Riparian vegetation provides a hiding and 
nesting place for otters.  Otters rarely live in areas without riparian vegetation 
(Min et al. 2004; Jeong and Jo 2004; Cho et al. 2005).  Thus, a place of no

Chapter 6. Conclusions 
The use of habitat-suitability maps with spatially explicit habitat variables 
is an important advancement for conservation planning, because habitat 
quality is a critical link between human land-use decisions and wildlife 
population viability.  Habitat suitability models were originally developed 
in the United States to assess the environmental impacts of human-induced 
developments on wildlife populations (U.S. Fish and Wildlife Service 1980a), 
and they are now widely used around the world, including Canada and 
Europe.  In this study, we introduced suitability maps derived from habitat 
suitability models to implement different wildlife management strategies. 
Given the current situation of data availability and ecological knowledge 
of habitat requirements, habitat models have been developed for leopard cat, 
wild boar, and Eurasian otter in Korea.  Habitat suitability maps were 
effectively used in the site selection for wildlife overpasses, designation of 
the boundaries of a wildlife protection area, and selection of watersheds for 
stream restoration and preservation.  The suitability maps were more 
effective when they included additional habitat suitability maps for other 
wildlife species related to each wildlife management objective. 
The benefits of GIS-based approaches to developing habitat suitability 
models are currently limited by our understanding of wildlife-habitat 
relationships.  The validity of habitat suitability models may be questionable, 
and the direct effects of variation in habitat suitability on wildlife survival 
rates are often unknown.  It is, therefore, important that model users 
evaluate these uncertainties and make them explicit, so that the results of 
habitat models can be interpreted with caution. 
Despite potential limitations, the GIS-based approach demonstrated here 
provides significant benefits.  The effect of land use on the viability of 
wildlife populations is a common management concern.  A spatially explicit 
habitat model can address this concern in the quantities of primary interest, 
i.e., the relative abundance and spatial distribution of wildlife species and 
their habitats. 
Habitat suitability models are an effective tool for managing wildlife 
habitat at local, regional, and national scales.  Although the model was

originally designed to delineate the most suitable habitat, collected data and 
models can serve numerous other purposes.  The data are a useful starting 
point for other efforts designed to protect wildlife habitat.  Some important 
applications include the ability to note spatial and temporal changes in the 
extent and distribution of vegetation types.  When coupled with information 
on other stressors, such as road construction and clear-cutting, habitat 
models can provide a stable planning and forecasting environment for 
assessing the impacts of human-induced changes. 
Additional efforts need to be made to refine and validate the models with 
additional data and expand the modeling efforts to cover additional species 
to represent different guilds of wildlife species and evaluate the tradeoffs 
between them in the planning process.  Particularly, efforts can be expanded 
to consider minimum sizes of the habitat patches for different species and 
possible meta-population dynamics considerations in the planning process.  
Combine the efforts with those of modeling to anticipate the trend and 
spatial pattern in land use change especially urbanization would be critical 
for effective planning for wildlife conservation and habitat restoration. 
The results of this research suggest the following procedures to develop a 
GIS-based habitat model for wildlife species in Korea: (1) organization of a 
three- to five-member panel of experts in landscape ecology, conservation 
biology, plant biology, animal behavior, and wildlife management; (2) 
acquisition of ecological knowledge through literature reviews, Delphi, field 
surveys, and other methods by selected panelists; (3) definition of spatial and 
temporal scales based on the study areas and life stages of the wildlife 
species of concern; (4) selection of appropriate habitat variables based on life 
requisites of the wildlife species; (5) development of a suitability index curve; 
(6) evaluation of the habitat model against real-world observations; (7) 
creation of habitat suitability maps for individual species; and (8) 
modification and improvement of the habitat models by adding or removing 
habitat variables and changing suitability index curves.

본 연구의 목적은 국내 여건을 감안하여 대표적인 생물종에 대해 서식모

널리 활용되고 있는 보호구역 설정이나 야생동물 이동통로의 설치 그리고 
생태하천 조성 등 중요한 서식지의 보호·관리에 있어 서식적합성지도를 효과
적으로 적용할 수 있도록 필요한 기반지식이나 현황자료, 그리고 모형 적용

대표적인 관리 대상종을 선정하고, 이들 생물종의 공간적 분포, 행동권 및 서
식환경에 대해 파악하였다.  서식환경에 대한 분석·평가를 토대로 서식적합
성모형을 개발하고, 지리정보시스템을 이용하여 서식적합성지도를 제작하여 
야생 동·식물의 서식지 보호 및 관리에 활용하도록 하였다. 또한 제2차 전국
자연환경조사를 비롯한 각종 자연환경 조사자료를 서식적합성지도와 중첩함

관련된 각 부분별 연구수행과정 및 결과를 정리하면 다음과 같다.

본 연구에서 삵, 멧돼지, 그리고 수달에 대한 서식모형을 개발하고, 이를
바탕으로 서식적합성지도를 제작하여 보호구역의 설정, 생태통로의 설치, 그

있음을 보여주었다. 미국에서 1980년대 개발사업에 따른 멸종위기 야생 동·
식물의 서식지 훼손을 평가하기 위해 최초로 도입된 서식적합성모형은 현재 
캐나다, 유럽연합 등 외국에서 서식지 보호 및 관리를 위해 널리 활용되고 
있으나, 우리나라에서는 일부 수렵동물이나 멸종위기종에 대해서만 부분적으

수립하기 위해서는 먼저 다음과 같은 애로사항을 해결해야 한다.
