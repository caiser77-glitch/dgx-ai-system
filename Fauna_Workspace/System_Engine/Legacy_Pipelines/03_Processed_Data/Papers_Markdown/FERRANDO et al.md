MONITORING RE-INTRODUCED OTTERS (LUTRA LUTRA) IN CATALONIA (NORTH 
EASTERN SPAIN) USING CYTOGENETIC AND MOLECULAR TECHNIQUES

Abstract.  The re-introduction programme of the Eurasian otter in north east Spain-”Projecte Llúdriga” led to 
the release of 42 individuals captured in different localities on the north and south west Iberian Peninsula 
between 1995 and 2002.  The viability and evolution of this population is being monitored using cytogenetic and 
molecular techniques. Karyotypes analysed for 16 re-introduced animals from different Iberian populations are 
all uniform in 2n=38, morphology and G-banding patterns.  No chromosomal rearrangements, that could affect 
fertility or viability of offspring, have been detected.  Heterochromatin polymorphisms have been found in C-
banding patterns. These polymorphisms are not related to the geographic origin of the individuals. The 
sequencing of 361-bp of the 5’ side of the mitochondrial control region showed that the 20 founder individuals 
analysed belong to a unique lineage and share the same haplotype. This haplotype is also present in 92% of the 
73 otters studied from eight European countries. PCR amplification and genotyping of individuals using ten 
polymorphic microsatellite markers showed between two and six alleles in preliminary analyses of a group of 22 
founder and offspring individuals. The genotyping is based on DNA successfully extracted from faeces and will 
allow the determination of basic demographic parameters of the re-introduced population, such as distribution 
and population density. 
 
Keywords. Re-introduction, Eurasian otter, Lutra lutra, karyotype, mtDNA control region, genotyping 
 
 
1   INTRODUCTION 
 
The first attempt to re-introduce Eurasian otters (Lutra lutra) to Spain, where the possibility 
of the population recovering was less than elsewhere in the country (RUIZ-OLMO AND 
DELIBES, 1998) was named “Projecte Llúdriga” and was intended to help the recovery of the 
species in north eastern Catalonia (Spain) after the disappearance of local populations during 
the 1980s (SAAVEDRA AND SARGATAL, 1997; SAAVEDRA, 2002).  Between 1995 and 2002, 42 
Eurasian otters captured in Portugal, western (Extremadura) and northern Spain (Asturias), 
were released in the “Parc Natural dels Aiguamolls de l’Empordà” and the Muga and Fluvià 
Rivers. 
 
The evolution of the re-introduced population has been monitored by radio- tracking, 
otter surveys and visual censuses (SAAVEDRA, 2002), as well as by cytogenetic and molecular 
studies.  We compared karyotypes of the founders to detect chromosomal particularities that 
could affect the viability of the re-introduced population.  We then analysed the mitochondrial 
genetic diversity of Iberian otters and compared sequences to those obtained in otters from six 
European countries and to sequences available in the literature and in Genbank.  We are 
currently analysing the evolution of the genetic diversity of the re-introduced population, 
using non-invasive methods by genotyping of spraints with ten microsatellite markers.  This 
genotyping will also allow us to determine basic demographic parameters, such as individual 
distribution and population density. 


2   MATERIAL AND METHODS 
 
2.1 Karyotyping with uniform and G/C banding 
We obtained cultures of peripheral blood lymphocytes for chromosomal analyses from 16 re-
introduced otters (Portugal n=11; Spain n=5). Metaphases were stained sequentially (uniform, 
G bands and C bands) and karyotypes were analysed (GARRABOU ET AL., 2003). 
 
2.2 Mitochondrial DNA control region  
We included samples from 20 founder and seven non-founder Iberian otters, and 46 samples 
from the United Kingdom, Norway, Sweden, Finland, Belarus and Russia.  We sequenced a 
fragment of mitochondrial control region using primers L-Pro and H-Phe (MUCCI ET AL., 
1999), MelCR2, MelCR3, MelCR4 and MelCR6 (MARMI ET AL., unpublished) following the 
amplification and PCR cycle and sequencing conditions described by MARMI (2004) or by  
MUCCI ET AL. (1999).  
When working with DNA extracted from spraints for genotyping with microsatellite 
markers, we first amplified a fragment of the mitochondrial control region expected to be of 
265 bp, using primers L-Pro and MelCR2.  This step was performed to check the quality of 
DNA extracted and the correct species identification of field samples.  PCR amplified 
samples that showed bands with low density in ethidium bromide-stained agarose gels or 
bands that presented a size different than expected were discarded from further analyses.  
 
2.3 Microsatellite genotyping 
We genotyped DNA from blood samples of founder individuals obtained during health 
inspection at the Parc Zoològic of Barcelona and from tissues of offspring found dead in the 
field. DNA from specimens living in the area of re-introduction, that could be either founders 
or their descendants was obtained from spraints.  These were collected and  stored at –80ºC.  
DNA was extracted using QIAamp DNA Stool Mini Kit (Qiagen).  The genotyping of 
individuals was performed using ten microsatellite markers: Lut435, Lut604, Lut701, Lut715, 
Lut717, Lut733, Lut818, Lut832, Lut833 and Lut902 (DALLAS AND PIERTNEY, 1998; DALLAS 
ET AL., 1999).  PCR products were run in an ABI 3100 automated DNA sequencer (Applied 
Biosystems) and we used GeneScan analysis software to measure the fragment sizes, 
including ROX-500 (Applied Biosystems) as a marker size. Heterozygosity values were 
calculated with GIMLET ver. 1.3.2 (VALIÈRE 2002).  
 
 
3   RESULTS AND DISCUSSION 
 
3.1 Karyotyping with uniform and G/C banding 
All otters presented 2n=38 chromosomes, and no numerical nor structural chromosomal 
abnormalities were detected in any karyoptype.  Heterochromatin polymorphism found after 
C-banding analysis showed size variations in the centromeric band of 13 chromosome pairs, 
in the C+ band of the short arm in three pairs, and in the terminal band of four pairs.  No 
polymorphism could be considered related to the geographical origin of individuals.  No 
chromosomal rearrangements that could affect adult fertility or offspring viability were 
detected. 
 
3.2 Mitochondrial DNA control region fragment analysis 
We sequenced a 361-bp fragment from the left side of the control region from 73 individuals 
and found five polymorphic sites resulting in seven haplotypes (H1-H7). Haplotype H1 were 


shared by 92% of the individuals from different countries and was the only found in the 
Iberian Peninsula.  We found that the genetic variability among all individuals was extremely 
low, as previously reported (MUCCI ET AL., 1999; CASSENS ET AL., 2000).  
We extracted DNA from 53 spraints, and prior to microsatellite genotyping, amplified 
a short fragment of mitochondrial control region known to be 265-bp in otters.  Fifteen 
samples were then discarded, four because the fragment amplified was not of 265 bp and thus 
were identified as non-otter sample, and 11 because of poor DNA intensity in the band of the 
agarose gel.  
 
3.3 Microsatellite genotyping 
All microsatellite markers, Lut435, Lut604, Lut701, Lut715, Lut717, Lut733, Lut818, 
Lut832, Lut833 and Lut902 studied in 22 otters samples, including founders and descendants, 
were polymorphic and presented between two and six alleles.   Observed heteregosity values 
ranged between 0.14 and 0.82, with a mean of 0.59. We have currently amplified between one 
and ten microsatellite loci from 31% of spraints and 60% of anal jelly samples collected, with 
a higher amplification success in these last samples.
