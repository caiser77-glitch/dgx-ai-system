THE RENAISSANCE OF THE OTTER (LUTRA LUTRA)  TO ENGLISH RIVERS 
 
Rob STRACHAN1 and Andrew CRAWFORD2

Abstract. The fourth National Otter Survey for England was carried out from January 2000 to February 2002. Signs 
of otters were found at 34% of the 3,327 sites visited.  This represents a 527% increase between 1977-79 and 2000-
02, based on the original 2,940 sites of the baseline survey. 
The 2000-02 survey confirms that the increase in otter distribution seen since the first survey is continuing. 
Every one of the 12 regions and catchments into which England is divided for the survey, has recorded an increase 
in otter activity. The scale of increase varies considerably but it is significant that the leading edge of the recovering 
population continues to expand. This appears to be creating large areas with otter populations at low density 
followed by consolidation which seems to be some years after the leading edge has passed. Full recovery is thought 
to be several decades away. 
Increases in range have taken place without wholesale improvements to habitat. Poor in-stream habitat 
limits fish stocks on some rivers and may hinder otter recovery. Other potential barriers include deaths from road 
kills, remaining a serious problem for otters colonising new areas in England. As the otter returns in many areas, 
new potential conflicts with fishery interests have also arisen. 
 
Keywords. Eurasian otter, Lutra lutra, National Survey, England, recovery, conservation concerns 
 
 
1   INTRODUCTION 
 
The Eurasian otter (Lutra lutra) is often seen as a flagship of biodiversity conservation in the UK 
and is regarded as an important biological indicator of the health of rivers and wetlands. 
Monitoring the status of the otter, therefore, gives us a valuable measure of the state of our 
watercourses and wetland ecosystems. Water quality in English rivers has vastly improved since 
the 1950s and is still improving. However, the 1950s witnessed a dramatic and widespread 
decline of the otter over its entire range in England, coinciding with the agricultural use of a suite 
of organochlorine pesticides (particularly aldrin and dieldrin that were used in sheep dip and as 
seed dressing). These highly toxic and persistent chemicals were banned from use between 1966 
(sheep dips) and 1975 (seed dressings) but by this time they had already bio-accumulated within 
the food chain and impacted on the local otter populations (STRACHAN AND JEFFERIES, 1996). By 
the mid 1970s, the population of otters on English rivers had been reduced to a point where only 
remnant numbers remained. 
In England, as in most of its range, the otter is largely nocturnal and rarely observed in the 
wild, but it is possible to determine its presence by searching for its distinctive faeces (spraints) 
and footprints.  
   
1.1 Baseline surveys 
The first National Otter Survey of England was carried out during 1977-79 (LENTON, CHANIN 
AND JEFFERIES, 1980). The sampling strategy involved the systematic searches of alternate 50 
kilometre squares across the country and covered a total of 2,940 sites. These core sites formed 
the baseline for future repeat surveys. Similar surveys were also established at the same time in 


Wales, Scotland and Ireland (CRAWFORD ET AL., 1979; GREEN AND GREEN, 1980; CHAPMAN AND 
CHAPMAN, 1982).  
Of the 2,940 sites surveyed in 1977-79, only 170 (5.8%) showed evidence of otters. This 
confirmed that the species had become absent or sparsely distributed over much of lowland and 
central England. Signs revealed that individuals still remained in the south west of the country 
and along the Welsh borders. Small and fragmented populations were recorded in East Anglia 
and northern counties. Subsequent surveys and research have demonstrated that this period was 
probably the nadir of the decline that began in the late 1950s (STRACHAN AND JEFFERIES, 1996). 
The national survey of England was repeated in 1984-86 (STRACHAN ET AL., 1990) and 
1991-94 (STRACHAN AND JEFFERIES, 1996) using the same methods and visiting the same sites. A 
further 249 sites were added during the 1984-86 survey to improve coverage, bringing the total to 
3,189 sites. Of these, 284 (8.9%) were positive in 1984-86 and 706 (22.1%) were recorded as 
positive in 1991-94, confirming that the populations were gradually recovering. 
Since the 1991-94 otter survey of England, the UK has fulfilled its commitment to the 
Convention on Biological Diversity signed at the 1992 Rio Summit by producing the UK 
Biodiversity Action Plan (BAP). The otter is one of 400 priority species for which a species 
action plan has been written (HMSO, 1995). Highlighted in the plan was the need for co-
ordinated action and monitoring to ensure the restoration of fully viable populations across its 
historic range in the UK. The UK Otter BAP Steering Group (made up of representatives from 
statutory conservation bodies and other key organisations such as The Wildlife Trusts) has acted 
as the management group for promoting and overseeing the present survey. 
 
 
2   DISTRIBUTION AND STATUS 2000-02 
 
The Fourth National Otter Survey for England was carried out January 2000 to February 2002 
(CRAWFORD, 2003) and re-visited all previous survey sites. These were originally selected for the 
1977-79 survey at five to eight kilometre intervals along main rivers, lake shores and coasts 
within alternate 50 kilometre squares (north western and south eastern quarters of each 100 
kilometre square). Due to time constraints in the baseline survey only 28 squares were covered 
(LENTON, CHANIN AND JEFFERIES, 1980). The second national survey added four more squares 
(STRACHAN ET AL., 1990) and this compliment was repeated for the third survey (STRACHAN AND 
JEFFERIES, 1996). For the 2000-02 survey it was decided that all alternate 50 kilometre squares 
should be surveyed, even where they might contain very few sites (such as where the corner of a 
square clipped the English coastline). This now gave 38 squares and 3,327 survey sites in total 
(CRAWFORD, 2003). 
 
2.1 Methods 
The standard otter survey methodology was used following that adopted by LENTON, CHANIN 
AND JEFFERIES, 1980. At each survey site, a 600 metre search was carried out, usually 300 metre 
upstream and downstream of a bridge or other access point. To reduce survey time, the 2000-02 
survey stopped the search effort as soon as the first signs of otters were detected. As far as 
possible, the surveys were re-examined in the same chronological order and at the same time of 
year (conducted within four weeks) as the date of the previous survey. Severe flooding in the 
autumn and winter of 2000 caused some delays which were further compounded by the outbreak 
of foot and mouth disease in 2001, which prevented access to sites. These events led to the 


completion of some sites being delayed 12 months, but it is not thought that this had altered the 
results in any way (CRAWFORD, 2003).

Of the 3,327 sites surveyed during 2000-2002, 1,137 (34%) were found positive. Of the 
2,940 sites examined in all four surveys, 1,066 (36%) were positive. This represents an increase 
of 527% since the baseline survey of 1977-1979. 
The percentage of positive sites has increased in every English region or river catchment, 
into which the otter survey had been divided. However, the increases vary considerably between 
regions and catchments (Table 1).  
Figure 2 depicts relative change in number of sites occupied by otters in five 
representative English regions. Recovery has been strongest in south west England with the 
number of sites occupied in excess of 80%. Major increases have been observed in Northumbrian 
and Anglian regions as well as the Severn and Trent catchments. Southern region on the other 
hand has only revealed minor improvement with the number of positive sites still less than 6% of 
the total sites examined.

The prediction for 2002 was for site occupancy of between 44-50%, but these figures 
were of course only approximations, especially since the survey only examined alternative 50 
kilometre squares and some river catchments such as the Thames have had major expansions in 
otter populations outside of the survey areas. The increase to 34% of sites positive in 2000-2002 
demonstrates a real and continuing recovery of the otter population in England. Nevertheless, 
there were significant gaps in distribution and on many of the catchments the otter populations 
are clearly at lower numbers than the potential carrying capacity of these areas. The most 
important message from the recovery curve predicted by STRACHAN AND JEFFERIES (1996), was 
that full recovery was still “several decades away” and that 99.9% occupancy would take 112 
years. 
The overwhelming majority of the expansion in the range of the otter recorded in the 
2000-2002 survey has occurred by natural re-colonisation. The consolidation in south west 
region, the expansion in north west, Northumbrian, Wessex regions and the vast majority of the 
expansion in Severn and Trent catchments has been by natural re-colonisation. These results of 
the survey are consistent with a “leading edge” expanding to create areas with low otter 
populations followed by consolidation of the areas behind the leading edge continuing for many 
years.  
In addition to the natural expansion, at least 140 otters have been released on to English 
rivers since 1983. The majority have been from captive bred origins and released into the Anglian 
region (conducted by the Otter Trust). In addition to these, The Vincent Wildlife Trust released at 
least 49 rehabilitated otters between 1990 and 1996. The upsurge in otter activity in East Anglia, 
Thames, Yorkshire and the east Midlands detected by the 2000-2002 survey can be directly 
attributed to the various otter releases.  
In Anglian region numerous discrete populations have now been established by 
introductions, where natural re-colonisation usually takes the form of a leading edge advancing 
from an adjacent otter population. It is thought that each of the discrete, introduced populations is 
likely to go through a period of expansion followed by consolidation (CRAWFORD, 2003). 
 
 
3   DISCUSSION 
 
Since the 1950s there has been major degradation of river and riparian habitats in England, 
mostly as a result of agricultural intensification and the associated drainage of wetlands and river 
engineering for land drainage. There has also been considerable and continuing urban 
encroachment along river corridors and over floodplains. However, the results of the present 
survey seem to indicate that habitat is not a major limiting factor as otters are now using many 
areas previously thought severely sub-optimal.  It would appear that even on catchments with 
highly fragmented riparian habitat, otters find sufficient refuge sites to prevent exclusion.  Otters 
are also using rivers in urban areas in many parts of lowland and western England, a trend noted 
in the 1991-94 survey report (STRACHAN AND JEFFERIES, 1996).  Although disturbance had once 
been considered a problem (O’CONNOR ET AL.1977), otters in urban fringe habitats have been 
observed with young cubs (HAMILTON-JAMES, 2002) so it is to be assumed that they can and do 
breed successfully in such environments.  However, this does not mean that such habitats can 
support a viable breeding population.  Breeding female otters may be strongly dependent on safe 
refuge sites free from deliberate or accidental disturbance and with a readily available food 
supply (LILES, 2003). It is not possible to know whether breeding success in urban areas is at the 


same levels as that in more rural environments or if there is a greater risk of road-kill on busier 
urban roads.  It would therefore be unwise to be complacent about ‘urban otters’ and their 
apparent return to sub-optimal habitats. The quality of river and riparian habitat is still considered 
vital to the full restoration of otter populations (CRAWFORD, 2003).  
 
3.1 Habitat and food availability 
If habitat in its broadest sense, including in-channel habitat, is considered, then the impact on 
aquatic productivity in general and on fish populations in particular may be of considerable 
importance.  Fish species are dependent on a variety of river habitats for different life stages such 
as spawning, fry survival and juvenile and adult habitat and some species such as the eel and 
salmon must be able to migrate.   
As with all species, the carrying capacity of any environment is largely a function of food 
availability and the availability of lying up and/or breeding sites although human persecution can 
be a major limiting factor particularly with predators. Other effects of human activity such as 
road deaths and disturbance may also be important.  It is difficult to separate these impacts but 
where refuge sites are plentiful then food supply could be the controlling factor.  As otters will 
take a wide variety of prey, but are very much fish specialists, the biomass and availability of fish 
species would be expected to be a key issue (KRUUK ET AL., 1993).  The prey requirement and 
food needs of otters were recently reviewed in CHANIN (2003). However precise food 
requirements are not known and this, combined with the very large and variable home range, 
makes it difficult to determine the lowest level of fish biomass below which it ceases to be 
possible for an otter to exploit a river system. The abundance of prey such as amphibians may 
also be important for otters that may search and travel over small watercourses of low 
productivity in order to locate spawning ponds in the spring. For instance, population estimates of 
up to 480 frogs per hectare have been calculated from a spawning marsh in north east Scotland 
where otters are known to hunt (WEBER, 1990). 
The main factors which may currently be limiting fish populations are poor water quality 
and the historic physical degradation of river habitat through engineering works.  Water quality 
along all English rivers, particularly in urban areas, is generally improving, mainly due to 
massive investment in sewage infrastructure, to reduce the impact of episodic pollution from 
storm-water run-off, and in sewage works to improve the quality of the final effluent 
ENVIRONMENT AGENCY (2001). There is, however, still significant room for improvement on 
many rivers.  In addition, the impact of agricultural pollution from farm wastes, artificial 
fertilisers and other agri-chemicals is now a growing focus of concern for both the statutory 
agencies and the agricultural sector.  The insidious impact of chronic diffuse pollution (such as 
agricultural run-off) on the quality of our river environments is a very difficult problem to solve.   
General water quality, even acting through a reduction in prey biomass, is unlikely to be a 
significant obstacle to the continuing recovery of the otter, but there is a need for the continued 
surveillance of toxic environmental contaminants in watercourses. In addition to organo-chlorines 
and poly-chlorinated bi-phenyls, it may be important to sample for other compounds that may 
bio-accumulate in otters with as yet unforeseen affects. These compounds include synthetic 
pyrethroids, brominated flame retardants and other hormone mimicking or endocrine disrupting 
substances (BRADSHAW AND SLATER, 2002). 
 
3.2 Otter breeding requirements 
The breeding requirements of otters were recently reviewed in LILES (2003). The term breeding 
site is used to describe an area of land that contains one or more den sites, security from 


disturbance, play areas for cubs, no risk of flooding and access to a good food supply. This has 
been given as distinct from the term natal holt, which describes a small space occupied by the 
female when she gives birth and where the cubs stay for up to three months. Due to the secretive 
nature of the female when cub rearing, natal dens can be difficult to find (CHANIN, 1985; KRUUK, 
1995). 
LILES (2003) recognised three conservation actions that were likely to be required in order to 
ensure that breeding sites are available: 
 
• 
Protecting existing breeding sites (both actual and potential). 
• 
Creating or enhancing new sites for otter breeding. 
• 
Protecting or creating feeding sites associated with breeding sites. 
 
One of the main aims for otter conservation is to ensure that otter populations are maintained at 
favourable conservation status at the river catchment or sub-catchment level. For this to be 
achieved, suitable breeding sites must be available throughout the associated river catchments, 
lochs, coastline or islands. The provision of refuge sites has been the focus of otter habitat 
restoration work by The Wildlife Trusts’ Otters and Rivers Project (now known as Water for 
Wildlife). This has included the provision of underground or log-pile holts, the provision of 
riparian woody cover and the creation of wetlands (ENVIRONMENT AGENCY, 1999). The focus of 
this work has been to use the otter as a flagship for the wider aquatic environment and on using 
the enhancement work as an educational tool to advance the cause of habitat.    
 
3.3 Stillwater fisheries 
One of the issues that has grown in prominence since the 1991-1994 survey is the impact of 
otters on fisheries interests. Fish farms and specialist carp fisheries have both reported levels of 
predation by otters which are of concern. Otter predation on fish is a natural occurrence and it is 
the uniquely artificial nature of some fisheries that makes them particularly vulnerable to otters. 
The conflict occurs only in circumstances where the monetary value of individual fish is very 
high (for example in carp ponds where specimen fish may be worth several thousand pounds) or 
in intensive rearing units (where the loss of individual fish has a direct cost to the farm). In these 
cases advice from otter conservationists has led to the erection of otter-proof fencing (such as 
permanent or temporary electric fencing) and decoy/sacrificial ponds. There is a theory that the 
costs of the fencing should lie with the fisheries involved. However, it is now accepted by the UK 
otter Biodiversity Action Plan steering group that specimen carp fisheries may be a special case 
and a funding route is being sought to address this problem.  
   
3.4 Road casualties 
Road casualties have been recognised for many years as a potential threat to otters and GREEN 
(1991) considered that road traffic was the largest and most rapidly expanding cause of otter 
mortality.   
In south west England there has been a direct rise in the number of annual otter road 
casualties in relation to an increase in the local otter population (Figure 4). 
 


 
 
Figure 4: Annual otter road casualties in south west England in relation to increase in local population. 
 
The Highways Agency has funded investigations into the extent of otter road mortalities 
and developed detailed advice on mitigation techniques to reduce otter mortality (GROGAN, 
PHILCOX AND MACDONALD, 2001). The study gave clear evidence that otter road deaths peak at 
such times as high rainfall and spate conditions, when they are forced out of rivers by high flows 
and surcharged culverts. Following this report greater effort is now being put into the provision 
of otter road kill mitigation techniques for new road schemes, bridge replacements and road 
widening schemes. A significant but un-quantified resource has been put into underpasses, ledges 
and otter-proof fencing.  
Otter road deaths continue to be recorded throughout England and at present do not 
appear to be limiting otter population growth (although they may lead to temporary local losses 
until the area is replaced by immigration of dispersing animals).

4   CONCLUSIONS 
 
The fourth National Survey of the otter in England has confirmed that the species has continued 
to increase its population and range throughout the area. The scale of increase varies considerably 
but it is significant that the leading edge of the recovering population continues to expand. This 
appears to be creating large areas with otter populations at low density followed by consolidation 
which seems to be some years after the leading edge has passed. In some parts of its range (such 
as in the south west) the otter can now be considered ubiquitous, occupying sites in urban areas 
and in highly disturbed waterways where it seems unaffected by human activity. Full recovery, 
however, is thought to be several decades away. 
Water quality improvement and improved fish stocks are thought to have assisted the 
continued recovery of the otter, although there are still concerns about pollution issues within 
some watercourses (particularly the insidious effects of diffuse pollution). As the otter returns in 
many areas, new potential conflicts with fishery interests have also arisen, but otter 
conservationists are actively seeking solutions to resolve these conflicts.
