DISTRIBUTION SURVEY AND HABITAT PREFERENCES OF THE EURASIAN OTTER 
(LUTRA LUTRA) IN THE CZECH REPUBLIC 
 
Kevin ROCHE1 & Aleš TOMAN2 
 
1.  Czech Otter Foundation Fund, P.O. Box 53, Třeboň 379 01, Czech Republic (email: Otter@cbox.cz) 
2.  SOF AOPK, Ledeč nad Sáyavou, Czech Republic (email: pavlov.nature@worldonline.cz) 
 
Abstract: The first Czech national otter survey to use physical signs took place over 1989-92. A total of 28.5% of 
squares were registered positive and three separate sub-populations noted. Between 1997-2003, a second 
national survey took place, which also noted habitat variables. Fifty percent of the 646 squares surveyed were 
recorded as positive. Both northern and southern sub-populations have increased in range and have now 
connected. Immigrants from Poland and Germany probably contributed to expansion in the north. Twenty-nine 
otters re-introduced in the north east will help connect the latter population with that in the east. The use of 
many volunteers gave problems with survey form completion and assessment of density was not possible.  Otters 
were found in all habitat categories, with significant relationships with increasing depth up to two metres, no 
apparent pollution, natural banks, vegetation of mixed forest, and altitudes of between 400-1000 metres. Though 
not significant, otters were also found more often in rivers of increasing width and in moving water (slow). No 
relationship was noted with type of pollutant, probably due to the subjective nature of the question. Ninety-five 
percent of all positive signs were found within 50 metres of a bridge.  
 
Keywords: Lutra lutra, Eurasian otter, distribution, recovery, habitat preference, Czech Republic 
 
 
1   INTRODUCTION 
 
The first national survey for otters, using field observation of evidence (spraints and tracks), 
took place in the Czech Republic between 1989 and 1992.  Formerly widespread, otters were 
now reduced to three separate sub-populations (Figure 1), with scattered individuals in the 
west. The population in the north was connected with that in Germany and Poland, that in the 
east with Poland and Slovakia, and the largest in the south, a true meta-population spreading 
into Germany and Austria.  The main reasons for the evident decline were believed to be 
pollution and over-hunting, with survival of the southern population ensured by readily 
available food from carp fishponds.  A total of 28.5% squares were registered positive and 
total numbers were estimated at 300-350 individuals (TOMAN, 1992).

2   METHODS 
 
In an attempt to reduce time and cost, the survey was undertaken with the help of volunteers, 
mainly from the State nature protection agencies, all of whom had previously taken part in 
otter surveys.  Surveyors were asked to monitor three sites per square (Grid Mapping System 
of Central Europe (EHRENDORFER AND HAMANN, 1965), square ca. 11.2 x 12 kilometres), 
with a further two spot-checks if these original sites proved negative.  Sites were to be 
monitored in spring and autumn in order to increase the chances of finding positive signs (see 
REUTHER ET AL., 2000).  Sites within a square were first chosen from a 1:100,000 map.  
Where possible, a bridge was chosen as the start point and 600 metres of bankside searched.  
A site was recorded as positive, and surveying stopped, when either spraints or tracks were 
found. Surveyors filled out a survey record form that included data on site characteristics (up 
to 5 categories) for analysis of possible factors associated with presence or absence.  Site 
characteristic data were calculated as the percentage of positive sites of all sites monitored in 
a category, and was tested for significance using the χ2 goodness of fit test (Table 1).

3    RESULTS AND CONCLUSIONS 
3.1.  Distribution survey 
A total of 2,342 sites were monitored in 646 squares.  Thirteen squares could not be 
monitored but were noted as believed positive or negative based on local site knowledge. 
Eighty-seven percent of all sites were monitored between 1998 and 2000, and 84% in spring 
or autumn.  Of the 646 squares surveyed, 323 (635 sites) were recorded as positive for otter 
presence (Figure 2), or 50% of all squares (27% of sites).  This does, however, include at least 
20 squares where otters were introduced.  Minimum numbers are now estimated at between 
800-1000 individuals.  Otters have increased their distribution in all areas, except those on the 
eastern border.  The southern and northern populations are now connected, and the introduced 
otters (29 individuals by 2002) are complementing rapid natural expansion (note the increase 
in range between 2002 and 2003 – Figure 2).  By the end of the survey, it was clear that all 
three formerly isolated populations would be fully connected in the immediate future, only the 
main industrial and agricultural areas now remaining uncolonised.  
In the north, immigration from Poland & Germany is likely to have contributed to 
southward spreading, however, immigration from the north east is unlikely as BRZEZÍNSKI ET 
AL. (1996) found almost no otters in this area of Poland.  
2.2  Habitat survey 
Otters were found in all categories monitored, but were found significantly more often at 
water bodies with increasing depth up to two metres, no apparent pollution, natural banks, 
bank side vegetation comprising mainly mixed forest and at altitudes of between 400 and 
1000 metres (Figure 3, Table 1). Though not at significant levels, otters were also found more 
often in water bodies with increasing width and at sites with moving water, slow rather than 
fast.

The lack of any pattern in type of pollutant was probably due to the subjective nature of the 
question (i.e. not based on water quality, rather on site appearance). In addition, much of the 
widespread household waste (e.g. plastic, paper, etc) had been deposited following heavy 
flooding in the late 1990s, and did not necessarily reflect water quality.  Ninety-five percent 
of all positive signs were found within 50 metres of the bridge start point (Figure 4), 
supporting the idea that search distance could be reduced to save time, at least in areas of 
higher otter density (see REUTHER ET AL., 2000).

3.3.  Improvements 
Whereas the present survey fulfilled its purpose, there were a number of areas where future 
surveys could be improved.  The use of a high number of volunteers (eventually >40) proved 
a false economy, resulting in incomplete or missing site forms, delayed return of forms, 
inability to do sites, etc.  The inconsistent number of forms for each square also meant it was 
not possible to estimate otter density.  Further, the survey took too long to complete, earlier 
survey sites being found to be positive later.  Therefore, the minimum number of experienced 
surveyors will undertake future surveys and four sites will be surveyed in every square.  The 
survey should not take more than two years, and should be repeated every five.  Subjective 
questions, such as pollution type, resulted in inconclusive answers; therefore, questions on 
habitat should always be based on measurable data.  Finally, the grid system used, whilst the 
standard in the Czech Republic, does not allow comparison with other national surveys.  
Therefore, the 10x10 kilometre UTM grid should become the standard for all national species 
surveys, or GPS site co-ordinates taken for eventual transfer to a UTM map.  (For further 
discussion of improvements, see REUTHER ET AL. (2000)).
