Abstract
During the last century, otter populations in the
Mediterranean area of the Iberian Peninsula were dramat-
ically reduced and disappeared in many localities. A rein-
troduction programme was established in north-eastern
Spain (Muga and Fluvia` basins and the ‘‘Aiguamolls de
l’Emporda`’’ wetlands), by releasing 42 otters from four
different Iberian populations, between 1995 and 2002. In
order to evaluate the success of the reintroduction pro-
gramme, we investigated microsatellite variation in the
native populations of released otters as well as in the
population present in the release area in 2004. We used
non-invasively collected samples as the DNA source to
avoid disturbing the animals in the wild. Laboratory pro-
cedures included the screening of samples and a multiple-
tubes approach to detect and correct genotyping errors. Our
results show that founders have been replaced by descen-
dants in the release area and the population is in Hardy-
Weinberg equilibrium, with a 1:1 sex ratio. Western
Iberian otters—representing two-thirds of the founder
group and released earlier—have greatly contributed to the
genetic composition of the current population. The genetic
patterns of otters found in a basin north of the studied area
suggest a common origin with the released population. We
also detected in a few otters the presence of alleles not
identiﬁed in the original founder group, and we shall dis-
cuss the possible origin of these alleles.

Introduction
The Eurasian otter, Lutra lutra, is a semi-aquatic carnivore
of the family Mustelidae, fully protected in Spain since
1973.
Formerly,
the
species
was
widely
distributed
throughout Eurasia, following a Paleartic distribution
(Kruuk 1995). During the second half of the twentieth
century, the species suffered a severe decline, especially in
western-central Europe, mainly due to water pollution
(leading
to
bioaccumulation
of
contaminants
in
the
otter’s tissues), decrease of food availability, loss of habi-
tat, over-exploitation of water resources, persecution and
other man-induced causes of mortality (Macdonald and
Mason 1994). In the Iberian Peninsula, many local popu-
lations in Mediterranean rivers disappeared. However, otter
populations remain abundant in the west of the Iberian
Peninsula (Ruiz-Olmo and Delibes 1998), and they show
high levels of microsatellite allelic diversity (Randi et al.
2003).

During the last few decades, many efforts have been
made to conserve habitat, i.e., establishing Natural Parks
and regenerating wetlands. In 1993, a programme was
designed to reintroduce the otter in the Muga and Fluvia`
basins and the ‘‘Aiguamolls de l’Emporda`’’ wetlands
(Saavedra and Sargatal 1998). A viability study showed
that the proposed reintroduction area was suitable and
could sustain a population of more than a hundred indi-
viduals (Saavedra 2002). The closest native otter popula-
tions were located in western basins and the closest
individuals inhabited the River Segre, about 100 km away.
Native populations were spontaneously recovering due to
habitat improvement, and naturally colonising new streams
(Ruiz-Olmo and Delibes 1998; Ruiz-Olmo 2001). Telem-
etry studies show that otters usually have large home ran-
ges that can extend over tens of kilometres, and they can
travel over long distances in a short period (Kruuk 1995;
Sjo¨a˚nsen 1997; Saavedra 2002). However, the existence of
migration barriers (i.e., highly polluted basins, high
mountains without rivers nearby, dams difﬁcult or impos-
sible to cross) made the colonisation of the proposed
reintroduction area of the Muga and Fluvia` basins unlikely
(Ruiz-Olmo and Delibes 1998).
Between 1995 and 2002, 42 otters were progressively
reintroduced into the Muga and Fluvia` basins and the
Emporda` wetlands. A total of 41 otters were released
between 1995 and 2000, and one in 2002. All of them
originated from western Iberian thriving populations,
except one specimen from the River Segre, in the north-
east (Fig. 1). Before release, otters were kept at the
Barcelona Zoo for sanitary inspection and quarantine, and
for intraperitoneal transmitter implantation (Ferna´ndez-
Mora´n et al. 2001, 2002). Blood and serum samples were
conserved. A few animals died for different reasons, and
tissue samples were also preserved. Once released, rein-
troduction success was evaluated by monitoring the
population by radiotracking, visual census and detection
of otter spraints and footprints (Saavedra 2002). The
monitoring showed that the population persisted over
time,
bred
regularly
and
was
gradually
dispersing
throughout the release area and to the neighbouring Ter
and Tec basins, to the south and north, respectively
(Saavedra 2002).
Reintroduced populations are usually small and genetic
aspects must be considered for their management (Leberg
1990; Arrendal et al. 2004). A genetic study was started to
assess the evolution of the reintroduced otter population, by
comparing genetics of the founder group to the population
living in the reintroduction area in 2004, as a primary ge-
netic evaluation of the success of the reintroduction pro-
gramme. As mentioned above, surveys conducted before
release failed to detect the presence of any native indi-
vidual in the area, and no signs of otters were found in
rivers located between the reintroduction area and the more
western otter populations. Therefore, it was assumed that
only released otters (founders) contributed to the genetic
composition of this population. Non-invasively collected
samples were used as the DNA source to obtain genotypes
from a representative sample of the population living in the
reintroduction area. Otters have a sprainting behaviour and
deposit small drops of faeces (spraints) or anal secretions
(jellies) in evident places (Kruuk 1995). Spraints and jellies
are usually easy to recognise and have been commonly
used as a signal of otter presence; their use as a DNA
source for genetic studies on Eurasian otters is increasing
(Coxon et al. 1999; Dallas et al. 2003; Hung et al. 2004)
Non-invasive molecular techniques have beneﬁted the
study of rare, elusive and protected species, avoiding
disturbance to wild populations. Scats are one of the
easiest samples to obtain from carnivore species and have
been used for genetic studies in brown bear (Taberlet
et al. 1997; Murphy et al. 2003; Bellemain and Taberlet
2004), Malayan sun bear and American black bear
(Wasser et al. 1997), grey wolf (Lucchini et al. 2002;
Creel et al. 2003), red wolf and coyote (Adams et al.
2003), wolverine (Hedmark et al. 2004), Eurasian badger
(Frantz et al. 2003), mountain lion (Ernest et al. 2000),
and Iberian lynx (Palomares et al. 2002). Non-invasive
techniques have been employed for different purposes: to
identify species and individuals (Ernest et al. 2000;
Lucchini et al. 2002; Palomares et al. 2002), to clarify
paternities and relationships (Constable et al. 2001), to
study individual foraging behaviour (Fedriani and Kohn
2001), and to estimate population size (Kohn et al. 1999).
The use of non-invasive sampling methods has well-
known limits, especially when genotyping microsatellite
markers, which can accumulate errors produced during
PCR due to: allelic dropout (ADO), i.e., only one allele
of a heterozygous individual is ampliﬁed, thus producing
a false homozygote; and false alleles (FA), i.e., a PCR

monitor the evolution of the population. Our aim is to:
(a) detect and measure changes in the genetic composi-

release, as a ﬁrst evaluation of the programme’s success,

PCR ampliﬁcation and genotyping with microsatellite
markers
The 39 otter samples were genotyped with 10 polymorphic
microsatellite markers—two dinucleotide and eight te-
tranucleotide markers—isolated from Lutra lutra: Lut435,
Lut604, Lut701, Lut715, Lut717, Lut733, Lut782, Lut818,
Lut832 and Lut902 (Dallas and Piertney 1998; Dallas et al.
1999), and otter male-speciﬁc SRY gene markers (Dallas
et al. 2000), labelled with dyes 6-FAM, NED or HEX.
Microsatellite PCR ampliﬁcation of DNA extracted from
blood, tissue or serum was performed in a 10ll volume
containing 50–100 ng of DNA or 2ll of DNA extract, 0.3
units of Ecotaq (Ecogen), 1 · PCR buffer, 2.5 mM MgCl2,
1.6 mM dNTPs and 0.3lM of primers. The PCR pro-
gramme was started with a denaturation step at 94C for
3 min, followed by 32–40 cycles (94C for 45 s, 58–62C
for 45 s, 72C for 45 s), and an extension step at 72C for
10 min. We added 0.02% BSA to all reactions when
working with DNA templates extracted from serum,
spraints and jellies.

Genetic analyses
Otters were grouped as follows: donor populations (DP),
release area (RE), Tec (Tc), Segre (Se) and Tet (Tt) basins
located north of the release area. DP and RE groups were
subdivided according to the geographical location (DP1–4)
or sampling period (RE1 before 2002 and RE2 in 2004)
(Table 1). All genotypes detected from non-invasive sam-
ples and the genotype of an otter killed in the winter of
2004 were grouped in RE2.
We used GIMLET v1.3.3 software (Valie`re 2002) to re-
group identical genotypes among all consensus genotypes
obtained. Genotypes scored at eight to ten loci were ﬁrst
regrouped, and we repeated the test with samples scored at
ﬁve to ten loci, to detect if individuals were lost by dis-
carding low-scored samples. Whether mistyping could
have increased the number of genotyped individuals was
also investigated. Two samples were considered to belong
to different individuals when at least three alleles appeared
at one locus in pairwise comparisons, or when they had
more than two allelic differences among loci. When two
genotypes were almost identical (i.e., one or two allelic
differences), more PCRs were performed until reaching
seven replicates per sample, to detect if the difference was
due to mistyping or to samples belonging to different
individuals. Only samples scored with eight to ten markers
were used in this matching approach. Sex was attributed
according to the results of two successful LutSRY analy-
ses. Genotypes with none or only one sexual type that
perfectly matched another genotype were grouped to-
gether; if a SRY-untyped genotype did not match another
one, the sex of the individual was left undetermined. The
collection site was always considered before assuming that
samples with the same multilocus genotype belonged to a
single individual. The rates of allelic dropout and false
alleles occurring at each locus after repeated ampliﬁcations
were also estimated with GIMLET.
Individuals identiﬁed were then compared to founder
proﬁles. The probability of identity P(ID) among unique
genotypes was calculated with GIMLET. P(ID) is the proba-
bility that two individuals drawn at random from a popu-
lation will have the same genotype at multiple loci (Waits
et al. 2001). Two values were calculated to estimate the
upper and lower boundaries of P(ID). The lower boundary,
P(ID)unbiased, includes a sample-size correction and assumes
that the population is in Hardy-Weinberg equilibrium
(HWE) and at random mating (Paetkau et al. 1998). In the
ﬁeld, observed P(ID) is expected to be higher, especially in
small populations where inbreeding is not rare. The upper
boundary of P(ID), P(ID)sib, is computed considering that the
population is only composed of siblings (Evett and Weir
1998). Waits et al. (2001) analysed large data sets and
showed that observed P(ID) always fell between theorical
P(ID) and P(ID)sib.
Population genetic-diversity indices were computed as
follows: genotypic disequilibrium between all pairs of loci
was tested with FSTAT v.2.9.3.2 (Goudet 2001) with an
adjusted P-value for a 1% nominal level after Bonferroni
correction. Deviations from Hardy–Weinberg equilibrium
(HWE) for each locus and each population were tested by
exact tests (Guo and Thompson 1992) implemented in
GENEPOP v.3.4 (Raymond and Rousset 1995). Both analyses
were performed on groups with a sample size equal to or
larger than 10. Allelic frequencies, observed heterozygos-
ity (Ho), expected non-biased heterozygosity (Hn.b., Nei
1978), mean number of alleles among all loci per popula-
tion, FIS inbreeding coefﬁcient for each population and FST
genetic differentiation among groups were calculated using
GENETIX v.4.05.2 (Belkhir et al. 2004). FST was calculated
with Weir and Cockerham’s h estimator (Weir and Cock-
erham 1984) of Wright’s FST (Wright 1969). The signiﬁ-
cance of the deviation from zero of FIS and FST was tested
using a permutation test (10,000 replicates). To correct the

Results
Consensus genotypes by multiple-tubes approach
DNA was extracted from a total of 124 spraint and jelly
samples collected in the winter of 2004. Twenty were ex-
cluded after mitochondrial ampliﬁcation screening and 104
(83.87%) were analysed with microsatellite markers. Only
one sample showed unexpected allele sizes when typed
with markers Lut435 and Lut701. Thus, the 265 bp frag-
ment of the mtDNA was sequenced and 152 bp were ob-
tained that matched a Mustela vison sequence (GenBank
Access Number AJ585350).
All loci were polymorphic, with 3 to 6 alleles per locus.
In one case, two jellies collected at the same site the same
day differed in just one allele at locus Lut604, with the
second allele different in only one repeat. This difference
could be the result of ADO or of recording a stutter band as
a true allele. Collection details strongly suggested that both
samples belonged to a single individual. More PCRs were
performed until reaching seven positive replicates per
sample to detect if the record was due to misprinting.
Based on the scored alleles of all replicates for that locus
(two heterozygotes and 12 homozygotes), both samples
were recorded as homozygotes. All genotypes recorded as
heterozygotes at locus Lut604 were closely investigated to
detect misprinting due to stutter bands, especially if both
alleles only differed in one repeat. Nonetheless, no other
cases were detected. Consensus genotypes scored with
eight to ten loci (N = 26) were obtained, averaging 4.69
positive PCR/locus (range 2–10), and yielded a mean of
9.54 scored loci per sample. The proportion of PCR errors
estimated by comparing consensus genotypes to each po-
sitive replicate result was variable among loci and samples;

Individual identiﬁcation and probability of identity
The match of consensus genotypes of samples typed with
eight to ten loci (N = 26) revealed the presence of 19
individuals. When we repeated this analysis including
consensus genotypes of samples scored with ﬁve to ten
markers (N = 39), one additional individual was detected.
The number of allelic differences among individual geno-
types ranged from four to 16. Sexual typing revealed the
presence of nine females (coded from F1 to F2 and from F4
to F10), nine males (M1 to M9), and two unique genotypes
of unknown sex (D1–D2, Fig. 1) with a sex ratio of 1:1.
None of the genotypes matched any of the available
founder proﬁles.
The theoretical and sibling probabilities of identity in
RE2, calculated for ten microsatellite loci, were 4.99 · 10–8
and 1.38 · 10–3, respectively. According to the values of
P(ID)sib , the P(ID) upper boundary, the six most informative
loci could distinguish two full-sibling individuals with a
99% probability.
Distribution of individuals in the release area in 2004
Eleven animals (six females and ﬁve males) were detected
at the Muga basin, eight at the Fluvia` basin (three females,
three males and two sexually untyped otters) and, ﬁnally,
one male otter was also detected at the River Ter (Fig. 1).
In some sites, more than one individual was detected the
same day (F2–M2; F4–D2), or over the same ﬁve-day
period (F10–M5–M9) (Fig. 1C). Individuals F10, M5 and
M9 were found at the conﬂuence of two rivers (Manol and
Muga). Comparing their genotypes, we found a possible
parent–offspring relationship between F2 and M2, F4 and
D2, F10 and M9, but not between F10 and M5. One of the
males partially typed with ﬁve loci (M8) was not retained
for further analyses among different population groups.
The RE2 population thus consisted of a minimum of 19
genotypes recorded from non-invasively collected samples
scored with eight to ten markers, and one genotype of an
otter found dead just before the sampling period (RE2,
N = 20).
Genetic diversity
All loci were polymorphic in the reconstructed founder
group (DP), DP1 and DP2. DP3 presented some mono-
morphic loci, but this could be due to its small sample size.
The number of alleles per loci ranged from 3 to 6 in DP
(mean 5) and RE2 (mean 4.4), and the overall number of
alleles per loci across all samples ranged from 4 to 8 (mean
5.6) (Table 2). Some alleles of the founder group were only
present in one of the DP1 (7 alleles) or DP2 (2 alleles)
donor populations. Most of them were also detected in the
reintroduced population. Three otters sampled in the re-
lease area (M4, M5 and M7) had 6 alleles not detected in
the founder group: two unique alleles at locus Lut604, two
alleles shared with an otter from the Segre basin (loci
Lut435 and Lut717), one allele shared with an otter from

the River Tet (locus Lut715), and one allele also detected
in the release area in both RE1 and RE2 periods, and shared
with an otter from the River Tec (locus Lut818). This allele
was not rare in the release area and was probably inherited
from founders not analysed.
No signiﬁcant genotypic disequilibrium was detected
between any pair of loci within groups DP and RE2, nor
across all the data set. We did not detect departures from
HWE within RE2, but a signiﬁcant departure from HWE
was found in DP (P £ 0.05). Allelic richness and the
number of alleles of the reconstructed founder group were
higher than each individual donor population and higher
than RE2. Levels of genetic variation were high in both the
donor populations and in the release area. Average non-
biased expected heterozygosity (Hn.b.) ranged from 0.59
(RE2) to 0.62 (DP1 and DP2), and average observed het-
erozygosity (Ho) was above 0.60 (Table 2); the highest
value was found in DP2 (Ho = 0.68). The levels of
inbreeding were low and not signiﬁcantly different from 0
in all groups.
Genetic differentiation among populations
Otters inhabiting the release area are expected to descend
only from founder otters, and to be genetically very similar
to them. Signiﬁcant genetic differences were found be-
tween the reconstructed founder group DP and RE2
(FST = 0.045, P < 0.01). Differences were slightly higher
between DP1 and RE2 (FST = 0.050, P < 0.01), and more
pronounced between DP2 and RE2 (FST = 0.130, P<0.01).
The genetic differentiation pattern among genotypes is
represented by a factorial component analyses (FCA) plot
(Fig. 2). The ﬁrst two factorial correspondence axes (Axis
1 and Axis 2) explained 10.32 and 9.71% of the total
inertia, respectively. However, the FCA genotype scores
plotted into the second (9.71%) and third (7.43%) axes for
purposes of clarity are also represented, since distinct
groups spatially separated in the 3-D plot overlapped in the
2-D plot. Genotypes from DP1 and DP2 followed a barely
overlapping continuous distribution. According to the
FCA, otters from the release area (RE1 and RE2) mainly
overlapped with DP1, and a few of them grouped either
with DP2 or DP3. Three otters (M4, M5 and M7) which
presented 6 alleles not detected in the founder group were
outliers. The presence of these outliers may explain the
genetic differences detected by FST between reintroduced
otters and the founder group. Otters from southern French
basins (Se, Tc and Tt) did not form a unique cluster; otters
from the River Tec grouped with DP1 and DP2 while the
two other otters from the Segre and Tec basins joined
outliers from the release area.
One adult male from the River Tec (Tc1) clearly
grouped with DP2 and shared at least 50% of his alleles at
each locus with a founder female. Besides, both shared an
allele not found in any other individual. This could be due
to a parent–offspring relationship, as one generation-time-
period could have elapsed since the release of the founder
female and the ﬁnding of that male. The other otter from
the Tec (Tc2) was a juvenile male sharing many alleles at
each locus with Tc1. It could be his relative, but this
assumption is more hazardous and could only be veriﬁed
by increasing the number of loci in the analyses.
Discussion
Non-invasive methodology
The multiple-tubes approach was essential to detect and
correct errors produced during genotyping, mainly pro-
duced by ADO. Quantity and quality of extracted DNA are
major issues when performing genetic studies based on
non-invasive methods. PCR ampliﬁcation success depends
on a large number of factors such as species diet and
temperature at time of collection (Monteiro et al. 1997;
Frantz et al. 2003; Murphy et al. 2003; Nsubuga et al.
2004; Piggott et al. 2004; Ha´jkova´ et al. 2006). Our
genotype success rate was low compared to other carnivore
species but in the range of previous studies on otters
(Coxon et al. 1999; Dallas et al. 2003).

The reliability of surveys on endangered species based
on scat morphology has been questioned in some cases due
to high error rates on species identiﬁcation detected by
molecular analyses (Davison et al. 2002). Otter droppings
and spraint marks are generally different from those of
other species found in the study area, and highly predict-
able in terms of the point of deposition (Ruiz-Olmo 2001).
We only detected one mink sample in our study, thus the
species identiﬁcation error rate in the ﬁeld was low.
Individual identiﬁcation of reintroduced otters
The 26 genotypes obtained in our study corresponded to a
minimum of 19 individuals ranging in the sampled area.
The otter home-range can cover tens of kilometres, gen-
erally larger in males than in females (Sjo¨a˚nsen 1997;
Saavedra 2002). The main aim of this study is to obtain a
representative sample of the current population, and we
focused on maximising sampling coverage and extension
rather than intensive local sampling. This may explain the
high number of individuals detected from 26 genotyped
samples.
The genotypes identiﬁed in the release area were dif-
ferent from the available founders’ proﬁles. The lack of
founder proﬁles suggests high turn-over, and the popula-
tion in 2004 was mostly composed of their descendants.
This is consistent with lifespan expectancy in the species,
as the average lifespan of an Iberian otter in the wild is
3.3 years, 4.4 years if they reach sexual maturity (Ruiz-
Olmo 2001), and is consistent with the time elapsed since
the last main release in 2000. Thus, identiﬁed individuals
were most likely born in the reintroduction area. However,
we cannot exclude the presence of non-sampled living
founders or the presence of untyped founders among
genotypes identiﬁed from spraints (especially the otter re-
leased in 2002).
When using captured animals for a reintroduction pro-
gramme, it is assumed that animals are unrelated and come
from outbred populations. Close geographical origins can
explain some degree of relatedness among individuals.

The establishment of the current genetic pool in the
release area
The reintroduced otter population sampled in 2004 was in
HWE, the sex ratio was equilibrated and all markers used
were polymorphic. The diversity indices were similar to
those of founders, but a slight decrease in allelic richness
and heterozygosity has been observed. A certain reduction
of the number of alleles was expected. The post-release
mortality within the ﬁrst year was 22%, mainly by road
accidents (Saavedra 2002), therefore most of these otters
did not reproduce, and some alleles at low frequencies may
have been lost.
The FCA plot showed that otters from the release area
were more closely related to source population DP1 than to
DP2 or DP3 (Fig. 2). Otters from DP1 have apparently
contributed most to the genetic pool of the current popu-
lation. Different factors might have played a role: ﬁrst,
otters originating from DP1 were more numerous, repre-
senting two-thirds of the founder population. Second, they
were the ﬁrst released, and could occupy the most suitable
sites for feeding, resting and breeding, establishing them-
selves within the ﬁrst weeks or months (Saavedra 2002),
increasing their chances of survival and breeding success-
fully. It has been observed that otters released in an
unoccupied territory become established faster than in
occupied habitats; in the latter case, they tend to move
further before becoming established (Sjo¨a˚nsen 1997). In
this reintroduction programme, otters released in already
inhabited areas tended to disperse more or to produce
changes in the territory of neighbouring otters (Saavedra
2002). Before the last main release in 2000, the reintro-
duced population easily colonised the Tec and Ter basins,
partly due to the dispersal of juveniles and sub-adults born
in the area (Saavedra 2002). Otters from DP2 arrived in
2000 in an inhabited, but not saturated, area; thus, they
could have moved to neighbouring and/or suboptimal
habitats. We sampled a wide area, focusing on main rivers
and main sites where fresh signs of otter presence were
easily found to obtain as many fresh samples as possible
per area in a short collection time. Covering a larger area
would be needed to gather samples from the whole range
actually occupied by the population, and to detect if the
lack of some alleles carried by founders is due to a sam-
pling bias or if it reﬂects a progressive loss of genetic
diversity in the population.
There are three possible explanations for the presence of
unique alleles in the reintroduced individuals. First of all,
these alleles may have been inherited from the untyped
founder female originating from the River Segre. Another
hypothesis suggested by other authors after studying rein-
troduced populations of marmots and ﬁshers (Kruckenha-
user and Pinsker 2004; Vinkey et al. 2006) is the
possibility that the autochthonous population had never
disappeared completely and alleles not found in source
populations could be the remnants of the gene pool of a
relic population. Since 1984, no otters were detected de-
spite a national survey and additional surveys carried out
before the release. Surveys carried out in the release area
just after the ﬁrst releases showed that with only four otters
moving in the area in early 1996, more than 20% of 83
surveyed sites were positive (Ruiz-Olmo et al. 2001).
Therefore, it is unlikely that a remnant native individual
contributed to the genetic pool of the reintroduced popu-
lation. Finally, they can reﬂect the genetic contribution of
migrants from closer, expanding otter populations. Otters
were absent from the headwaters of the River Segre for
10 years, but they reappeared in 1994 and have inhabited
the stream since then. Recently, otters have also colonised
the Tet basin, north of the Tec basin. It is not clear if they
have spread from the Tec or the Segre, because the head-
waters of these rivers are very close (Saavedra 2006). Our
results suggest that both otters found in the River Tec
originate from the reintroduced population, as expected,
since this basin was rapidly colonised a few years after the
releases (Saavedra 2002). The otter sampled in the River
Tet is genetically closer to the otter from the River Segre
than to otters from the River Tec.
It is possible that both native and reintroduced popula-
tions are currently connecting due to geographical prox-
imity. The establishment of gene ﬂow between native and
reintroduced populations can minimise losses of genetic
diversity, but at the same time it can obscure the genetic
signature left by the founding event. However, studies on
wild reintroduced turkeys have shown that the source ge-
netic signature can be retained in the established population
several decades after reintroduction (Latch and Rhodes
2005). Nonetheless, a prolonged and severe bottleneck can
obscure the genetic legacy of the source population

Conclusions
The otter population has been restored to its former range
in the north-eastern Iberian Peninsula and maintains mod-
erate to high genetic diversity without immediate concerns
about inbreeding. Long-term monitoring combining ﬁeld
work and genetic analyses will be needed to assess the
viability of the reintroduced population. The current
expansion of both native and reintroduced populations may
suggest that they will probably contact, if they have not
already done so. At present, the reintroduced otter popu-
lation is breeding in the new area, expanding to the north
and south, and our genetic results together with ecological
data give an encouraging picture for the future of the
species in the north-eastern Iberian Peninsula.
