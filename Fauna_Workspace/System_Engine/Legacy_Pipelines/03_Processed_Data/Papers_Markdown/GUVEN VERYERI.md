1. BĐODĐVERSĐTY OF TURKEY 
 
Turkey is shaped by the northern Anatolian Mountains and in the south by the Toros 
Mountains, two  parallel ranges.  As such, it is a high altitude country characterised by 
geographic irregularities.  Inland waters cover 1.6 % of the land area. 
In terms of biodiversity, Turkey is one of the richest countries of Europe and the 
Middle East and ranks ninth on the European Continent in this regard.  All of the seven 
geographical regions of the country display different climate, flora and fauna characteristics.  
Turkey is rich in biodiversity in terms of species diversity, with its 120 mammals, 
more than 400 birds, 130 reptiles and close to 400 fish species.  
 
2.   CURRENT STATUS 
 
The wetlands of Turkey are currently being destroyed due to several human-induced factors, 
such as diversion of the river courses and inappropriate irrigation projects; pollution from 
agricultural, industrial and residential waste; draining and rehabilitation activities for 
agriculture and settlements; excessive and.illegal fishing and hunting; tourism; absence of 
water management.  
Turkey became a party to the Ramsar Convention in 1994, and by 1998 the number of 
wetlands in the list of the Convention had reached nine.  The country also became a signatory 
to  the Bern Convention in 1984. 
The international agreements and conventions to which Turkey is a party need to be 
reflected in our national law and the necessary legal arrangements for enforcement need to be 
made. 
 
3.   OTTER SURVEY 
 
Regarding the Eurasian otter (Lutra lutra), there are only individual efforts supported by the 
International Otter Survival Fund (IOSF).  In addition, the Turkish Society for the 
Conservation of Nature declared the year 2000 to be the “Otter Year”.  They prepared a 
booklet and organised an otter symposium.  The otter survey which was carried out on the 
eastern Mediterranean rivers in Turkey is the only survey to have collected regular scientific 
data on the biology and distribution of the species. 
This  project was the first detailed survey of  otters in Turkey.  It started in November 
1996 with the financial support of IOSF.  Following initial enquiries and field research 
studies, the Sultan Marshes and Zamantı River were selected to be the survey areas. 
A 30 kilometre part of the Zamantı River, lying between the Aladağ Mountains, was 
selected for the survey area and this was visited regularly to obtain information and collect 
otter spraints. 
 
Apart from the habitat surveys, “the river otter sighting data” obtained from local 
people and fishermen were recorded on “standard observation forms”.  The river otter 
sighting data clearly indicated that the otter’s presence in the Zamantı River is highly related 
to undisturbed aquatic and terrestrial habitats. 


4.   OBJECTIVES OF THE STUDY  
 
The objectives of the study were to: 
 
a.  Collect data about otters for developing suitable strategies for their conservation in 
Turkey; 
b.  Determine the occurrence and distribution of otters in the research area; 
c.  Collect spraints, footprint measurements and various signs of otters and analyse 
them; 
d. Study the diet of the otter and estimate the proportions of each type of food 
consumed; 
e.   Work in co-operation with local people and related associations to extend the otter 
study; 
f.  Carry out campaigns to develop public interest and decrease otter deaths by 
educating people; 
g.  Inform local people and teachers about otter conservation and regulate co-operative 
studies with them; 
h.  Investigate factors affecting otter habitats and examine hunting pressure on the 
species. 
 
5. INITIAL RESULTS 
 
Investigations and many interviews with local people were undertaken in the Sultan Marshes, 
and these directed us to the Zamantı River and Aladağ Mountains National Park. We studied 
30 kilometres of the Zamantı River in the Aladağ National Park (but at times the Zamantı 
canyon’s geographical position did not allow us easy access).

6.   THE FUTURE? 
 
Turkey is a kind of bio-reserve continent in between Europe and Asia with vital habitats and 
elements of flora/fauna.  In Turkey, there is a need for countrywide detailed studies.  Besides 
the lack of sufficient scientific data, geographical areas/habitats are unique and varying in 
character.  Thus the probability of encountering a Lutra sub-species in the south east and 
north east is very high. 
 
The international agreements and conventions to which Turkey is party, need to be reflected 
in our national law and the necessary legal arrangements need to be made for enforcement.  
Effective control mechanisms need to be activated.
