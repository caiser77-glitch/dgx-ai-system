Movements of the flying squirrel Pteromys volans in corridors and 
in matrix habitat 
Vesa Selonen and Ilpo K. Hanski 
Selonen, V. and Hanski, I. K. 2003. Movements of the flying squirrel Pteromys volans 
in corridors and in matrix habitat. - Ecography 26: 641-651. 
Elements of the landscape, such as patches of preferred habitat, matrix between 
patches, and corridors linking patches, differ as movement habitat for animals. To 
understand how landscape structure influences the movement and thus, population 
dynamics of animals, clear empirical knowledge on patterns of movement is needed. 
The Siberian flying squirrel inhabits spruce-dominated 
boreal forests from Finland to 
eastern Siberia. Numbers of flying squirrels have declined severely in Finland in past 
decades, probably due to modern forestry. We studied the movement of radio-col- 
lared adult flying squirrels in preferred (spruce forest) and in matrix habitat (open 
areas and other habitats with trees) in Finland 1997-2000, and determined whether 
the woodland strips connecting patches of preferred habitat could function as 
ecological corridors for flying squirrels. 
Flying squirrels used woodland strips for inter-patch movements, but also used 
matrix with trees and were able to cross narrow open gaps. Males moved longer total 
distances and crossed edges more often than females. Males used matrix habitats for 
movement between spruce patches, and moved faster and more directly in the matrix 
than in the spruce forest. Females seldom changed spruce patches, but instead used 
the matrix for foraging. For both sexes probability of leaving the spruce forest patch 
correlated negatively with the size of the patch, but the type of connection the patch 
had to other patches did not affect the leaving probability. Due to efficient movement 
abilities of the flying squirrel and forest-dominated landscape structure of southern 
Finland, we suggest that conservation acts for maintaining viable populations of 
flying squirrels should focus on the quality of managed forest and the area of suitable 
breeding habitat (i.e. on habitat loss), but not necessarily on ecological corridors. 
V. Selonen (vesa.selonen@helsinki.fi) and I. K. Hanski, Dept of Ecology and Systemat- 
ics, P. 0. Box 65, FIN-00014 Univ. of Helsinki, Finland. 
Landscapes are comprised of several different features 
that influence how animals can move (Wiens et al. 
1993, Ims 1995). Of those main features are patches of 
suitable habitat, matrix habitat, and corridors linking 
patches. The matrix, i.e. the habitat between preferred 
patches, may be the most spatially dominant feature of 
the landscape (Forman 1995). It is often heterogeneous, 
and can influence animal movements in a species-spe- 
cific way. 
Landscape connectivity has been identified as a key 
factor preserving populations (Taylor et al. 1993), and 
linear habitats have been suggested to enhance connec- 
tivity of the landscape by acting as movement corridors 
(Wilson and Willis 1975, Diamond 1976). The corridor 
hypothesis originated from the generalized assumption 
that organisms do not use matrix for movement. The 
theory has been criticized mainly because the empirical 
data supporting the idea have been weak (Hobbs 1992, 
Simberloff et al. 1992, Mann and Plummer 1995, Har- 
rison and Bruna 1999), and the view that the matrix is 
not used for movement is found inappropriate for 
several species (Verboom and Apeldoorn 1990, Celada 
et al. 1994, Wauters et al. 1994a, b, Aberg et al. 1995, 
Delin and Andren 1999, Bowne et al. 1999). In addi- 
tion, the concept of landscape connectivity often has 
been misinterpreted as corridors (Tischendorf and

Fahrig 2000). The interpretations of landscape connec- 
tivity should be based on the movement of individuals 
between patches and not the structure of the landscape 
(Tischendorf and Fahrig 2000). 
The way an organism responds to habitat edges is 
thought to predict the organism's response to corridors 
and matrix habitat (Wiens et al. 1985, Stamps et al. 
1987, Haddad 1999). A species that avoids approaching 
an edge may not be able to use effectively either the 
matrix or corridors for movement between patches 
(Laurance 1991, 1995). However, it should be noted 
that an organism's response to landscape elements 
might vary, for example, seasonally, by age, by repro- 
ductive status, and with social status (Lidicker and 
Stenseth 1992). Especially during dispersal, which is a 
critical life history component determining the abun- 
dance and persistence of populations (Lande 1987, 
Fahrig and Paloheimo 1988), an animal may use habi- 
tat differently than when living in a permanent home 
range (Palomares et al. 2000). How corridors and ma- 
trix contribute to landscape connectivity depends on 
the nature of the corridors, the nature of the matrix, 
and the nature of the organism's response to them 
(Rosenberg et al. 1997, Beier and Noss 1998). Clearly, 
empirical knowledge on how different elements of the 
landscape influence abilities of animals to move and 
thus, population dynamics, are needed (Bowers 1997, 
Tischendorf and Fahrig 2000). 
The Siberian flying squirrel Pteromys volans is a 
nocturnal, arboreal rodent, which nests in tree cavities, 
twig dreys, and nest boxes. It inhabits spruce-domi- 
nated boreal forests from Finland to eastern Siberia 
and Japan (Ognev 1966). In Finland, the fragmentation 
of forest landscapes by humans is due to the cutting of 
mature spruce forests, which is the preferred landscape 
type for flying squirrels. The decline of flying squirrel 
numbers in Finland is thought to be due to intensive 
forestry (Hokkanen et al. 1982, Rassi et al. 2000). 
According to the data of Hokkanen et al. (1982) the 
flying squirrel population has declined severely in Fin- 
land. In their abundance index based on game inquiry 
data, there was a linear decline to half from 1950 to 
1982. The decline appears to have continued since 1982. 
It has been suggested that during the last decade the 
population has decreased by ca 30% (unpubl.). In the 
Finnish red data book, the flying squirrel has been 
classified as a vulnerable species (Rassi et al. 2000), and 
the habitat directive of the European Union classifies it 
as a priority species whose conservation requires special 
areas for conservation (Anon. 1992). 
To predict how flying squirrel populations will sur- 
vive in fragmented landscapes, it is crucial to know how 
landscape structure influences their movement. In this 
paper, we analyse whether movements of adult flying 
squirrels differ in the patches of preferred habitat and 
in the matrix between patches. We also study whether 
the woodland strips connecting patches of preferred 
habitat could act as ecological corridors to facilitate the 
movement of adult and juvenile flying squirrels. Our 
aim was to determine if forest fragmentation or the 
structure of the matrix influences the movement pat- 
terns of the flying squirrel. 
Methods 
Study areas and data collection 
The study was done in three areas in southern Finland 
in 1997-2000. Two study areas, in litti (60055'N, 
26030'E) and in Anjalankoski (60050'N, 26050'E), were 
in managed forests owned by private landowners, and 
the third was in Nuuksio National Park (60'18'N, 
24?32'E). The main tree species in the study areas are 
Norway spruce Picea abies, Scots pine Pinus sylvestris, 
birch Betula pendula and B. pubescens, and aspen Pop- 
ulus tremula. Forests in all study areas consist of 
patches of spruce-dominated, pine-dominated and de- 
ciduous-dominated forest types. Clear cuts, sapling 
stands, fields, and bogs fragment the forests. For more 
details about the landscapes of study areas, see Selonen 
et al. (2001). 
Flying squirrels were captured from their roosting or 
nesting cavities and fitted with radio collars (Biotrack, 
U.K.). The movements of 18 adult male and 19 adult 
female flying squirrels were followed by continuous 
tracking from March-July. This period includes the 
mating season of flying squirrels. During mating season 
male flying squirrels are moving more actively than 
during the rest of the year. Movement patterns of 
females do not change considerably during the year 
(Hanski et al. 2000a). Number of tracked individuals 
was in Anjalankoski, litti, and Nuuksio, respectively, 
1997: 0, 2 males 2 females, 2 males 3 females; 1998: 1 
males, 4 males, 2 males 2 females; 1999: 7 males 6 
females, 2 males 2 females, 0; 2000: 3 males 4 females, 
0, 0. Five males were tracked during two separate years. 
Tracking usually started half an hour after sunset and 
lasted 60-120 min. The number of tracking periods per 
individual varied between one and eight (on average 
3.6 + 1.8, two individuals were tracked only once). 
Flying squirrels were tracked from ca 30-m distance. 
While an animal was stationary, the tracker waited 
silently and motionless until the animal, based on the 
radio signal, started to move (the direction and/or 
strength of radio signal changed). Places where an 
individual were located were marked with flags and the 
coordinates were later obtained with a Magellan or 
Alto-G12 Global Positioning System (GPS) receivers. 
All the places where the squirrel was located were 
marked, i.e. when it stopped and when it was located 
while moving. When the tracker encountered the 
tracked flying squirrel, it did not seem to be disturbed 
unless the animal was in very small trees or low down

Landscape maps and corridor data 
Landscape maps from the study areas were digitised 
from aerial photographs using MapInfo software. 
Based on the results of habitat use by the flying squirrel 
(Selonen et al. 2001), we classified landscape elements 
as preferred habitat (spruce- or deciduous-dominated 
forests) and three matrix types, good movement habitat 
(pine forest, young spruce, young deciduous forest and 
clear cuts with trees), poor movement habitat (sapling 
stands, young pine forest, pine bogs and clear cuts), 
and open areas (fields and other open areas, e.g. open 
bogs and lakes). In the sapling stands trees were 1.5-8 
m high and in young forests < 15 m high. In clear cuts 
with trees (seed-tree cutting) several trees were inter- 
spersed over the cut area. Clear cuts sometimes had a 
few isolated trees, but considerably fewer than clear 
cuts with trees. Fields and other open areas were the 
only landscape elements entirely without trees. 
We defined a corridor as a narrow linear woodland 
strip (5-40 m wide), which connected preferred habitat 
patches in a matrix of open areas or habitats with 
considerably smaller/fewer trees than the corridor itself 
(Fig. IA). Thus two types of putative corridors oc- 
curred: 1) spruce/deciduous forest strip in a matrix of 
good or poor movement habitat, or open areas, 2) good 
movement habitat strip in a matrix of poor movement 
habitat or open areas. Corridors of poor movement 
habitat were not common in the landscape and were 
not used by the tracked flying squirrels. In practice, the 
difference between corridor and matrix was always 
considerable, so that for example no spruce forest 
corridors in the matrix of pine forest occurred. Move- 
ments of flying squirrel in corridors and in the matrix 
were quantified by observing whether an individual 
used these elements for inter-patch movement. 
In addition to the tracked individuals above, we had 
home-range data from 12 males and 10 females that 
were not tracked continuously. However, based on 
single independent radiolocations (hereafter point loca- 
tions) and some short-duration tracking periods not 
included in this study, we were able to approximate the 
movement routes of those individuals (data on these 
individuals were used as additional data in describing 
corridor and matrix use of flying squirrels and not in 
any other analyses). Point locations were also done 
during night time, when each animal was recorded by 
walking under a single tree or a small group of trees 
where a flying squirrel was located (for more details, see 
Hanski 1998, Hanski et al. 2000a). In addition, we 
summarize the corridor and matrix use of 61 tracked 
juveniles before and after natal dispersal. These data 
are based on the dispersal path of juveniles that is 
analysed in more detail elsewhere (unpubl.). Natal dis- 
persal distances of juvenile flying squirrels are long, on 
average 2.5 km, but up to 9 km for both sexes 
(unpubl.). 
Movement paths 
The straight-line distance moved by a tracked flying 
squirrel between marked locations was considered as 
one step. Different steps formed a movement path for 
each individual. For movement paths we calculated 
step lengths, turning angles between steps and total 
distances moved using the Ranges V computer package 
(Kenward and Hodder 1996). Because the time of 
tracking periods varied, the total distance moved was 
calculated in relation to a fixed 90 min period. Move- 
ment speed was calculated from the total distance 
moved and time tracked, thus it included stops made by 
flying squirrels. The number and duration of these 
stationary periods was counted. Duration was counted 
from the time when the tracker arrived at the point (the 
tracker usually arrived shortly after the squirrel) to the 
time when the radio signal indicated that the squirrel 
left the point. However, in some cases only the time 
when the tracker arrived to the point was marked down 
and not the time when the squirrel left. Thus some 
short stationary periods could not be separated from 
movement periods. To avoid this problem only the 
stops lasting more than 10 min were included. 
For each individual we divided paths into sections 
moved in spruce forest and in matrix habitats using 
MapInfo. Due to small sample size the three matrix 
habitat types above were grouped for these analyses. In 
practice flying squirrels did not use matrix of open 
areas except to cross narrow openings and thus matrix 
included two types of movement habitat. Distances 
moved, turning angles, and movement speeds were 
calculated for the matrix and the spruce forest. Differ- 
ences between these two habitats were tested with a 
paired t-test. Mean angles were calculated according to 
Zar (1999, p. 599), and tested with paired-sample test- 
ing for angles following Zar (1999, p. 645). 
To avoid pseudoreplication each individual was 
treated as one sampling unit. For each individual we 
calculated means of the measured variables. To meet 
assumptions of a normal distribution variables were 
log-transformed, and proportions were arcsine trans-

Probability of leaving a patch 
We calculated the probability of leaving for all spruce 
patches that were visited by tracked flying squirrels. 
The probability of leaving was the number of tracking 
periods during which an individual moved out of a 
patch divided by the total number of tracking periods 
when the individual was found in that patch (i.e. moved 
out from that patch + did not move out from that 
patch). Thus each patch got a probability of leaving 
value that ranged from 0 to 1, value 0 indicating that 
the patch was never left and 1 indicating that patch was 
always left during tracking periods. The fact that it will 
take longer to cross a large patch than a small patch 
could influence our results. However, even the largest 
patches in our study areas could be crossed in less than 
ten minutes (own observation), and we assumed flying 
squirrels had the chance to leave every patch within our 
tracking period regardless of the size of the patch. 
The number of tracking periods that were used to 
calculate the probability of leaving ranged from 1 to 10 
per patch. The number of tracking periods did not 
correlate with the probability of leaving the patch and 
all the data were used for analyses. Mean number of 
visits per patch was 2.4 + 1.9, and 29% of patches were 
visited during only one tracking period. 
A logistic regression model was used to analyse the 
probability of leaving the spruce patch. The probability 
of leaving data was binomially distributed. Thus the 
dependent variable used in logistic regression was the 
number of cases that flying squirrel left the patch and 
the binomial denominator was the number of tracking 
periods. The logistic regression was computed on this 
ratio, i.e. squirrel left the patch/all tracking periods (see 
Crawley 1993 for more details on testing with binomial 
distributions. Tests were done with Statistix 7). Inde- 
pendent variables added to the model were: Patch size: 
size (ha) of the preferred habitat patch. Proportion of 
deciduous trees within the patch: the number of decidu- 
ous trees within a patch was counted in sampling plots 
(one plot/ha) using a relascope and transformed to a 
percentage of all counted trees. Proportion of different 
matrix habitats surrounding the patch: the length of the 
edge of the spruce patch facing each matrix habitat type 
(good or poor movement habitats) was measured. The 
length was transformed to a percentage by dividing by 
the total perimeter of the spruce patch to make differ- 
ent sized patches comparable with each other. Type of 
connection to other patches: the spruce patches used by 
flying squirrels were divided into three groups. Those 
connected to other spruce patches 1) by linear spruce/ 
deciduous forest strips, 2) by good movement habitat, 
and 3) by poor movement habitat. Connection by good 
movement habitat included both linear forest strips 
(5-40 m wide) and larger habitat areas ( > 40 m wide). 
Connection by poor movement habitat meant habitats 
that were much larger ( >>40 m wide) than what were 
defined as corridors. None of the patches were sur- 
rounded totally by open areas. Distance to the destina- 
tion patch: if the flying squirrel changed the spruce 
patch during tracking, then the distance between the 
patch left and the destination patch was measured. If 
the flying squirrel did not change patches during track- 
ing, a distance to the nearest spruce patch was 
measured. 
In addition study area and study year were included 
as independent variables. Because flying squirrels were 
using all the patches, none of the patches were truly 
isolated. However, the first and second variables char- 
acterized the spruce patch in a way that could influence 
the willingness of a flying squirrel to leave that patch. 
The last three variables described the landscape outside 
the patch that could influence the willingness of flying 
squirrels to move through the matrix. The change in the 
deviance after adding variables to the model was tested 
against X2-distribution. By removing and adding vari- 
ables in the model we tested the effect of each variable 
to the model. 
Results 
Movements in preferred habitat and matrix 
Females moved shorter total distances per tracking 
period, and in shorter steps than males, although the 
number and duration of stops did not differ between 
sexes (Table lA). During stops flying squirrels usually 
were foraging in deciduous trees, although it was not 
always possible to see their activities. 
Males crossed edges between different habitats more 
often than females (Table IB). Sixty-four percent of 
edges crossed by both sexes were towards good move- 
ment habitat, 11% to poor movement habitat, and 25% 
to narrow open areas (six crossed open gaps, 34 + 20 m 
wide, max 70 m). For males, crossings of spruce forest 
edge led in most cases to inter-patch movement be- 
tween spruce patches. However, females usually foraged 
near the edge of the spruce forest. Males moved longer 
distances in the matrix than females, but the time spent 
in the matrix did not differ between sexes (Table IB). 
Males moved more slowly in spruce forest than in 
matrix habitats (Table 2), because they stopped more 
often in spruce forest than in matrix. The actual speed

Use of matrix and woodland strip corridors 
Adults and juveniles before natal dispersal 
The use of corridors and matrix for tracked adults and 
the proportion of patches with different connection 
types are summarized in Table 4. When patches were 
connected with woodland strips, altogether (including 
also point location individuals, see methods) 12 males, 
3 females, and 11 juveniles (before dispersal) used 11 
corridors for patch change. Corridors used were on 
average 194 + 124 m long (range 70-480 m) and 5-40 
m wide. All corridors available were used, although we 
may have missed some unused corridors from aerial 
photographs. Six of the corridors were mature spruce 
forest strips. Three of these were surrounded by a field 
or other open area on both sides and three others were 
surrounded by fields and sapling stands. The other 
corridor types used were young spruce or deciduous 
forest surrounded by field on both sides (two corridors)

Dispersing juveniles 
At least two juveniles (out of 49 who dispersed) used 
linear strips of trees to cross wide, open areas during 
natal dispersal. One young female moved through a 900 
m long spruce and deciduous tree corridor along a 
ditch in the middle of a field. Another female crossed a 
300 m wide field. To cross the field it probably used 1.5 
m high bushes along a ditch. In addition, one female 
crossed 150 m wide field using a few trees in the middle 
of the field and one male was observed to cross a 125 m 
wide field that was probably more than it could cross 
with one glide. 
To summarize matrix and corridor use, flying squir- 
rels used corridors, but they also used a wide range of 
different habitat types with trees to move between 
separate spruce patches. Clear cuts with a few big trees 
were crossed. Adults tended to use habitats with small 
trees (sapling stands and pine bogs) when no other 
alternative for patch change was present, and crossed 
completely open areas only when they were narrow 
enough to be crossed with one glide. However, even 
wide fields (few hundreds of meters) can be crossed at 
least during juvenile dispersal if there are at least some 
trees or bushes present. 
Discussion 
Patches and matrix 
Male flying squirrels stopped less frequently and moved 
in a straighter line in other habitats than in spruce 
forest. This fits the model of Zollner and Lima (1999) 
that predicts that animals moving through an inhos- 
pitable matrix would follow straighter paths than ani- 
mals moving through a more benign matrix. Results 
supporting this were found in darkling beetles Elodes 
obsolete, which moved in straighter paths in areas with- 
out grass (high-risk area) than in areas with grass 
(low-risk area) (Crist et al. 1992, Wiens et al. 1997). 
Thus one interpretation of our result could be that 
flying squirrels moving in less preferred matrix habitat 
were trying to reduce mortality risk by moving faster 
(with less stops) and straighter. However, a simpler and 
more likely explanation is that, when individuals have 
experience in an environment and are capable of re- 
membering locations of resources or preferred patches, 
it is inefficient to use a movement strategy other than 
moving straight towards the subject (Ims 1995, Zollner 
and Lima 1999). Male flying squirrels usually moved 
between patches to visit the home range areas of differ- 
ent females (unpubl.). 
Females turned backwards more often than males. 
This could be explained by the fact that females moved 
less than males and might retreat, for example during 
feeding, more often than males. However, while feed- 
ing, flying squirrels usually spent a long time in one 
spot and did not move at all. More likely explanation is 
that, as some of the females had juveniles in the nest or 
were pregnant, they often came out from the nest, fed 
somewhere near, and returned to the nest, thus making 
backward turns. Males instead usually either foraged 
near the nest during the whole tracking period or 
moved to another patch. After these visits some males 
returned back during the same night, whereas others 
stayed in another patch for some days (Hanski et al. 
2000a). 
The patch size influenced the probability of leaving 
the patch, which is consistent with our earlier result 
where patch size correlated with the home-range size of 
male flying squirrels (Selonen et al. 2001). Based on 
results here we are not able to determine whether flying 
squirrels drifted outside from the small patches or 
whether they had some other reasons to leave the small 
patches. However, the influence of patch size on animal 
behaviour may indicate that all the resources needed 
cannot be found from one small patch (Dunning et al. 
1992). For female flying squirrels this resource is mainly 
food, but for males also females. The proportion of 
deciduous trees within the spruce patch could have 
been expected to explain the willingness of flying squir- 
rels to use matrix habitat (Selonen et al. 2001), but it 
did not have a very strong influence in the decision of 
either males or females to enter the matrix. 
Corridors 
Our data on movement in matrix and corridors by 
flying squirrels show that corridors were used when 
present, while matrix habitat was used where no corri- 
dors were available. In addition, the probability of 
leaving the patch was not related to a connection type, 
indicating that the type of connection was not influenc- 
ing their willingness to move between patches. Thus 
corridors probably had not much effect on adult flying 
squirrels ability to move between preferred patches in 
the forested landscape of our study areas.

The use of corridors probably was due to a prefer- 
ence of flying squirrels for spruce and deciduous forests, 
and, in general, habitats with large trees over habitats 
with small trees (Selonen et al. 2001). Adult females 
changed patches only a few times, probably because 
they did not have any reason to change patch due to 
their smaller area requirements (Hanski et al. 2000a, 
Selonen et al. 2001) and not because of a lack of 
connectivity. 
Dispersing juvenile flying squirrels crossed much 
wider forested matrix areas than adults (Selonen 2002). 
They were also able to cross fields using trees and 
bushes. These results indicate that juveniles move better 
or have a higher motivation to move in fragmented 
landscapes than adults. However, totally open gaps that 
exceed gliding ability of the flying squirrel (> 100 m), 
like large fields and wide highways, are not permeable. 
In these cases corridors would be needed for coloniza- 
tion of isolated patches, like reported for North Ameri- 
can flying squirrels in agricultural areas in Midwest 
U.S. (Rosenblatt et al. 1999, Nubb and Swihart 2000). 
However, we did not have any individuals in patches 
surrounded by wide, open areas without any tree or 
bush connection. Such patches are not common in 
forested landscape of Finland, and populations pre-ex- 
isting in such patches have probably gone extinct earlier 
due to intensive farming. For example, in Iitti and 
Anjalankoski fields wider than 100 m surrounded less 
than two percent of all spruce patches. 
In general, conclusions on effect of corridors differ 
greatly for different species. For example Bowne et al. 
(1999) found a similar result for hispid cotton rats 
Sigmodon hispidus, as we did for flying squirrels. Corri- 
dors had no significant effect on the number of animals 
leaving connected patches compared with isolated 
patches, but corridors, if available, were used. Simi- 
larly, in some other studies it was concluded that 
corridors are not needed for the movement of the 
species in question (two species of kangaroo, Arnold et 
al. 1991; koala, Prevett 1991). However, the finding that 
corridors increase inter-patch movement is found in 
many studies of small mammals (e.g. Merriam and 
Lanoue 1990, Andreassen et al. 1996a, b, 1998). Fur- 
ther, the population densities in connected populations 
of meadow voles were higher than in unconnected 
populations (La-Polla and Barret 1993). Apeldoorn et 
al. (1994) found from a four-year study of the red 
squirrel Sciurus vulgaris that the density of possible 
movement corridors had significant effects on the pres- 
ence of that species. 
The response obtained depends also on the scale of 
the landscape heterogeneity (Turner 1989, Beier 1993, 
1995, Lidicker and Koenig 1996, Beier and Noss 1998). 
For example the situation of flying squirrels may differ 
between southern and northern Finland. In northern 
Finland, the flying squirrel is living at the northern limit 
of its range and occupies mainly good-quality patches 
of old-growth forest. In northern Finland the size of 
single landscape elements is also larger and thus the 
landscape is more coarse-gained than in southern Fin- 
land (Mdnkk6nen et al. 1997, Hanski et al. 2000b, 
Reunanen et al. 2000). In other squirrel species, isola- 
tion of patches influenced occupancy patterns in areas 
dominated by agriculture (Apeldoorn et al. 1994, 
Rosenblatt et al. 1999, Nubb and Swihart 2000), but 
had no marked effect on presence/absence of red squir- 
rels in fragmented forest landscapes (Andr6n and Delin 
1994, Rodrigues and Andr6n 1999). 
Presently, we are not able to define the required area 
of suitable spruce forest for flying squirrels or the 
threshold where the effects of fragmentation (isolation 
and patch size) would add to those of simple habitat 
loss. Flying squirrels are dependent on big aspens as a 
source of cavities and deciduous trees in general as a 
food source (Eronen 1991, Hanski 1998), both of which 
often are absent or scarce in mature managed spruce 
forests. For example, in the litti study area 19% of the 
total area of spruce patches contains mature aspen and 
other deciduous trees, and this is 3% of the total land 
area (unpubl.). Yet we do not know whether this is 
sufficient for the long-term persistence of the flying 
squirrel population in litti. At present, there are flying 
squirrels in the area, but the majority of forest cuttings 
in southern Finland have occurred in the decades after 
1950s. During 1950-1990 there was a big change in age 
structure of spruce forests, so that a large amount of 
50-70 yr old forests was replaced by 10-30 yr old 
forests (Anon. 2000). Therefore, it is possible that the 
population in litti has not yet fully responded to the 
changes in the environment. 
However, considering the efficient movement abilities 
of the flying squirrel in matrix habitat and the land- 
scape structure in southern Finland, we conclude that 
forest planning focused on ecological corridors for 
flying squirrels is not necessary. Of the area of Finland 
68% is forest, 10% lakes and 6% fields (Anon. 2000). In 
most cases, flying squirrels should be able to move over 
large areas in managed forest landscapes. Thus our 
results seem to indicate that with the current mixture of 
agriculture and managed forests habitat loss may be 
more important than effects of fragmentation for flying 
squirrel population dynamics (see also Selonen et al. 
2001). In several species of boreal forest birds, habitat 
loss also seemed to be more important than effects of 
fragmentation (Schmiegelow and Mdnkkinen 2002, see 
also Fahrig 2002). Hence, our data support the view, 
that effects of fragmentation are not such an important 
issue in boreal forests, at least to the same extent as for 
forest species in more agriculturally driven landscapes, 
because of the transient nature of the matrix (Fahrig 
2001, Schmiegelow and Mdnkk6nen 2002).
