1. Current status 
Formerly widespread throughout much of the UK, the otter 
underwent a rapid decline in numbers from the 1950s to 1970s and 
was effectively lost from midland and south-east counties of 
England including Worcestershire by the 1980s. The decline has 
now halted and otters are re-colonising former habitats.  
Recent surveys carried out by WWT have revealed that otters are 
now present on all of the county’s major watercourses with 
apparent strongholds within the Teme catchment and in the middle 
Severn. Within the last few years otters have re-colonised the Avon 
catchment with individuals spreading down the Severn and Teme. 
However, otters are still only sparsely distributed in the south and 
east of the county. Surveys of neighbouring catchments, e.g. River 
Blythe appear to suggest that some have already crossed over 
watersheds from the Severn catchment. However, whilst spraint 
surveys indicate how distribution has expanded recently they do 
not tell us much about the strength of populations. Breeding 
undoubtedly occurs in Worcestershire as young otters have been 
found. 
 
2. Current factors affecting the species 
• 
Pollution of watercourses - including from heavy metals and synthetic pyrethroid 
sheep dips (the River Stour remains polluted largely due to its historic industrial 
associations, which may be affecting otter distribution on the river)  
• 
Insufficient prey associated with poor water quality and low flows - otters may 
not be able to re-colonise all of their former haunts in the County such as the 
headwaters of the Bow Brook if fish habitat is reduced by lack of water; fish 
biomass on the River Stour is limited by water quality; eel populations (Anguila 
anguila),which form a significant part of the otter’s diet, are declining throughout 
Western Europe although as yet the reason for this remains unknown  
• 
Degraded bankside features - on many river stretches there is a lack of dense 
emergent vegetation, scrub and trees suitable for holts which are all required for 
resting and breeding.  
• 
Loss of extensive wetlands associated with rivers which are required for breeding 
including reedbeds, grazing marsh and wet woodland  
• 
Angling in close proximity to holts and hunting areas may cause disturbance to 
otters.  
• 
Mink hunting along rivers may be affecting the way in which otters re-colonise 
and use the County’s watercourses. There is at least one official mink hunt and 


several others which regularly hunt in the county. There are several reasons for 
concern. Firstly, dogs used in the hunt may disturb an otter and cause it to 
abandon the watercourse, if only temporarily. This is a particular cause for 
concern if females with cubs are present. Secondly, in attempting to reach mink, 
which have gone to ground in tree roots, damage to natural otter holts may occur 
when the mink are dug out.  
• 
Non-natural mortality mainly as a result of road accidents. Where roads cross 
rivers or where they run close by, otters are at risk. Road deaths are already 
increasing within the project area and these may have an effect on the population 
in the future. There may also be a similar problem where railways and rivers 
meet.  
• 
Introduction of otters including Asian short clawed (Aonyx cinerea) otters which 
may have been released recently on the River Arrow, a tributary of the Avon 
could explain some anomalies in survey data. The effect of this species in Britain 
is unknown.  
3. Current action 
3.1 Legal Status 
• 
The otter is listed on Appendix 1 of CITES, Appendix II of the Bern Convention 
and Annexes II and V of the Habitats Directive. It is protected under Schedule 5 
of the WCA 1981 and schedule 2 of the Conservation (Natural habitats etc) 
Regulations 1994 (Regulation 38). The European sub-species is also listed as 
globally threatened on the International Union for the Conservation of Nature Red 
Data List.  
3.2 Management, Research and Guidance 
• 
The JNCC has published a Framework for Otter Conservation in the UK 1995-
2000.  
• 
National surveys have been conducted every seven years since 1977 and in 
Worcestershire these are carried out in parts of two 50km squares, namely SO 
North - West and SO South - East.  
• 
County distribution surveys are carried out on a catchment basis each year by the 
River Severn Otter Project (RSOP), part of Worcestershire Wildlife Trust. Further 
surveys are conducted by volunteer groups.  
• 
RSOP which began in 1990, provides an advisory service for land managers, 
installs artificial holts, creates and restores habitat along rivers, advises on otter 
mitigation regarding new roads and development, provides training and produces 
publicity material.  
• 
The Environment Agency takes into account the requirements of otters in all its 
riverside capital and maintenance works and in carrying out all its regulatory 
functions e.g. land drainage consents for bridges must include an otter underpass. 
The EA has supported the River Severn Otter Project for several years.  


• 
The Environment Agency LEAPs contain actions that aim to safeguard and 
extend otter distribution as well as restoring and creating wetlands where 
appropriate. They also target certain river stretches for water quality 
improvements.  
• 
The Environment Agency/English Nature/RSPB Severn and Avon Vales wetland 
project aims to identify and restore large areas which could lead to significant 
increases in otter habitat.  
• 
The Environment Agency is leading the Severn Valley Wetlands Project which 
has already restored wetlands in the Upper Severn area. This initiative has now 
been extended to the Lower Severn where several other organisations (The 
Wildlife Trusts, RSPB and Wildfowl and Wetlands Trust included) have 
identified wetland areas in need of restoration.  
• 
Countryside Stewardship has resulted in improved management of waterside 
habitats in certain areas  
• 
Worcestershire Wildlife Trust’s new wetland reserve on the Avon will result in 
the County’s biggest reedbed and provide suitable habitat for breeding otters.  
• 
Severn Trent Water are working with RSOP to provide artificial holts on their 
operational sites as well as developing small wetlands at some of these sites.
