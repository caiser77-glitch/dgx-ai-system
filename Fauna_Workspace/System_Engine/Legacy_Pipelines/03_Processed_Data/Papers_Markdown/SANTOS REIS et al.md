MAN-OTTER CONFLICT IN PORTUGUESE FISHERIES: INTRODUCING A 
COMPREHENSIVE APPROACH 
 
Margarida SANTOS-REIS,  Dália FREITAS & Teresa SALES-LUÍS 
 
Universidade de Lisboa. Centro de Biologia Ambiental/Departamento de Biologia Animal. Faculdade de 
Ciências de Lisboa. Edifício. C2, 3º. Piso. Campo Grande. 1749-016 Lisboa. Portugal 
email:  mmreis@fc.ul.pt 
 
 
Abstract.   Due to the financial incentives of the EU, the number of fish farms in Portugal increased in the last 
decade. While this economic activity grew, so too did the problems between fish farmers and the otter (Lutra 
lutra). Such conflicts, between protected species and the exploitation of biological resources are common and 
can affect several different economic activities and species. Hence, a European project was designed for the 
development of a procedural framework for action plans to reconcile conflicts between the conservation of large 
vertebrates and the use of biological resources (FRAP), using fisheries in several countries and three species of 
fish-eating vertebrates as a model case. Portugal, Germany and Czech Republic are responsible for analysing 
the otter/fish farm conflict. This paper aims to introduce the conflict in Portugal by focusing on the inventory 
and distribution of fish farms, related to otter distribution and otter damage. Interviews with fish farmers were 
conducted focusing on the presence or absence of otters at the farms and the perceived damages caused by 
otters. Overall, 13.2% of the fish farms were not in use, 47.4% were considered negative for otter presence and 
39.5% positive. Otter presence at fish farms is quite common and conflict with fish farm owners exists, but this is 
usually taken care of privately by owners who test different methods of deterrence, such as fencing, electric 
fencing and the use of dogs but also traps, shooting or even poison. 
Preliminary results are presented but additional data on predation impacts are certainly necessary and 
the FRAP project will be a good step on that direction.  
 
Keywords:  Eurasian Otter, Lutra lutra, fish farming, predation, conflict, conservation 
 
 
1   INTRODUCTION  
 
With the financial encouragement of the EU, the number of fish farms in Portugal has 
increased in the last decade.  Such a Community Structural Policy is a means to reduce 
pressure upon sea stocks.  While this economic activity grew so too did the problems between 
fish farmers and the Eurasian otter (Lutra lutra), a fish-eating vertebrate.  Also, most of the 
Portuguese fish farms are located in estuaries, the majority of which are protected areas with 
high biodiversity values which should be preserved, such as the otter.  
This type of conflict between protected species and the exploitation of biological 
resources is common and affects several different economic activities and species.  In an 
attempt to solve, or at least minimise, this problem a European project was designed for the 
development of a procedural framework for action plans to reconcile conflicts between the 
conservation of large vertebrates and the use of biological resources, using fisheries and fish-
eating vertebrates as a model case (FRAP - Framework for Biodiversity Reconciliation 
Action Plans - contract number EVK2-CT-2002-00142-FRAP).  
The project, initiated in February 2003, includes two approaches (ecological and 
socio-economic) and uses three fish-eating species and different fishery types in eight 
European countries.  The three model species, selected as representatives of the fish-eating 
vertebrates, are the Eurasian otter, the great cormorant (Phalacrocorax carbo sinensis) and 
the grey seal (Haliochoerus grypus).  For each model species at least two countries were 
selected representing different population status and/or levels of conflicts with fisheries 
(www.frap-project.net).

Portugal, Germany and Czech Republic are responsible for analysing the otter conflict, 
using an ecological approach that aims to quantify otter visiting rates to fish farms and 
commercially important fish consumption, as well as to identify landscape factors affecting 
those parameters.  Complementarily, a socio-economic approach involves the analysis of 
instruments/policies that address the conflict and a social impact assessment, with a discourse 
analysis of involved stakeholders.  
Portugal is considered to have one of the most viable otter populations in Europe 
(FOSTER-TURLEY, MASON AND MACDONALD, 1990).  In 1995 a nationwide otter survey was 
conducted using a 10x10 kilometre UTM grid system and the resulting map showed a 
continuous distribution with very few absences, mainly in the coastal area around the main 
urban centres (TRINDADE, FARINHA AND FLORÊNCIO, 1998).  Otters in Portugal occupy a wide 
range of aquatic environments ranging from very small streams and rivers to high altitude 
lakes and large reservoirs, but also coastal and estuarine environments (e.g. BEJA, 1989, 1995; 
MATOS, 1999; PEDROSO, 1997; SOUSA, 1995).  Densities are unknown but suspected to be 
high on the basis of the abundance of signs of presence found in different studies conducted 
all over the country (e.g. SANTOS-REIS, TRINDADE AND BEJA, 1995 for a review and 
references thereafter, mostly written in Portuguese).  
This paper aims to introduce the conflict at a national scale by focusing on the 
inventory and distribution pattern of fish farms, related to otter distribution and on the 
selection of specific study sites to address the conflict from an ecological perspective, as well 
by presenting the methodology to be followed and the first results.  
 
 
2   METHODS 
 
In the frame of FRAP, an attempt was made, at a national level, to contact as many licensed 
fish farms as possible.  Interviews with owners focused on the presence or absence of otters in 
the vicinities of fish farms, the actual use of fish ponds and perception of damages caused.  
Inquiries were performed by phone and whenever possible or necessary (existence of 
preventive measures, outdoor facilities, claims of otter presence by owner) a visit was 
conducted to the fish farm to survey for otter signs.   The underlying questions of  the FRAP 
project related to otter predation on fish farms aim to identify the landscape factors 
influencing otter visiting rates to fish farms and to assess the loss of commercially important 
fish. To do so, two study areas were selected on the basis of the inventory/inquiries results:   
 
Marine fish farms 
In Sado estuary (south west Portugal), 14 semi-intensive coastal fisheries, located in old salt-
marshes, were selected for monitoring. Each varied in number (1 to18) and size (0.5 to 50ha) 
of ponds (Figure 1), and all mainly produce four marine fish species: sea bream (Sparus 
aurata), sea bass (Dicentrarchus labrax), Senegal sole (Solea senegalensis) and common sole 
(Solea solea), all potential otter prey.  Vegetation in the tidal area is typical of marshland 
areas, with dense and short halofitic shrubs (e.g. Arthrocnemum sp, Halimione portulacoides, 
Sarcocornia sp, Atriplex sp).  Around the estuary, cork oak (Quercus suber) woodlands and 
maritime pine tree (Pinus pinaster) plantations dominate the landscape. The freshwater lines 
include the main river (Sado), four streams (Comenda, Marateca, Vale de Cão and 
S.Martinho) and several smaller drainage lines.  The soil is mostly sandy with low capacity to 
retain water. Human population density is high but concentrated/aggregated in the main city 
(Setúbal) and other smaller urban areas.

Inland fish farms 
In the central region, inland farms are spread further apart and only six could be selected for 
monitoring (Figure 1).  All are directly related to a stream, have fewer (5 to 12) and smaller 
(0.5 to1 ha) man-made ponds, using concrete material, and just produce trout, brown (Salmo 
trutta fario) and rainbow (Oncorhynchus mykiss).  Nearby rivers are usually fairly preserved 
and have good riparian galleries dominated by different species according to location, 
including common alder (Alnus glutinosa), black poplar (Populus nigra), common sallow 
(Salix atrocinerea) and brambles (Rubus ulmifolius).  In these rivers only the brown trout is 
part of the wild fish community, together with several cyprinids.  Rainbow trout can also be 
found occasionally, but these individuals escaped from fish farms or were introduced by man.  
Human population density also varies according to each farm location. 
Starting in summer 2003, the two selected areas were surveyed, on a weekly basis at 
Sado estuary and monthly in the inland fish farms, to determine otter presence and to assess 
visiting rates. Spraints were collected for diet analysis.  Evidence and abundance of 
alternative prey and size distribution of wild fish species in the foraging area are being 
evaluated for comparison with otter consumption.  Also, a list of landscape factors that might 
influence otter visiting rates and, consequently, predation on commercially important species 
and thus the level of the otter–fishery conflict, was prepared on the basis of the knowledge of 
the species requirements as well as on the peculiarities of each study site.  Four primary key 
factors were identified (Ecography – e.g. sizes/area of water sources; Fishing effort – stocking 
density (species and size ranges); Otter status – e.g. otter distribution; Fishery-otter 
interaction – e.g. visiting rates) and are currently being evaluated, but no results are yet 
available. (Data presented in this paper refers only to summer 2003 results)

3   RESULTS 
 
3.1. Inventory and distribution pattern of fish farms   
Until the mid 1980s, aquaculture production consisted in the rearing of freshwater trout 
(Oncorhincus mykiss and Salmo truta fario) and in the bottom culture of bivalves in tidal 
estuaries, mainly clams, cockles and oysters (INE, 2002).  The culture of marine fish species 
was practically insignificant and restricted to saline reserve tanks.  When the Community 
Structural Policy was applied in Portugal, (mid 1980s) aquaculture began to be seen as an 
alternative method for the production of animal protein for human consumption and even as a 
complement of the traditional fishery production.  As a result of the EU subsidies, many 
saline areas in estuaries were converted to commercial fish farms and aquaculture production 
increased. 
In 2001, there were 1,412 licensed operators (INE, 2002).  The number of marine and 
brackish water farms is considerably larger than that of freshwater farms: 1,392 licensed 
marine farms, covering an area of 1549 hectares, and only 20 freshwater farms, established in 
38 hectares.  
Coastal aquaculture comprises two sectors: bivalve bottom culture with 1,289 licenses, 
and marine fish farms with about 103 registered establishments (6 of which are hatcheries).  
Most are in a semi-intensive regime ranging from 0.5 to 74.0 hectares, with an average size of 
10 hectares.  
Marine fish culture accounts mainly for the production of sea bream and sea bass, 
having in 2001 an output of 1,762 tonnes and 925 tonnes respectively, worthing a total of € 
13.7 million. 
For geographical reasons (temperature and water availability), most of the freshwater 
farms are located in the centre (30%) and north (65%) of the country.  This represents 98% of 
the total freshwater production in 2001 and accounting for 1,213 tonnes worth € 2.9 million. 
Marine fish farms are distributed in the estuarine areas along the coast but with an 
uneven distribution, with the higher proportion of production units located in the Tejo and 
Sado estuaries (central Portugal) where, in 2001, they reached 56% of all marine fish farms in 
Portugal.  These fish farms are located in Nature Reserves, which are also some of the best 
preserved areas for otters. 
 
3.2. Otter impact on fish farms 
3.2.1 Background to otter impact on fish farms: In 1990, a questionnaire was sent by the 
Portuguese Institute for Nature Conservation (TRINDADE, 1991) to 208 fish farmers, aiming to 
determine the impact of predation in their business and the financial losses attributes mostly 
to otter activities.  Although the response relatively was low (29%), some indications were 
obtained about the emerging conflict between fish-eating predators and fish farm owners.  
The otter was the most commonly referred to predator, causing damage all year round while 
some fish farmers perceived otters as a pest.  Complaints referred to predation and stress 
imposed on the fish, and the losses reported were high.  However, producers could not 
quantify damage levels, and nearby productions reported very contrasting damages. The 
absence of a compensation system, the perceived high otter numbers, its legal status and the 
constraints imposed by the protected areas to prevention measures led to a conflict that 
continues to exist, the impact of which remains largely unknown. 
 
3.2.2 FRAP inquiries:  Out of the known fish farm owners, 76 (61.8%) were interviewed. Of 
these, 32 (42.1%) owners were questioned by phone, while the remaining 44 farms were 
visited and surveyed for otter signs. Overall, ten (13.2%) fish farms were inactive, 36 (47.4%) 
considered negative for otter presence and 30 (39.5%) positive.

As expected, most of the negative results were from fish farms not surveyed once the 
selection was based on a high probability of otter occurrence. Of those 32 fish farms, nine 
(28.1%) were inactive, two (6.3%) considered positive to otter presence (but the survey visits 
were not possible) and 21 (65.6%) considered negative mostly because they were fenced or 
inaccessible to otters (n=8, 38.1%) or the owner demonstrated a fairly good knowledge of 
otters and was convinced of their absence.   
Of the 44 fish farms visited, one (2.3%) was inactive, 15 (34.1%) had no otter signs 
and 28 (63.6%) had signs.  
Of the 20 trout farms known in Portugal, 19 were contacted: two (10.5%) were 
inactive, eight (42.1%) considered to have no otter presence (but only two were visited) and 
nine (47.4%) were considered positive by survey. 
Of the 57 marine fish farms (55.3% of the 103 known in Portugal) were contacted, 
eight (14.0%) were inactive, 28 (49.1%) considered negative (13 visited) and 21 (36.8%) 
were positive (19 surveyed). 
In the trout farms all producers referred to otters, but also mentioned herons (Egretta 
garzeta, Ardea cinerea) predation upon the trout. The small trout (up to 6cm) ponds, when not 
protected by overhead wire, line, screen or netting, were also highly impacted by smaller 
birds. In the marine fish farms predation is attributed to otter, and herons, but particularly 
heavily to cormorants (Phalacrocorax carbo sinensis), especially in winter. When fish are 
small, the tern (Sterna hirundo) also seems to be an important predator.  Also, in general, fish 
farmers’ perception of otter daily consumption of fish was greatly overestimated reaching 
values as high as 10 kg per day. Some producers also claim negative impacts of pollution on 
water quality caused by bad agricultural practices and industry wastes.

4   CONCLUSIONS 
 
Otter presence in Portuguese fish farms is quite common and conflicts with fish farm owners 
exist but are usually taken care of privately by the owners who test different methods such as 
fencing, electric fencing and the use of dogs but also traps, shooting or even poison. 
Although results are preliminary, the conflict seems to have an ecological basis for 
more then half of the investigated fish farms.  Diet results in marine fish farms indicate the 
importance of alternative wild prey, such as crayfish and amphibians the availability of which 
may reduce predation upon stocked fish.  Nevertheless, the perceived high damages (by the 
fish farmers) and the prohibition of use of most mitigation measures (by the Nature Reserve) 
is increasing species aversion, and may lead to an increase in poaching.  
In the trout farms, although otter diet is based on commercially important fish species 
(trout), its impact on production is, probably, not as high as these preliminary results seem to 
imply.  The remains of the two trout species in the spraints are too difficult to distinguish and 
moreover, as the sizes of brown trout existing in the ponds and in the river are quite similar 
(from small to big fish), it is impossible to confirm, without using other elaborate fish bone 
analyses, if the origin of the consumed brown trout is the river or the trout farm.  
Additional data on the fish farms and on predation impacts is certainly necessary and 
the FRAP project will be a good step on that direction.
