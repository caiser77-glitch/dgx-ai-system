SURGICAL TECHNIQUE FOR INTRA-ABDOMINAL
RADIOTRANSMITTER PLACEMENT IN NORTH AMERICAN
RIVER OTTERS (LONTRA CANADENSIS)
Sonia M. Hernandez-Divers, D.V.M., George V. Kollias, D.V.M., Ph.D.,
Noha Abou-Madi, D.V.M., M.S., and Barry K. Hartup, D.V.M., M.S., Ph.D.
Abstract: Twenty-two free-ranging North American river otters (Lontra canadensis) from northern and eastern New
York were captured and surgically implanted with radiotransmitters as part of a relocation project. The surgical tech-
nique involved an incision in the paralumbar fossa and transection through the abdominal musculature to introduce a
radiotransmitter into the abdominal cavity. Two complications were encountered. Excessive hemorrhage occurred during
one procedure. The otter was treated for blood loss with ﬂuids, and it recovered uneventfully. Surgical incision infection
occurred in a second animal. The otter was treated with metronidazole and enroﬂoxacin, and the wound was cleaned
daily with chlorhexidine. The otter recovered uneventfully. Otters were released in western New York state. Postrelease
monitoring via radiotelemetry revealed that the otters became established in their new ranges. The intra-abdominal
implants did not affect their survival or reproductive potential.
Key words:
Otter, Lontra canadensis, intra-abdominal radiotransmitters, translocation.
INTRODUCTION
The North American river otter (Lontra cana-
densis) historically ranged throughout New York
state. As a result of habitat destruction, overhunt-
ing, and previously unregulated trapping practices,
the river otter was extirpated from the western part
the state.6 As part of a cooperative project including
the New York River Otter Project, the New York
State Department of Environmental Conservation
(DEC), and the College of Veterinary Medicine at
Cornell University, a translocation project was con-
ducted from September 1995 to November 2000.
The project goal was to relocate otters from the
Adirondack and Catskill mountain areas to western
New York state for the purpose of reintroduction.
Radiotelemetry was chosen as the primary method
of monitoring a representative number of released
otters for 18–24 mo postrelease. Radiotransmitters
were placed in the abdominal cavity of 22 otters
from September 1995 to November 1998. Here, we
describe the surgical technique used to implant ra-
diotransmitters through a paralumbar fossa incision.
Although the surgical placement of intra-abdominal
radiotransmitters has been previously described in

MATERIALS AND METHODS
Twenty-two free-ranging otters were trapped by
licensed trappers and transported by DEC staff
members to the Cornell University College of Vet-
erinary Medicine, where they were maintained in a
speciﬁc-pathogen-free carnivore holding facility, as
previously described.4 During their stay, they were
treated for capture-related injuries and intestinal
and pulmonary parasites. Otters were fed a highly
nutritious diet consisting lake trout (Salvelinus na-
maycush). The otters remained in this facility for a
minimum of 15 days and were then released by
DEC biologists into selected sites in western New
York that had recently been deemed capable of sus-
taining river otter populations.
Twenty-two adult otters (nine males and 13 fe-
males) were selected for radiotransmitter placement
from a group of 150 otters that were captured and
released between September 1995 and November
1998. The otters were sequentially selected if they
weighed .4 kg, had not sustained severe trap-re-
lated injuries, and were in good overall physical
condition as judged by two veterinarians. Trans-
mitter recipients were allowed to acclimate to cap-
tivity for an average of 13 days prior to implant
surgery. Food and water were withheld for 12 hr
the night before surgery. On the day of the surgery,
the otters (average weight 5 6.2 kg) were trans-
ferred to a portable primate squeeze cage, where
they were injected with a mixture of ketamine (Ke-
taset, Fort Dodge Laboratories, Fort Dodge, Iowa

Once anesthetized, otters were placed initially in
sternal recumbency and intubated with endotrache-
al tubes 3.0–4.0 mm inside diameter. Anesthesia
was maintained with isoﬂurane (Isoﬂo, Abbott Lab-
oratories, North Chicago, Illinois 60064, USA) and
oxygen through a rebreathing system at a rate of 2
L/min. Heart and respiratory rates, indirect blood
pressure, oxygen saturation, and rectal body tem-
perature were monitored. An i.v. catheter was
placed in either the jugular or cephalic vein, and
lactated Ringer’s solution was administered at 10
ml/kg/hr during the entire anesthetic period. The
otters were then placed in right lateral recumbency.
The hind limbs were extended caudally for better
surgical site exposure. The fur in a 12- 3 12-cm
area cranial to the quadriceps muscles of the hind
limb was shaved with electric clippers, and the site
was surgically prepared and draped in a standard
manner.
An 8-cm dorsoventral incision was made in the
center of this prepared area (Fig. 1), the subcuta-
neous tissues and fat were sharply transected with
a #10 scalpel blade, and the cutaneous trunci mus-
cle was sharply transected. This muscle was very
thick and was incorporated in loose connective tis-
sue. A thick layer of fat was present deep to this
muscle. Finally, the external abdominal oblique
muscle, internal abdominal oblique muscle, and
transverse abdominal oblique pars lumbalis were
bluntly transected. Both the external and internal
abdominal oblique muscles were thin. Deep to the
abdominal muscles, the transverse fascia and the
parietal peritoneum were sharply transected, allow-
ing entry into the abdominal cavity. Surgical radio-
cautery was used to achieve hemostasis throughout
the procedure. Once the peritoneal cavity was en-
tered, the implant was inserted and allowed to ﬂoat
freely in the abdominal cavity. The implant was
composed of a radiotransmitter and battery in a cy-
lindrical silicone case (Model IMP/200/L, Telonics,
Mesa, Arizona 85202, USA). The radiotransmitter
implants were 7 cm in length and 2 cm in width
and weighed 28.5 g (Fig. 2). Prior to surgery, the
implants were sterilized by submersion in a glutar-
aldehyde solution (Cidex, Johnson & Johnson Med-
ical, Arlington, Texas 76004, USA) for a minimum
of 40 min. Sterile distilled water was used to rinse
the implants prior to placing them into the abdom-
inal cavity. The abdominal muscle layers and peri-
toneum were closed with 3-0 polydioxanone (Max-
on, Sherwood, Davis & Geck, Manati, St. Louis,
Missouri 63103-1642, USA) in a simple interrupted
pattern. The subcutaneous tissue was apposed with
3-0 polygalactin 910 suture material (Vicryl, Ethi-
con, Johnson & Johnson, Somerville, New Jersey
08876, USA) in a simple continuous pattern. Po-
lygalactin suture material (3-0) was also used in an
intradermal suture pattern to appose the skin. In
some cases, surgical glue (VetBond, 3M Animal
Care Products, St. Paul, Minnesota 55144, USA)
was used over the surgical incison to provide a wa-
terproof seal.
Postsurgical bandaging was not utilized. The an-
esthesia and surgical procedures lasted an average
of 80 min. The otters were allowed to recover in
their holding cages, which had previously been
covered and padded with terry cloth towels. After
recovery, otters were given at least two dry terry
cloth towels per day to facilitate grooming and to
keep them free of feces, urine, water, and food de-
bris. The otters were not given access to water
sources in which they could submerge their bodies
until they were released. The surgical incision was
inspected daily for swelling, redness, or discharge.

The otters that underwent radiotransmitter implant
surgery were kept for observation and fur growth
for at least 1 wk (7–21 days) prior to release. DEC
biologists monitored the otters intensively for 18–
24 mo, until the batteries in the radiotransmitters
lost their power.
RESULTS
Twenty-two otters received surgically implanted
radiotransmitters during the fall from September
through November 1995, 1997, and 1998. No
deaths occurred as a result of the surgery, but two
minor surgical complications developed. During
one procedure, excessive hemorrhage occurred as a
result of inadvertent transection of a branch of the
circumﬂex iliac vein, which supplies the abdominal
muscles. This vessel was ligated, and the otter was
treated for acute blood loss with i.v. ﬂuids and sup-
portive care. Surgical wound infection occurred in
a second otter. This otter was lethargic for 20 days
postoperatively, although she continued to eat nor-
mally. Twenty-one days after surgery, the incision
dehisced and was draining purulent exudate. The
otter was anesthetized as previously described, and
the surgical incision was explored. Malodorous, pu-
rulent exudate was present in the subcutaneous
space, and the remaining intradermal and subcuta-
neous sutures were removed. Because there was no
evidence that the infection continued into the peri-
toneal cavity, the wound was debrided, copiously
lavaged, and left open to heal by secondary inten-
tion. The exudate was cultured for both aerobic and
anaerobic bacteria, and a few colonies of Pasteu-
rella multocida and Staphylococcus aureus and a
moderate number of colonies of Clostridium and
Fusobacterium were isolated. The otter was treated
with enroﬂoxacin (Baytril, Bayer, Shawnee Mis-
sion, Kansas 66201, USA; 10 mg/kg) and metro-
nidazole (Flagyl, Searle, Chicago, Illinois 60680,
USA; 50 mg/kg). The otter was restrained by pull-
ing the squeeze wall in its hospital cage forward,
and the wound was lavaged with chlorhexidine
(Nolvasan, Fort Dodge, Fort Dodge, Iowa 50501,
USA) and physiologic saline solutions once daily
for 7 days using a syringe and a blunted metal can-
nula. The surgical wound healed uneventfully, and
the otter was released 11 days after the initial sur-
gical wound debridement.
Three of the 22 otters (11%) implanted with ra-
diotransmitters died during the postrelease moni-
toring period. One otter that was released in July
1997 was found dead as a consequence of a motor
vehicle accident in March 1999; necropsy was not
possible due to severe trauma and autolysis. An ot-
ter that was released in November 1998 was found
dead in January 2000. Necropsy revealed that this
animal was a pregnant female. The surgical site had
healed, and there was no evidence of complications
associated with the implant. The radiotransmitter
from a third otter, which was released in October
1997, was found in January 1998. There was no
evidence of a carcass, and the radiotransmitter was
presumed to have been removed from the carcass
by a scavenger. The remaining 19 otters were mon-
itored via radiotelemetry on a regular basis for a
period of 18–24 months postrelease. The period of
monitoring was limited by the life span of the trans-
mitter batteries. At the time that the individual ra-
diotransmitters ceased emitting signals, the remain-
ing 19 otters had successfully become established
in their new range, as determined by the project
biologists using direct observations and other meth-
ods. Although no reproduction occurred during the
postrelease radiotelemetry monitoring period, biol-
ogists directly observed a pair of reintroduced ot-
ters in a den with young 1 yr later. In addition, the
otters that were not implanted with abdominal ra-
diotransmitters but that were monitored via direct
observation did not resume their normal reproduc-
tive behavior for at least 2–3 yr (Penrod, pers.
comm., 2001).
DISCUSSION
Monitoring animals postrelease is the key to
evaluating the success of any translocation project.
Radiotelemetry is an excellent tool for monitoring
the activity patterns, migration, and survival of re-
introduced river otters.7,8 Internal radio transmitters
are advantageous because they are likely to remain
functional much longer than traditional radio col-
lars or harnesses.3,5 Internal radiotransmitters are
particularly useful in otters, whose fusiform mor-
phology makes it difﬁcult to ﬁt them with radio
collars. In addition, their semiaquatic lifestyle and
frequent rubbing behavior to keep dry and clean
would shorten the life span of a collar or harness.
Internal radiotransmitters also do not interfere with
the animal’s locomotion or behavior. The battery
life span of these radiotransmitters is reported by
the manufacturer to be 10–12 mo; however, in our
study all batteries functioned for approximately 18–
24 mo.
With proper placement, radiotransmitters in the
abdominal cavity have been safe and useful in sea
and river otters.2,3,5,7,8,10–12 Possible disadvantages to
intraperitoneal radiotransmitters include morbidity
and/or mortality associated with the surgical pro-
cedure, delay of release due to postoperative mon-
itoring, and costs associated with the surgical pro-
cedure.

In our study, no major problems were associated
with the abdominal implant surgeries or the post-
surgical period. The hemorrhage experienced dur-
ing one procedure can be avoided by taking care
not to incise too far ventrally thus inadvertently
transecting a branch of the circumﬂex iliac vein.
However, if this vessel is transected, it can be safely
ligated without untoward effects. As in all surgical
procedures, surgical incisions can become infected.
Adherence to strict aseptic technique, including
shaving the fur to properly prepare the incision site,
is recommended to decrease the likelihood of in-
fection. Although the fur in the prepared area did
not regrow completely prior to release, survival
was not affected. During this project, otters were
released no later in fall than November, prior to
heavy snowfall in New York state.
The midventral approach to the abdominal cavity
of mammals is typically preferred because the linea
alba offers a relatively avascular plane of dissec-
tion. The main disadvantage of using a surgical ap-
proach through the paralumbar fossa is the need to
transect a group of large muscles.1 Otters frequently
rub themselves by sliding the ventral portion of
their bodies against vegetation in the wild or towels
in captivity. A surgical wound in the midventral
abdominal area is more likely to become wet, con-
taminated by feces, or disrupted by the frequent
rubbing motion. Previous reports of dehiscence and
infection with midventral surgical incisions suggest
that the paralumbar fossa approach to the abdomi-
nal cavity is superior to the ventral midline ap-
proach.5,8 We agree with this ﬁnding and recom-
mend the paralumbar fossa approach.
In contrast to the cutaneous trunci muscle of the
dog, this muscle in the river otter is very thick and
is incorporated in loose connective tissue. The sur-
gical exposure was greatly enhanced by sharply
transecting this muscle. In addition, the ﬁbers of the
cutaneous trunci muscle are oriented perpendicular
to the skin incision; therefore, adequate exposure
could only be obtained with sharp transection
across the muscle ﬁbers. Blunt transection between
the ﬁbers of the internal and transverse abdominal
oblique muscles provided adequate exposure with-
out the need for sharp transection. The otters in this
project did not traumatize their surgical incisions;
therefore, postsurgical bandaging was not applied.
The surgical abdominal implantation of radio-
transmitters in river otters appears to be a safe and
relatively easy procedure that allows the monitoring
of free-ranging otters. In addition, the intraperito-
neal transmitters did not negatively affect the sur-
vival of these otters. As indicated by the pregnant
implanted otter and reports of other released fe-
males that have reproduced, the abdominal trans-
mitters do not appear to affect the reproductive po-
tential of these animals.
