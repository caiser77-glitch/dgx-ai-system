Implementation of a GIS-based management tool for conservation
of biodiversity within the municipality of Oslo, Norway

Abstract
Tools to effectively apply biological information in daily physical planning cases are viewed as essential in order to
preserve and manage natural habitats in highly pressured urban areas. This paper describes a GIS-based management tool
for conservation and management of biodiversity and the process of implementation in the capital of Oslo, Norway. The
main objective of this multi-agency effort is to ensure a sustainable and responsible management of the biodiversity within
the municipality. A political decision on the state level encourages all municipalities to survey and classify their biological
diversity according to a scale of importance within 2003. Furthermore the results from the survey were encouraged to be
implemented in the daily physical planning at a local level. The assignment called for structured, classiﬁed and accessible
information about the biological resources of the municipality. The electronic tool presented makes it possible to combine
biological information with any other information that is relevant for a planning issue. We believe the GIS-based management
tool has a strong potential to be transferred to other municipalities in Norway as well as to other countries with organised
environmental management systems at both local, regional and national levels.

1. Introduction
The need for more sustainable approaches for plan-
ning and management of nature is increasingly recog-
nised world-wide. For the past two decades the con-
cept of urban ecology has become more understood
as a base for urban planning (Bornkam et al., 1980;
Gilbert, 1989; Sukopp et al., 1990, 1995). Tools to
effectively apply biological information in daily phys-
ical planning cases are viewed as essential in order to
preserve natural habitats in highly pressured urban ar-
eas (Botequilha and Ahern, 2002, Jensen et al., 2000).

This paper describes a GIS-based management tool
for conservation of biodiversity and the process of
implementation in the capital of Oslo, Norway. The
main objective of this multi-agency effort is to ensure
a sustainable and responsible management of the bio-
diversity within the municipality. The electronic tool
makes it possible to combine biological information
with any other information that is relevant for planning
cases. The system enables local authorities to early de-
tect any conﬂicts between conservation interests and
other types of land use as well as serving as an infor-
mation system for the public and consultants dealing
with environmental impact assessments. The manage-
ment tool comprises the entire municipality including
forested and agricultural land outside the urban and
suburban areas. The article however, focuses on the
urban and suburban aspect of the tool.
A political decision at the national level encourages
all municipalities to survey and classify their biodi-
versity according to a scale of importance within 2003
(Miljøverndepartementet, 1997). A further White Pa-
per (Miljøverndepartementet, 2001) describes the im-
portance and need of developing national systems for
mapping and monitoring biodiversity where biologi-
cal data are fully integrated in the physical planning at
the municipal level. In accordance, the municipality of
Oslo stated a goal in the White Paper “Urban Ecology
Programme” (Oslo kommune, 1998) to preserve and
further develop the biodiversity of Oslo through con-
servation and sustainable management of the natural
resources within the city. In 1999 the Directorate for
Nature Management published a national overview
of nature types of importance to biodiversity. At the
same time they encouraged all municipalities to sur-
vey localities within the municipality according to this
system. As an incentive the initial surveys were sub-
sidised by the state with
6250 to every municipality
in the country. To meet the common national and local
goal for preserving biodiversity, the assignment called
for structured, classiﬁed and accessible information
about the biological resources of the municipality. In
Norway 330 of the 434 municipalities have performed
the survey of biodiversity and described the survey
results in different unpublished and published reports
(Terje Klokk, Personal communication, Directorate
for Nature Management, 2002). A short literature re-
view, search on Internett and telephone contact with
Nordic colleagues did not identify any published ar-
ticles on the various kinds of GIS-based management
systems and the implementation process developed in
order to make biological data accessible in the daily
decision-making in the municipalities. Descriptions
of thematic GIS layers and the importance of inte-
grating these layers in physical planning are however
described in various publications (Lövenhaft et al.,
2002; Thomas, 2002; Theobald et al., 2000; Sukopp
and Weiler, 1988; Bernhardsen, 1999). There are
numerous other publications too.
2. Study area
The municipality of Oslo (454 km2) is located in
the inner part of the Oslo Fjord in Eastern Norway
(59′55N, 10′45E) (Fig. 1). Approximately two-thirds
(307 km2) of the municipality is covered by forest, wa-
terways and agricultural land and one-third (147 km2)
by a building zone that includes residential, commer-
cial and industrial areas (Oslo kommune, 2001). Ap-
proximately 30 km2 of the building zone is covered by
mixed boreal forest and deciduous trees (Norwegian
Institute for Land Inventory, 2000). The municipality
employs approximately 42,000 people and consists of
around 70 different agencies (Oslo kommune, 2001).
The local climate is characterised by mild winters
and warm summers (Oslo kommune, 2001) with the
city itself located in a south-facing valley. The ter-
rain slopes gently upwards from sea to the forested
hills around the city (300–700 m above sea level).
The inner Oslo Fiord belongs to the Oslo region.
Cambro-Silurian limestone and shales, rich in calcium,
folded in the Caledonian era, make up the central
parts of the city and westwards along the fjord. The
Oslo region is a result of an extended depression of
earth covered by sea and slowly ﬁlled with sediments.

2.1. Biodiversity in Oslo
Natural vegetation in the city of Oslo consists of
remnants of countryside that have persisted inside the
city, wasteland/ruderal areas and other areas regulated
of zoning plans by authorities. There are eight rivers
of different sizes ﬂowing through the town and its sub-
urban residential areas creating corridors of natural
greenstructure from the forest to the ﬁord. Approxi-
mately 60% of the 283 km of water corridors have been
canalised underground (Oslo kommune, 2002, 2003).
Due to a mild local climate, calcium-rich soil, var-
ied topography and a short gradient between sea level
and the surrounding boreal forests, the city of Oslo is
very rich in biodiversity in relation to its location and
latitude (Table 1). The different naturetypes of the city
is illustrated in Fig. 2. The richest biotopes of natural
vegetation are on the Cambro-Silurian layers up to ap-
proximately 220 m above sea level. Furthermore, Oslo

2.2. Threats to biodiversity in Oslo
An analysis of the reduction of green structure in
Oslo from 1952 to 1990 revealed a signiﬁcant impact
on three categories of green structure due to land use
changes from natural areas to residential, commercial
and industrial space (Guttu et al., 1997). The same
study found that there has been a strong fragmenta-
tion of areas, on average a 50% reduction from 1950
to 1990, which resulted in more, but smaller areas of
green open spaces. The same trend could be antici-
pated to take place from 1990 to the present day, but
the introduction of the Green Plan (Oslo kommune,
1992) and the Green Poster (Oslo kommune, 1997)
are actions that hopefully alter this development.
Along with the reduction of greenstructure follows
higher pressure on remaining areas. Wear and tear
increases as well as the need for more detailed and
skilled management. Oslo has become a multicultural
city during the last 20 years. Today different cultures
use the green open spaces differently, but the local
knowledge is so far too low in order to develop a man-
agement system that includes these aspects.
The overall strategy of the municipality concerning
the shortage of housing is in-ﬁll of existing central
residential areas in connection with the public trans-
port network. Although there is less green structure
in central urban areas the existing green open spaces
are often prone to loss and fragmentation. The strat-
egy is explained through four density categories: (1)
areas suited for city expansion; (2) areas with special
considerations concerning landscape and vegetation;
(3) area with protected building structure; (4) general
inn-ﬁll areas) for in-ﬁll of housing arrangements (Oslo
kommune, 1995).

3. Development of a GIS-based management
tool
3.1. Organisational structure
The Department of Transport and Environmental
Affairs initiated the multi-agency work by inviting
agencies in the municipality with responsibilities for
physical planning and nature management to partic-
ipate in a reference group for biodiversity in Oslo.
The main goals were to identify relevant biological
information, to prepare a database with a thematic
GIS layer and to implement the results where needed.
Along with this, the Commissioner stated that the
Recreation and Leisure Service would be responsible
for conservation and further development of biodiver-
sity through sustainable management in the munic-
ipality including maintenance of a nature database,
updating maps and implementation of the GIS-based
management tool in the municipality (Oslo kommune,
2002). Through a political process in 2002, the city
government selected four different agencies (Planning
and Building Authority, Recreation and Leisure Ser-
vice, Public Health Authority and Water and Sewage
Authority) dealing with planning and nature manage-
ment to be pilots in implementation of the GIS-based
management tool in all physical planning. The Plan-
ning and Building Authority with 400 employees is
the key agency in decision-making in physical plan-
ning issues. They were viewed as the key agency in
implementing the database system.
3.2. Classiﬁcation of nature types
The greenstructure was classiﬁed according to 56
standard nature types determined by the Direktoratet
for Naturforvaltning (1999) and divided into seven
main categories: (1) bogs, (2) rockslide, rocky area
and edge zones, (3) mountain vegetation, (4) cultural
landscapes, (5) freshwater and wetlands, (6) forest,
(7) coastal areas. The nature types have elements of
importance to vegetation, geology, zoology and land-
scape. The selection of the 56 nature types were based
on documentation of red-listed species, continuity in

3.3. Technical solutions
The database Natur2000 (Naturkart, 1999) is a
Norwegian commercial database system that has been
developed to handle data according to the national sys-
tem designed by the Direktoratet for Naturforvaltning
(1999). It is the most common database system for bio-
diversity mapping in Norway. Natur2000 is specially
designed for use in the management of natural re-
sources and biodiversity. It is built in the database sys-
tem FileMaker Pro (FileMaker Inc. 2002). Natur2000
is made up of separate databases or registers that are
linked together (relational database-system). The cen-
tral unit in Natur2000 is the locality registry where
every locality has to be registered. To this locality
various information can be linked through a unique
locality number. Examples of different registries are;
Key habitat (naturetype), Wildlife, Vascular Plants,
Observer, Literature, etc. Whereas species of vascular
plants and wildlife are registered in separate registers,

3.4. Implementation
An extract of the complete database information
was distributed to the pilot agencies through a regu-
lar worksheet. The full database information was not
included in order to simplify the information avail-
able for planners and decision-makers that in most
cases do not have specialised biological competence.
The most important ﬁrst hand information for planners
was found to be: key-habitat (naturtype), classiﬁca-
tion value, status and a short comment ﬁeld explaining
the most important biological features of the locality.
The polygons and database ﬁles were made available
through the computer network in each agency. The
tools are based on commercially available computer
applications that are proven inexpensive and easily ac-
cessible to multiple stakeholders in the physical plan-
ning in the municipality. Updated data was delivered
the pilot agencies free of charge one to two times a
year according to the availability of new information.
Along with the GIS-based management tool, a check-
point to remind each individual planner to check the
issue before closing a case was made part of the reg-
ular routines in the agency.
Intensive, short user courses of one hour were
developed as the most important tool in the imple-
mentation process. All ofﬁcers dealing with physical
planning and management of natural areas in the pilot
agencies were involved in the course. The courses
focused especially on explaining how the manage-
ment system can be used in day to day situations,
independent of the scale of the planning issue, where

how to use the system in the physical planning and
management processes. During the process of imple-
mentation an important motivational factor was how
each planner initially can detect areas with a conﬂict
between biodiversity and other land use issues. Doing
so can save work later in the process before it has
reached a stage where it is more difﬁcult and costly to
remedy.
4. Conclusion and further development
It is important that biodiversity is integrated in the
overall environmental policy and management system
of the city, and not regarded as a separate, specialised
issue. Many cities have good quality data about
biodiversity, but too little communication with the
physical planning system in the municipality. Often
the conservation of biodiversity has been dependent
on efforts from NGOs and other private or public LA
21 initiatives instead of being an intimate, integrated
and permanent part of the institution itself (Hackett,
1997). The described system is an attempt to give
the physical planner responsibilities and motivation
to incorporate and integrate biodiversity values in the
physical planning at an early stage of the process.
The GIS-based tool for supporting local authorities in
translating sustainable development goals on biodiver-
sity into all physical planning and decision-making is
one important step to avoid the above described trend.
We believe the GIS-based management tool has a
strong potential to be transferred to other municipali-
ties in Norway as well as to other countries with or-
ganised environmental management systems at both
local, regional and national levels. The GIS-based tool
is an example of a goal-to-practise translation process
where biological data are converted into accessible in-
formation for the physical planners. Making biolog-
ical data broadly accessible in the municipal system
is the only way to integrate the challenge of preserv-
ing, further develop and manage biodiversity in urban
environments in Oslo as well as other urban settings.

By 2005 all agencies in the municipality of Oslo
are supposed to introduce an Environmental Manage-
ment System following the so-called Demings-Circle
(Deming, 1950, 1986). This model is widely used
in environmental planning certiﬁcation processes of
businesses. When conducting an environmental cer-
tiﬁcation like EMAS or ISO 14001 businesses have
to elaborate plans, explain dues, check and ﬁnally act
according to the so-called PDCA circle (Fig. 6). Goals
and plans for biodiversity should be included in all the
relevant agencies according to the PDCA circle (Juran
and Gryna, 1988). The use of such a process insures
that the steps are conducted in a well planned way.
The GIS tool and the implementation process will
be evaluated within the framework of the revision of
the “Urban ecology Programme” (2006–2010). The
results will be described in a further paper explaining
the experiences and challenges met by implementing
the GIS-based system on a larger-scale in an urban
municipality.
