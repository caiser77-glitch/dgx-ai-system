EFFECTS OF SURGICAL IMPLANTATION OF TEMPERATURE
DATALOGGERS ON REPRODUCTION OF CAPTIVE STRIPED
SKUNKS (MEPHITIS MEPHITIS)
Yeen Ten Hwang, B.Sc.(Hon), Marie-Line Gentes, D.V.M., Dennilyn L. Parker, D.V.M.,
Serge Larivie`re, Ph.D., and Franc¸ois Messier, Ph.D.
Abstract: A total of 20 (14 females, six males) captive striped skunks (Mephitis mephitis) with miniature temperature
dataloggers implanted free in the abdominal cavity were examined for reproductive performance and pathology because
of implants. Eleven of 12 female skunks reproduced successfully 45.9 6 3.7 days after surgery to remove implanted
dataloggers. The pregnancy rate of 91.7% (11/12) was much higher than other captive studies and was comparable
with that of wild skunks. Eight striped skunks (six males, two females) that were euthanatized and necropsied after
having implants in the abdominal cavity for 5 mo showed no apparent pathology associated with the implant. Implan-
tation of the intraperitoneal devices did not lead to complications in abdominal tissues. Neither implant nor surgery
affected reproduction. We conclude that implanted dataloggers can safely be used in physiologic studies of striped
skunks or possibly other small carnivores in captive or ﬁeld studies.
Key words:
Reproductive performance, implantation, surgery, anesthesia, Mephitis mephitis, striped skunks.
INTRODUCTION
Implant devices such as telemetry transmitters
and dataloggers are regularly used in physiology
research for monitoring parameters such as animal
heart rate and body temperature. These devices can
be implanted either under the skin or within the
peritoneal cavity.1,6,9,16,22 Telemetry transmitters are
often larger and expensive compared with data-
loggers and require the setup of a computer system
to receive data at speciﬁed intervals, whereas da-
taloggers record data in a nonvolatile memory with-
in the device requiring the retrieval of the device
for data to be downloaded.
With increased use of intraperitoneal implants in
wildlife studies, the potential effects of implants on
animal health and behavior should be addressed.2,17
Reproductive performance of animals also can be
affected by anesthesia, surgery, and implanted de-
vices in the abdominal cavity. Moreover, implants
can have pathological effects on surrounding tis-
sues. Intraperitoneal implantation of transmitters in
ﬁve species of ducks (Anas acuta, Anas platyrhyn-
chos, Aythya americana, Aythya collaris, and Bu-
cephala clangula),10 river otters (Lontra canaden-

This study examines whether implantation and
surgical removal procedures of miniature tempera-
ture dataloggers in striped skunks (Mephitis me-
phitis) affected reproduction. The reproductive cy-
cle in female striped skunks includes spring breed-
ing, immediate development of eggs to blastocysts,
brief delay of implantation for a few days, and in-
trauterine implantation with a gestation of 59–77
days.20 Dataloggers were surgically implanted in
autumn and removed after spring breeding.
MATERIALS AND METHODS
Animals
Twenty captive striped skunks (14 females, six
males) were studied at the Delta Marsh Research
Station of the Delta Waterfowl Foundation located
in Manitoba, Canada. We caught three adult fe-
males and one adult male in the area in April 2002;
16 (11 females, ﬁve males) were juveniles born
from the wild-caught females in captivity from 1 to
14 May 2002. Animals were kept in outdoor 5-m2
pens made of 1.5-m-high plywood wall with ce-
ment ﬂooring. Each animal was provided with in-
dividual nest boxes (height 38 cm 3 width 30.1 cm
3 length 46 cm) ﬁlled with straw.
Captive skunks were fed a balanced diet of Om-
nivore Zoo Feed A (Mazurit, St. Louis, Missouri

From August to late October, food was provided ad
lib. to simulate the abundance of food in the ﬁeld.
Felitabs vitamins (Vetcom Inc., Upton, Quebec J0H
2E0, Canada) with taurine were also provided to
supplement the dry food to prevent cardiomyopathy
in captive skunks. In summer, adult males and fe-
males were kept separate, and females were housed
with their litters until young were weaned in July
2002. During winter from 1 November 2002 to 28
March 2003, striped skunks were kept together to
facilitate breeding in spring. Surgical implantation
of miniature temperature probes was performed on
30 October 2002, and all probes were surgically
removed on 28 March 2003. After spring surgery
to remove implants, female skunks were housed in-
dividually. Captive skunk colony operates under
Manitoba permits (WSP 02004) and animal care
protocol (MWACC #2002-03).
Datalogger implants
Miniature temperature dataloggers (Thermo-
chron iButtont DS1921, Dallas Semiconductor
Corp., Dallas, Texas 75244, USA) measure tem-
perature from as low as 2208C to 858C in 0.58C
increments and are able to log up to 2,048 consec-
utive temperature measurements in nonvolatile
memory. Dataloggers weigh 5 g and measure 17.35
mm in diameter and 5.89 mm in thickness. Data-
loggers were double coated with physiologically in-
ert Parafﬁn/Elvax (Mini Mitter Company Inc., Sun-
river, Oregon 97707, USA). Coated dataloggers
were disinfected with Cidext (Johnson & Johnson,
Markham, Ontario L3R 0T5, Canada) in individual
1-dl sterile containers for 24 hr and subsequently
immersed in sterile saline solution (NaCl 0.9%, Ab-
bott Laboratories Ltd., Montreal, Quebec H3C 3K6,
Canada) immediately before surgical implantation
into the abdominal cavity.
Surgical procedure: implant and retrieval
All surgical procedures were performed at the
research station located at Delta Marsh, Manitoba,
Canada. A room adjacent to the outdoor holding
facility was used as a ﬁeld operating theater. Tem-
perature in the surgical room was maintained at 20–
258C. A total of 38 surgical procedures were per-
formed from 2002 through 2003, including implan-
tation and recovery of miniature dataloggers.
Food was withdrawn 12 hr before surgeries.
Skunks were caught in their pens using plastic live
traps (Dura-poly livetrapt, Bert ‘‘Ram’’ Trap Ltd.,
Birtle, Manitoba R0M 0C0, Canada). On capture,
halothane (MTC Pharmaceuticals, Cambridge, On-
tario N3C 2W4, Canada) was used to immobilize
animals in live traps.11 Skunks were brought in-
doors to the operating theater after complete im-
mobilization was reached outside. Anesthesia was
maintained with a mask delivering isoﬂurane
(IsoFlot, Abbott Laboratories Ltd., St-Laurent,
Quebec H4S 1Z1, Canada) using a vaporizer. Each
skunk received 0.03 mg/kg i.m. injection of bupre-
norphine (Buprenext, Reckitt & Colman Products
Ltd., Hull HU8 7DS, U.K.) and 0.2 mg/kg s.c. in-
jection of meloxicam (Metacamt, Boehringer In-
gelheim Ltd., Burlington, Ontario L7L 5H4, Can-
ada). Skunks were placed dorsally recumbent be-
side a warm water bottle and on warm water cir-
culating pad. During surgeries, heart rate was
monitored using a Doppler ultrasound system
(Parks Medical Electronics, Aloha, Oregon 97007,
USA) on the palmar artery. Respiration was ob-
served visually, and rectal temperature was record-
ed with a digital thermometer. Physiologic data dur-
ing surgery were recorded and separated into three
categories, induction (when animal has reached
complete immobilization under isoﬂurane), surgery
(when incision was ﬁrst made), and recovery (when
incision was sutured and animal is removed from
isoﬂurane).
A 6 cm by 4 cm surgical site over the ventral
midline was clipped and scrubbed with chlorhexi-
dine (Medichem, S.A., Sant Joan Despı´, Barcelona
08970, Spain) and water. Standard surgical prepa-
ration, alternating passages of chlorhexidine and is-
opropylic alcohol 70%, was then applied to the site.
The animal was draped and 3-cm incision was
made on the linea alba, caudal to the umbilicus.
The datalogger was inserted and moved to the right
side of the abdominal cavity. Muscle layer and sub-
cutaneous tissue were individually closed. The skin
was closed with an intradermal simple continuous
pattern. Polydioxanone monoﬁlament suture (PDS
3-0, ETHICON Inc., Somerville, New Jersey
08837, USA) was used for the three layers. The
same procedure was applied for the removal of the
dataloggers, average incision length was 2.8 cm
(SD 5 0.6, n 5 8) and cranial to the scar from the
previous surgery. Surgery was approved by Uni-
versity of Saskatchewan Animal Care Protocol
#20020092.
Animal observation
After surgery, each skunk was then put back into
a live trap with a warm water bottle until it was
alert and transferred to an outdoor pen with a nest
box for recovery. Animals were provided with food
and fresh water. All skunks were feeding normally
the night after surgery, when released back into the
outdoor pen. The day after surgery, each nest box

RESULTS
Eleven of 12 adult females successfully repro-
duced in captivity with an average litter size of 5.5
6 0.9 (see Table 1). Parturition occurred with a
mean of 45.9 6 3.7 days after implant removal sur-
gery. All 11 animals bred before the removal of
implantation, and by the time of surgery, all would
have undergone the initial stages of pregnancy with
the implanted dataloggers in the abdominal cavity.
All 12 females had progesterone levels .3.18
mmol/L (1 ng/ml) with highest progesterone level
of 68.4 mmol/L (21.5 ng/ml) (Table 1).
All implants became encapsulated in a 10- to 15-
cm-long loop of omentum at the pyloric level, with
thin and well-vascularized ﬁbrous tissue covering
the dataloggers. Necropsies performed on eutha-
nized skunks showed that none of the skunks ex-
hibited signs of peritoneal infection or adhesions.
Also, necropsy performed on the dead individual
revealed severe emaciation with stomach ulcers in-
dependent of the implant device.
Mean time under anesthesia for the surgical im-
plantation and removal was 25.7 6 8.5 min and
39.8 6 9.0 min, respectively. During surgeries,
heart rate, respiration rate, and rectal temperature
were similar in the autumn and spring (see Table
2). All skunks became alert within 5–10 min after
cessation of exposure to isoﬂurane. Skunks under
anesthesia experienced body temperature of 34 to
358C.
DISCUSSION AND CONCLUSIONS
Surgery and intraperitoneal implantation of da-
taloggers did not affect reproduction or survival of
striped skunks in captivity. On the basis of our ob-
servations on implanted females, early stages of the
breeding cycle (including copulation and embry-
onic development) can proceed successfully and are
not impaired by the presence of a datalogger in the
abdominal cavity. Surgery to remove implant also
did not affect reproductive performance of study
animals.
In the wild, average recorded mean litter size for
striped skunks is from 6 to 7 young.3,8,18 In captiv-
ity, mean litter size usually is lower (mean litter
size 4).20 In our study, the average litter size was
6, which is intermediate between data from the wild
and data from captive studies, suggesting that the
effects of surgery and implantation did not lead to
reduced litter size. Also, use of isoﬂurane and hal-
othane during surgery did not affect the survival of
embryos as has been reported for ketamine hydro-
chloride, which crosses the placenta and produces
anesthetic effects in the fetuses.5

pregnancy rate of 91.7% (11/12) was much higher
than 55.4% reported in other captive studies.20 The

ovulation after copulation but fertilization was un-

Implantation of the intraperitoneal devices did
not lead to complications in the abdominal tissues.
All probes became encapsulated in an umbilicated
loop of omentum and were recovered from various
parts of the abdomen where they had migrated.
They could be easily retrieved by a small incision
into the thin ﬁbrous membrane capsule around the
implant because the dataloggers were coated with
physiologic inert wax. In summary, surgical im-
plantation of dataloggers does not affect reproduc-
tive performance of striped skunks. Use of such
devices may yield valuable insights into the phys-
iologic responses of striped skunks during critical
life history stages.
