SUMMARY 
 
 
1. The Eurasian otter is protected by national and international legislation  
which makes it an offence to disturb, kill, trap or harm the species as well 
as damaging and/or disturbing its resting and breeding sites, 
2. Orkney has long been recognised as an important area for otters with a 
population of national importance. 
3. The species is protected by national and international legislation which 
makes it an offence to kill and/or disturb the species, disturb and/or 
destroy its breeding, resting and feeding areas. 
4. The intention is to build a tidal test facility off the coast of the Island of 
Eday.  This will involve the construction of a shore substation connected 
by cable to four sites offshore where prototype generators will be 
deployed. 
5. Evidence of otter presence – spraints (faeces), urine marking, footprints 
and runs through the vegetation – were found at a number of sites along 
the six kilometres of cost surveyed, including a concentration of sites in 
the immediate vicinity of the site of the proposed substation. 
6. Although a number of potential holts were identified, no occupied ones 
were found. 
7. No natal holts were recorded. 
8. The results of the survey suggested that otters regularly visit the area 
around the site of the proposed development, there was no evidence that, at 
the time of the survey (May 2005), they were actually ‘living’ there (i.e. no 
occupied holts were found). 
9. An assessment of the results of the survey in light of the construction of 
the site and its subsequent operation concluded that it was unlikely that 
there would be any significant long-term impact on the local otter 
population. 
10. A number of recommendations are made which should minimise any 
effect that might be experienced.

1.   INTRODUCTION 
 
The Eurasian otter (Lutra lutra) has been described as having one of the widest 
distributions of all Palearctic mammals  (Corbet, 1966).  Its range originally 
extended from Portugal in the west to Japan in the east, and from the Arctic to the 
countries bordering the southern shores of the Mediterranean.  In Asia, it is found as 
far south as Indonesia (Conroy, Melisch & Chanin, 1998; Conroy & Chanin, 2002). 
It is the only species of otter indigenous to Europe.  
     Over the past 40 years, the number of animals declined throughout much of the 
otter’s range.  This was particularly the case in western Europe, where concern had 
been expressed for the survival of the species in several countries (Reuther, 1980). In 
the U.K., otters are currently listed in the Red Data Book of British Mammals 
(Morris, 1993). One of the strongholds of the species is the Highlands and Islands of 
Scotland (Green & Green, 1997).  Recent studies have shown, however, that otter 
populations are recovering throughout much of Europe (Conroy & Chanin, 2002; 
Conroy et al., 2004). 
 
Because of its precarious status throughout much of Europe, the species has 
been subject to increasing national and international legislation pertaining to the 
conservation of the species. Such is the legal framework established that it is not only 
an offence to kill, trap or harm the otter, it is also an offence to disturb the species, as 
well as its breeding and resting sites, and to damage its habitat (Appendix 1). 
 
The otter appears on both Annex II and Annex IV of the Council Directive 
92/43/EEC on the conservation of natural habitats and of wild fauna and flora (the 
Habitats Directive).  Annex II species require the designation of protected areas by 
Member States (Special Areas of Conservation – SACs) as set out in Articles 3, 4 
and 6 of the Directive.  Annex IV species require strict protection measures by 
Member States in accordance with Article 12 of the Directive.  These measures not 
only include the prohibition of capture, killing, deliberate disturbance at e.g. breeding 
sites and destruction of breeding and resting sites, but also include a requirement to 
monitor the incidental capture and killing of Annex IV listed species.  If any 
operation is deemed to cause disturbance to the species, then applications for the 
relevant licences in Scotland need to be made to Scottish Natural Heritage (SNH) 
and the Scottish Executive (SE), before commencement of operations. 
The Habitats Directive introduces for the first time for protected areas, the 
precautionary principle, i.e., developments can only be permitted having ascertained 
no adverse effect on the integrity of the site. Developments may still be permitted if 
there are no alternatives, and there are imperative reasons of overriding public interest 
 
The Eurasian otter was for a long time classified as ‘vulnerable’ in the IUCN 
Red List of Threatened Species.  However, in the most recent issue of the Red List 
(2004), the status of the species has been lowered to ‘near threatened’ i.e. the species 
is considered to be near threatened when they have been evaluated against the criteria 
but do not qualify for Critically Endangered, Endangered or Vulnerable now, but are 
close to qualifying for or are likely to qualify for a threatened category in the near 
future.

2. THE OTTER IN ORKNEY 
 
As a species, the otter has been long established on Orkney, the earliest remains 
being recorded from the Neolithic period (Clarke in Renfrew, 1985).  By the 17th 
century the population was such that otter pelts were regularly exported from the 
islands, hunting continued at least into the 19th century (Fenton, 1978).   
 
The actual status of the species over the year has, however, never been 
clearly identified.  Ross (1986) discussed the otter status, and concluded that at the 
time of writing, the mid 1980s, the species might be considered abundant. 
 
To date there have been three national otter surveys of Scotland in which 
Orkney was surveyed – 1977-79, 1991-94 and 2004-05, the results of the first two 
have been published (Green & Green, 1980; 1997).  Of 55 sites visited during each 
survey, 52 (95%) and 51 (93%) had evidence of otter presence, making Orkney, 
along with Shetland, the Outer Isles and parts of Northern Scotland among the most 
important areas in Europe for the species. 
 
Ross (1986) undertook a detailed survey of the islands between 1979 and 1985 
and examined the existing historical data on the presence of the species on Orkney.  
He concluded that despite the productive nature of the seas around Orkney, the 
population, while widespread, was less dense than those populations on the other 
island regions and parts of Mainland Scotland, citing the topography of the area as the 
reason for this.  A consideration later confirmed by Yoxon (1999; 2005) in his study 
on the affects of geology on the distribution of coastal otters across Scotland.  Kruuk 
(1995), agreed that the geology of the area might, in part, be responsible for the 
relatively low density of otters on the islands, but he also linked this to human land-
use resulting in a region dominated by rich, well-drained farmland.  Together, the 
geology and land-use, result in little surface water along the coasts of the islands.  
Thus the Orcadian coasts are relatively well drained, a misery for species, such as the 
otter, which require fresh water to wash the salt from its pelt (Kruuk & Balharry 
(1990).  Fresh water, in small pools or streams, appears to be the key habitat feature 
for otters along all sea coasts (Moorhpusse, 1988). 
  
3.  BIOLOGY OF THE OTTER  
 
Otters are largely solitary, nocturnal animals, and in many areas scarce.  Radio 
racking has shown that in northeast Scotland, the species ranges over extensive tracts 
of water, the average being some 40 kilometres for males and 21 kilometres for 
females, while in coastal areas on Shetland ranges averaged about five kilometres for 
females and eight or males (Kruuk, 1995).  
 
Although otters can breed at any time of the year, there is strong evidence to 
suggest that in this part of Scotland most cubs are born in winter (Conroy & Bacon, 
2005). Litter size varies between 1 and 4 (usually 2-3).  Cubs are born in natal holts 
which are usually some distance from major watercourses.  The normal situation on 
Shetland is for the majority of births to take place in summer (Kruuk, Conroy & 
Moorhouse 1987; Kruuk, 1995), but in years when food is considered to be minimal, 
seasonality breaks down and cubs are born at any time of the year (Conroy & Bacon, 
2005).   
 
The data from Orkney are few, but the existing information which was 
collected between 1965 and 1985, suggested that most cubs, 36%, (n = 31) were

recorded in winter (December – February), fewest in autumn 16% (Ross, 1986).  The 
situation on Orkney appears to be more similar to the situation on Mainland Scotland 
than that on Shetland.  Because there is no indication of the age of the cubs from 
Orkney, these records must be treated with a degree of caution. 
 
Newborn cubs remain in their natal dens until about eight weeks old.  For 
about the next ten months, the family sleep in at a number of sites, some of which 
may be above ground.  The species is most at risk from disturbance (by people and 
dogs) when the cubs are in their natal holts.  
 
 
4.   COUNTING OTTERS  
 
Because otters are largely solitary, nocturnal animals, and in many areas scarce, it is 
rarely possible to see individuals, let alone count them.  Most studies have been 
based on the use of field signs, in particular their faeces (spraints).  These are used 
because otters tend to defaecate on prominent places, such as stones, under bridges, 
stream junctions etc., often traditional sites that can be regularly used over a number 
of years.  Resting sites (couches) and holts (otter dens) are can usually, but not 
always be recognised. 
 
  
5.   DEFINITIONS OF OTTER ACTIVITY 
 
• A spraint is defined as a single bowel evacuation. 
• A spraint site is defined as a location where one or more otter spraints are 
found.  Sites separated by more than one metre or by a physical feature, such 
as a stream, are deemed to be different. 
• Otter footprints have a characteristic pattern, and, where more than one set of 
prints are found in the same place, can give an indication of the presence of 
otter families. 
• Couches are places where otters regularly rest above ground and are 
characterised by areas of flattened grass. They are usually associated with 
spraint sites. 
• A holt is defined as a tunnel system with signs of regular use by otters – 
evidence included spraints, tracks and the characteristic smooth wear at 
entrances and smell. On riverbanks, holts can be found amongst tree roots, 
under brush or bushes, piles of boulders, or small holes in the bank.  In 
addition, in some coastal areas, such as Shetland, otters occupy holes in peat 
banks, making use of rabbit holes.   
• A natal holt is a den in which otter cubs are born.  These are very difficult to 
find, tending to be situated in areas some distances from the coast or 
significant fresh water sources.  They can be in small holes or above ground, 
e.g. in a nest of reeds (Taylor & Kruuk, 1990; Kruuk, 1995; Liles, 2003).  In 
most instances, there is very little evidence that these holts are being used, as 
females with newly born cubs do not usually spraint at their entrances.  The 
best ways of locating  them is by direct observations of animals or by radio 
tracking.  In this study, any holes which may be considered as natal holts, but 
with no evidence of use, have been identified as potential natal holts.

Spraints are used as the principal means of identifying otter presence, because: 
 
(a) they are relatively easy to find; 
(b) they can be relatively long-lasting; 
(c) they are easily recognised; 
(d) otters usually spraint at the entrances of their holts (except probably  
natal holts) and on prominent features within their range, for example,  
stones, river confluences, vegetation swards, under bridges, trees  
stumps etc;  
(e) spraint sites can be used for a number of years; 
(f) for areas that are well used, smell can also help detect presence.   
 
 
 
6.  THE REASON FOR THE STUDY 
 
As part of the on-going Prime Contracting Framework Agreement, Highlands and 
Islands Enterprise has engaged Tulloch Prime Contracting Limited (Tulloch) to 
undertake the design and construction of the proposed European Marine Energy 
Centre (EMEC) tidal test facility to be located within the Fall of Warness, Eday, 
Orkney. 
 
The proposal is to build a tidal test facility off the coast of the Islands of Eday 
in the Orkneys.  He test facility will involve building a new substation/control 
building at Cauladale on the west coast of the island, with four cable ends to link to 
individual prototype generators located offshore in the Falls of Warness. 
 
 
7.   POTENTIAL EFFECTS OF THE DEVELOPMNET ON THE LOCAL OTTER 
POPULATION 
 
The development has the potential to affect otter locally at two different phases viz the 
construction (and de-commissioning) and operations. 
 
Construction phase.  This comprises building a single storey substation at a site near 
the beach in an area of grassland close to existing occupied properties. It is also 
proposed that part of the existing road be upgraded.  
Offshore there will be four test bed locations connected to the substation by an 
underwater cable which will make landfall on the beach immediately below the 
proposed building.  From the map supplied by Aurora Environment, the nearest 
seabed location will be more than 1.5 kilometres from the nearest point of Eday and 
over 0.5 kilometres from Muckle Green Holm.   
 
The construction work is expected to last about three months, commencing 
July 2005. 
 
The most likely effects the construction phase will have on the local otters are 
disturbance, possible increase in mortalities due to increased vehicular use of the road, 
and destruction and damage to otter habitat, i.e., holts, resting sites and feeding areas.

Operation phase. Four types of potential devices are described in the Aurora 
Environment scoping document.  These relate to the noise of the operation and the 
servicing of the facilities from the shore.  There is also a possibility that any open 
faced turbine might attract inquisitive otters and cause them to become entangled in 
the turbines.

The survey of the west coast of Eday, from the Bay of Greentoft (Grid Ref 
HY561289) to Sealskerry Bay (HY530318) was undertaken by Christopher Booth on 
3rd and 4th May 2005 (Booth, 2005). The author (Booth, 2005) divided the area into a

Bay of Greentoft to Warness.  There is an area of dunes at the Bay of Greentoft, 
otherwise most of the coastline is a low earthy bank with a small strip of rough 
grassland above and fields of improved grassland beyond.  The shore is stony leading 
to Warness Point. 
The Kirk to the point south of Dyke End. Here the cliffs are higher and rocky with

Rowen to south of Newbigging.  An area of low earthy cliffs with heather moor land 
behind. 
 
The coast was walked and evidence of otter presence noted.

10.   LIMITATIONS OF THE METHODOLOGY 
 
While these surveys can give an index of distribution, the methodology is fraught 
with problems (Conroy & French, 1985, 1987; Kruuk et al., 1986; Kruuk & Conroy, 
1987).  Such studies can detail changes in otter distribution, but their results cannot 
be used to determine subsequent numerical changes in populations.  Also it should be 
remembered that while the presence of faeces confirms the presence of otters, their 
absence does not necessarily mean that otters are not present. 
 
The principal constraints in the survey methodology can be summarised as follows:  
 
• 
Otters are difficult animals to observe because of their behaviour. For 
instance, otters are largely solitary animals and, in many areas nocturnal, and 
difficult to observe.   
• 
There is therefore no cost-effective or efficient means by which the species 
can be counted. 
• 
The survey technique used monitors the presence of otter signs rather than 
counting actual individuals. It is therefore an indirect means of assessing 
otter presence. Indirect methods include determining the distribution of otter 
faeces (spraints), holts (dens) and footprints. 
• 
The absence of signs, however, does not necessarily mean that no otters are 
present in the area. 
• 
Spraining activity (and breeding) can be seasonal and so may affect the 
surveyors’ ability to find signs. 
• 
Such studies can only detail changes in otter distribution, their results cannot 
be used to determine subsequent numerical changes in populations. 
• 
In some circumstances, the growth of vegetation, especially along river and 
loch banks, in summer can also affect the surveyors’ ability to find otter 
signs. 
 
It must be emphasised that such studies can detail changes in otter distribution, but 
their results cannot be used to determine subsequent numerical changes in 
populations 
 
Despite these limitations and concerns, spraint surveys are accepted as the 
most cost effective means of determining otter presence.  They have been accepted 
as the basis for otter monitoring programmes by the IUCN Otter Specialist Group 
(Reuther et al., 2000).

12.   DISCUSSION 
 
Construction phase. 
It is possible that the construction of the new substation will increase disturbance in 
the area and has the potential to affect any otters that might be living in the 
immediate vicinity.  However, the effect of this on the local otter population needs to 
be examined critically. In general, otters can tolerate high levels of disturbance.  For 
example, since the early 1980s, otters on Shetland have regularly bred under one of 
the jetties at Sullom Voe Oil Terminal.  Also during 2003-2004, a new ferry terminal 
was built on the Island of Yell.  No sooner had the work on the breakwater been 
completed that a female and three cubs as well as a male took up residence in it.   
 
The most likely scenario, should a ‘resident’ otter/s be disturbed by the 
construction, would be that it/they would move to another holt within its range.  
Even if the animal/s do not use the original holt/s again during building, this is no 
cause for worry, because all the evidence is that after construction it will be 
reoccupied.  The only concern would be the presence of a natal holt in the immediate 
vicinity, at the time of building.  Again, based on research elsewhere, this is unlikely 
to happen, such natal holts usually being found away from the coasts and main 
sources of freshwater, e.g. rivers and steams (Taylor & Kruuk, 1999; Kruuk, 1995).  
 
It is recommended, however, that about one month before the construction 
begins, the area immediately around the proposed site is surveyed for otter presence, 
in particular evidence of occupied holts – this should include a survey of the ditch 
which runs close to where the substation is to be located.  If a natal holt is found, 
SNH should immediately be made aware of the situation and measures taken to 
maintain its integrity until such time as it is no longer in use.  Disturbance of a natal 
holt could be deemed a contravention of the Habits Directive.  In light of this, 
discussions should take place with SNH and the Scottish Executive to determine 
whether or not an application should now be made for an otter handling licence. It 
should, however, be remembered that the survey in May did not find any occupied  
 
 
 
- 8 - 


holts in the immediate area of the development, or elsewhere along the length of 
coast examined, suggesting that, at that time, the animals were visiting that part of 
the coast, but not ‘living’ on it. 
 
Otters on Orkney are largely nocturnal, while any building operations will 
take pace during daylight hours.  To mitigate against the potential of disturbance, it is 
recommended that operations on the site be restricted to the hours of daylight, 
between 0800 and 1800hrs or between two hours after sunrise and one hour before 
sunset, which ever is the later. 
 
During construction, there will be a significant increase in road traffic, both 
in the number and size of vehicles.  Also, should accommodation for the construction 
workers be built on site, there is also the possibility of increased use of the road in 
the evening and night.  Thus there is the possibility of an additional risk of collisions 
between otters and vehicles.  Again this is not considered serious, but to reduce this, 
it is recommended that all contractors be made aware of the potential risks, as are all 
drivers visiting the site. Considerations should be made to have a low speed limit for 
all construction traffic on this road and that signs reminding drivers of the presence 
of otters be placed on the road, especially near any bridges.  If it is necessary to 
upgrade a bridge, then the new bridge design should incorporate measures to benefit 
otters. 
 
The laying of the cable etc offshore is unlikely to affect otters as long as 
there is no significant damage to, or loss of, kelp beds inshore, the area where otters 
are most likely to feed. 
 
It should also be remembered that the construction of the building will have 
two components, the actual building of the substation and its internal finishing.  The 
period of most concern would be the former, the latter is unlikely to cause any 
serious disturbance to the species.  Therefore, the working restrictions listed above 
relate to outside work on the substation. 
 
If during the construction phase, any concern relating to the otter population 
develops or is discovered, the constructor will immediately contact Aurora 
Environmental who will discuss the matter with Celtic Environment and decide on 
what action need be taken. 
 
Operations phase. 
 
The possible concerns about the open inlets of the turbines of the sea bed has been 
highlighted.  However, most otter activity, in particular feeding, is close to the shore 
(< 100 metres) and in relatively shallow water (<12 metres) (Kruuk, Conroy & 
Moorhouse, 1987; Kruuk, 1995).  The turbine sites are more than one kilometres 
from the shores of Eday (> 0.5 km from Muckle Green Holm) in water exceeding 12 
metres in depth. Thus it is unlikely that the siting of the turbines will have any 
serious effect on the otter population.  However, it should be remembered that the 
otters are inquisitive animals, and it is always possible that an individual might be 
attracted to the turbine and try and enter the chamber.  If the animal is unable to turn, 
or is ‘pulled in’ by the current, the effect on the machinery could be serious.  It is 
therefore recommended that a risk assessment be made as to whether or not there 
will be a need to fit otter guards, similar to those used on fyke nets (Vincent Wildlife 
Trust, 1988) to the mouth of the turbines.

Noise generated by the operation might be considered as being a cause for concern, 
but experience elsewhere shows that noise from offshore installations, e.g., fish 
farms, does not appear to affect otters.  Individuals are regularly recorded on fish 
cages.  Similarly, evidence shows that the species is not affected by boats ravelling to 
and from installations for maintenance etc 
 
Other concerns. 
 
Discussing the potential impacts the development, in particular disturbance, has on 
otters, Booth (2005) states that if the otters were disturbed and attempted to move 
into neighbouring territories, this could lead to conflict as there.  As there is a high 
density of otters in the area it could upset the balance of the otter population on the 
island. Again no evidence is presented to substantiate this statement m the riverbank 
to the gorse. 
 
From what is known from detailed studies on coastal otters elsewhere in 
Scotland, females range over relatively long sections of coast, on average about five 
kilometres, while males average about eight kilometres (Kruuk, Conroy & 
Moorhouse, 1987; Kruuk, 1995).  Also female ranges regularly overlap.  Within their 
range, otters will occupy a number of holts, remaining in them for one to several 
days at a time.  The exception to this being the use of natal holts.  These are dens 
where the cubs are born and remain with their mother for about 10 weeks.  These 
holts, however, tend to be away from the coast (up to 1 km on Shetland) and often 
have little evidence of use (Kruuk, Conroy & Moorhouse, 1987). 
 
Current research, therefore, would suggest that female otters (and their cubs) 
using the coasts on Orkney would occupy a range of which the area of the proposed 
development would be a relatively small part.  Should a family currently be present, 
i.e., living in a holt, in the development area and were to move from there, they 
would readily find another holt in within their range (Booth’s report identified a 
number of potential holts along the coast.)  The possibility of conflict and an upset in 
the balance of the otter population seems, therefore, to be highly unlikely.   
 
 
 
 
13.   CONCLUSIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
  
From our knowledge of the ecology and behaviour of otters living on Scotland’s 
coasts it seems highly unlikely that the proposed development will have any 
significant impact of the otter populations living on Eday.  While it is possible that 
there might be some effects during construction in the area immediately adjacent to 
the site of the proposed substation, these are seen as being short term and any impact 
they might have being minimal and not significant. 
 
To reduce the potential effects the following are recommended: 
 
• 
A further otter survey, restricted to the immediate vicinity of the proposed 
development, is undertaken about one month before construction is 
scheduled to start to ensure that no natal holts are present.

APPENDIX 1 
U.K., EUROPEAN LEGISLATION AND INTERNATIONAL CONVENTIONS UNDER WHICH THE 
EURASIAN OTTER IS PROTECTED. 
 
Below are detailed that various pieces of national and international legislation and 
regulations which offer very strict protection to both the Eurasian and its habitat. 
 
The Habitats Directive 
The primary piece of European conservation legislation relevant to the otter is the 
‘Habitats Directive’ (Council Directive EC/92/43,).  This requires EU Member States 
to adopt necessary measures to conserve the habitats of wild fauna and flora deemed 
to be of particular ‘community interest’, and to adapt specific conservation measures 
for a range of named species. 
 
The otter appears on both Annex II and Annex IV of the Directive. Annex II species 
require the designation of protected areas by Member States (Special Areas of 
Conservation – SACs) as set out in Articles 3, 4 and 6 of the Directive.  Annex IV 
species require strict protection measures by Member States in accordance with 
article 12 of the Directive.  These measures not only include the prohibition of 
capture, killing, deliberate disturbance at e.g. breeding sites and destruction of 
breeding and resting sites, but also include a requirement to monitor the incidental 
capture and killing of Annex IV listed species. 
 
 
The otter’s inclusion in Annexes II and IV of the Habitats Directive provides a strong 
basis for its protection throughout the EU.  However, the designation of protected 
areas and strict protection from intentional harm for a species does not guarantee of 
course either the integrity of the habitat throughout the species range or the effective 
recovery of significantly reduced populations.  In this respect two articles of the 
Directive are of note. Article 10 requires member states to encourage the 
management of landscape features of major importance, and gives as an example 
linear features essential for migration and dispersal such as rivers and their banks. 
Article 11 also requires Member States to undertake surveillance of the conservation 
status of habitats and species of community interest – in essence, all member states 
should be ensuring that they have comprehensive monitoring tools in place for the 
otter. 
International Conventions 
Two conventions are of particular note with reference to otter conservation in a UK 
and European context.  The UK is a signatory to the convention on the Conservation 
of European Wildlife and Natural Habitats (the Bern Convention) (1979).  This 
Convention made provisions for the protection of species, and the otter is listed on 
Appendix II, species for which Article 6 requires special protection.  In a European 
context the Habitats Directive effectively enshrines the provisions of the Bern 
Convention into European law, although for instance the UK had substantially 
implemented the convention in domestic legislation as early as 1981 (see below). 
The Convention on International Trade in Endangered Species and of wild 
flora and fauna (CITES) (1973) has been implemented throughout the EU through  
 
 
- 13 - 


Regulations 3626/82 and 338/97.  The otter is listed on Appendix One of CITES, for 
which trade is only allowed in ‘exceptional’ circumstances.  No EC Member State has 
entered a reservation with respect to the otter, and therefore all should be taking strict 
measures to ensure adequate policing of compliance with CITES and the detection of 
any illegal wildlife trade in Lutra lutra. 
 
UK legislation 
 
Wildlife and Countryside Act 
The Wildlife and Countryside (W&C) Act 1981 is the primary wildlife conservation 
legislation in the UK (although Northern Ireland has its own legislation, see below).  
The Act superseded and considerably improved upon the provisions of the 
Conservation of Wild Creatures and Wild Plants Act 1975, an amendment of which 
first gave the otter statutory protection in England and Wales in 1978.  The W&C 
Act not only extended this protection to Scotland but also strengthened the protection 
for the species.  The otter is listed on Schedule 5 of the W&C Act, which in 
accordance with the provisions of Part 1 Section 9 makes it an offence to kill, injure 
or attempt to kill or injure an otter, to damage or obstruct access to an otter’s place of 
shelter or intentionally disturb it while in occupation, or to be in possession of a live 
or dead otter or part thereof unless it can be demonstrated that it has been legally 
obtained.  Schedule 6 restricts methods by which otters can be killed or taken, and 
the licensing for the taking or removal of otters (and other protected species) is 
allowed for in Section 16 (3) where it can be shown that they are causing serious 
damage.  With respects to this last, it should be noted that licenses are very rarely 
issued, and the Habitats Directive effectively provides tighter provisions on the 
issuing of licenses with respect to the need to ensure that such licenses do not 
represent a threat to the integrity of the population. 
 
 
The Wildlife (Northern Ireland) Order 1985 implements the provisions of the W&C 
Act 1981 in Northern Ireland.  The otter is listed again on Schedule 5, and Part II 
Article 10 is very similar to Part I Section 9 of the W&C Act in the protection it 
offers to listed species. 
 
 Conservation Regulations 
The Conservation Regulations 1994 transpose the obligations of the EC Habitats 
Directive into domestic UK legislation.  The otter was already strictly protected in 
the UK by the Wildlife and Countryside Act, and Section 39 (1) of the Conservation 
Regulations serves to reinforce Schedule 5 of the W&C Act.  However, the 
Conservation Regulations also require the designation of Special Areas of 
Conservation for which the otter is either the primary or a significant feature.  The 
system of site protection set up by the W&C Act – Sites of Special Scientific Interest 
or SSSIs - rarely were of sufficient scale to be designated with otters as a prime 
species of interest, nor has the SSSI system been particularly suited or intended for 
wide ranging, low-density terrestrial species such as the otter, until perhaps the more 
recent designation of a significant number of river and coastal SSSIs from the mid-
1990s.   
 
The system of candidate SACs now proposed for the UK include a number of 
river systems and costal areas for which the otter is a primary reason for designation,  
 
- 1 4 - 


as well as a number of other freshwater and coastal sites for which the otter is a 
secondary ‘qualifying feature’. All SACs must first be designated as SSSIs under the 
W&C Act before they can be formally proposed as SACs. 
 
 Countryside and Rights of Way Act 
The Countryside and Rights of Way (CROW) Act 2000 covers a number of activities 
affecting the environment and countryside.  With relevance to the otter, it strengthens 
the powers of statutory nature conservation agencies in relation to SSSIs, 
overcoming some of the weaknesses in the W&C Act 1981, parts of which are 
replaced by updated provisions in the CROW Act. 
 
Another significant aspect of the CROW Act was the quasi-statutory status it 
conferred on priority habitats and species listed in the UK Biodiversity Action Plan, 
of which the otter is one.  The Act requires the Secretary of State for the 
Environment and the National Assembly of Wales, and therefore all relevant 
statutory bodies coming under their jurisdiction, to promote action to further the 
conservation of these habitats and species.  This requires that positive action be 
undertaken to conserve the otter, a priority BAP species, by all those statutory bodies 
with a remit for nature conservation.

Otter population and distribution 
 
 
Method 
 
The coastline was walked on 3rd and 4th May from the Bay of Greentoft to the 
west side of Sealskerry Bay, opposite Seal Skerry.   
 
A thorough search was made along the banks and cliff edges and, where 
accessible, the beach area above high water mark, for obvious signs of otters 
in the form of spraints (otter faeces), footprints and any other indications of 
otter activity. 
 
The waters off this coastline are shallow and provide an excellent feeding 
area for otters. 
 
 
 
 
Bay of Greentoft to Warness 
 
There are dunes at the Bay of Greentoft, but most of the coastline consists of 
low earthy banks with a small strip of rough grassland above and fields of 
improved grassland behind.  The shore is stony leading to Warness Point. 
 
 
There was strong evidence of otter activity all along this coastline.  In the first 
section rolling beds were noted at two sites and one probable lying-up site, 
together with sprainting sites and burrows.  Below the water pumping station 
there were several sprainting sites, obvious tracks through the vegetation and 
footprints in the mud.  Also a number of burrows and a possible holt site. 
 
At the Burnt Mound extensive areas of urine scorching and spraints, some 
footprints and obvious tracks in the vegetation were seen, continuing towards 
Warness. 
 
 
 
- 16 - 
 


The Kirk to the point south of Dyke End 
 
The cliffs are higher here and rocky with rough grassland above.  In several 
places grassy slopes give easy access to the rocky beach. 
 
 
There were sprainting sites, with scorching, all along the cliff top and a 
number of burrows which were possible holt sites. 
 
The cliff edge from south of Dyke End to Whitemaw Bay was not searched 
due to the presence of a pair of nesting Peregrine falcons.  Passage through 
this area was made as quickly as possible to minimise disturbance.  
 
 
 Whitemaw Bay to Rowen 
 
The first part consists of steep earthy cliffs with no access to the beach.  The 
cliff becomes lower further north with some grassy slopes down to the shore.  
The adjoining land is peaty with heather moorland and rough grassland. 
 
 
There were no signs of otter activity along the higher cliffs but several 
sprainting sites were found south of Rowen, where the cliffs are lower.  There 
were also a number of holes in the peaty ground suitable for holt sites. 
 
 
Rowen to south of Newbigging 
 
This area consists of low earthy cliffs with heather moorland behind. 
 
 
At Kibbens Geo there were sprainting sites, one of which was by a small fresh 
water pool which would be suitable for otters to use for bathing.  (Fresh water 
pools are important in order for the animals to wash salt water from their fur).  
Also there were several burrows in the peat. 
 
Below Zoar there were extensive signs of otter activity, with sprainting sites, 
fresh water pools with a sprainting site alongside, footprints in the sand on the 
beach, and several sprainting sites close by. 
 
 
Zoar to Sandybank 
 
This stretch of coast consists of low earth cliffs and sand dunes with farmland 
behind.

There were spraints in the ditch south of the proposed building site.  Also, 
further up the ditch where there was a small fresh water pool. 
 
Along the burn valley, just before Sandybank, there were considerable signs 
of otter activity.  Numerous spraints, some very fresh, were found, also many 
obvious tracks through the vegetation and burrows in the sandy banks 
suitable for holt sites.  These signs suggest the presence of a family group.  
There were also spraints further up the burn beyond the road. 
 
 
Sandybank to Sealskerry Bay 
 
Here there are low earth cliffs, then sandy links, becoming rockier towards the 
west point of Sealskerry Bay.  There is an area of tussocks above the bank 
just before the sand links and behind the shore is improved grassland.  There 
is a pool of fresh water behind the links. 
 
 
Spraints were found below Swenstay, where there has been a holt in the past.  
There has also been a holt site in the tussock area.  A sprainting site was 
found by the fresh water pool and also on the rocks at the west point.

Other protected wildlife species. 
 
 
Peregrine falcon

Black guillemot

Common seal 
 
Approximately 40 Common seals were hauled out at The Graand and there

Cetaceans 
 
There has been no systematic cetacean watching in this area and records

Minke whale 
 
There is a record of a sighting in July.  This species is fairly common around

Harbour porpoise 
 
This is the commonest species in the area with sightings in February and then

Killer whale

Pilot whale

Unidentified dolphins

Turtles 
 
There have been several sightings of Leathery turtles in Orkney waters, also  
strandings, in the last 20 years, but none as yet in this particular area.

Potential impacts on otters, cetaceans and turtles during the 
construction phase 
 
 
Otters 
 
The otter is a UK Priority List species of conservation concern.   
 
The area is relatively quiet and the otters are accustomed to the farming 
activities that take place around the Sandybank area.  However, the 
establishment of a construction site, including pile driving and laying of cables 
on the beach, would cause disturbance to the otters both at Sandybank and 
possibly also at Zoar.   
 
If the otters were disturbed and attempted to move into neighbouring 
territories, this could lead to conflict.  As there is a high density of otters in this 
area it could upset the balance of the otter population on the island.   
 
Traffic using the road would be a hazard, particularly where it crosses the 
ditch near Sandybank and on the south side of Caldale. 
 
Litter left around the construction site could prove dangerous to otters. 
 
Because of the obvious otter presence in the area, probably including a 
family group, it would be preferable that an alternative location is found 
for the onshore facility. 
 
 
Cetaceans 
 
The species most likely to be affected by the cable laying would be the 
Harbour porpoise.  The use of boats with powerful outboard motors is 
particularly disturbing and the porpoises would move away from the noise, 
abandoning the feeding area.  There is always the risk of collision with boats 
moving at speed.   
 
Loose ropes, especially in coils, can become entangled around an animal’s 
body.  Larger species such as Minke whales are particularly at risk. 
 
All cetaceans are a UK Priority List species of conservation concern and 
it is an offence to knowingly disturb them. 
 
 
Turtles 
 
Loose ropes can lead to entanglement.  In Orkney three Leathery turtles 
have been found entangled in creel ropes in the last 10 years.  There is a risk 
of collision if high-speed boats are used.

Potential impacts from long-term presence of the tidal facility 
 
 
Otters 
 
Once the construction phase has passed, the otters would probably adjust to 
a low level of disturbance at the site.   
 
Road traffic is always a potential danger.   
 
Danger of entrapments in the turbines, litter such as netting, cans, plastic etc., 
can all form a hazard. 
 
There could be disturbance to feeding otters if work with cables is carried out 
close inshore. 
 
 
Cetaceans 
 
Boat movements in the area could be disruptive to feeding cetaceans and 
there is always the risk of collision.   
 
Noise from the turbines has the potential for disturbing their feeding and 
causes them to move away from the area.  The noise level of the turbines 
would need to be investigated before they are installed to see that it is not 
within the range to cause disturbance to cetaceans.   
 
Trailing ropes could lead to entanglement.   
 
There is also a danger that animals may get entrapped in the turbines. 
 
 
 
 
Additional 
 
Noise from the turbines could also affect the local fish population, reducing 
not only the food supply for porpoises, otters and seals but also the local 
seabird populations.

Recommended mitigation measures for any potential impacts 
 
 
Construction site and onshore facilities 
 
• Work not to be undertaken during the early morning or late evening, 
the time when otters are most active.   
 
• Keep noise level and outdoor lighting to a minimum.   
 
• Avoid, if possible, heavy traffic to the site.  There will be a need for a 
speed limit, perhaps put in humps and notices to slow down.   
 
• The bridge over the burn at Sandybank is important.  If alteration to this 
bridge is necessary, an underpass may need to be installed to allow 
otters easy access to the upper reaches of the burn without having to 
cross the road.  This work, however, could cause further disturbance.   
 
• Remove any litter 
 
 
Turbines and cables 
 
• Keep turbines and cables well offshore.  (Kruuk 1995 found that, in his 
study in Shetland, 98% of otter feeding dives were within 80 metres of 
the shore). 
 
• Preferably do not use boats with high powered outboard motors when 
visiting the turbine moorings. 
 
• Turbines should be tested for noise emission before installing to ensure 
that they would not disturb feeding cetaceans. 
 
• Grills should be attached to turbines to prevent otters, cetaceans, 
seals, diving birds and fish from becoming entrapped. 
 
• There should be no loose or trailing ropes to cause entanglements. 
 
• Keep offshore work well away from Dyke End area. 
 
 
Navigational buoys 
 
• These should only be installed and visited at times when there will be 
least disturbance to wildlife.
