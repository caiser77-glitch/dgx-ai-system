IMPORTANCE OF RUFFE (GYMNOCEPHALUS CERNUUS) IN THE DIET OF OTTERS 
(LUTRA LUTRA) IN LOCH LOMOND, SCOTLAND. 
 
Dominic J. MCCAFFERTY 
 
Department of Adult & Continuing Education, University of Glasgow 
St Andrew’s Building, 11 Eldon Street, Glasgow G3 6NH, Scotland, U.K. 
email:  d.mccafferty@educ.gla.ac.uk  
 
 
Abstract.  The recent introduction of the ruffe to Loch Lomond, Scotland has dramatically changed the diet of a 
number of fish eating predators. Ruffe have not previously been considered to be one of the main prey of otters 
in Britain. In this study, a total of 214 spraints were analysed from four sites on Loch Lomond, collected 
between January and June 2002. Overall, 97% of spraints contained at least one species of fish, and 
amphibians, invertebrates, birds and mammals were found in 55, 24, 4 and 1% of spraints, respectively. The 
most frequent prey item at all sites was the ruffe which was found in 78% of spraints. Eels, Cyprinids and Salmo 
spp. were also relatively common and were recovered in 54, 41 and 25% of spraints, respectively. Pike (18%), 
perch (16%), stickleback (15%), powan (14%) and lampreys (3%) were less common. The frequency of 
occurrence of ruffe in the diet did not vary between sites but there was some evidence to suggest that ruffe were 
taken more frequently in early summer. This study indicates that the ruffe is now the most important prey of 
otters in Loch Lomond and provides further evidence of the effects of ruffe introduction on this ecosystem. 
 
Keywords:  Otter, Lutra lutra, diet, ruffe, Gymnocephalus cernuus, Loch Lomond 
 
 
1   INTRODUCTION 
 
Loch Lomond is the largest area (71 km2) of freshwater on mainland Britain and has one of 
the most diverse fish communities of any area of freshwater in Scotland.  A total of 20 
species of fish have been recorded, including five non-indigenous species which have become 
established in recent years (ADAMS, 1994; ADAMS AND MAITLAND, 1998).  In particular, the 
ruffe (Gymnocephalus cernuus) was introduced by anglers as a live bait for northern pike 
(Esox lucius) and has now become the most abundant fish in the loch since it was first 
recorded in 1982 (ADAMS AND MAITLAND, 1998).  The ruffe is found in lakes, slow moving 
rivers and canals throughout northern Europe and across central and northern Asia, also 
occurring in low salinity areas of the Baltic Sea.  This species is the smallest member of the 
perch (Percidae) family in Britain and has been indigenous to south east England since the 
last Ice Age.  It has only spread into northern England, Wales and Scotland within the past 
two decades.  In Scotland it has established populations in Loch Lomond and Loch Ken 
catchments (MAITLAND AND CAMPBELL, 1992). 
The introduction of ruffe has had wide ranging effects on food web dynamics of Loch 
Lomond.  It is known to predate the eggs of the powan (Coregonus lavaretus), where Loch 
Lomond has the larger of only two populations in Scotland (ADAMS AND TIPPETT, 1991; LYLE 
AND MAITLAND, 1994).  Ruffe are now the main prey of the great cormorant (Phalacrocorax 
carbo), grey heron (Ardea cinerea) and northern pike in the loch (ADAMS, 1994) but it is not 
known to what extent the otter (Lutra lutra) feed on ruffe throughout this ecosystem.  
Previously, the ruffe has not been considered to be an important prey of otters (see review by 
MASON AND MACDONALD, 1986).  Where both ruffe and perch (Perca fluviatilis) do occur 
together otters were reported to prey mainly on the latter (WEBB, 1975; OZOLINŠ AND 
RANTINŠ, 1992; BIRZAKS, OZOLINŠ AND ORNICĀNS, 1998). 
The aim of this study was to determine the diet of otters in Loch Lomond, in 
particular, to establish how frequently ruffe were predated.  It was hoped that this would 


contribute towards our understanding of trophic changes in the loch as a result of the 
introduction of this species.

2   MATERIALS AND METHODS 
 
2.1 Fieldwork 
The diet of otters was determined by identifying indigestible remains of prey in spraints 
(faeces). Spraints were collected at monthly intervals from January to June 2002 from four 
sites along the eastern shore of the loch at Endrick National Nature Reserve (NS427877), 
Ross Point (NS375956), south of Inversnaid (NN339075) and Ardleish (NN327153) (Figure 
1).  Ten fresh spraints, collected each month by searching approximately one kilometre of 
shoreline, were transferred to individually marked polythene bags and stored in a freezer at -
20oC for subsequent analysis.  The same length of shoreline was checked each month and any 
remaining spraints were removed to prevent collection in future months. 
 
2.2 Laboratory work 
Frozen samples were transferred to glass jars and covered with a saturated solution of 
biological detergent (Biotex ®, Blumøller Ltd).  Samples were gently shaken and then left in 
an oven at 40oC for 3-4 hours to separate.  The sample was then washed with water through a 
plastic hand sieve with one millimetre square mesh, through a 250µm metal sieve and finally 
onto filter paper (No. 1, Whatman International Ltd).  The prey remains were left to dry at 
room temperature overnight and then stored in marked universal plastic tubes. 
Remains were identified using a binocular microscope (x10) and compared with a 
reference collection of fish bones and using an identification key (CONROY ET AL., 1993).  
Species of Cyprinid were identified by their pharyngeal bones (MAITLAND AND CAMPBELL, 
1992) and invertebrates were identified using CROFT (1986).  Reference fish bones were 
prepared by initially boiling the fish to remove large amounts of flesh and then left to digest 
in a detergent solution at 40oC for 1-3 days, depending on size. 
The Chi-square test was used to compare the proportion of spraints containing prey 
items between different months and from different sampling locations. 
 
 
3   RESULTS 
 
3.1 Diet composition 
A total of 214 spraints were collected from the four study sites between January and June 
2002.  Fish identified included the ruffe, eel (Anguilla anguilla), roach (Rutilus rutilus), dace 
(Leuciscus leuciscus), minnow (Phoxinus phoxinus), trout/salmon (Salmo spp. not 
distinguished), pike, perch, three-spined stickleback (Gastoerosteus aculeatus), powan and 
lampreys (species not distinguished).  Two spraints contained fish vertebrae that could not be 
identified.  Amphibians included the common frog (Rana temporaria) and common toad 
(Bufo bufo).  Invertebrates (less than 2 cm in length) consisted of insects, molluscs, crustacea 
(Cladocera, Isopoda and Amphipoda), arachnids, diplopods and oligochaetes.  Bird bones, 
feathers and mammal bones and hairs were recovered from a number of samples but were not 
identified to species level. 
Overall 97% of spraints contained at least one species of fish, 24% contained 
amphibians, invertebrates were found in 55% and mammals and birds were found in 5% of 
spraints.  The most frequent prey item from all sites was the ruffe which was found in 78% of 
spraints (Table 1).

3.2 Seasonal differences in diet 
There was no difference in the proportion of spraints containing fish at different times of the 
year (pooled 2 month data χ2 = 3.3; d.f = 2: P = 0.19) or birds and mammals and time of year 
(χ2 = 0.39: P = 0.82). However, amphibians were more common in the diet during winter and 
early spring (χ2 = 22.6: P < 0.0001) and invertebrates increased in occurrence during May and 
June (χ2 = 9.1: P = 0.01). 
Salmo spp. occurred more frequently in the diet during January to February (37%) 
than in March to April (14%) or May to June (24%) (χ2 = 9.9; d.f =2: P = 0.007).  Lampreys 
did not occur in January to February and were only found in 6.5% and 1.5% in March to April 
and May to June, respectively.  There was no difference in the frequency of ruffe at different 
times of the year (χ2 = 5.6: P = 0.06) and for all remaining species (P > 0.06 in all cases). 
 
3.3 Geographical differences in diet 
There was no difference in the frequency of occurrence of fish, amphibians and invertebrates 
in spraints between the four sampling locations (d.f = 3: P > 0.07 in all cases).  However, 
birds and mammals were most common in spraints from the Endrick site (11.9%) and were 
not found in any spraints at Ardleish (χ2 = 9.47: P = 0.024). 
There were differences in the frequency of occurrence of perch, cyprinids, pike, eel, 
powan and stickleback between sites (P < 0.05 in all cases).  There was however no 
difference in the occurrence of ruffe (χ2 = 6.7: P = 0.08), Salmo spp. and lampreys between 
sites (P > 0.58 in both cases). 
 
 
4   DISCUSSION 
 
4.1 Composition of diet 
This study showed that the diet of otters in Loch Lomond was similar to other freshwater 
areas of Britain, with fish dominating and amphibians being particularly common during late 
winter and spring (MASON AND MACDONALD, 1986; WEBER, 1990).  Birds and mammals 
represented a small proportion of the diet and although these were not identified to species, 
these most likely consisted of water-birds and a variety of riparian mammals.  The large 
number of invertebrates recorded in spraints probably reflected the diet of fish, however it is 
also likely that species such as earthworms were directly taken as prey (MASON AND 
MACDONALD, 1986). 
Ruffe was the most frequently occurring prey item in otter spraints from Loch 
Lomond.  This contrasted with previous diet studies which indicate that ruffe are rarely found 
in otter spraints in Britain (WEBB, 1975).  In rivers and lakes in Latvia they occur in 0.1-6.5% 
of spraints (OZOLINŠ AND RANTINŠ, 1992; BIRZAKS, OZOLINŠ AND ORNICĀNS, 1998).  
Although the vertebrae are similar in structure to perch and both species have ctenoid scales, 
ruffe have an operculum which ends in a rear pointing spine while the preoperculum has 10-
12 rear pointing spines.  These bones are characteristically different from perch (MAITLAND 
AND CAMPBELL, 1992).  Ruffe also have relatively large otoliths that were frequently seen in 
spraints in this study and therefore it is unlikely that ruffe would be overlooked.  
The importance of ruffe in the diet suggest that this species is frequently encountered 
by otters in Loch Lomond.  They  would appear to be a medium sized prey for otters, having 
been recorded up to 125mm in length  (ADAMS AND  MAITLAND, 1998) and do not grow much 
over 150mm in Britain (MAITLAND AND CAMPBELL, 1992).  Ruffe is now the most abundant 
and widespread species of fish in Loch Lomond.  They remain active in winter and come into 
shallow water to breed in early summer (ADAMS AND MAITLAND, 1998).  Although the pooled 


analysis did not show any significant differences in the occurrence of ruffe in spraints at 
different times of the year, the greatest percentage (92.3%) did occur in May (Table 1).  This 
suggests that otters may feed extensively on this species when they are most vulnerable to 
predation. 
The dominance of ruffe in the diet of otters is in broad agreement with changes that 
have taken place in the diet of several other fish eating predators in the loch.  Grey herons 
showed a shift in the occurrence of roach (63%) to ruffe (61%) in their diet and pike altered 
their diet from powan (57% of prey occurrence) to ruffe (44%) in response to ruffe 
introduction.  Monitoring of the diet of the great cormorant between 1990 and 1991 also 
showed that 85% of their prey by number was ruffe (ADAMS, 1991; 1994). 
 
4.2 Geographical differences in diet 
Although there was no significant difference in the frequency of occurrence of ruffe in 
spraints between all four sampling sites, it  appeared to be more common in the diet at the 
southern end (Endrick 84.7%) and least frequent in spraints at the northern end of the loch 
(Ardleish 65.5%) (Table 2).

In 1989-89 gill net captures at five sites around the loch showed that ruffe were common in 
the littoral zone (ADAMS, 1994) and they were equally abundant in north and southern ends.  
In this study there were differences in the frequency of occurrence of perch, cyprinids, pike, 
eel, powan and stickleback between sites.  These geographical differences may reflect the 
relative abundance of species within the loch and differences in limnology throughout the 
loch (SMITH, LYLE AND ROSIE, 1981). 
 
 
5   CONCLUSIONS 
 
The diet of otters in Loch Lomond between January and June 2002 consisted predominantly 
of fish and amphibians.  The most frequently occurring prey was the ruffe.  Although this 
species is not an important prey of otters elsewhere in Britain, the ruffe is the main prey 
because of its high abundance in Loch Lomond.
