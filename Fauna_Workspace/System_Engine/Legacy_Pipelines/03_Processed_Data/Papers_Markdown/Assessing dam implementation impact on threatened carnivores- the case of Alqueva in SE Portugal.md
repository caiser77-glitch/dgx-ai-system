Assessing dam implementation impact on threatened
carnivores: the case of Alqueva in SE Portugal

Joaquim P. Ferreira & Hugo M. Matos &

Abstract Large dam construction in water deficient
areas is a management decision often controversial.
Besides providing water storage, economical
benefits, and a source of renewable energy, the
construction and flooding caused by large dams
cause disruptions in natural systems. We monitored
the pre- and post-Alqueva dam impacts on the
threatened carnivore species (polecat, otter, wildcat
and Iberian lynx) populations in SE Portugal, and
assessed which factors mostly contribute to post-dam
distribution. Major short term impacts of large dams
are: (1) increase in accessibility and human presence;
(2) movement of heavy machinery and dam-
workers; (3) deforestation with habitat loss and
fragmentation; (4) change from lotic to lentic
system; (5) lower prey availability and harsher
capture; and (6) changes in land use adjacent to the
reservoir. Thus, the response to those impacts can be
predicted as a decline of polecat, wildcat and lynx
distribution ranges, and a recovery of the otter from
the severe short term impacts. Our results corrobo-
rate this hypothesis for all the species, especially
during deforestation/early flooding. Otter’s distribu-
tion range increased in the phase of greater impact,
with a subsequent decrease with flooding. Our results
suggest carnivores used “escape” areas with favour-
able habitat and prey conditions, however, the areas
with higher probability of species presence decreased
by two fold showing a drastic range reduction. To
ensure populations’ survival of these charismatic
threatened carnivore populations of Mediterranean
landscapes of south-east Portugal, we propose con-
tinuing the monitoring program and the development
of a conservation program for the subsisting areas of
optimal and suboptimal habitats.

Introduction
Large dam construction in water deficient areas is a
management decision often controversial (e.g., Hughes
1994; Bizer 2000). A large dam can be defined as
having a wall with height ≥15 m or wall height
between 5–15 m and reservoir volume greater than 3
million m3 (World Commission on Dams 2000). Dams
store water and provide economical benefits (employ-
ment and water supplies) to the region where they are
implemented (Bednarek 2001). They also provide a
renewable energy source – hydropower. In spite of
these benefits, the construction and flooding phases are
associated with two main factors of disturbance:
upstream formation of a permanent artificial lake and
downstream decrease of water run-off and stabilization
of patterns of high and low flows (Adams and Hughes
1986; McCartney et al. 1999; Bizer 2000).
Alqueva dam was projected for the gorge of
Guadiana River. Its construction (1998–2000), resulted
in the creation of the Europe’s largest artificial lake with
a flooding surface of 25,000 ha (Fig. 1). Moreover,

Alqueva dam project includes a massive irrigation
system across all southern Portugal affecting
120,600 ha, with irreversible changes in landscape
and agricultural conversion. Furthermore, Alqueva
dam project has associated high environmental impacts
once it is being implemented in a high biodiversity
region, which has been identified as one of the world’s
25 hotspots (Myers et al. 2000).
Due to the growing concerns over the impact of large
infrastructures on biodiversity, the European Union
directories have implemented Environmental Impact
Assessment (EIA) frameworks which include monitor-
ing programs. Alqueva dam EIA required a monitoring
protocol for threatened species (Portuguese Red Book of
Vertebrates; SNPRCN 1990) whose distribution ranges
would overlap with the impact area of the Alqueva dam.
Four species of threatened carnivores in Portugal met
these criteria: the European polecat (Mustela putorius),
the European otter (Lutra lutra), the European wildcat
(Felis silvestris) and the Iberian lynx (Lynx pardinus;
Mathias et al. 1998). The range of these species has
decreased in most European areas over the past decades
(e.g., Santos-Reis 1983; Fernandes 1996; Delibes et al.
2000; Baghli and Verhagen 2003; Pires and Fernandes
2003; Randi 2003; Sarmento et al. 2004), with the
exception of the polecat in the UK (Birks 1998; Birks
and Kitchener 1999) and the otter in Portugal (Trindade
et al. 1998). In Portugal, these species are of conserva-
tion concern, and when the dam was being projected,
the lynx was listed as endangered, the polecat and the
otter as unknown, and the wildcat as undetermined
(SNPRCN 1990). Their status was currently revised
accordingly to the new IUCN categories (IUCN 2001),
being the lynx categorized as critically endangered, the
wildcat as vulnerable, the polecat as data deficient, and
the otter downgraded as least concern species (Cabral
et al. 2005).
Building and implementing such a large infrastruc-
ture can be predicted to impact local populations of
these species by some of the following aspects: (1)
increase in accessibility and human presence in areas
with a priori low disturbance; (2) movement of heavy
machinery and dam-workers; (3) deforestation with
habitat loss and fragmentation; (4) change from lotic to
lentic system; (5) lower prey availability and harsher
capture; and (6) changes in land use adjacent to the
reservoir (e.g., Adams and Hughes 1986; McCartney
et al. 1999; Bednarek 2001). Sensitive species, as
species of conservation concern, are expected to use
“escape” areas to respond to these impacts. “Escape”
areas are those where species would find refuge to
avoid direct conflict with human interventions due to
the implementation of the large dam. To our knowl-
edge, no previous study has monitored the effects of
dam pre- and post-implementation on threatened
carnivore populations, however, carnivore response to
dam impacts can be predicted as a generalized decline
of polecat, wildcat and lynx distribution ranges, and a
recovery of the otter from the severe short-term
impacts. Thus, we aimed at understanding the pre-
and post-impacts through monitoring the already
threatened populations of these charismatic species of
the Mediterranean landscapes of south-eastern Portu-
gal, through answering the following questions: (1)
what is the pre-dam reference population status of the
focal threatened carnivores? (2) how does each of the
species respond to dam construction impacts and
which phase is most critical? (3) how does deforesta-
tion/early flooding affects focal species population
distribution? and (4) which factors are driving post-
disturbance species distribution – are there “escape”
areas?
Study area
Alqueva dam was constructed in south-eastern Portu-
gal, in Alentejo region (Fig. 1). This region is
characterized by a highly heterogeneous and complex
landscape structure, dominated by agricultural fields
(cereal grounds, orchards and olive groves), with
holm oak (Quercus ilex) and cork oak (Q. suber)
woodlands interspersed within the agricultural
landscape. Few patches of forest plantations (euca-
lyptus and pines) are also found. The climate is
characterized by mild winters and extremely hot
summers (Carmel and Flather 2004), with average
temperatures between <18 and >22°C, respectively
(Chicharo et al. 2001). Annual precipitation levels
vary between 400–600 mm (Chicharo et al. 2001).
Rivers, streams and creeks have intermittent character.
Human settlements are concentrated in cities and
farmhouses scattered throughout the landscape.
Two sub-areas are considered for this study: the
flooding area of the artificial lake created by the construc-
tion of Alqueva dam wall (designated as Alqueva-lake),
and the surrounding watershed of this large dam
(designated as surrounding-impacted area; Fig. 1).

Materials and methods
Alqueva dam was intended to monitor impacts on
population trends before and after deforestation and
flooding. Unexpected changes in task scheduling by the
development company delayed both wall construction
and deforestation, which allied with low precipitation
winters that delayed flooding, resulted in a combined
deforestation/flooding phase. Because of these delays,
it was not possible to monitor the post-flooding phase
within the 5 years timeframe of the monitoring
program. For correctness we decided to name the two
studied phases as: pre-deforestation/flooding (January
1999 to December 2000), and deforestation/early-
flooding (January 2001 to December 2003). However,
we believe the results from this deforestation/early-
flooding phase are a good surrogate for post-flooding
population distributions, since forest clear-cutting and
major land-conversion has already occurred, being only
expected changes in Alqueva-lake water level.

Field work
The design of monitoring program was based on
available information on these species from literature
review from other European locations since there was
little available information for the study area. How-
ever, since it could be anticipated that in Mediterra-
nean environments species would have different
ecological requirements, we used a hierarchical
sampling design, to include a “scouting” phase that
would inform the methodology for the remaining
monitoring program.
During the first year of the monitoring program a
reference situation was established through a composite
approach of oral interviews and field surveys. The study
area was subdivided into a grid of 25 km2 cells of the
Hayford–Gauss coordinate system (Fig. 2a). Linear
transects were walked by two observers. In each cell,
transects lengths were selected in matching proportions
to land cover availability within the cell, which added
up to 10 km. Transect length was adjusted to 2 km
whenever grid cells were smaller than 25 km2 due to
unevenness of the study area boundaries. Carnivore
signs of presence (footprints, scats, latrines, burrows,
scent marks) were identified and the geographical
location was recorded for a posteriori mapping. This
approach was selected as a generalized method to
assess simultaneously all the focal species presence/
absence in the study area, gain awareness of species
specific requirements, and mainly to inform the
methodology for the subsequent years of monitoring.
The results of the first year of survey indicated that two
pairs of species could be created due to their greater
similarity in habitat requirements – polecat and otter (see
section polecat and otter), and size of home-ranges –
wildcat and Iberian lynx (see section wildcat and
Iberian lynx). We then designed a field sampling
methodology for the second and third years of monitor-
ing (2000 and 2001) for each of the pairs of species,
bearing in mind the extensiveness of the study area and
aiming at answering the posed questions and achieving
the goals of the monitoring program.
Polecat and otter
Due to the reported association of these two species to
riverine and riparian habitats (e.g., Mason and
Macdonald 1986; Lodé 1993, 1994; Kruuk 1995),
and the results of the first year of survey, a specific
sampling design was directed to these areas. Riverine
habitats were determined in the area of Alqueva-lake
and surrounding-impacted area, and superimposed a
1 km2 grid. Eighty-four cells were randomly selected
(20% of the cells with freshwater) due to the high
availability of these habitats in the study area; 48
located within the future Alqueva-lake area, whereas
36 in the surrounding-impacted area (Fig. 2b). These
cells were surveyed for species presence in linear
pedestrian transects (Mason and Macdonald 1986)
along water components (rivers, streams, creeks and
small reservoirs) found within the cell, of at least
600 m. Each sample unit was surveyed every three
months to account for seasonal variation.
Wildcat and Iberian lynx
These two species have been described as dependent
upon Mediterranean scrubland (e.g. Blanco 1998;
Palomares 2001a), which occurs in a highly frag-
mented pattern in the study area. We identified
potential habitat for these species in the Alqueva-lake
and surrounding-impacted area land cover maps. A
grid of 4 km2 cells was superimposed to the area, and
all cells with land cover >40% of optimal habitat were
selected (n=69). Thirty-eight of the cells were located
in the Alqueva-lake area, whereas 31 were located in
the surrounding-impacted area (Fig. 2c). Species signs
of presence (latrines and scats) were surveyed in the
optimal habitats by random linear transects, with
variable length (2 to 3 km) to incorporate the
extension of favourable habitats within a cell. The
same monitoring protocol was kept for the two
species in 2002 and 2003. Surveys were limited to
the accessible areas.
Prey availability
In order to examine the role of prey availability in
carnivores’ distribution, the wild rabbit (Oryctolagus
cuniculus) was considered the main prey item for
polecat, wildcat and lynx, and fish was regarded as
the main food resource for otters. Wild rabbit
distribution was assessed following the method of
Palomares (2001b) from May to September 2001. We
subdivided the study area into a grid of non-
contiguous 1 km2 where a 250 m transect in dirt
roads was surveyed for wild-rabbit latrines. Latrines
were categorized as: “droplets”, “small latrines”,

Data analysis
A Geographic Information System (GIS) was created
for the Alqueva-lake and surrounding-impacted area in
ArcView\ version 3.1 (Environmental Systems Re-
search Institute, Inc. 1996). The digital layers in the
GIS were from diverse origins, some of which
updated, and were used to evaluate species-habitats
relationships (Table 1). Remote sensed aerial photo-
graphs were used to create a land cover map for the
area, and accuracy assessment was performed by
cross-checking field visits. Data on species presence/
absence was recorded as geographical locations. All
species presence data were plotted in maps of each
species distribution. Qualitative comparisons were
performed to evaluate population trends along
Alqueva-project development.
Population nucleus
Species population nucleus area and boundaries were
determined using a fixed kernel method applied to
100% of the geographic locations of each species
(Worton 1989; Brunsdon 1995). Presence data was
considered as independent data points when locations
were obtained with at least 24 h of interval or at a
distance greater than a species home range size (e.g.,
Swihart and Slade 1985, 1986). This method was
used to assess species distribution range expansion
and contraction pre and post the development of
Alqueva dam (Brunsdon 1995). Data was analyzed in
ArcView\ version 3.1 using Animal Movement SA
Version 2 for ArcView (USGS-BRD, Alaska, USA;
Hooge and Eichenlaub 1997).

was used to determine the most suitable areas and
evaluate which factors are contributing for species
distribution after disturbance. We examined the
effect of land cover, hunting regimes, livestock
production areas and prey as potential explanatory
variables driving range variation and/or habitat
selection patterns (Table 1).
We used the landcover units from GIS data (Table 1)
and extracted layers of suitable habitats for each focal
species. Suitable habitats were selected accordingly to
species requirements described in the literature and the
results from our monitoring program. Thus, for the

areas of high probability of carnivore presence were
considered the most influential, whereas variables that

Results
Pre-deforestation/flooding phase
During this phase, the studied carnivores were present
throughout the Alqueva-lake and surrounding-impacted
area, except for the lynx (Figs. 3 and 4). The polecat
(40%) and the wildcat (21%) were frequently found,
but still occupying less than 50% of the surveyed area
(Fig. 3). The otter showed the greater incidence,
covering 84% of the sampled area (Fig. 3). We only
collected two faecal samples potentially of Iberian
lynx, however genetic results only confirmed the one
outside the study area limits, thus the species was not
addressed in further analysis.
Deforestation/early-flooding phase
The sampling strategy was delineated to follow up all
the phases of the implementation of the Alqueva dam.
However, delays in the construction of the dam wall
and in the deforestation activities resulted in a
combined phase of deforestation and flooding. Our
results reflect these simultaneous events (deforesta-
tion/early flooding events), since the flooding was
still incomplete at the end of the study.

The presence of the threatened carnivores in the
Alqueva-lake and surrounding-impacted areas was
more restricted during this phase. A decrease was
observed in the polecat and wildcat populations
(Fig. 3). The polecat was only detected in half of the
previous occurrence area and the wildcat decreased
to 11% of the study area (Fig. 3). Only five scats of
wildcat were found and two direct observations
of wildcat adult individuals (Fig. 3). The number of
cells where otter was present decreased but it is
close to that found during the pre-deforestation/
flooding phase (79% of the surveyed area; Fig. 3).
Species range variation
Polecat and wildcat decreased the extent of their
distribution range after the construction of the
Alqueva dam, whereas the otter slightly increased its
range. The wildcat and the otter had the most
restricted ranges (Table 2), which in the case of the
wildcat decreased by three fold after the dam
construction (Table 2). The polecat had a higher pre-
dam distribution area (Table 2) covering a region 1.5
times larger than that of the wildcat and the otter.
However, after the dam implementation the polecat
and otter denoted different distribution patterns, with
a decrease in the polecat range and an increase in the
otter range (Table 2).
Pre-dam carnivore distribution was mainly frag-
mented, with the exception of the otter. In fact,
presence probability of both polecat and wildcat was
subdivided into three main nucleuses located in
different areas of the study area (Fig. 4). The wildcat
was more concentrated in the valley and confluence of
rivers Guadiana and Degebe, whereas the polecat was
found associated with either the major rivers and
northern locations of study area (Fig. 4). The otter
had a more continuous distribution throughout the full
extent of the study area (Fig. 4). Post-dam construction
reversed the scenario, with the polecat and wildcat
concentrating into a main core area and the otter
slightly fragmenting its distribution (Fig. 4). In fact, the
wildcat remained associated with a restricted area
northern to that previously occupied, which was also
the area where polecat distribution was mainly con-
centrated (Fig. 4). This is associated with a two fold
decrease of the areas with highest probability –
nucleuses (kernel 50%) – of detecting all the monitored
species (Table 2).
Explanatory variables of species range expansion/
contraction
Overlap analysis demonstrated that suitable habitats
and main prey were the mostly associated with
“escape” areas. The otter selected mostly areas with
suitable riverside habitats (88.6% points in riverine
habitats and 11.4% in riparian vegetation). No
information was available for the distribution of otter
main prey items (Fig. 5; Table 3).
The assessment of main prey availability (wild
rabbit) in the study area has shown that this variable,
together with habitat, was driving escape areas for
polecat and wildcat (Fig. 5; Table 3). The logistic
regression model for wild rabbit presence had an
overall classification rate of 66.9%, with 69.7%
correctly classified presences and 64.2% correctly
classified absences. The model included 9 variables
(Table 4) and showed a good-fit to the data (χ2=
428.13, p=0.228). The logistic regression model
equation was used to build the wild rabbit probability
map. Thus, in regions with high wild rabbit probabil-
ity of presence (average=0.553), polecat escape areas
are most associated with oak woodlands (48.3%) and
cereal plantations (20.7%). The wildcat shows a more
specialized habitat preference with escape points
concentrated in shrubs (62.5%) and oak woodlands
(31.3%), with higher wild rabbit presence probability
(average=0.689).
None of the species showed avoidance from areas
with high human disturbance factors. Explanatory
variables such as undetermined hunting regime and
low livestock numbers did not influence the selection
of escape areas.
Discussion
Monitoring mammal populations not only provides a
basis for quantifying ecological impacts but also
assesses rates of change (Harris and Yalden 2004).
The Alqueva project represented a unique situation
for monitoring. Threatened carnivores were moni-
tored since the early construction of Alqueva-
dam wall (reference scenario) and their status
reassessed as the Alqueva dam and lake were
implemented (post-dam construction scenario). Our
results show an a priori fragmented distribution of
most species, with the exception of the otter. A major

Polecat status is critical in European countries
with the exception of the UK (e.g., Birks 1998; Birks
and Kitchener 1999; Baghli and Verhagen 2003;
Lodé et al. 2003). It was considered an individual-
istic species with scattered breeding sub-units with
strict spatio-temporal segregation between males and
females, resulting in high inbreeding populations
(Lodé et al. 2003). Although no consistent data was
available for Alqueva dam area before its construc-
tion, the species low detection rate (<50% of the
surveyed area) suggests a fragmented population
structure as described by Lodé et al. (2003). In
addition, polecat is negatively influenced by human
activities such as increasing road densities (Birks
and Kitchener 1999) and additional mortality as a
by-product of pesticide use (Birks 1998). In fact, a
total of 16 animals were found dead in one year of
monitoring of a high traffic-intensity road (A. Mira,
personal communication) and six road casualties
were found in our study area. Thus, the polecat
populations seem to be vulnerable to the road
network and the increased car traffic and movements
in the study area during the deforestation/early
flooding phase may have negatively affected these
populations. Indirect causes, such as habitat loss and
fragmentation and prey density decrease also seem to
have contributed to the observed patterns (see
below). Most surprisingly was the observed associ-
ation of polecats with oak woodlands and cereal
plantations. Literature from northern European pop-
ulations indicates habitat selection patterns that focus
on riverine and riparian areas (Lodé 1994), and some
abandoned human structures in the U.K. (Birks and
Kitchener 1999). The observed pattern in Mediter-
ranean woodlands may suggest that the species shifts
its habitat selection towards the most available
habitats, oak woodlands and cereal plantations.
Furthermore, these habitats may offer alternative
prey items and shelter characteristics that are not
found in other areas of their distribution range.
Future studies should clarify this interesting and
unique association.

The Iberian lynx conservation status in Portugal is
being highly debated (e.g., Ceia et al. 1998;
Fernandes et al. 2001; Santos-Reis 2003; Sarmento
et al. 2004). This species has been facing a substantial
decline since early 1980’s due to habitat loss and prey
decline (wild rabbit). Ceia et al. (1998) determined a
species numbers between four to seven lynxes within
an occupied range of 270 km2 in the Guadiana valley.
However, Sarmento et al. (2004) in the Iberian Lynx
National Census have not confirmed the species
presence in Portuguese territory and classified the
species as pre-extinct. Nonetheless, the unconfirmed
scat found in 2001, during the deforestation/early-
flooding phase and the confirmed presence outside
the study area showed that there are still some
individuals present. Alqueva dam was implemented
in a crucial area of the species national distribution
range – Contenda-Barrancos (Palma 1980; Ceia et al.
1998; Sarmento et al. 2004) and irreversibly affected
the residual highly vulnerable population of this
species.
Contrary to the other monitored species, otter
populations seem well established and less impacted
by the Alqueva project than the other studied species.
Portugal is known to have one of the best established
European otter populations (Santos-Reis 1983; Mason
and Mcdonald 1986; Kruuk 1995; Trindade et al.
1998). Main impacts were expected during deforesta-
tion and flooding phases. During deforestation, key
habitat elements as riparian vegetation were removed
from the system. Riparian vegetation is known to
provide shelter, den sites and thermal stability (in
particular during the hot summers) for otter popula-
tions in these areas (e.g., Jenkins and Burrows 1980;
MacDonald and Mason 1982; Bas et al. 1984;
MacDonald and Mason 1984; Lunnon and Reynolds
1991). During flooding, there is a gradual transfor-
mation of the lotic to a lentic system, thus affecting
otter prey access and capture rates (Kruuk 1995;
Pedroso 2004). Furthermore, this change in the
system characteristics has been described as greatly
influencing the icthyofauna diversity and abundance
in the aquatic habitats and thus changing prey
community and availability (Filipe et al. 2004;
Pedroso 2004).
At the end of the study, after the increase in
distribution at early flooding, the otter distribution
was lower and more fragmented than the pre-dam
implementation. This decrease is a result of the
negative 25 km2 cells in the main rivers (due to
submersion) and also negative cells located outside
inundation area that otter occupied in 2001 and
2002, which have small water systems of intermit-
tent flow. However, these smaller intermittent water
systems do not allow otters to establish long term
territories especially during dry season (e.g.
MacDonald and Mason 1982; Prenda et al. 2001).
Overall, otter situation after dam implementation
reveals itself worse than before, especially due to
habitat loss after flooding which may have confined
animals’ movements to the remaining refuge/habitat,
the isolation effect of dam wall in the upstream and
downstream populations.
Our results reveal that polecat, wildcat and otter
were affected by the deforestation/early-flooding
phase. Two responses can then be inferred from these
results to the effects of Alqueva dam implementation:
(1) species as the polecat and the wildcat may be
highly affected by deforestation, since large patches of
suitable habitat have been removed, restricting species
range and potential “escape areas” (areas that reflect
optimal and suboptimal habitat); and (2) other species
as the otter are mostly affected by the flooding itself
due to their ecological constraints to aquatic environ-
ments. Future research should clarify if these responses
are generalized or if they result of the combined
deforestation/flooding phase. Furthermore, it would be
extremely important to determine if the species are in
fact responding to each of the actions separately, which
would inform further mitigation and sensitive timing
for future monitoring programs.

“Escape areas” – habitat
During the implementation of a large dam, vast
areas of vegetation are removed to reduce the
biomass and potential for eutrophization of the
dam reservoir (Bizer 2000). Alqueva dam flooding
area extended for 250 km2, resulting in a large area
of removed trees and shrubs. To minimize the
impact of such removal, Alqueva dam deforestation
occurred by sections and multiple areas were set
aside so that species could use them as refuge as
water level rose. The monitored carnivore species
have variable habitat requirements: (1) riverine and
riparian habitats for otter and polecat (Lodé 1993,
1994; White et al. 2003); (2) woodlands, abandoned
barns and cereal areas for polecat (Birks and
Kitchener 1999); and (3) dense forested areas with
thick understorey cover and some rocky areas for
wildcat (Stahl and Léger 1992; Ferreira 2003).
Riparian habitats are transitional zones between
aquatic and terrestrial habitats, highly vulnerable to
disturbance, which have been identified as high
biodiversity areas (e.g., Naiman et al. 1993). In
Alqueva dam flooding and surrounding-impacted
area, riparian areas are restricted to narrow strips
along major water courses. During the implementa-
tion of a large dam, these areas are completely
destroyed being predicted that species such as the
otter and the polecat to be highly affected. Our
monitoring program data showed that otters occurred
in a wide variety of freshwater habitats, with its
distribution range increasing during the beginning of
the most impact phase of deforestation/early-flooding.
Final otter deforestation/early-flooding distribution
range also decreased, resulting in a more fragmented
range than before dam construction, which could
indicate habitat loss and barriers to movement, may
have been created. Polecat presence signs were
detected not only in linear and riparian habitats (one
capture in 2001 in a riparian habitat) but also in oak
woodlands and cereal plantations, indicating a
broader use of landscapes. Polecat distribution range
decreased after the deforestation/early-flooding by
two fold, suggesting that other variables may be
driving polecat “escape” areas.
Oak forests with dense understorey are restricted to
some non-altered regions of the study area, mostly
higher slope with lower human accessibility. These
areas maintain climax Mediterranean oak habitats that
have been classified as biodiversity hot spots (Myers et
al. 2000). Large extents of dense oak tree and shrub
cover were removed during deforestation. Few areas of
remainder habitat were left in a highly fragmented
landscape pattern. These areas can be considered as
optimal or suboptimal habitats for wildcat and Iberian
lynx and were expected to be used as potential escape
habitats after Alqueva-lake flooding. In fact, overlap
analysis results show that the wildcat has selected these
remaining fragments of habitat. No conclusive data
was obtained for the Iberian lynx; however previous
studies by Palomares (2001a) have showed that in
Doñana National Park in Spain, sites used by resident
individuals had less tree cover, shorter trees, higher
percentage of understorey cover, and taller shrubs.
Monitored carnivores escape areas were defined
accordingly to species habitat selection character-
istics. This effect is more pronounced if associated
with these losses of habitat are also losses of prey.
Lodé (1994) stated that polecat habitat selection
characteristics cannot be dissociated of its food
selection, and the same can be applied to other
predator populations. Thus, we extended the analysis
of the escape areas’ explanatory variables (for
polecats and wildcats) to include main prey.
“Escape areas” – prey
Wild rabbit populations in Mediterranean systems are
more abundant in open scrubland/parkland habitats
(oak woodlands), often combining olive groves with
pastureland, with easily burrowable soil but restricted
public access (Fa et al. 1999). This species popula-
tions are facing a generalized decline associated with
natural and artificial factors: (1) outbreaks of mix-
omatosis (Sumption and Flowerdew 1985); (2)
haemorrhagic disease (Fa et al. 1999; Cooke 2002);
(3) increased hunting (Moreno and Villafuerte 1995);
(4) decrease of suitable habitat (Moreno and
Villafuerte 1995; Fa et al. 1999); (5) decrease of
feeding resources (Martins et al. 2002); and (6)
increased predation (Moreno and Villafuerte 1995;
Gil-Sanchez et al. 1999). This decrease in prey
population effectives is directly reflected in feeding
specialist predator populations.
A review on polecat and wildcat staple food items
reveals that diets are composed mainly by wild
rabbit in the southern tip of the carnivores’ distribu-
tion (Stahl and Léger 1992; Moreno and Villafuerte

Monitoring mammals
Large scale mammalian carnivore surveys are known
to be crucial because they provide important infor-
mation on species distribution, abundance and habitat,
serve as complement/substitute traditional time con-
suming and expensive field surveys, and may serve as
potential indicators of the impacts of human activities
(Batterby and Greenwood 2004; Zielinski et al. 2000).
It is important to acknowledge that the scale at which
these surveys are often conducted is directed towards
regional scale assessments and planning, thus
disregarding finer scale information. Nonetheless,
these studies provide systematic assessments of the
status, spatial-temporal dynamics of species, in particular
those already threatened.
Our study shows that the implementation of such a
survey methodology allows responding to manage-
ment concerns of generally overlooked large scale
human structure development – large dams. To our
knowledge very few studies have addressed this
problem, in particular because they are time consum-
ing, expensive and exhaustive. We believe that the
scale at which we conducted this survey and
implemented the monitoring protocol allows assess-
ing adequately the change the large dam has created
in the system.
Large dams often result in irreversible loss of
habitat, which is particularly crucial for threatened
carnivores as those we addressed herein. Our results
show that the proposed monitoring method offers
results in a timely manner to conservation and
management concerns (Zielinski et al. 2000). Mam-
mal carnivores are mainly nocturnal, have elusive
habits, low capture rates and are hard to detect (Lodé
et al. 2003; Harris and Yalden 2004). Consequently,
these species are harder to detect and some caution is
needed when designing surveys to realistically access
their status, especially when dealing with threatened
species. We believe that some underestimation may
have occurred on the populations’ status in the study
area. For the wildcat and polecat a trapping effort was
conducted simultaneously to the monitoring program
with very few results [from March to May 2001: no
wildcats in 423 night-traps (rate between the number
of traps and the number of nights a trap is set); one
polecat in 1,443 night-traps]. These results may
indicate that these species populations are restricted;
however a systematized capture program should be
implemented to clarify population status.
Potential shortcomings of these surveys are the
scale of sampling, being sensitive to the nature of the
study and the ecology of the target species (Wilson
and Delahay 2001; Zielinski and Stauffer 1996).
There is a trade-off between the detail of the data
acquired and the time to respond to management
concerns. When the objective of the study is to
sample at large scales, the finer habitat selection
characteristics are “dissolved” to understand the larger
scale patterns that species are responding to. More-
over, the scale of sampling should also be selected to
mimic the scale at which species respond to large
scale environmental impacts. A priori knowledge on
species habitat requirements may inform the selection
of the appropriate scale of survey; however, this
should not be the only parameter for survey design
and the selection of the sampling scale.
In conclusion, Alqueva dam monitoring program
for threatened carnivore populations have shown that
this project has major impacts in the populations’
distribution during deforestation and flooding events.
Iberian lynx, wildcat and polecat seem to be the most
affected species, whereas otters were less impacted
and may adjust population levels to the new condi-
tion. Future monitoring programmes, designed to
evaluate the impact of the implementation of this
type of infrastructures, should particularly focus on
these phases. Management prescriptions, including
the development of new large dams, as predicted for a
close future in Portugal, should consider these
impacts in the mitigation and complementation
actions. In addition, habitat and key prey seem to be
a good surrogate for escape areas for threatened
populations, indicating that future research should
