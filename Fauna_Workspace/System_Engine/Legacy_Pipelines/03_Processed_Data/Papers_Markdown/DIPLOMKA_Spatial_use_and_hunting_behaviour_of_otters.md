Abstract 
 
Although the Eurasian otter (Lutra lutra) is one of the most intensively studied mustelid 
species, basic data on the species and its ecology are still insufficient due to its mainly 
nocturnal activity and elusive behaviour. Especially some ethological data can be obtained 
only by direct observation, either in captivity or in the wild, which is difficult. The basic 
aim of this study was to assess ethological data, especially on spatial use and hunting 
behaviour, of wild otters in the Třeboňsko BR & PLA, which is an extremely rich fresh-
water habitat and of vital importance for the otter population in Middle Europe. During 
155 observation sessions, 29 otters were observed. Parallels to otters living in rich coastal 
habitat were found, including patch fishing and at least temporal group ranges. Especially 
some rich river sites were found to be used as some kind of ‘communal’ foraging sites, but 
probably only during severe winter conditions. The otters were shown to change their 
spatial usage patterns according to the surrounding conditions. Hunting success of the 
otters was extremely high, even at sites used by more otters, thus confirming the impor-
tance of the Třeboňsko BR & PLA as a uniquely rich otter habitat. Two concepts of 
hunting success measurement were considered, showing to be comparable at least in fresh-
water habitat. Successful dives were found to last longer than unsuccessful ones, thus con-
trasting to results obtained on coastal otters. Small fish ponds were found to be important 
especially for female otters as defendable and rich feeding patches. 
Although the method of direct observation of wild otters is difficult and time-consuming, 
thus being suitable rather for small studies, its importance for obtaining ethological data 
was confirmed.

Introduction

Introduction 
 
1.1. 
The Eurasian otter 
 
1.1.1. Biology and ecology 
 
The Eurasian otter (Lutra lutra L., 1758) is a semi-aquatic member of the family Musteli-
dae, standing on the top of the aquatic food chain. It inhabits various aquatic habitat types, 
including rivers, ponds, ditches, marshland and even coastal areas. The otter is a secretive 
animal, with most populations being night active from one hour before dusk to one hour 
after dawn (Mason & Macdonald 1986, Carss 1995, Kruuk 1995). The main activity peak, 
however, is around dusk and the first part of the night, followed by a second peak, often 
less intensive, at the end of the night (Mason & Macdonald 1986, Kruuk 1995, Veselovský 
1998, Kučerová & Roche 1999). 
 
Otters are primarily solitary (e.g. Erlinge 1967b, Chanin 1985, Mason & Macdonald 1986, 
Poledník et al. in press), i.e. they never cooperate except mating, but they are social to 
some extent, i.e. they interact more or less regularly and have a social hierarchy (e.g. 
Chanin 1985, Carss 1995). Resident otters know their place in this hierarchy, thus pre-
venting fights. During severe conditions they can even tolerate the simultaneous presence 
of other otters at rich feeding places. Even under such conditions, there is no evidence for 
co-operative hunting, however, aggressive interactions are rare (Foerster 1996, Kučerová 
& Roche 1999).  
 
The otter is able to breed at any time of year, but a number of studies have shown that there 
is evidence for seasonality, with most cubs being born between May and August (e.g. 
Kruuk et al. 1987). It is believed that this ensures higher cub survival as they are born 
when food is most available (for the mother to feed them) (Kruuk 1995). This, however, is 
only likely to be so where food availability is also guaranteed to be high when the cubs 
leave the holt and can begin to feed themselves, i.e. in winter (e.g. in pond habitats or 
coastal areas) (Roche & Roche 2004). Between 1 and 3 cubs are usually produced and they 
will stay with the mother for up to one year before dispersing. Males play no part in the 
bringing up of the cubs and will part from the female soon after mating (Veselovský 1998).

Introduction 
1.1.2. Spatial use 
 
The otter utilises a large range of different habitat types and is highly adaptable to 
(changing) habitat conditions. It can exploit all types of water and waterways (Chanin 
2003a).  
 
The size of an otter’s home range largely depends, among others, on habitat conditions and 
can range from a quarter of a square kilometre in habitat with abundant food and good 
conditions to 80 km of waterway (Chanin 1985, Durbin 1993). The size and shape of an 
otter’s home range also depend on factors like age, sex, and social position of the otter, and 
can vary seasonally (Durbin 1993, Kruuk 1995, Poledník et al. in press). In riverine habi-
tat, home ranges are quite linear, whereas in pond habitat they are usually smaller in size, 
but still can comprise large distances of shore-line and a large water surface area 
(Kučerová & Roche 1999).  
The distance travelled by an otter can reach more than 20 km per night (Jenkins 1980). 
 
It is not quite clear whether otters have (defended) territories or (not defended) home 
ranges (Poledník et al. in press). The system of spatial usage seems to be variable and 
modified according to habitat and food conditions, with the abundance and distribution of 
resources determining home range size, otter density and patterns of movement (Chanin 
1985). The “classical” mustelid spacing pattern (Erlinge 1968, Powell 1979, Green et al. 
1984), with females having smaller, partly overlapping, home ranges and males maintain-
ing relatively exclusive territories which coincide with the home ranges of one or more 
females, can be found in habitats where food availability or other resources, e.g. holts, are 
limited. In environments where food and other resources are rich and patchily distributed, 
e.g. the coastal areas of Shetland (Kruuk 1995), there is a different spacing pattern. 
Although the otter is a primarily solitary animal (e.g. Erlinge 1967b, Chanin 1985, Mason 
& Macdonald 1986, Carss 1995, Kruuk 1995), group ranges are maintained here. Several 
females can have totally overlapping home ranges, with each individual living solitarily 
and using the whole range. Still, every individual has its own “core area”, where it spends 
more than half of its time and which is avoided by the other otters in the range (Kruuk & 
Moorhouse 1991, Roche 2001). This spacing pattern is profitable where the presence of 
different types of foraging areas, being used under different conditions, is important. 
Therefore, more otters share a larger area, instead of one maintaining a smaller area with

1.1.3. Foraging behaviour 
 
Foraging behaviour of otters is defined as “that which is required to stalk, catch, manipu-
late and eat prey” (Carss 1995), and wild otters spend 40–63% of their active time foraging 
(Nolet & Kruuk 1989, Roche 2001). In freshwater habitat, fish form 49–94% of their diet 
(mean 70%), in coastal areas even more (Chanin 2003a). The fish are dived for and caught 
with the jaws (Chanin 1985). Otters are typical opportunistic predators, the relative num-
bers of different species in their prey changing accordingly to their abundance and tempo-
ral and spatial availability (Chanin 1985, Carss 1995, Roche 2001). The otter’s daily 
consumption is 15–20% of its body weight, which amounts to 0.4–0.9 kg of food, and in 
lactating females or in winter, when energy requirements are higher, up to 1.5 kg (e.g. 
Chanin 1985, Veselovský 1998). Small fish up to 20 cm of length dominate in the diet, 
being the most abundant in most ichthyocoenosis (e.g. Erlinge 1967a, Kruuk et al 1990, 
Carss 1995, Hájková 2001, Roche 2001), and seem to be preferred (Kranz 2000, Roche 
2004c).  
 
Foraging behaviour is principally related to prey abundance or availability (i.e. changes in 
fish populations and behaviour), with dominant species or feeding sites changing in an 
effort to reduce energy costs, particularly in winter (Chanin 1985, Kruuk et al. 1993, 
Kučerová & Roche 1999, Chanin 2003a). In riverine habitat with less food available, otters 
hunt “catching fish on their way and always being on the move” (Kruuk 1995), whereas in 
rich patchy environments they can stay at the single patches for a longer time. The patchy 
exploitation of food resources can take place where food availability in the patches is rich, 
recurrent and predictable to the otter (Kruuk 1995), as it is in coastal areas or in rich pond-
and-river habitat (Roche 2001).

The otter’s hunting technique is based on diving for prey. Single dives can last from a few 
seconds to around 100 seconds, with the mean diving time being around 20 seconds (e.g. 
Foerster 1996, for a review see Kruuk 1995). Unsuccessful dives in freshwater habitat 
seem to be shorter than successful ones (Foerster 1995, Kranz 1995, Foerster 1996), 
whereas in coastal habitat, successful dives are shorter (e.g. Chanin 1985, Kruuk 1995). 
The otter probably tunes its diving time to an expected success rate at the corresponding 
depth and conditions (Kruuk et al. 1990, Nolet et al. 1993, Kruuk 1995). Success rates 
range from 7% of the dives being successful (at freshwater lochs in Scotland, Conroy & 
Jenkins 1986) to 24% (coastal areas of Scotland, Watt 1993) and even 31% in rich fresh-
water habitat in Central Europe (Foerster 1996). Hunting bouts are made up by one or 
more dives and are separated by travelling to or from hunting sites or other behaviour like 
grooming, mating etc. 
 
 
1.1.4. Other behaviour 
 
Grooming is of vital importance for otters. Their extremely thick fur (50 000 hairs per 
square centimetre, Veselovský 1998) has to be kept clean to maintain thermal insulation, 
and wild otters at the coast spend 13–28% of their active time with grooming (Nolet & 
Kruuk 1989). The percentage is lower in freshwater habitat, because there otters do not 
need to wash salt out of their fur. 
 
Exploratory behaviour includes walking around, sniffing, looking, and travelling. It is 
important for territory maintenance, communication and orientation in the environment.  
 
Sprainting (spraints are otter faeces) serves as an indirect communication channel for 
otters. By sprainting, territory boundaries are marked, social status, sex and age are 
communicated, and scent marking before, during and after hunting bouts prevents simulta-
neous patch depletion (Durbin 1993, Kruuk 1995).

Introduction 
1.1.5.  Legal status and conservation 
 
In the Czech Republic, the otter is classified as a severely endangered species; and there-
fore it is protected under Czech Nature Law (no. 114/1992 and no. 395/1992). Endangered 
species are protected in all stages of development, as well as their habitats. 
International legislation protects the otter through the IUCN Red list, the EU Habitats 
Directive (no. 92/43/EEC), the CITES Convention and the Bern Convention, and indirectly 
through the Ramsar Convention (Poledník et al. in press). 
Otters are a high priority species for conservationists throughout Europe. As the top 
predator in the aquatic food chain, the otter is an important species for monitoring possible 
problems below it in the food chain, respectively in the habitat. Otters need high food 
availability, low pollution, low disturbance, and good habitat. When any, or all, of these 
factors deteriorate, they may be one of the first species to indicate problems (Kučerová & 
Roche 1999). Also, the otter serves as a flagship species and thus helps in conservation of 
valuable aquatic ecosystems (Kučerová & Roche 1999, Kranz 2000, Kruuk 2002). 
In natural habitat, opportunistic predators such as the otter have a regulating function in the 
ecosystem, but over most of its distribution range this function is reduced because of the 
abnormal state of the aquatic ecosystems, mostly being managed by anglers and fisheries 
and being highly stocked with marketable fish. Especially commercial carp ponds are 
strongly out of ecological balance, and otter predation is a way of “bringing them back to a 
normal state”, which, of course, interferes with the interests of fish farmers (Dulfer et al. 
1998, Moravcová 2004, Poledník et al. in press). For further conservation of the species, 
these interests also have to be taken into account, to prevent illegal otter killing by fish 
farmers and anglers, which is assumed to be one of the biggest causes of otter mortality in 
fish pond areas such as the Třeboňsko BR & PLA (Kučerová & Roche 1999, Roche 2004b, 
Poledník et al. in press).

Introduction 
1.2. 
Otters in the Třeboňsko BR & PLA 
 
The Třeboňsko Biosphere Reserve and Protected Landscape Area (Třeboňsko BR & PLA), 
with its abundant food (the rivers and ponds offer a total available biomass of 30–120 ton-
nes of fish throughout the year, Dulfer & Roche 1998), suitable habitat, and high level of 
legislative protection, supports a thriving population of otters and, as such, has a vitally 
important role to play as a “core area of otter distribution” in Central Europe, and will be 
important in the future reconnection of the presently isolated populations of otters remain-
ing in Europe (Kučerová & Roche 1999, Kranz 2000). Compared with other parts of 
Europe, the density of otters in the Reserve is very high (Zemanová 2006) – about one 
adult animal per 57–81 ha of water bodies and marsh land (Šimek & Springar 1996), or 
112–172 otters in the whole Reserve (Roche & Roche 2004).  
 
The environment of the Třeboňsko BR & PLA provides patchy resources with high prey 
availability. Some patches offer easily accessible food all over the year, while other 
patches become inaccessible for part of the year, mainly due to ice cover in winter (Roche 
2004c). Otters within the Reserve show a high adaptability to changing habitat conditions; 
they shift from semi-exclusive territories in linear habitats, to territory sharing and a high 
degree of intra-specific tolerance where food is plentiful, patchy and constantly renewed 
(e.g. the core area of the Reserve). Contact is avoided, however, by using alternate feeding 
and activity patches (Roche & Roche 2004). Females and family groups usually occupy the 
most favourable feeding places, mainly around the ponds, with up to four females sharing a 
home range. The males have larger home ranges and overlap one or more female ranges 
(Roche & Roche 2004). Foerster (1996) even observed the breaking down of the territory 
system in an extreme winter, and up to 11 otters, principally females with cubs, have been 
observed using the best feeding sites simultaneously or over a very short period. These 
sites were mainly the Small lagoon and Rozvodí, which were also included in this study 
(see chapter 2.2. Description of the study sites).  
 
The distribution of otters in the Reserve is highly uneven, especially in winter – around 
45% of the otters are found in the core zone of the Reserve, which covers only 14% of the 
Reserve’s surface area (Kučerová & Roche 1999).

Introduction 
1.3. 
Aims of this study 
 
The Eurasian otter is one of the most intensively studied mustelid species, but nevertheless 
basic data on the species and its ecology, for example habitat utilisation, population struc-
ture and foraging strategies, are still insufficient (see e.g. Kruuk 1995, Reuther et al. 2000). 
  
The otter is mainly nocturnal, cryptically coloured, and very elusive in its behaviour 
(Veselovský 1998, Chanin 2003b), therefore, most information on its ecology is obtained 
by indirect methods, e.g. distribution surveys using tracks and spraints (otter faeces), 
spraint analysis, research on found otter carcasses or analysis of DNA extracted from 
tissues or spraints. 
  
Taking into account the elusiveness of the otter, ethological data are particularly difficult to 
obtain. Telemetry studies provide valuable information on the movements of single otters, 
their home ranges and activities (e.g. Green et al. 1984, Kranz 1995, Hussain 2002, 
Poledník 2005), but this method has several disadvantages. Otters are very difficult to 
catch, and the radio transmitter has to be implanted into their abdomen. Stress during 
catching, operation and recovery time can seriously affect the otter’s ability to survive 
(König & König 2002, Fernandez-Moran et al. 2004), and even change the behaviour of 
the otter after release. The transmitter stays inside the otter for the rest of its life. Further, 
telemetry studies are very expensive and time-consuming. 
 
Many ethological data can be obtained only by direct observation, either in captivity or in 
the wild, for example data on foraging behaviour or interactions with other otters. Data 
obtained from captive otters (Erlinge 1968, Fumagalli et al. 1995, Gnoli & Prigioni 1995, 
Polotti et al. 1995, Adams 1997) are valuable and more easily accessible, yet need not be 
always comparable with data on wild otters. Otters are highly adaptable to varying envi-
ronmental conditions, and can adjust their territorial patterns, social system and activities 
according to their surroundings (e.g. Kruuk & Moorhouse 1991, Lott 1991, Knollseisen 
1995, Roche & Roche 2004). For example, captive otters spend only 11–16% of their time 
foraging (Adams 1997), while wild otters have to expend 40–53% of their time to catch 
enough food (Nolet and Kruuk 1989).

Direct observation in the wild is difficult because of the elusiveness and nocturnal activity 
of the otter, but still useful data can be obtained (Foerster 1996, Chanin 2003b, Roche & 
Roche 2004). Many direct observation studies on wild otters have been carried out on 
otters living at the coast (e.g. Nolet et al. 1993 in Shetland, Kruuk 1995 in Scotland, 
Clavero et al. 2004 in southern Spain, Ruiz-Olmo et al. 2005 in north-east Spain), because 
they are often active during the day and thus easier to observe, but there are also some 
studies from freshwater habitats in Central Europe (e.g. Foerster 1995, Kranz 1995, 
Foerster 1996, Dulfer et al. 1998, Hauer et al. 2002). The Třeboňsko BR & PLA is a highly 
suitable otter habitat with a dense and viable population (Šimek & Springar 1996, Dulfer et 
al. 1998, Roche & Roche 2004), and can provide valuable information on the otters’ 
ecology, behaviour and adaptability. 
 
Knowledge about the selection and use of different habitat types by the otter provides 
essential information needed for conservation. Numerous studies have identified food 
availability, fresh water and shelter suitable for resting and breeding as the key factors in 
habitat selection (e.g. Macdonald & Mason 1994, Kruuk 1995). Most of these studies 
investigated otter populations in habitat with somehow limited resources. The habitat 
structure in the Třeboňsko BR & PLA, on the other hand, seems to favour a dense otter 
population. This is enabling the study of a small otter community under optimal conditions 
and the investigation of, amongst others, density and range limitations, activity patterns 
and habitat use under conditions where food biomass is not the limiting factor and where 
open water can be found even in the most severe winters (Dulfer & Roche 1998). 
 
Finally, investigations on otters’ use of various fish ponds and /or river habitat help solving 
the ecological, economic and social conflicts between fish farming and otter conservation 
(Myšiak et al., submitted). An increased number of fish predators in the environment is a 
natural response to the high amount of food repeatedly made available by the fish farmers 
(Moravcová 2004). On the other hand, fish farming has a long tradition in South Bohemia, 
and the high density of otters rises the number of claims regarding killed or damaged fish, 
especially at small fish ponds. Detailed knowledge of otter spacing patterns and foraging 
habits is vital for the effective assessment of the damages and for solving the conflict on a 
long-term basis.

Material and methods 
 
2. 
Material and methods 
 
2.1. 
Characteristics of the area 
 
(adapted from Kučerová & Roche 1999, see for further information)  
All observations were made within the Třeboňsko Biosphere Reserve and Protected Land-
scape area (Třeboňsko BR & PLA), which is centered on the South Bohemian town of 
Třeboň in the Czech Republic. The Reserve, with a surface area of approximately 700 km2, 
is largely situated in the Třeboň Basin (average altitude 410–450 m above sea level), an 
oligotrophic inland depression rich in both surface and ground water. The River Lužnice, 
with its tributaries in the Austrian and South Bohemian hills, and the river Nežárka, carry 
large amounts of water into the area and, after entering the basin, slow and widen into a 
large meandering floodplain with extensive wetlands.  
Carp pond fishing has a long historical tradition in the area and, during more than seven 
centuries of management, an intricate network of ponds, supply and drainage canals, 
brooks, marshes, peat bogs and wetland meadows has developed into a unique artificial 
wetland ecosystem. The 460 man-made fishponds, ranging from 0.1 to 450 ha, cover more 
than 7000 ha in total, representing about 10% of the surface area of the Třeboňsko BR & 
PLA. Around 84% of these ponds (6289 ha) are managed by one company, Třeboň 
Fisheries Ltd. The ponds and their related wetland habitats, as well as the species reliant on 
them (including the Eurasian otter) are central to the unique and internationally important 
natural heritage of the Třeboňsko BR & PLA.

Material and methods 
 
2.2. 
Description of the study sites 
 
Four study sites have been selected for this study (Fig. 1): 
 
1. A large bend and pool on the river Lužnice, called the Small lagoon (“lag” in the 
figures, Fig. 2), location 49°1’24.51”N, 14°49’22.54”E. Around the year 1995, this 
site consisted of a deep pool which remained open even in winter due to water 
flow, making it a good site for fish to overwinter, and also for otter foraging. The 
productivity of this site was very high, with up to 1060 kg of fish/ha (Jurajda & 
Roche 1994 and 1995). Since then, the depth has been reduced from approx. 3 m to 
around 1 m (centimetres in places), due to sand deposition. But still the water 
remains open all year, and the site is considered to be one of the best otter sites in 
the Reserve (Roche K. pers. comm., Kučerová M. pers. comm.).

Material and methods 
 
2.3. Observations 
 
2.3.1. Timing and location 
 
Observations were carried out over a period of two years, from October 2002 to September 
2004. Each of the four study sites was visited monthly, with one observation night for 
each. Observation nights were divided in two observation periods, each lasting 2–3 hours 
and being situated around dusk and dawn, which are the activity peaks for otters in most 
freshwater habitats (Munz and Macfarland 1973 in Carss 1995, Carss et al. 1990, Carss 
1995, Kruuk 1995) and also in the Třeboňsko BR & PLA (Foerster 1996, Kučerová & 
Roche 1999). A total of 155 observation periods was reached, which equates 87 observa-
tion nights (some of the observation periods had to be left out because of bad weather con-
ditions and electric storms, which could damage the night vision equipment).  
 
The points from where observation took place were chosen according to previous experi-
ence by the Czech Otter Foundation Fund (Roche K. pers. comm., Kučerová M. pers. 
comm.), and stayed the same over the two years of observation. They were chosen to 
provide a good overview of the whole site, but to prevent visiting otters from seeing the 
observer.  
 
 
2.3.2. Material 
 
The observations were made with the aid of binoculars and night vision equipment. 
Binoculars were used whenever light conditions were sufficient; often even the moon pro-
vided enough light to use them. Night vision equipment amplifies available light (moon or 
starlight) using sensitive photo multiplying electronics. The device was equipped with an 
infra-red illuminator, which provided additional light at poor lighting conditions. Infra-red 
light is not visible for otters, thus could not cause any disturbation, but is converted to 
visible light by the night vision equipment.  
The distance to the animals was on average 30 m. 
 
 
19


Material and methods 
 
A hand-held data recorder was used to record the beginning and end of the observation 
period, the date and site name, the behaviour of the otter(s), the period of each activity and 
observations on foraging behaviour. All of this information was whispered to prevent 
being noticed by the otter. Whispering, avoidance of movements and dark clothing con-
tributed to successful observations, and in the two years, only two otters seemed to have 
noticed the observer.  
 
On return to the home base, the recorded observations were timed with a stopwatch and 
transferred to an Excel file. The observations and activities of individual otters were 
divided into categories and statistical analysis was undertaken on the data. 
 
 
2.3.3. Categories recorded 
 
The categories were defined in accordance with categories used by Foerster (1996), to be 
able to compare and contrast the results.  
 
- 
Time of observation start and end 
- 
Time of each recording session (i.e. when an otter arrives and when any otters have 
left) 
- 
Start and finish of all relevant movements and activities of the otter(s) whilst they were 
present, timed to the nearest second (hunting and feeding behaviour, grooming, explor-
ative behaviour, interactions with other otters or other species, sprainting) 
- 
Direction of travel 
- 
Presence and activities of any other otters 
- 
Single animal/group 
 
The time of single otter presence started with the arrival of one otter at the observation site, 
and ended with its leaving. 
 
The category of foraging behaviour (e.g. hunting and feeding behaviour) includes hunting 
style (porpoise diving/diving), success of the dives (coming up with fish or not), prey 
landing (prey is eaten in the water/on the bank), activities after a foraging period 
(grooming, sprainting etc.), and any other related interesting behaviour or food type.  
 
20


Material and methods 
 
A sequence of dives which may also include excursions on land for feeding is called a 
hunting bout, containing one or more hunts (dives). It starts with the first hunt after the 
appearance of an otter at the observation site or after resting behaviour on land, and ends 
with the disappearance of the animal or a resting behaviour on land.  
 
Recovery time is the time period spent on the water surface and on land following an 
unsuccessful dive during a hunting bout. After successful dives, prey handling and 
recovery times were not separately measured.  
 
Two definitions of success rate were applied: (a) proportion of successful dives (i.e. dives 
with a prey capture) in all dives and (b) number of successful dives per time hunting.  
 
Porpoise diving is defined as a sequence of at least three consecutive dives (often more) 
which are separated only by a very short dolphin-like visit to the surface for inhalation.  
 
Explorative behaviour includes walking on the bankside, sniffing and looking around and 
also travelling to or from the observation site, because these activities could not be distin-
guished exactly.  
 
It was not possible to distinguish individual otters. Otters appearing longer than 10 minutes 
after the disappearing of a previous otter were considered as new individuals, if the time 
span was shorter, the otter was considered to be the same one. The same criteria were used 
by Kranz (1995) and Foerster (1996) in this study area.

2.4. 
Data analysis 
 
Data were treated separately for seasons (autumn/winter/spring/summer) and for sites 
(lag/roz/nov/ort), due to a small data set it was not possible to divide them into seasonal 
data for each site.  
 
The activities of otters present at the sites could not always be identified accurately, due to 
dense bankside vegetation, otters shortly being out of sight etc. These phases were noted as 
“unknown” and not included into the time counts of total otter presence and activities.  
 
Data on otter presence and on the different behaviour categories were analysed using the 
Kruskal-Wallis-H-test (using Statistica for Windows 7). Two of the observed otters could 
not be included in the statistical analysis, as their activities could not be timed exactly. Due 
to the small data set (maximum n=27), none of the calculations was found to be 
statistically significant. 
 
Success of morning and evening observations was compared using the χ2-test.

4. Discussion 
 
4.1. 
The method of direct observation 
 
The method of direct observation of otters in the wild is difficult and time-consuming, and 
the success rates of observation are quite low; and yet important data on behaviour, 
foraging strategies and sociality may be obtained. Indeed, many of these data can only be 
collected through direct observation, such as information on social behaviour during inter-
actions or hunting strategies.  
 
Most behavioural studies that have utilised direct observation in the wild to date, have 
tended to be carried out on otters living in coastal areas (e.g. Erlinge 1967a and 1967b, 
Nolet et al. 1993, Kruuk 1995, Clavero et al. 2004). Coastal otters, which feed mainly in 
the sea, tend to be active during the daytime, probably as sea fish show decreased activity 
at this time, making them easier to catch (Kruuk 1995). This pattern of activity makes 
observation much easier when compared to the typical night activity of ‘freshwater’ otters. 
Furthermore, otters in the sea, or on rocky beaches such as those found on the Shetland 
Isles (Scotland), utilise wide open spaces with little or no vegetation, thus making it diffi-
cult for the otter to “disappear” in dense bankside vegetation as may be the case at inland 
sites (e.g. Kruuk 1995).  
 
Another fact making observations on coastal otters easier is the high food availability of 
the environment they live in. Rich feeding patches are constantly being renewed, which al-
lows the otters to maintain relatively small territories, often being group territories (see 
chapter 1.1.2 Spatial use). They stay at the same feeding places for a longer time, and are 
not forced to hunt “on the move”, as tends to be the case for otters living in less well 
stocked and/or linear freshwater habitats (Kruuk 1995). This, therefore, enables longer 
observation periods in one place.  
 
Due to the difficulties highlighted, observational studies on wild ‘freshwater’ otters in 
Europe have been few and far between, especially in the more ‘natural’ habitats to be 
found in Central Europe. Exceptions, however, include Foerster’s study of social and

foraging behaviour in 1996, based at a number of the same sites as this study, and those of 
Kranz (1995) and Dulfer et al. (1998). 
 
The Třeboňsko BR & PLA represents an almost unique otter habitat, maintaining a large 
percentage of water surface (made up of many fish ponds (≤ 450 ha, av. 50 ha), channels, 
river stretches, and other wetland features) in a relatively small area, offering a large area 
of shallow riparian habitat, the otters’ favoured type of feeding site (Kruuk 1995). These 
water bodies offer very high food availability all over the year, as they are managed in 
such a way as to keep many ponds open during winter and are re-stocked regularly (Dulfer 
et al. 1998, Roche 2001). As a reaction to this extreme habitat richness, otters in the 
Reserve show characteristics similar to those in coastal habitat (Roche 2001). This includes 
patchy habitat use (Kranz 1995, Foerster 1996, Roche 2004c), at least temporal group 
ranges (Foerster 1996, Dulfer et al. 1998, Kučerová & Roche 1999, Roche 2001), and a 
high population density (Šimek & Springar 1996, Roche & Roche 2004, Zemanová 2006). 
Observation of wild otters in the Reserve, therefore, should be easier than in most other 
freshwater habitats. Despite this, the actual number of positive observations made over the 
two years of this study was surprisingly low. 
 
Due to the low observation success rate, the data did not allow statistical significance to be 
calculated. All presumptions and results presented, therefore, should be treated with 
caution. On the other hand, assuming that all otters present at a site were recorded, ‘low 
observation success’ may, in fact, imply a relatively low otter presence at the sites, and this 
need not necessarily mean bad news. Foerster (1996) observed high otter concentrations at 
the best feeding sites due to extreme weather conditions. The winters during this study 
were relatively mild, confirming the assumption that (Foerster 1996, Roche 2001, Roche 
2004c) such aggregations were exceptional, and not the norm, i.e. that ‘normal’ otter 
density would be much lower. Additionally, habitat conditions outside the Třeboňsko BR 
& PLA have shown signs of improvement, and the Czech otter population has increased its 
distribution range considerably in recent years (Reuther et al. 2002, Reuther 1995, Roche 
et al. 2004). It is feasible, therefore, that population pressure has reduced around the core 
zone of the Reserve, and that otter numbers may even have reduced. Such a comparatively 
low otter presence at small fish ponds could mean that the damages being claimed by the 
owners of such ponds may be overestimated.

In this study, 16% of all observation sessions were successful, with at least one otter seen 
during those sessions. This relates to one otter per approx. 2.5 observation nights. The 
observation success of Foerster (1996) was higher, with one otter per approx. 1.6 
observation nights in spring, summer and autumn, and one otter per 0.75 observation 
nights in winter. Therefore, though the number of successful observations was lower, the 
pattern was the same, i.e. highest observation success rate in winter. The lower observation 
success of this study could be due to two factors: firstly, Foerster’s study was conducted 
primarily on river sites (two of them being identical with the river sites used in this study) 
and on sites on large ponds, which are known to show higher otter activity than the small 
ponds used in this study (see Roche & Roche 2004). Secondly, as previously mentioned, 
Foerster’s observations took place in winter and under unusually severe conditions, with 
most of the ponds and parts of the river being frozen and otters concentrating at the 
remaining feeding patches. This enabled the study of social patterns and foraging 
behaviour under extreme conditions, yet should not necessarily be considered as ‘normal’ 
for the Reserve’s otter population.  
 
As mentioned earlier, an important factor negatively influencing observation success in 
freshwater habitats is dense bankside vegetation, especially as otters seem to prefer 
foraging at secluded sites with overhanging tree branches and dense bankside vegetation 
(Fumagalli et al. 1995, Chanin 2003b), presumably due to the cover it provides. In this 
study, periods with ‘unknown otter activity’ (otter presumed to be present through calling 
or behaviour, but unseen) sometimes made up a large part of the overall ‘known otter 
presence’ (see Figs. 7 and 8). At the river sites, these ‘unknown’ periods were mostly due 
to dense bankside vegetation and, at the Little lagoon, also due to the system of ditches and 
oxbows surrounding the site, which allowed the otters to stay near the lagoon, but not to be 
seen. At the pond sites, reed beds surrounding the ponds mostly caused ‘unknown’ periods. 
The pond in Novosedly has a much broader reed bed than the Ortvínovice pond, which is 
reflected by the fact that, in Novosedly, unknown activity made up 84% of overall otter 
presence, whereas it was only 33% in Ortvínovice (Fig. 7). The important role of bankside 
vegetation is further confirmed by the fact that, in winter, when vegetation cover is at its 
lowest, ‘unknown’ activity made up only 34% of overall otter presence, whereas it was 
around 80% in the other seasons (Fig. 8).

4.2. 
Spatial and seasonal trends in otter presence 
 
There were considerable differences in the usage of the sites by otters. As expected, and as 
found by Foerster (1996), observation success was highest at the Small lagoon, where it 
was, on average, two times higher than at the other sites, and where 45% of all observed 
otters were seen. Since 1995, when the study of Foerster (1996) took place, water depth at 
this site has decreased due to sand deposition (upstream erosion and deposition after heavy 
flooding in 2002), nevertheless the site remains one of the richest ‘natural’ hunting patches 
in the Reserve. Foerster observed that the Small lagoon was intensively used in winter but, 
during the rest of the year, the observation sites were used more evenly. 
 
The other river site, Rozvodí, had been a similarly rich feeding site for otters around 1995. 
However, observation success was low there in this study, and the duration of single otter 
presence was lowest at this site. There could be two reasons for these results, both 
probably playing a role: firstly, this site was much more affected by sand deposition than 
the Small lagoon, following the floods of 2002, as the weirs at this site slowed the fast cur-
rent which would normally carry at least part of the sand away. Though much of this sand 
was later dredged and removed, the floods removed a large proportion of the riparian 
vegetation, leaving the banks exposed. Due to this lack of cover, it is possible that otters 
were avoiding the site. Secondly, in the autumn of 2002, a large reconstruction of the weirs 
was started, causing noise and disturbance from construction machinery and people 
working; even in the night a security guard was present. Otters are highly adaptable 
animals, and can acclimatise to a low level of disturbance (Durbin 1998, Chanin 2003a, 
Roche et al. 2004), some even living in or near towns (Kranz 2000). Otter activity was still 
recorded at this site, but to a lesser extent than might have been expected prior to the floods 
and reconstruction work. 
 
At both river sites, fresh spraints and tracks were regularly found suggesting, therefore, the 
effects of deposition, in combination with milder winters, is likely to have resulted in a 
reduction in the number of individual otters using the sites, rather than reduced use of the 
sites by otters overall, i.e. temporal patterns of use are little changed, only the number of 
individuals using a site. Indeed, the results of this study suggest that there was a striking 
reduction in the number of otters using the sites, with two otters at one time seen only once 
during the two years of observations, Foerster (1996) recording encounters of presumably

The owner of the pond in Ortvínovice reported that the otter at his pond was a young

In behavioural studies, and particularly those undertaken under controlled laboratory con-
ditions, conditions are usually assumed to be equal. In the wild, however, most data 
(including otter presence and different activity categories) has to be treated separately for 
every site. The river sites differ regarding sand deposition (depth and living space for fish), 
surroundings and level of human disturbance. Further, the ponds also show differing sur-
roundings and habitat conditions. This will all potentially result, therefore, in different otter 
behaviour at each site, and over time. Furthermore, individual otters may show significant 
variation in their behaviour and foraging preferences (Kruuk and Moorhouse 1991, 
Knollseisen 1995). 
 
Seasonal differences in observation success were low, with observations in summer and 
autumn being only slightly more successful than in winter and spring. Also the numbers of 
otters observed in the different seasons were very similar. This confirms the assumption 
that the high observation success of Foerster (1996) in winter was due to the extremely low 
temperatures and that the patterns of spatial usage of otters are apt to change only in 
extreme conditions. In normal environmental conditions, territories and the usage of 
different foraging sites are expected to stay unchanged. 
 
Considerable seasonal differences were found both in the duration of single otter presence 
(Fig. 14) and in otter presence as a percentage of observation time (Fig. 15). Both figures 
show a high duration of otter presence in winter, which was a trend also found by Foerster 
(1996). Therefore, though the number of otters (measured as the number of successful

temperatures, the duration of single otter presence did. Otters rely on their thick fur to 
prevent heat loss during foraging bouts (Kruuk 1995, Kruuk & Carss 1996). In winter, and 
when much time is spent underwater, however, the otter must face the possibility of not 
only losing too much body heat but possibly overheating as well. The otter combats this by 
a) foraging in those places where food is most likely to be caught (see Roche 2001, Roche

to return to normal (see Kruuk 1995). It is this latter point that, in large part, dictates how 
long the otter spends at a site before or after foraging.  
 
The duration of single otter presence, and also otter presence as a percentage of observa-
tion time, was higher at the small ponds than at the river sites. Previous studies have shown 
that particularly good feeding sites in the Reserve, such as large ponds and certain river 
sites are shared by more than one otter, resulting in overlapping home ranges. Small ponds 
tend to be found within the exclusive range of single otters (Roche 2001, Roche & Roche 
2004, Roche 2004c). As otters actively avoid potentially aggressive encounters outside of 
the mating season, otters tend to use such ‘communal’ sites for as little time as possible. 
Where sites are within an otters’ exclusive range, there is far less pressure to reduce 
foraging time, or to leave the site once foraging has finished. 
 
In all seasons, and at all sites except Rozvodí, observation success was higher during 
evening observations (Figs. 13 and 16). This supports the assumption that otters in the 
Třeboňsko BR & PLA follow the ‘typical’ activity pattern for freshwater otters, with one 
activity peak at the beginning of the night and a second, smaller, peak in the morning (e.g. 
Green et al. 1984, Mason & Macdonald 1986, Kučerová & Roche 1999). The lower 
evening observation success at Rozvodí may have been caused by the weir reconstruction 
work at this site, and the presence of canoeists, anglers, and other visitors, often present 
until dark. Despite this, the difference in observation success was rather small, and may 
have been down to chance.

4.3. 
Behaviour categories 
 
4.3.1. Foraging behaviour and hunting success 
 
As expected, foraging behaviour was common, being one of the two most frequently 
observed activities, the other being exploratory behaviour. Arriving otters usually started 
hunting immediately, though sometimes following a period of exploratory behaviour; a 
behaviour pattern also observed by Foerster (1996). Relatively small areas were often 
repeatedly dived and searched before moving on, indicating that patch fishing, typically 
found with coastal otters, is also an important foraging pattern for otters hunting in the 
study area. Patch fishing has been observed before in this area (Kranz 1995, Foerster 1996, 
Roche pers. comm.). 
 
Foraging time as a percentage of total otter time was lowest at Rozvodí, which again, may 
have been due to the human disturbance. However, it is possible that food availability was 
lower at this site since 1995, following sand deposition after flooding. Though no fish sur-
veys were undertaken during this study, anecdotal evidence suggests that fish density was 
indeed lower, as water depth was reduced. The highest percentage of foraging behaviour 
was found in Ortvínovice, due to the three observations of the otter(s) described in chapter 
3.1.1. (Otter presence at the sites). Small ponds, therefore, seem to be important to female 
otters as safe, defendable, rich feeding patches. The Small lagoon was the only observation 
site where foraging behaviour made up to 100% of otter presence, thus confirming the 
importance of the lagoon as a feeding site.  
 
Regarding seasonal differences, the highest percentage of foraging behaviour was observed 
in autumn. The reasons are two-fold; firstly, there is the high availability of millions of 
small fish in autumn, which are released from the fish ponds through the nets during the 
autumn pond harvesting, and thus increase fish density and biomass in the adjacent river 
stretches and narrow channels to extremely high values (Dulfer et al. 1998). As an oppor-
tunistic predator, the otter tends to switch to the prey types most available and most easily 
caught (Roche 2001, Roche 2004c). Whilst there are large amounts of small fish, the otter 
has to spend more time foraging to reach the required prey biomass. Secondly, winter is a 
time of great stress for the otter, with high energy demands and lowered food availability. 
During autumn, therefore, the otter spends a lot of time building up fat reserves.

Foerster (1996) found foraging time in females with young ranged between 58–63%, with 
males foraging only 27% of the time. Nolet & Kruuk (1989), however, studying coastal 
otters in Scotland, found an average foraging time for all otters ranging between 40–53% 
of active time. The average percentage of foraging time in this study, combined for all sites 
and seasons, was 40%, lying at the low end of the intervals mentioned in the Scottish 
study. Despite the harder winters, therefore, foraging would appear to be more successful 
in the Reserve, even with a high density of competing otters present, thereby demonstrat-
ing the extreme prey richness available in the Reserve. 
 
The duration of hunting bouts was relatively short, with the maximum duration being 15 
minutes and the average 2–3 minutes. Hunting bouts typically consisted of only 2 dives on 
average. The results of Foerster (1996) averaged 9–13 minutes in winter and 4 minutes in 
the other seasons, with the longest hunting bout observed (in winter) lasting more than one 
hour. Again, the numbers in winter could be explained by the severe conditions during the 
time of Foerster’s study, and the higher energy demands of the animals.  
 
It could be a matter of discussion whether hunting bouts are a proper base unit describing 
hunting behaviour in freshwater habitat. At small fishponds or other small, sharply 
delineated foraging patches, the definition of a hunting bout is probably the same from the 
point of view of the observer and the otter. The otter has to travel to the pond (often using 
overland tracks, Kranz 1995), uses it for foraging, eats the prey, spends a period recovering 
(resting, grooming, etc.), and then goes away or starts another behaviour (see Roche 
2004c), with “foraging” and “not foraging” being exactly separated and possible to be 
distinguished by the observer. In riverine habitat, or at large ponds, where foraging patches 
are present but not sharply confined, the observer cannot always confirm whether an 
‘arriving’ otter (first observed) is starting its hunting bout or not, or whether a leaving otter 
continues hunting elsewhere or not, as otters can combine patch fishing with fishing “on 
the move”. On the other hand, single dives are not independent of the preceding and 
following dives. Otters have to optimalise their hunting efforts and, taking into account the 
success of the preceding dives, they have to decide whether to stay at one foraging site or 
whether to go on to another. Thus, the definition and use of the concept ‘hunting bout’ 
helps finding relations and patterns in otter behaviour which otherwise may be overlooked, 
but it has to be treated with caution, bearing in mind the constrictions mentioned above.

The hunting technique of the otters was based mainly on normal diving, ‘porpoise diving’ 
being encountered only twice. ‘Porpoise diving’ (described by Erlinge 1968, Watson 1978, 
Conroy & Jenkins 1986, Kranz 1995) is an effective method of active prey pursuit, yet for 
the otter it might be easier to find slow moving, bottom-dwelling fish than to pursue active 
prey. Foerster (1996) found a high percentage of ‘porpoise dives’ in large ponds. As these 
ponds were not included in this study, a lower percentage of ‘porpoise diving’ was 
expected, which proved to be true.  
 
The longest underwater time observed was 74 seconds, being shorter than the maximum 
diving time seen by Foerster (1996), which was 105 seconds. However, the average dura-
tion of dives was 22–25 sec., longer than Foerster’s results (10–18 sec.). These results are 
similar to those observed in other studies (ranging around 22 sec., for a review see Kruuk 
1995). Foerster (1996) found successful dives lasting longer than unsuccessful ones, which 
was confirmed in this study (Fig. 20). This is unusual, as other studies showed underwater 
times of successful dives being shorter (Conroy & Jenkins 1986, Kruuk & Moorhouse 
1990, Kruuk et al. 1990, Watt 1993). However, these latter studies were conducted on the 
Scottish coast, where there seems to be an expected success rate in diving, mainly because 
of constantly rich feeding patches. Most of the feeding sites are situated at around 3 metres 
off the coast, and are situated in deep, rocky, sea-weed beds. This provides much cover for 
the bottom living, high energy, species the otter preys upon and, consequently, the otter 
must spend much time searching for its prey, often in fast moving, unpredictable currents 
and coloured water. Otters in freshwater habitats, however, tend to forage for lower energy 
prey and rarely encounter such severe conditions and, therefore, will usually give up their 
dive when they do not encounter any prey quickly.  
 
With only one exception, prey items caught were landed after catching in this study. Eating 
prey while floating on the waters surface is efficient where the energy costs for travelling 
to the bank would be disproportionately high due for example, to the relatively high 
distance to the shore in otters hunting in the sea (Nolet et al. 1993, Kruuk 1995). In the 
Třeboňsko BR & PLA otters consuming prey whilst floating on the water were observed 
mostly in large ponds where, again, the distance to the bank was relatively high (Kranz 
1995, Foerster 1996). Further, otters hunting in rivers seem to land prey in order to avoid 
the great energy demands of contending with the current while eating (Kruuk 1995).

Hunting success 
It has been a matter of discussion whether the percentage of successful dives represents an 
adequate measure of hunting success in otters (Kruuk et al. 1990, Ostfeld 1991, Kruuk 
1995). The amount of biomass, caught per unit time spent hunting, was proposed as an 
alternative. In this study, it was not possible to estimate the size, and therefore biomass, of 
captured prey. Under the assumption that otters in the study area choose the dominant fish 
species and size classes (Roche 1996), and that these do not change to any great extent 
through the year, single prey biomass should not vary to any great extent (although there 
may be changes due, for example, to the high availability of small fish released from ponds 
harvesting in autumn). Therefore, the number of successful dives per unit time spent 
hunting may serve as a comparable measure (Foerster 1996). Nevertheless, the percentage 
of successful dives still remains a useful measure of foraging success, at least in freshwater 
habitat, which is confirmed by the results of this study, with very similar patterns found for 
both types of figures (Figs. 21 and 25/Figs. 22 and 26). Foerster (1996) also found the 
results obtained by both methods as comparable. 
 
From a total of 59 observed dives, 41% were successful. In freshwater habitat, this is a 
very high percentage, and is even higher than the figures found for coastal areas (e.g. 
Conroy & Jenkins 1986: 27.5%, Kruuk & Moorhouse 1990: 26.9%, Kruuk et al. 1990: 
31%, Watt 1993: 28.4%). Foerster (1996) found a success rate of 31% in the Třeboňsko 
BR & PLA. These numbers confirm the extremely high food availability in the Reserve, 
with the river sites proving to be especially profitable feeding patches. Such profitable sites 
are of vital importance during times of low general food availability in the study area, i.e. 
winter. The increase in hunting success shown in this study, compared with Foerster 
(1996), could be caused by the apparent decrease in numbers of otters using the sites, 
mentioned in chapter 4.1. (The method of direct observation), therefore increasing relative 
food availability for individual otters. 
 
The Small lagoon was expected to be the site with highest hunting success due to the high 
fish biomass previously observed at the site (Jurajda & Roche 1994 and 1995) and due to 
high otter presence. However, this hypothesis did not prove to be true as the highest 
hunting success was found at Rozvodí and at the pond in Novosedly, being confirmed by 
both methods of success measurement (percentage of successful dives and successful dives

unsuccessfully must decrease and the otter move on (see Roche 2004c).  
 
 
4.3.2. Grooming 
 
Grooming mostly occurred after feeding or after (unsuccessful) dives, which was the ex-
pected pattern. The average percentage of grooming in total otter time was 16%, which is 
similar to other studies on wild otters. Nolet & Kruuk (1989) reported that otters at the 
coast spent on average 13–28% of their time grooming. Otters in freshwater habitat need

Discussion 
 
less time for grooming, as they do not have to wash salt water out of their fur (Fairley 
1972) that would otherwise interfere with thermoregulation. 
 
Most otters spent very short time periods with grooming, afterwards continuing in foraging 
or travelling away. Two of the otters were seen grooming repeatedly and for longer time 
periods, and both of these otters were observed at the ponds. Supposing the small ponds 
being part of only one otter territory (as they are not large enough to provide food for two 
or more otters), they form the otter’s living space, where the animal can spend a large 
amount of its time. On the contrary, the rich river sites would serve mainly as foraging 
sites, where contact with other otters has to be avoided by leaving soon after hunting, thus 
explaining the short grooming periods.  
 
 
4.3.3. Exploratory behaviour 
 
Exploratory behaviour made up a large percentage of total otter time, ranging around 50%. 
The percentage of exploratory behaviour at the river sites was found to be far greater even 
than foraging time. This could be explained by the fact that the river sites are rich in prey 
abundance and are visited by many foraging otters, which leave tracks, spraints (faeces) 
and scent marks. For an arriving otter, it is important to find out whether another otter has 
used the site recently (i.e. has food availability been lowered recently (see Kruuk 1995 for 
more on the ‘Resource Dispersion Hypothesis’)), and “who this otter was” (i.e. a dominant 
male or perhaps an available female). Thus, the frequently used feeding sites could also 
serve as “information centres”, providing information on other otters using the site. Con-
stant vigilance whilst at the site, and exploratory behaviour before foraging, therefore, are 
important survival techniques, whereby the otter is able to a) avoid potential aggressive 
encounters, b) meet fertile females, and c) avoid foraging in sites with potentially lowered 
food availability. 
 
Regarding seasonal differences, most exploratory behaviour was found in spring, where it 
averaged almost 90% of observed otter time. This could be due to possible re-establish-
ment of home range boundaries, which may have shifted or changed during winter when 
lowered food availability and high energy costs may have resulted in a lowering of the 
‘normal’ social restrictions (Foerster 1996, Roche 2001, Roche 2004c). During winter,

born around summer/autumn (Šimek & Springar 1996). This is a period of great stress for 
the female as, not only must she expend much energy feeding herself and the young, she 
must also protect those young from potential predators. Whilst the young are learning to 
feed and play, therefore, she must be extra vigilant. 
 
 
4.3.4. Other behaviour 
 
Except for one short observation with two otters present at the same time (but not showing 
any interaction), all of the observations were made on single otters, alone at the observa-
tion site. Foerster (1996) observed 21 encounters of presumably unrelated otters, and 28 
pairs or family groups. Most of these observations, however, took place in winter, with the 
severe weather conditions causing a breaking-down of ‘normal’ activity and avoidance 
patterns. However, an encounter with a single otter or only one family-group/pair at any 
one time might be assumed to be more ‘normal’ during less stressful years. 
 
 Although fresh spraints were found at all sites during the study, no otter was observed 
whilst sprainting. Foerster (1996) found almost 63% of all observed sprainting occurring 
before, during, or after hunting bouts, yet only around 6–8% of all otters observed in her 
study were seen sprainting. With the relatively low number of sightings in this study, such 
a percentage would equate to an expected 1–2 otters observed sprainting. This is a very 
low number and lowered the chances of observing such behaviour considerably.
 
46


Conclusions 
5. 
Conclusions 
 
Direct observation of wild otters in a rich freshwater habitat proved to be providing 
valuable information on various aspects of otter ecology, despite the fact that observation 
success was relatively low. During 155 observation sessions, 29 otters were observed, 
showing the activity pattern typical for otters in freshwater habitat, with the main activity 
peak occuring at dusk and another one, though smaller, at dawn. 
 
The otter population in the rich habitat of the Třeboňsko BR & PLA was confirmed to 
show some characteristics similar to those in coastal habitat, e.g. patch fishing. However, 
the patterns of spatial usage in the Reserve are apt to change according to habitat condi-
tions, which is a remarkable divergence from the normal Mustelid spacing pattern. During 
severe winter conditions, patch fishing seems to be important, although the feeding patches 
are not as constant as in coastal habitat and seem to occur mostly in winter. The richest 
feeding patches at some river sites serve as some kind of ‘communal’ foraging sites, being 
used by a large number for otters, mainly for hunting and as ‘information centres’. The 
duration of otter presence is high in winter, especially at these foraging sites. 
 
Compared to other freshwater studies and even to studies at the sea, hunting success of the 
otters in the Reserve proved to be very high, thus confirming the importance of the Reserve 
as an extremely rich otter habitat. The concept of measuring hunting success as the 
percentage of successful dives showed to be comparable to hunting success measured as 
successful dives per unit time hunting, respectively biomass caught per unit time hunting, 
at least in freshwater habitat.  
 
Successful dives were found to last longer than unsuccessful dives, which is a pattern dif-
ferent from coastal otters, probably being due to lower energy prey found in freshwater 
habitats. Prey landing occurred regularly.  
 
Small fish ponds showed to be important for female otters as safe, defendable and rich 
feeding patches, and the otters’ preference for habitat providing secluded foraging places 
with dense bankside vegetation was confirmed.  
 
47


Conclusions 
Most exploratory behaviour was found in spring, being contributed to the re-establishment 
of home range boundaries and the vigilance of females protecting their young from poten-
tial predators. The highest percentage of foraging behaviour was found in autumn, due to 
the extremely high availability of small fish released from pond harvesting and to the otters 
creating fat reserves for winter. Percentages of grooming time were found to be compa-
rable to results obtained from other studies on wild ottes.  
 
The method of direct observation of wild otters in freshwater habitat proved to be very 
time-consuming and difficult, yet important data on the otters’ ecology and behaviour were 
obtained, thus confirming the importance of direct observation in ethological research.
