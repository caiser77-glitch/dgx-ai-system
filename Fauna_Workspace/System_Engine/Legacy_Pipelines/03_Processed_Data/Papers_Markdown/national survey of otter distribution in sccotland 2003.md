Background
Systematic searches for the signs of otters were first conducted as a structured series of national surveys in
the late 1970s to inform the statutory conservation organisations about the relative distribution and status of
the species in Scotland, England and Wales.
Survey data up to 1994 were collected by the Vincent Wildlife Trust (VWT) and made available to 
the Scottish Natural Heritage in 2002. The three VWT surveys were undertaken in 1977–79, 1984–85
and 1991–94. From 2003 onwards, the data have been collected by SNH, incorporating site condition
monitoring on 44 Special Areas of Conservation (SAC) and one Special Site of Scientific Interest for otters.
The data comprise presence/absence information at sample sites in both freshwater and coastal localities
across the country. At each SAC an overall assessment was made with respect to whether the site was in a
favourable condition for otters. It was not intended that the survey should represent an exhaustive search for
all signs of otter activity and every possible holt site, but provide an overview of the relative value of the
habitat provided by the SAC, in the context of the other habitats nearby or other parts of a watercourse,
and the apparent level of use of these areas by otters. An assessment was also made of the relative
importance of the various resting sites found. The report provides an individual assessment of the condition
of each of these sites.
Main findings
l
A total of 1376 survey sites within 574 different 10km squares were surveyed over the two year period
2003–04. 1267 sites (92.08%) were recorded as positive.
l
288 sites within 58 10km squares were visited in areas of higher sampling intensity known as the 
Focus Zone. These were revisits to a series of 10km squares where the previous national otter surveys
had recorded less than 50% of the sites positive for otters in those squares. On average five sites were
visited in each 10km square that comprised the Focus Zone. All 58 10km squares within the zone were
found to be positive for otters with 82.63% of the sites positive.
l
The survey revealed otter signs within the large urban areas, such as Glasgow, Edinburgh and Dundee.
Here it would appear that the otter’s return reflects the improvement in water quality and the consequent
increase in fish stocks in urban rivers.

Summary


l
Water quality improvement and improved fish stocks are thought to have assisted the continued recovery
of the otter, although there are still concerns about acidification within some watercourses.
l
Road traffic accidents remain a substantial cause of non-natural otter mortality. Accidental drowning in
lobster creels or crab pots around coastal and island inshore waters is another concern but the scale of
the problem and subsequent effect on otter populations have not yet been quantified.
l
All except one of the SACs where otters are listed as part of the qualifying interest of the site, were
confirmed to support otters and to be in favourable condition with respect to them. However, some of
the smaller sites would not be able to sustain the presence of otters in isolation from adjacent river
systems and coastal habitats outside of the SAC boundaries.
l
The fourth national survey of the otter in Scotland has confirmed that the species has continued to
increase its population and range throughout the area. It can now be considered ubiquitous, occupying
sites in urban areas and in highly disturbed waterways where it seems unaffected by human activity.
l
It is recommended that future monitoring includes all sites identified in the 2003–04 survey with a survey
interval of once every six years for SACs and once every 12 years for all other sites.

INTRODUCTION
The UK Biodiversity Action Plan (UKBAP, 1995) and the EC Habitats Directive (1992) place obligations on
the UK Government to undertake surveillance of Eurasian otters (Lutra lutra).
The last systematic survey for the distribution and status of the otter in Scotland was conducted by the Vincent
Wildlife Trust 1991–94 (Green & Green, 1997). This was the third in the series of national surveys that
began in 1978 (repeated at a seven year interval). Similar surveys were simultaneously conducted in both
England and Wales. Collectively these surveys have documented the recovery of the species in Britain.
In Scotland the surveys clearly demonstrated that the species had strongholds in the Highlands, Hebrides
and Shetland, but had undergone a decline in the 1970s in the Central Belt and parts of the South East 
(but not to the same degree as in England or Wales). The 1991–94 survey indicated that a recovery was
well underway in many of these areas, but a few gaps in the distribution remained (Green & Green, 1997). 
In the 25 years since the national surveys started there have been a number of changes which could affect
the otter in Scotland. When the first survey started the otter was not protected and hunting with otter hounds
or trapping and killing was still legal. Scottish otters were first protected by inclusion on Schedule 5 of the
Wildlife and Countryside Act 1981 (implemented in Scotland in September 1982). Today persecution is
now a less serious threat than accidental deaths on roads or drowning in fishing gear (such as lobster creels
and crab pots), both of which are difficult to quantify but occur sufficiently frequently to cause concern.
Pollution of freshwaters was already declining when the first otter survey of Scotland was published 
(Green & Green, 1980). At that time sewage and industrial effluents were the most frequently cited sources
of pollution in the river classification scheme designed by the River Purification Boards (RPBs). In some places
these sources of pollution were sufficient to cause rivers to be classed as grossly polluted and devoid of fish
life. Through the 1980s and 1990s the powers and duties of the RPBs were strengthened by UK and
European legislation so that water quality improvement could be enforced through prosecution of polluters.
More stringent controls on the use, handling and disposal of agricultural and industrial chemicals have
reduced serious chemical pollution incidents. Diffuse pollution however is a growing area of concern as it
includes many types of polluting inputs and is not easily controlled. This is because it is derived from run-off
from industrial, urban and agricultural areas rather than from point sources.
Under the provisions of the Environment Act 1995 the River Purification Boards have been superseded by
the Scottish Environment Protection Agency (SEPA). Since its inception in 1996 SEPA has built up an
extensive monitoring network designed to represent the quality of all significant waters. Thus river lengths are
assigned the quality of a downstream monitoring point. The total length of rivers under assessment in
Scotland is 24,404km. In 2003, about 800km of these rivers were considered to be of poor quality 
or seriously polluted, with a further 2,400km of only ‘fair’ quality. In 2000, based on the 1999 results, 
SEPA set new goals, to be achieved by 2006. This target took account of SEPA’s planned activities and
Scottish Water’s planned investment programme to upgrade unsatisfactory sewage treatment works, and
sewage collection systems. The 2006 goal is to decrease the length of rivers classed as poor or seriously
polluted by a further 351km (see http://www.sepa.org.uk/publications/corporateplan).
Acidification of rivers and lochs has been implicated in the loss of fish, amphibians and aquatic invertebrate
fauna. Green & Green (1997) highlighted this as a worsening problem in south west Scotland and parts of
the Highlands with potentially severe consequences for otters. It is therefore important that any new survey

takes account of these concerns and collects information on the persistence of otters within a number of 10km
squares where threats exist that may present a risk to the local otter population. These include a high number

The results of the 1991–94 survey, coupled with anecdotal evidence, indicate that over the vast majority of
Scotland, otters are now ubiquitous. It is considered that future survey and monitoring effort need not be at
the same level as previous surveys, for sufficiently meaningful information on population trends to be
provided. However a focus of any new survey should be on the designated Special Areas of Conservation
(SACs), for which otters are cited as a ‘qualifying interest’ (also known as Natura 2000 sites). Site condition
monitoring of the various SACs is required on a six yearly cycle. To this end new data are needed on the
current distribution and relative abundance of the otter within these sites in order to assess whether the site
is in favourable condition for the species. The information might also be used to produce better management
guidelines for enhancing less optimal sites to facilitate the further expansion of the otter populations within
these areas.

AIMS AND OBJECTIVES
The 2003–04 survey was structured differently from previous surveys with an emphasis on the monitoring of
the SACs/SSSIs where otters had been identified as a qualifying feature, combined with wider countryside
fieldwork that could provide good geographic coverage of the species range.
The overall aim of this project was to replicate as closely as possible the previous surveys while reducing
the level of sampling over much of the country where otters are ubiquitous.
The survey had the following objectives:
l
to undertake essential site condition monitoring on all SAC and SSSIs where otters were identified as 
a qualifying feature;
l
to assess whether otters were continuing to recover in places with low levels of activity in 1991–94;
l
to assess how well otter populations were faring in areas of particular risk (such as areas with high
acidity levels and high otter mortality due to road deaths);
l
to assess trends in otter populations in the remainder of Scotland.
The following two parameters of otter activity were used to address these objectives.
An index of otter occupancy in each 10km square visited, as measured by the percentage of positive sites
in each square.
An index of otter activity, as measured by the mean number of otter signs per positive sites.

METHODS
3.1
Designing the survey: the power to detect changes in otter populations
Repeated national otter surveys such as this one are designed to provide information on changes in the
distribution and relative abundance of otters in relation to the baseline. Population trends can then be
identified and information provided on increases, declines or population stability.
The three previous surveys for Scotland were conducted at the 10km square level. Coverage was most
extensive in 1977–79 and 1991–94 when nearly every 10km square in Scotland was visited. Within each
10km square, around five or six sites were visited, each separated by at least 5km of connecting waterway.
These systematic surveys are best regarded as providing distribution information at the 10km square level.
The standard methodology of ecology and experimental science in general, is based on the proposition that
there is no difference between samples in some parameter of interest. This is stated in terms of a null
hypothesis, H0, that there is no statistical difference between two independent data sets. The null hypothesis
is tested against the observed data and we obtain a probability, p, the value for the likelihood of the
observed outcome if the null hypothesis were true. As probability is measured on a continuous scale between
0 and 1 it is necessary to decide at what point between 0 and 1 the observed results are sufficiently unlikely
for the null hypothesis to be rejected (Ellison, 1996). This is usually taken at p<0.05 (ie there is a greater
than 95% chance that the null hypothesis is incorrect).
Statistical power is the likelihood that an analysis will detect a trend or a difference when there really is one.
Typically we specify a power of 0.8 or higher as acceptable. Power is increased by greater sampling effort.
When the effect we are looking for is small, high sampling effort may be needed (Macdonald et al., 1998).
For this otter survey the basic design can be envisaged as being specified by the number, N, of 10km squares
to be visited and the number, n, of sites to be visited in each square. To determine the power of the sampling
design we must begin by determining the sampling distribution of observations under the null hypothesis of
no change (Brewer et al., unpublished). Since previous otter surveys in Scotland were repeated visits to a
series of sites first visited in the 1977–79 baseline survey, it is possible to derive a probability of detecting
the presence of otter activity at sites where otters were known to be present in the 10km square. In the scoping
study for the present survey, Brewer et al., (op.cit.) estimated p by assuming all 10km squares occupied
during the 1977–79 survey will also be occupied during the 1991–94 survey. From the estimate of p it
was possible to calculate the probability of a false negative for each square as q = (1– p)n and hence derive
the probability for each number of false negatives. They examined a number of sites within each 10km
square and found that the probability of detecting otter spraint in a given site if the square as a whole was
occupied was 98% (ie 98% of survey sites would contain otter sign if the square they were in was occupied).
If two sites are surveyed in these 10km squares, there was a 1% chance of failing to detect spraints at both
sites (ie if the number of sites is doubled, the probability of detecting no spraints in either site is halved).
3.2
Site selection for a stratified survey
The scoping study by Brewer et al., (op.cit.) identified a number of survey strata that would provide information
on how otters are faring in particular areas. They recommended that the Focus Zone squares had a sampling
effort of five sites in all squares in the group. For all SACs and other survey strata outside of the Focus Zone,
the sampling effort would involve two sites per square. In reality there was considerable overlap, with many
squares falling into more than one stratum. Given this level of duplication, there was considerable scope for

extending the geographical range and survey coverage outside of the initial sampling strata. An additional
sample of “wider countryside” 10km squares were selected across the non-target areas, by visual assessment
to ensure that coverage would be as even as possible in these areas.
The strata for survey included:
a)
the various SACs where otters are named as a feature of interest (see Table 1 and Map 1);
b)
remote island groups where declines may not be buffered by recolonisation from the mainland (Shetland,
Orkney, Outer Hebrides – see Maps 2 and 3);
c)
areas with high recorded road mortality (see Map 2);
d)
areas with high critical load exceedance for acidity (see Map 2);
e)
a small number of squares along the east coast where otter occupancy had been low in previous surveys
(see Map 2);
f)
a Focus Zone of around 60 10km squares, where the 1991–94 survey revealed less than 50%
occupancy of sites in each 10km square, with three or more sites surveyed in each square (see Map 3);
g)
an additional sample of squares from the wider countryside, identified by visual assessment (see Map 3).
Based on the recommendations of Brewer et al., (op.cit.) the overall level of sampling was determined as
being two sites in each of the 10km squares. The squares in the proposed Focus Zone (predominantly south-
east Scotland) were considered to require a greater sampling effort of around five sites/10km square,
(range: 3–7 sites/10km square).
The 2003–04 survey included nine of the southern Scotland SACs/SSSIs and 36 of the northern Scotland
SACs/SSSIs, (see Table 1). All the SACs/SSSIs to be surveyed fell within 513 10km squares. For each SAC
the number of survey sites was proportional to the number of different 10km squares that the SAC boundary
intersected. For instance, if the boundary fell across four adjacent squares, eight survey sites would have
been chosen. Where possible, sites that had previous data from Vincent Wildlife Trust surveys were selected.
However in many cases no previous VWT site occurred within the SAC boundary for particular squares,
requiring the selection of new sites. A total of 231 such new sites were required to fully assess the
compliment of SACs/SSSIs.
Sites for the Focus Zone were revisits to sites from the previous national otter surveys.
Fifty eight 10km squares comprising 288 sites were visited in the Focus Zone in 2003 (mostly in the south
east of Scotland). In 2004, a further five Focus Zone squares, comprising 24 sites, were included within the
survey. These were all located in the SNH Grampian Area.
Outside the Focus Zone and SAC boundaries, a stratified sample of sites was randomly selected in order 
to maximise the chances of detecting any atypical/local population trends (such as in areas of high
acidification, high road mortality or isolation among offshore islands).
Brewer et al., (unpublished) suggested a total of 459 10km survey squares and 1363 survey sites should
form the basis of the 2003–04 survey (ie approximately a third of the sites visited in the 1991–94 survey).
In total 1376 sites were examined for the presence of otters and other riparian mammal species across 
576 different 10km squares during the present survey. Maps 1–3 depict the distribution of the 10km squares
for the various sampling strata visited in this fourth national survey of Scotland.

3.3
Survey protocol
As a precursor to the fieldwork, all the sites to be visited were marked onto a series of maps to facilitate 
the logistics and practicalities of surveying. On a day to day basis a cluster of sites could be visited by each
of the survey team with an average of six sites per man day.
The survey protocol was to search a full 600m survey length for each site within the boundaries of each
SAC/SSSI and a minimum length of 100m for all other sites. If no spraints were found the search was
extended up to a maximum distance of 600m along the watercourse/coastline. This enabled spraint counts to
be calculated over a minimum distance of 100m for all sites, while presence/absence data would be directly
comparable with previous surveys. In the previous surveys, the protocol dictated that the search effort should
stop as soon as the first spraints were located. Consequently different distances were investigated for different
sites resulting in difficulties with the analysis of spraint count data from those surveys (Green & Green, 1997).
3.3.1
Field survey
At each site a fixed distance of 100m and up to 600m was searched, and spraints, otter resting sites and
other field signs counted. Field sign evidence of both mink and water voles was also recorded when encountered.
A standard form (see Appendix I) was used at each site to record information on habitat, disturbance and
factors that may have influenced survey results. A GPS was used to accurately record the location of all 
field signs and the position of any photographs taken. This information was then transferred to an 
Excel spreadsheet to facilitate analysis and GIS mapping.
For the SAC/SSSI survey sites, information was collected over the whole 600m survey length. These data were

3.3.3
Common Standards Monitoring
The concept of Common Standards Monitoring (CSM) has recently been introduced to provide consistent
and repeatable survey effort to determine population change among otters and other protected mammals,
where they are notified or qualifying interest features for SACs and SSSIs. CSM ensures that the various
SACs/SSSIs are monitored consistently across the UK.
The presence of otters can be determined by carrying out standard sign surveys. Chanin (2003) recommends
a monitoring protocol using natural and artificial sprainting sites that could provide population trend
information on individual sites. For the principally riverine SACs/SSSIs, Chanin (2003) recommends that
approximately 60 spot-check sites (usually a bridge and the adjacent banks within 50m) should be
monitored in each SAC for signs of otters in order to assess population trends. This report also recommends
that large SACs/SSSIs should be split into sub-catchments or sections and a total of 60 sites monitored in
each of these. This method is being trialled in England and Wales with a recommendation for annual
monitoring for the first five years to test the effectiveness of the method.
Monitoring otter populations in the Scottish SACs/SSSIs was based on a slightly modified version of the
method used in the national surveys and the recommendations of Brewer et al. The survey methodology is
as described in this report (see Appendix I for an example of the standardised recording sheet). For each
of the large riverine SACs in Scotland between 54 and 187 sites were visited across the catchments
involving 600m searches, spraint counts and counts of resting sites.
The five largest otter SACs in Scotland are probably capable of supporting theoretically self-contained viable
otter populations – individual areas of the Tay catchment, the Tweed, Dee and Spey courses, and the
Caithness and Sutherland Peatlands. The remaining otter SACs are much smaller in area and, while they
can support otters, they are unlikely to do so in isolation.

3.5
Site access
Paramount to the success of this project has been the co-operation and goodwill of the riparian landowners,
estate workers and farmers. For every SAC/SSSI access permission was agreed in advance of any field
survey work. Early liaison with the appropriate SNH Area officer facilitated access to the designated sites
and provided contacts for access permission. Previous experience with the various surveys in which the team
has been involved has shown that most landowners/farmers were willing to assist and appreciated
feedback from site visits made to their property. Over the entire survey, access was only refused at one site.
Here an alternative adjacent site was selected on a neighbour’s land in its place.
Some survey stretches by their nature were in remote locations. Under such circumstances appropriate health
and safety requirements (such as an appropriate lone working procedure, pair working, carrying suitable
safety equipment etc) were strictly adhered to.
3.6
Workplan and timing
Observations of otters on Shetland suggest that breeding is predominantly in the early summer to coincide
with the arrival of migratory fish in coastal waters (Kruuk, 1995). Elsewhere, however, there is a slight peak
of otter births in the UK between the months of January and March (Chanin, 1985; Kruuk, 1995), but
reproduction is remarkably un-seasonal and cubs can be born in any month. Otter field signs, such as
spraints can be found in every month of the year (Mason & Macdonald, 1987), therefore there was no
seasonal constraint on the work schedule, other than ones of practicality, safety and the traditional variability
of British weather.
All river systems in Scotland are prone to spate conditions after heavy rainfall, causing flood events, which
together with winter snowfall may limit access to field sites. Optimal periods for habitat surveying, holt
investigation and spraint finding were therefore considered to be during periods of reasonably stable
weather between the months April–October, although dense vegetation growth in river channels and on
banks in high summer and autumn made fieldwork difficult at some sites.
Where possible, revisits to previous national survey sites were carried out in the same season as previously
(ie within four weeks of original dates). This helped minimise any seasonal variations in sprainting behaviour
and sign detection rates and allowed for comparisons to be drawn between surveys.
Southern Scotland was surveyed during 2003 during the period June–November.
Northern Scotland and most of the islands were surveyed in 2004 during the period April–November.

and suggest that direct observation and holt counts were more reliable measures of otter activity, especially
in coastal waters such as around Shetland. Nevertheless, Green & Green (1997) and Strachan & Jefferies
(1996), have both demonstrated a link between otter distribution and the frequency of otter signs recorded
in those surveys. They used data sets from national surveys and found a statistically significant relationship
between the number of sites occupied in a geographic area and the number of spraints found at each site,
suggesting that otter populations had increased across the first three national surveys in both geographical
expansion in range and increased density.

4.2.1
Northern Isles
The island groups of Shetland and Orkney represent the northernmost region of Scotland, with the
archipelago of Shetland lying 177km north of the mainland, whereas Orkney lies only 10.5km offshore.
The Shetland land area comprises a total of 1,430km2 and the coastline extends to over 4,800km in total
length. The highly indented coastline, sea loch (voes) and abundant small offshore islands provide rich and
sheltered marine habitat for otters. Inland lochs, lochans and fast flowing coastal burns provide ample
freshwater, although productivity is low in such sites. Brown trout, eels, three-spined stickleback and
migratory salmonids are the commonest fish species present in these freshwaters. The inshore waters around
Shetland are very productive seasonally with a wide diversity of fish and crustacea available to otters during
the summer months. Although crofting is the main land-use in Shetland, salmon and shellfish farming, sea
fishing and the oil industry provide the main economic prosperity for the islands. Road improvement schemes
and new house building have increased over the last 20 years.
The 67 islands in Orkney cover an area of 976km2 with the largest islands low-lying, fertile and well settled.
Agriculture and fish-farming are important economies although the oil industry has expanded in recent years.
The improved network of roads (leading to a rise in otter road mortality), and accidental drowning of otters
in fishing gear, lobster creels and crab pots are important issues concerning otters in Orkney (Green &
Green, 1997).
During 2004 a total of 33 10km squares were surveyed for otters within the Northern Isles. 62/66 (93.9%)
sites were positive, with all the negative sites found in the Orkney Isles. When compared to previous years,
51 sites were common to all surveys (15 sites were new in order to improve coverage amongst the SACs).
50/51 (98.0%) sites were positive in 2004 compared to 96.1% positive in the1978–79 baseline but the
increase is not significant at the p<0.05 level. No sites were surveyed in 1985–86 and 96.1% sites were
again positive in 1991–94. This represents a stable population at or near carrying capacity. There has been
concern that the density of otters in the Orkney Isles is lower than that achieved in the Shetlands, and this
was reflected in the average spraint density recorded on the two island groups. The average number of
spraints recorded from positive sites in Orkney was 4.8, whereas the average number of spraints per positive
site in Shetland was 46.6. The reasons for this are unclear but it is possible that sources of freshwater for
coastal living otters in the Orkney Isles are limited due to the local geology.

given below.

4.2.2
Western Isles
The Western Isles comprise the 225km long archipelago of Lewis, Harris, the Uists, Benbecula, Eriskay, Barra
and numerous smaller islands/islets. Most of the area is low lying with mountainous terrain occurring only in
Harris, south-west Lewis and parts of South Uist. The western coasts of the islands are prone to Atlantic storms
and the whole archipelago has high rainfall. Long shell-sand beaches occur in the Uists, Barra and Harris as
machair habitat. Elsewhere, much of the islands are overlain by thick acid peat. Although river systems are
few and running water is largely confined to coastal burns, there are abundant freshwater lochs and lochans,
particularly in Benbecula and the Uists. The freshwater fish fauna is similar to that of the Highlands, with migratory
salmonids, brown trout and eels the only common species. Productivity of the burns and lochs is generally low
although the water quality is high. In a number of sea lochs, particularly on the more sheltered eastern coast
of the islands a number of salmon farms have been established. In recent years, road improvement schemes,
such as widening and straightening, have led to a rise in reported otter road mortality incidents (local SNH data).
During 2004 a total of 29 10km squares were surveyed for otters within the Western Isles. 60/60 (100%) sites
were positive. When compared to previous years 37 sites were common to all surveys (23 sites were new in
order to improve coverage of the SACs). 37/37 (100.0%) sites were positive in 2004 compared to 97.3% positive
in the1978–79 baseline but this increase is not significant at the p<0.05 level. No sites were surveyed in 1985–
86 and all sites were again positive in 1991–94. This represents a stable population at or near carrying capacity.

The North Highland Area comprises a large expanse of peatland and high mountainous ground. The coast

4.2.4
West Highland
The West Highland area comprises typically high mountainous ground and deep valleys with associated
sea or freshwater lochs as a dominating feature of the landscape. The area includes the Isle of Skye and
the Small Isles (Rum, Eigg, Muck and Canna). The coast line is typically exposed to Atlantic storms in winter
but has some sheltered sea lochs and associated bays. Typically the rivers flowing to the west are short,
spate prone and oligotrophic. As a result, this area has one of the most restricted fish faunas in Scotland
with only salmon, sea trout, brown trout, sticklebacks and eels being widespread or numerous. A few
freshwater lochs however, are noted for species such as arctic charr and river lamprey.
During 2004 a total of 54 10km squares were surveyed for otters within the West Highlands. 113/118 (95.8%)
sites were positive. When compared to previous years 80 sites were common to the 1978–79 baseline
survey (38 sites were new in order to improve coverage of the SACs). Seventy six of these sites (95.0%)
were positive in 2004 compared to 93.8% positive in the1978–79 baseline but this increase is not
significant at the p<0.05 level. Only five sites were surveyed in 1985–86 and a reduced sample of 62/66
(93.9%) sites were found positive in 1991–94. This represents a stable population at or near carrying capacity.
Eight SACs were assessed in the West Highlands.

In addition to searches along the woodland cover of the loch shore, surveys were also carried out on the
islands of Inchconnachan, Inchmoan, Inchmurrin, Creinch, Torrinch and Clairinch. In total 10 survey sites

Fresh otter spraint was found on all islands surveyed and in addition, single holts were discovered on five
of them. The nature of the habitat (rocky shoreline, exposed tree roots and undisturbed cover) provide otters
with a suitable refuge for natal holts. Otters with cubs were reported to frequent the islands although none
were recorded during the survey. The fish biomass of Loch Lomond is reported to be high with large
populations of pike, perch and introduced ruffe. The latter first appeared in the loch in 1982 and is now
the most commonly encountered species, an observation confirmed by its abundant presence in otter spraint
from the loch sites.
Easy access to the lochside woodlands by campers, walkers and anglers may cause local disturbance,
restricting the number of potential breeding sites in these areas. Signs of mink were located on Inchmoan
(fresh scats) and Creinch (older droppings).
The SAC was assessed as being in favourable condition for otters, with the undisturbed islands providing
ideal breeding habitat.

DISCUSSION
5.1
Interpretation of the results
A total of 1376 survey sites within 574 different 10km squares were surveyed over the two year period
2003–04. 1267 sites (92.08%) and 568 10km squares were recorded as positive. This compares to
national figures of 57% in 1977–79, 65% in 1984–85 and 88% in1991–94 and suggests a steady rise
in the overall population of otters in Scotland (Green & Green, 1980, 1987, 1997).
The present survey is the fourth in the series of national surveys for Scotland. Similar surveys have also been
carried out in England and Wales – taking place approximately every seven years. These surveys began in
the 1970s and noted the presence and absence of otters at selected sites based on field signs. The data
collected have provided information on changes in otter distribution and inferences have been drawn about
changes in population size (eg Strachan & Jefferies, 1996; Green & Green, 1997) but there are still no
robust data on population trends.
The main problem with monitoring otter populations is the lack of a clear relationship between the density
of signs and the density of otters. There is currently no way of reliably estimating otter density (Kruuk, 1999;
Chanin, 2003), although the use of DNA extracted from spraints may provide a solution to this in the future.
Coastal otter population density has been related to holt density in Shetland (Jim Conroy, pers. comm.) but
this relationship has not yet been reliably applied elsewhere. Around the coast of Scotland, sea lochs and
sheltered bays provide excellent habitat for otters and were generally found to support a higher density of
otters per linear length (of coastline) compared to the same linear length of freshwater loch or river system.
The interpretation of the results described in the preceding section must be made with care, as otters can
have extremely large home ranges – up to 40km or more for the males (Kruuk et al., 1993). An increase in
the number of positive sites may not necessarily equate to an increase in the number of otters present.
However, the sampling strategy was designed to detect trends in the relative abundance of otters within the
whole of Scotland and its constituent SNH areas. The analysis of the trends in each SNH Area (see Figure 1
and Table 5) confirmed highly significant increases in the number of sites with otter signs for East Highland,
Grampian, Argyll & Stirling, Strathclyde & Ayrshire, Tayside & Clackmannanshire and Forth & Borders when
compared to the previous surveys. The increase in the number of sites occupied was consistent with a
population of otters that had consolidated its range. In many of these areas the relative distribution of otters
had not changed when expressed as presence within 10km squares.
Figure 1 and Table 5 also clearly showed that the Northern Isles, Western Isles, North Highland, West
Highland and Dumfries & Galloway Areas had no significant increases or decreases, suggesting that the
otter population was stable and probably at or close to carrying capacity.
At each Special Area of Conservation it was not intended that the survey should represent an exhaustive
search for all signs of otter activity and locate every possible holt site, but should investigate the relative value
of the habitats at any one site, in the context of the other habitats nearby or other parts of a watercourse,
and the apparent level of use of these areas by otters. Only four of the 44 SACs failed to reveal the presence
of active holts or recently used resting sites. An assessment could be made of the relative importance of the
various resting sites found; for instance, the presence of above ground couches may allow the otter to be
readily displaced to alternative sites, whereas subterranean natal holts may be critically important for otters

All the other SACs were assessed as being in favourable condition for the species.
5.2
Focus Zone squares
In the 2003–04 survey, 63 10km squares and 312 sites were visited in the Focus Zone. Squares within this
zone comprised areas where few survey sites had any evidence of otters in the previous national otter
surveys. Evidence of otters was found in all 63 10km squares, with a total of 258 (82.69%) sites positive.
The results of the present survey indicate that habitat is not a major limiting factor as otters are now using
many areas previously thought severely sub-optimal. It would appear that even on catchments with highly
fragmented riparian habitat, otters find sufficient refuge sites to prevent exclusion. Otters are also using rivers
in urban areas in many parts of lowland Scotland, a trend noted in the 1991–94 survey report (Green &
Green, 1997).
The resurveys of all sites in the Focus Zone squares revealed otter signs within the large conurbations, such
as Glasgow and Edinburgh. Here it would appear that the otter’s return reflects the improvement in water
quality and the consequent increase in fish stocks in urban rivers. Elsewhere in the UK, otters in urban fringe
habitats have been observed with young cubs (Hamilton-James, 2002) demonstrating that they can and do
breed successfully in such environments. However this does not mean that such habitats can support a viable
breeding population. Breeding female otters may be strongly dependent on safe refuge sites free from
deliberate or accidental disturbance and with a readily available food supply. It is not known whether
breeding success in urban areas is comparable with that in more rural environments or if there is a greater
risk of road-kill on busier urban roads. It would therefore be unwise to be complacent about ‘urban otters’
and vigilance of the situation is recommended.

Habitat and food availability
If the definition of habitat is considered in its broadest sense, it provides otters with both food and a place
of refuge. Although in-stream habitat has a particular importance for fish, many fish species are dependent
on a variety of river habitats for different life stages such as spawning, fry, juveniles and adults. In addition
to these life stages some species such as salmon, sea trout and eel must be able to migrate between rivers
and oceans.

As with all species, the carrying capacity of any environment is largely a function of food availability and
the availability of lying up and/or breeding sites, although human persecution can be a major limiting factor
particularly with respect to predators. Other anthropomorphic effects such as road deaths and disturbance
may also be important. It is difficult to separate these factors but where refuge sites are plentiful then food
supply could be the limiting factor (Kruuk, 1995). As otters will take a wide variety of prey but are very much
fish specialists, the biomass and availability of fish species would be expected to be a key issue. The prey
requirement and food needs of otters were recently reviewed in Chanin (2003). The daily energetic
requirements for the otter suggest that between 1–1.5kg of fish must be consumed. This fact, combined with
the very large and variable home range size of otters, makes it difficult to determine the lowest level of fish
availability below which it ceases to be possible for an otter to exploit a river system. The abundance of
prey such as frogs is also important for otters, such that they may search and travel over small watercourses
of low productivity in order to locate spawning ponds in the Spring. Population estimates of up to 480 frogs
per hectare have been calculated from a spawning marsh in north-east Scotland where otters are known to
hunt (Weber, 1990).
The main factors which have historically limited fish populations were poor water quality and the historic
physical degradation of river habitat through engineering works. Today the water quality in Scottish rivers,
particularly in urban areas, such as the Forth or Clyde are generally much improved, mainly due to
investment in sewerage infrastructure, to reduce the impact of episodic pollution from storm-water run-off, and
in sewage works to improve the quality of the final effluent. There is, however, still significant room for
improvement on many rivers. In addition, the impact of agricultural pollution from farm wastes, artificial
fertilisers and other agri-chemicals is now a growing focus of concern for both the statutory agencies and
the agricultural sector. The insidious impact of chronic diffuse pollution (as agricultural run-off) on the quality
of our river environments is a very difficult problem to solve.
The report on the National Water Quality Classification for 2003 by the Scottish Environment Protection
Agency (see http://www.sepa.org.uk/data/classification/river.htm), stated that good progress had been
made towards reducing the length of the poorer quality Class C and D watercourses. Out of some
25,436km of watercourse under assessment, 6,815km (26.8%) was classified as A1 – Excellent, and
9,540km (37.5%) as A2 – Good. 2,374km (9.3%) was classified as B – Fair, and 751km (3%) was Class
C – Poor. Only 52km (0.2%) was recorded as Class D – seriously polluted, while a further 5,903km (23.2%)
went unclassified. Much of the net reduction in Class C and D watercourses in the period 2002–03,
compared to previous assessments, took place in south-east Scotland, particularly Fife and the Lothians. It is
interesting to note that it was the south-east of Scotland where the expansion of otters has been most marked
compared to previous surveys.

5.4
Otter breeding requirements
The breeding requirements of otters are reviewed in Liles (2003). The term breeding site is used to describe
an area of land that contains one or more den sites, security from disturbance, play areas for cubs, no risk
of flooding and access to a good food supply. This is distinct from the term natal holt, which describes a
small space occupied by the female when she gives birth and where the cubs stay for up to three months.
Due to the secretive nature of the female when cub rearing, natal holts can be difficult to find.
In terms of conservation, the identification and protection of breeding sites should be regarded as a priority.
Natal holts are obviously important but their existence and security will often depend on the integrity of the
wider breeding site in which they are found (Liles 2003).
One of the main aims for otter conservation in SACs, is to ensure that otter populations are maintained at
favourable conservation status. For this to be achieved, suitable breeding sites must be available throughout
the associated river catchments, lochs, coastline or islands. Only the four large river catchments of the Spey,
Dee, Tay and Tweed, together with the extensive area of the Caithness & Sutherland Peatlands, were considered
as probably capable of supporting theoretically self-contained viable otter populations. The remaining SACs
where otters are a feature of interest were much smaller in area. Thus, they are regardeded as sites that
could support otters in association with the wider countryside, but not in isolation.
Liles (2003) proposed that the conservation of otter breeding sites should be tackled at the river catchment
or sub-catchment level. Within the catchment, optimal breeding sites are those that can be used by otters
over a period of many years, whereas sub-optimal sites may be used on a temporary basis, or may be prone
to problems such as submergence during flood events. The smallest of the SACs investigated may be prone
to such events and so impact on the presence of otters at those sites (cf. Loch of Isbister – see section
4.2.1.3)
Liles (2003) recognised three conservation actions that are likely to be required in order to ensure that
breeding sites are available:
l
Protecting existing breeding sites (both actual and potential).
l
Creating or enhancing new sites for otter breeding.
l
Protecting or creating feeding sites associated with breeding sites.
All three actions should be incorporated into the various site management plans associated with the
designated sites where otters occur.

5.5
Contaminant data for otters in Scotland
The bioaccumulation of persistent contaminants (such as organochlorines and mercury) in aquatic food
chains is well known (see Strachan & Jefferies, 1996). Otters are top predators in aquatic systems and being
relatively long lived, prone to accumulating contaminants in tissue, especially the liver (Jefferies & Hanson
2001). Carss & Shore (2003) provide a review of contaminant data for otters in Scotland based on three
studies of the levels of pollutants in otter carcasses during the period 1984–1992. The first study was
confined to Shetland, whereas the remaining two included carcasses from other parts of Scotland. Their key
findings suggested that pollutants such as PCBs (polychlorinated biphenyls) were present in otters in widely
dispersed areas in Scotland, in concentrations that may be relatively high (maximum value = 14.40mg/kg
wet weight. Sample size = 116 otter carcasses). PCBs were a mixture of congeners in which higher-
chlorinated ones predominated (especially congeners 138, 153, 170 and 180) a pattern comparable 
to that observed in otters from continental Europe. The concentration of other pollutants such as dieldrin 
and DDE (dichlorodiphenyldichloroethylene) appeared to be low and unimportant in a Scottish context
(Carss & Shore 2003).
Kruuk & Conroy (1996) found that otter body condition was significantly negatively correlated with
concentrations of PCBs in the liver. They found that the geometric mean values for PCB concentrations in
particular geographic areas (such as Shetland and south west Scotland) were relatively high, particularly 
if compared to the published level thought to cause reproductive failure in mink (50mg/kg lipid, or 
1.24mg/kg wet weight – see Jensen et al., 1977).
Suggestions that otter populations are able to do well despite high levels of PCB contamination, if the
necessary resources are available (high fish biomass, ample breeding sites, low disturbance), were
supported by Kruuk & Conroy (1996). They based their argument on the fact that the population of otters
on Shetland was relatively high and probably increasing from an estimated 700 adults in 1989 to around
1000 adults by 1995. Thus the Shetland population could be described as thriving and limited by habitat
and food availability (Conroy & Kruuk, 1995).
5.6
Acidification
High acidic rainfall combined with the afforestation of many upland areas was highlighted as a growing
cause for concern linked to the local decline and density of otters (Green & Green, 1997). The estimated
maximum amount of deposited and scavenged (acidic pollutants taken directly from the atmosphere by
conifer trees) acid which a given ecosystem can absorb without harm is called the critical load. In the UK
the critical load is set at pH4.5 (this is lower than that for both the USA – pH5.5 and Scandinavia – pH5.0).
Acidification results from depositions of both sulphur and oxidised nitrogen (NOx) and the critical load
function and its exceedance depend on the amount of both these pollutants.
Acid critical loads represent a quantitative estimate of the exposure risk below which significant harmful
effects on certain sensitive biological components do not occur. By definition, critical load exceedance
represents a significant risk of ecological damage.
Acidic water can not only reduce the availability invertebrates that fish prey on, but can elevate aluminium
levels in the water to a toxic level for fish. A severe reduction or complete absence of fish is likely to impact
on otter distribution.

Surface waters acidification has been recognised as a major problem in the UK since the early 1980s. Early
studies were based on the analysis of diatoms, trace metals and fly-ash particles in lake sediment records
and provided definitive evidence for the causes of acidification in the UK (eg Battarbee et al., 1985). These
findings contributed significantly to Government decisions to introduce sulphur emission reduction policies in
the late 1980s, leading to the establishment of the UK Acid Waters Monitoring Network (UKAWMN) in
1988. In the early 1990s, work funded by the Department of the Environment (now DEFRA) and carried out
in collaboration with the Freshwater Fisheries Laboratory (FFL) Pitlochry, and the Institute of Hydrology (now
CEH, Wallingford), was concerned with establishing the spatial extent of surface water acidification in the
UK, based on the first ever comprehensive national survey of the water chemistry of the UK’s lakes and
streams. The data from this survey were used in the development of a series of empirical critical load models
for freshwaters, including the diatom model (Battarbee et al., 1996), and a catchment prediction model
(Kernan et al., 1998), both developed in the Environmental Change Research Centre, University College
London. The critical loads research led to the publication of a major report for the Department of Environment
in 1995. One outcome of this national survey was the finding that nitrate, as well as sulphate, was
contributing to the acidity in many upland waters, especially in the Pennines, North Wales, Cumbrian Lake
District and Galloway regions (Allott et al., 1995). Subsequent research has focused especially on the
complex dynamics of nitrogen in upland catchments and the formulation of the First order Acidity Balance
(FAB) model. The FAB model, recommended by the United Nations Economic Commission for Europe
(UNECE), for freshwater critical load modelling, is now used in the UK for mapping critical load and
exceedance values for freshwaters in the UK (Curtis et al., 2000).
So far, despite large reductions in national sulphur emissions, evidence of chemical and biological recovery
from acidification is slight. There are several reasons why a delayed response might be expected, but one
of the most intriguing factors is climate change. At many Scottish sites it has been argued that increased
winter precipitation associated with a recent peak in the North Atlantic Oscillation, is leading to lower water-
column pH values, possibly offsetting the increase in pH that might be expected from a reduction in sulphur
deposition (Curtis, pers. comm.)

Road traffic mortality
Road casualties have been recognised for many years as a potential threat to otters and Green (1991)
considered that road traffic was the largest and most rapidly expanding cause of otter mortality. 
An indication of the problem in Scotland was expressed by Green & Green (1997) who reported a total 
of 453 otter bodies being recovered from roads in the period 1986–1995. That number represented 86%
of the total reported otter mortalities in the Scottish database for that time and it was likely that the proportion
was exaggerated, as road kills are highly visible and therefore most likely to be reported. Nevertheless, the
authors considered that road mortality was a significant and increasing cause of otter mortality in Scotland
and across the rest of the British Isles (Green & Green, 1997).

Mortality of coastal dwelling otters
The incidental capture and drowning of coastal dwelling otters in lobster creels, crab pots and fyke nets set
for eels is well known, but to date, under recorded and poorly quantified.
Deaths in fyke nets set for eels or wrasse (used for the salmon farming industry) are still occurring when the
nets do not have otter guards fitted (R. Green, pers. comm.). The Vincent Wildlife Trust has provided them
free of charge to fyke net manufacturers, suppliers and the netsmen themselves (Green & Green, 1997) 
and the widespread use of otter guards has undoubtedly led to a reduction in accidental drowning.
Otter mortality caused by drowning in lobster creels and crab pots is another cause for concern. For instance
Green & Green (1987) reported 81 Scottish otter deaths due to crustacean traps in the 10 year period
1975–85. Anecdotal information from Sutherland and Wester Ross suggests that mortality may be high
along parts of the west coast and around many of the islands (see Section 4.2.3.2). Similarly, a chance
conversation with a local fisherman in Shetland resulted in the author being told about otters being frequently
drowned in lobster creels at a few inshore hot spots along the Yell Sound Coast SAC (R. Strachan, pers.
obs.). Many other instances are likely to go unreported and therefore unquantified (Jefferies et al., 1984).
Where otters deaths have been reported there have been a number of compounding factors. For instance,
lobster creels and crab pots set in a water depth greater than 10m are very unlikely to catch otters. However,
lobster creels baited with whitefish and set in relatively shallow inshore water, where there was a rocky
substrate presented the most risk to otters, ie; within a zone most frequently utilised by foraging otters. Pots set
to catch crabs are frequently located in deeper water, often where there is a sandy substrate. Such areas tend
to be avoided by otters (Jefferies et al., 1984). Twelves (1983) collected data on otter mortality over a four
year period (1978–81) along the east coast of the Uists. She found that fish such as saithe, small cod and

5.10
Conclusions
The fourth national survey of the otter in Scotland has confirmed that the species has continued to increase
its population and range throughout the area. It can now be considered ubiquitous, even occupying highly
disturbed waterways where it seems unaffected by human activity. In its northern part of its range in
Scotland, together with the outer islands and Dumfries & Galloway the species may have reached carrying
capacity. Elsewhere the species has consolidated its range and shown spread in to the urban centres of
Glasgow and Edinburgh.
Water quality improvement and improved fish stocks are thought to have assisted its continued recovery,
although there are still concerns about acidification issues within some watercourses. There is little commercial
exploitation of freshwater and migratory fish in Scotland. Today, around 30% of salmon and sea trout are taken
by commercial fixed engine and net and coble fisheries. There is little commercial trapping of eels, which
although common in Scotland, grow slowly and are easily over-exploited. Around half of Scotland’s 40
freshwater and migratory fish species are exploited by anglers. In contrast to England and Wales, where
fishing for coarse species dominates, the most valuable angling sector is undoubtedly for salmon, sea trout,
brown trout and rainbow trout. Pike, perch, common carp and grayling also have their devotees. The true value
of angling to the Scottish economy has been recently estimated at more that £100 million per annum.

Recommendations
Future surveys
The present survey has highlighted the continued recovery of the otter in Scotland which is anow considered
to be widespread and abundant. However, monitoring should be continued in order to assess any potential
future threats across the species’ range in Scotland. It is recommended that the following long-term approach
to the surveillance and monitoring of otters is adopted in Scotland:
l
Consideration of a full survey of all previously documented sites within each of the 10km squares that

l
Full survey of the subset of sites identified in the present survey that comprise each of the SACs every

Survey protocol
Otter survey of Scotland 2003–04
Survey methodology
Three types of site are included in the 2003–04 survey:
(i)
Special Areas of Conservation sites. These require a full 600m search of the site in order to locate
and count actual and potential resting places, couches and holts and to count the total number of
spraints present. This will give an indication of the relative importance of the site for otters and allow a
measure of site condition or favourable status to be made.
(ii) Focus Zone sites. These require a search for spraints and other otters signs to be made for a minimum
of 100m or continued to 600m if no evidence can be found. In each Focus Zone 10km square up to
six VWT sites will need to be visited, as these may have all been negative in previous surveys.
(iii) Random sample sites. These involve two sites in each of the 10km squares in the survey. These require
a search for spraints and other otters signs to be made for a minimum of 100m or continued to 600m
if no evidence can be found.
