CENTRAL NEUROLOGICAL PROBLEMS: A FEW CASES 
 
Helene JACQUES 
 
DMV, 13 Place de Verdun, 38320 Eybens, France  email: h.jacques.otter@wanadoo.fr 
 
Abstract:  In carnivores, intracranial hypertension stems from three main causes: tumours, hydrocephalus and 
cranial trauma.  Depending on the degree of the brain compression the animal may be asymptomatic or present 
dramatic clinical signs.  Although brain tumours do not seem to be frequent in otters, five cases of 
hydrocephalus have been recorded in otter cubs.  Hydrocephalus occurs when cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) 
accumulates as a result of a blockage in the circulation path or an overproduction of this fluid. Clinical signs of 
hydrocephalus usually indicate cerebral dysfunction and often progress, although some animals may remain 
asymptomatic. Inflammatory conditions of the central nervous system like rabies, toxoplasmosis and distemper 
have been described in otters. Clinical signs are seldom specific and very often the diagnosis is difficult to attain 
without a blood and cerebrospinal fluid analysis. 
 
Keywords: Neurological problems, hydrocephalus, rabies, toxoplasmosis, distemper, Eurasian otter, Lutra lutra 
 
 
1   INTRODUCTION  
 
Otter neurological problems do not seem to be frequent in the wild but those otters which do 
suffering in this way probably die.  Observation of hydrocephalus in cubs cared for by otter 
centres, or post mortem examination of dead otters suffering from rabies, toxoplasmosis and 
distemper could be a way to understand these conditions better. 
Among 96 Eurasian otter cubs (Lutra lutra) cared for by the Vincent Wildlife Trust, 
three presented signs of hydrocephalus, and among 70 cubs cared by the International Otter 
Survival Fund, two had hydrocephalus (R. GREEN and G. YOXON, personal communications).  
The percentage seems high compared to humans where hydrocephalus occurs in one per 
1,000 births.  For dogs 70% of abnormalities at birth are hydrocephalus (FANUEL BARRET, 
1984). Causes of hydrocephalus could be multiple.   
 
Inflammation of the central nervous system due to rabies, toxoplasmosis, and 
distemper could also cause death in otters and only post mortem analysis can prove the nature 
of the disease. 
In this study we present some information and data on such kind of central 
neurological problems and its implications.

2   DESCRIPTION OF HYDROCEPHALUS  
 
Inside the skull there is nervous tissue (composed of neurons and gliales cells), the vascular 
system and cerebrospinal fluid.  The skull is a box which cannot extend, (at least in adults) 
and any change in the volume of one of these three compartments will increase the pressure 
therein (FANUEL BARRET, 1991). 
Cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) is a clear, water-like fluid produced primarily within the 
lateral ventricles of the brain [0.047 ml/min in dogs (FANUEL BARRET, 1984)].  In each 
ventricle, small flower-like tufts called choroid plexus produce CSF at a constant rate.  The 
CSF circulates from lateral ventricles to the third ventricle, then to the fourth ventricle.  
Afterwards, the fluid flows through a discrete channel into the space around the brain and 


 
2
spinal cord, where it also functions as a cushion, and it is then reabsorbed back into the blood 
stream.  A small amount of CSF is also produced by the spinal cord.  The CSF contains 
important chemicals and carries waste products away from tissues in and around the brain 
(MVM).  
 
The CSF is absorbed into the bloodstream, and the amount and pressure of the fluid 
are normally kept within a fairly narrow range.  If the drainage pathways are blocked at any 
point, the fluid accumulates in the ventricles inside the brain, causing them to swell, resulting 
in compression of the surrounding tissue (MVM).  In cubs, the head will enlarge as the 
fontanels are not closed. In adults, the head size cannot increase as the bones of the skull are 
completely joined together. 
 
Hydrocephalus occurs when CSF accumulates as a result of a blockage in the 
circulation path or an overproduction of the fluid.  As hydrocephalus is most common in 
brachycephalic dogs (Pekinese, Yorkshire terrier, chihuahua, pincher, poodle), it is possible 
that the shape of the otter skull itself could predispose to this abnormality (R. GREEN personal 
communication). 
 
Hydrocephalus exists in two forms; communicating (non-obstructive hydrocephalus) 
caused by inadequate absorption of CSF when the ventricular pathways are not obstructed; or 
non-communicating (obstructive hydrocephalus) caused by blockage in the ventricular 
pathways through which CSF flows.  Hydrocephalus can also be either congenital, where the 
condition is present at birth or detected soon thereafter; or acquired, where the condition is 
acquired as the result of infection, head trauma, brain tumours, cysts, etc.   
Known causes include  
 
- 
atresia of the mesencephalic aqueduct,  
- 
perinatal encephalitis or meningitis: the inflammation and debris from this 
infection block the drainage pathways resulting in hydrocephalus, 
-     
      intraventricular haemorrhage at birth because of adhesions caused by a clot (babies    
      born prematurely are more at risk), 
-     
      other causes (e.g. lack of vitamin A in calves). 
 
Clinical signs of hydrocephalus usually indicate cerebral dysfunction and often 
progress, although some animals may remain asymptomatic.  The fontanels are often patent, 
and affected animals may have ventrolateral strabismus.  Blindness due to polymicrogyria and 
asymmetrical dilatations of the lateral ventricles has been described in standard poodles 
(MVM).  Rosemary Green (Vincent Wildlife Trust) cared for 96 cubs for the Vincent Wildlife 
Trust and had three cases of hydrocephalus (R. GREEN, personal communication).  All were 
females, undersized and found alone, probably abandoned by their mothers.  The first, from 
Tiree, Inner Hebrides, was completely blind with vestigial eyes, and deaf.  She was incapable 
of eating solids, and lay in her bed most of the time, sometimes throwing her head back over 
her spine.  Finally she died and the post-mortem indicated the hydrocephalus.  The second, 
from Devon, south west of England, arrived when, like the others, her permanent dentition 
was just erupting (four to five months old) and was nearly normal at first.  However she never 
gained weight and her gait became abnormal, pacing into circles.  She started having fits and 
was euthanased.  The third, from the River Almond in the eastern part of central Scotland, 
was also walking in circles and was difficult to feed with solids and liquids.  She was 
euthanased.  On X-rays, the enlarged cranium, compared to that of a normal otter, can be seen 
(R. GREEN, personal communication).  
Grace Yoxon (personal communication) cared for 70 cubs at IOSF and had two cases 
of hydrocephalus.  The first, a female cub from Dunoon (west coast of Scotland) was nearly 
blind and deaf.  She was eating well but never gained weight.  Although very active, inco-


 
3
ordination and strange habits such as clawing frantically at the side of her mouth suggested 
brain lesions.  She died two months later - the post mortem indicated hydrocephalus.  The 
second cub from Banchory (Aberdeenshire, Scotland) was deaf and also showed inco-
ordination, chewing the bowl instead of the fish inside or standing in the corner of the pen 
with the head against the wall.  He also died three weeks later (G. YOXON, personal 
communication). 
 
For humans, treatment relies on either corticosteroids or the use of surgery to shunt 
CSF into the peritoneum (MVM).  For wildlife and pets, euthanasia is the best solution. 
 
 
3   DESCRIPTION OF INFLAMMATIONS OF THE CENTRAL NERVOUS SYSTEM 
 
Inflammation of the meninges (meningitis) and inflammation of the brain (encephalitis) often 
occur simultaneously (meningoencephalitis) in the same animal.  Causes include bacteria, 
viruses, fungi, protozoa, parasite migrations, chemical agents, and immune-mediated diseases 
(MVM).  The incidence of meningitis and encephalitis is fairly low compared with that of 
infections of other organs.  This appears to be the result of the better protection offered to the 
nervous system by its barriers.  
Direct extension of bacterial or mycotic infections to the central nervous system 
(CNS) can occur from sinusitis, otitis (media or interna), vertebral osteomyelitis, or 
diskospondylitis; these infections can also be secondary due to deep bite wounds or traumatic 
injuries adjacent to the head or spine (MVM).  
. 
Among 26 cubs treated for infected bites by Rosemary Green, one died from 
meningitis resulting from a superficial bite on the face infected with a group G Streptococcus.  
This appears to be the first verified case of meningitis, which is surprising as the usual sites of 
otter bites predispose to infections of the CNS (GREEN AND GREEN, 2002). 
 
The usual symptoms of meningitis are: fever, hyperesthesia, neck rigidity, and painful 
paraspinal muscle spasms, depression, blindness, progressive paresis, cerebellar or vestibular 
ataxia, opisthotonos, cranial nerve deficits, seizures, dementia, agitation, and depressed 
consciousness (including coma).  Such symptoms will appear, depending on the rapidity of 
onset, severity of pathology, and location of the lesions.  
 
The analysis of the CSF is one mean of identifying meningitis or meningoencephalitis. 
The technique of collection is not easy and the analysis of the CSF must be performed within 
30 minutes after its collection because the cells begin to degenerate soon after.  So in most 
cases this analysis cannot be made for wild animals.  
 
3.1 Rabies 
Rabies is an acute viral encephalomyelitis that principally affects carnivores and insectivorous 
bats, although it can affect any mammal.  It is almost invariably fatal once clinical signs 
appear. 
Transmission is almost always by introduction of virus-laden saliva into the tissues, 
usually from the bite of a rabid animal or, rarely, by ingestion.  Clinical signs of rabies are 
rarely definitive.  Rabid animals of all species exhibit typical signs of CNS disturbance, with 
minor variations among species.  The most reliable signs, regardless of species, are 
behavioural changes and unexplained paralysis (MVM).  Behavioural changes may include: 
anorexia, signs of apprehension or nervousness, irritability, and hyperexcitability.  
Commonly, rabid wild animals lose their fear of man, and species that are normally nocturnal 
may be seen wandering about during the daytime.  
 
Rabies has been described in otters in North America for the Canadian river otter 
(Lontra canadensis) with sometimes no other symptoms than a change in behaviour. A case 


 
4
was reported in Pennsylvania (SERFASS, 1995).  Another case was reported in Massachusetts 
in January 2001 and in Pennsylvania in April 2002 (www.promedmail.org).  Rabid raccoons 
(Procyon lotor) were the likely vectors. 
 
Of 1,207 cases of rabies reported in Europe (April-June 1998), one concerned a 
Eurasian otter [745 were wild animals, including 634 foxes (Vulpes vulpes)] (MULLER, COX 
AND HOHSNBEEN, 1998a). 
Of 1,873 cases of rabies reported in Europe (October-December 1998) one concerned 
an otter (1,127 were wild animals, including 957 foxes) (MULLER, COX AND HOHSNBEEN, 
1998b). 
 
3.2 Toxoplasmosis 
Toxoplasma gondii is a protozoan parasite that infects most species of warm-blooded animals, 
including birds and man, throughout the world.  Members of the cat family are the only 
known definitive hosts for T. gondii and, therefore, serve as the main reservoir.  Infection may 
be acquired by carnivorism, ingestion of faeces containing oocysts, or congenitally.  
 
In young animals, this virus can cause interstitial pneumonia, myocarditis, hepatic 
necrosis, meningoencephalomyelitis, chorioretinitis, lymphadenopathy, and myositis (MVM).  
The prevalence of antibody titles to T. gondii in Canadian river otters from eastern North 
Carolina, USA, was investigated.  Sera from 103 live-trapped individuals were tested for 
antibodies to T. gondii using a commercially available latex agglutination kit.  Forty-five per 
cent of the sera were positive with titles ranging from 1:16 to >1:2,048.  The high prevalence 
of antibodies to T. gondii indicates that many animals in this population were exposed to the 
parasite (TOCIDLOWSKI ET AL., 1997). 
 
In California, T. gondii is more common in water than previously recognised and 
could be the cause of death of many sea otters.  One hundred southern sea otters (Enhydra 
lutris) have been washed up dead on Californian beaches since January 2003.  One hundred 
and five carcasses found in California from February 1998 to June 2001 show that 47% were 
four to nine years old.  Sixty four per cent died of some form of disease, and of these 38% 
died from parasitic infections including acanthocephalans (thorny worm) and protozoa (T. 
gondii and Sarcocystis neurona).  Otters with toxoplasmosis infections were four times more 
likely to be killed by sharks than those without. Christine Kreuder, a veterinarian at the UC 
Davis Wildlife Health Centre, says: "This parasite invades the otter's brain and can cause 
neurological problems such as seizures. That could make the otters confused and disabled. 
They would be less able to evade sharks, more likely to swim to unprotected offshore waters, 
and more likely to shake and twitch, which attracts sharks." (www.promedmail.org).   
 
3.3 Distemper 
Canine distemper is a highly contagious, systemic, viral disease of dogs seen worldwide.  It is 
characterised by a diphasic fever, leucopenia, gastrointestinal and respiratory catarrh, and 
frequently pneumonic and neurological complications.  The disease occurs in Canidae (dogs, 
foxes, wolves), Mustelidae (e.g., ferret, American mink, skunks), most Procyonidae (e.g., 
racoon, coati mundi), and some Viveridae (binturong).  
 
Chronic distemper encephalitis (old dog encephalitis, [ODE]), a condition often 
marked by ataxia, compulsive movements such as head pressing or continual pacing, and 
unco-ordinated hypermetria, may occur in adult dogs without a history of symptoms related to 
systemic canine distemper (MVM).  The disease is caused by an inflammatory reaction 
associated with persistent canine distemper virus infection in the CNS.  
 
Domestic dogs, mink and seals are potential sources of infection (KEYMER, 1992).  
Most dogs are now vaccinated against CVD so there is probably less risk than previously. As 
otters and American mink may use the same dens, it is easy to appreciate how cross infections 


 
5
with CDV might occur in the wild.  There is always the worry that the seal or phocine 
distemper virus (PVD) could "jump the species barrier" and infect coastal otters.  However 
this could not occur easily because in canids, inhalation is considered to be the main route of 
infection.  The virus is instable and readily inactivated by heat and light. CDV and other 
morbilliviruses therefore, are more likely to spread between gregarious animals such as seals 
and cetaceans than species such as otters (KEYMER, 1992). 
 
Two cases have been described by GEISEL (1979) for otters with dermatitis, purulent 
bronchopneumonia and hemorrhagic gastroenteritis.  The diagnostic was based on the 
demonstration of cytoplasmic inclusion bodies in many epithelial cells.  Neither virus 
isolation nor immunohistology were performed. 
 
Two other cases have been also described by LOUPAL ET AL. (2002) on wild otters.  
Both animals died soon after capture.  Post-mortem examination revealed severe necrotic 
dermatitis, gastritis and corneal ulcers.  In smears from trachea and urinary bladder 
cytoplasmic inclusion bodies could be observed in epithelial cells.  Immunohistochemically, 
the CDV antigen was found in many epithelial cells, e.g., in stomach, lung, cornea and skin. 
No neurological signs have been reported. 
 
During post-mortem examination, distemper virus inclusion bodies have been seen in 
six otherwise healthy wild otters in Denmark, without any signs of clinical distemper 
(MADSEN ET AL., 1998).  The infected otters were collected from the Limfjord area at a time 
when the distemper virus was present both in the common seal (Phoca vitulina), and in a 
major outbreak of distemper in farmed mink in this area, indicating a wide range of host 
species for the distemper virus. 
 
Among ten cubs found abandoned in the Czech Republic and cared for until re-
introduction, one of them presented symptoms of anaemia, vomiting and convulsions when 
dying. Post mortem examination and histology indicated distemper (A. TOMAN, personal 
communication). 
 
4   CONCLUSION 
This paper is intended to give some information about possible neurological problems in 
otters.  
Hydrocephalus could be a more important problem than previously thought.  Cubs can 
die early in the den or later once being trained by the mother but this affliction could not be 
compatible with a normal wild otter life.  More data are needed to assess how frequent is this 
condition. 
Rabies, toxoplasmosis and distemper seemed to be rare, but the recent mortality 
episode in sea otters partly caused by toxoplasmosis proves that these viruses can be a great 
problem threatening an otter population. 
 
Post mortem examinations are essential to improve our knowledge about neurological 
otter diseases.
