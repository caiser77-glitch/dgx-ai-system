The large difference in chemical structures of environmental contaminants that were found to be 
relevant for wildlife and humans increased the difficulties and costs of chemical analyses.  In 
addition the evidence of low dose effects of many of these compounds further increased the costs 
as requirements on technical equipment were also increasing and sophisticated chemical analyses 
are also time and labou consuming.  During the last years several different bioassays were 
developed that are able to detect compounds that have a common mechanism of action, such as 
binding to the Ah-receptor or oestrogen receptor.  Biological test systems that use this common 
mechanism can provide very useful and rapid screening methods by measuring the biological 
potency of single compounds, mixtures or environmental extracts.  The receptor determines 
whether a compound is relevant or not (Figure 1).  When from in vivo research the relationship 
between toxic potency via such specific receptors and biochemical/physiological and toxic effects 
is known, it is possible to predict possible consequences of the toxic potencies determined in the 
in vitro bioassays (Figure 1). 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
Figure 1. Principle of bioanalysis 
 
Within the last years a vast variety of bioassays were proven to be efficient models to detect 
hazards of compounds, to elucidate their modes of action or to screen for the presence of a wide 
range of chemicals (MURK ET AL., 1996, 2002; LEGLER ET AL., 1999; HAMERS ET AL., 2000; 
Bioanalysis


BEHNISCH ET AL., 2003; EGGEN AND SEGNER, 2003; GUTLEB ET AL., 2004; HOUTMAN ET AL., 2004; 
WHYTE, SCHMITT AND TILLITT, 2004; VETHAAK ET AL., 2005; WEMMEL ET AL., 2005).  The 
challenge for bioassays is the rapid and cost-efficient detection of chemicals or combinations that 
are responsible for sublethal, chronic toxic effects.  Mechanism-based bioanalytical assays 
indicate that chemicals with a specific mode of toxic potential are present integrating both known 
and unknown agonists and antagonists of the mechanism in question. 
 
Exposure assessment is usually performed by means of chemical analytical methods and 
concentration of known and expected pollutants are reported.  Effects assessment is usually 
performed using laboratory experimental animals measuring the effects of single compounds or 
mixtures.  Whereas in the past many contaminants were present in high concentrations and crude 
endpoints such as death or retarded growth were sufficient in the current situation of decreasing 
concentrations of many compounds more dedicated approaches are needed.  There are several 
indications for compounds with subtle but important negative effects on wildlife in the 
environment, including long-term effects of low dose exposure, agonistic, potential and 
antagonistic effects of compounds present in realistic exposure mixtures and different effects of 
parent compounds and metabolites. This asks for a more integrated approach. 
Chemicals belonging to different chemical structural classes have been identified to 
biological responses through binding through certain receptors and the most well-known and 
best-studied example is the binding of certain dioxins, PCBs, PAHs etc. to the aryl hydrocarbon 
receptor (AhR).  This binding of certain environmental contaminants to a specific biological 
receptor protein can be utilised for bioanalytical purposes.  A bioanalytical tool, usually a cell 
that contains the receptor, is able to selectively detect specific ligands (Figure 1). 
In reporter gene assays such as the DR-CALUX assay (Chemically Activated LUciferase 
eXpression assay for the Dioxin Response) the induction and reduction of transcription following 
contaminant exposure is measured.  Binding of a ligand to the AhR results in a conformational 
change that facilitates binding of this activated receptor to specific DNA sequences, which in turn 
results in modulation of gene expression.  In the DR-CALUX assay the product of AhR 
dependent gene activation, luciferase can be analysed after addition of luciferin (Figure 2). 
 
 
Figure 2. Schematic representation of the DR-CALUX assay 


Gene induction assays have the advantage compared with receptor binding assays in that they can 
distinguish between agonists and antagonists when single compounds are used in an experimental 
setting respectively that the total potential of an extract, integrating agonistic and antagonistic 
activity can be read out.  In addition such assays are more sensitive than receptor binding assays 
as in these assays the ligands have to compete with the (usually radioactive) natural ligand to the 
receptor, as has been shown for assays for estrogenic compounds (MURK ET AL., 2002). 
Reporter gene assays have frequently been applied for the screening of compounds, 
environmental samples or tissues (MURK ET AL., 1998; BEHNISCH, HOSOE AND SAKAI, 2003; 
HOUTMAN ET AL., 2004; VETHAAK ET AL., 2005). Generally, reported correlations between 
chemical analyses and CALUX assay are good (BOVEE ET AL., 1998; MURK ET AL., 1998).  
However as gene induction assays tends to indicate higher dioxin equivalents than GC/MS 
analysis it is obvious that chemical analysis misses chemicals with dioxin-like activity 
(STRONKHORST, LEONARDS AND MURK, 2002;LEGLER ET AL., 2003). 
 
Reporter gene assays, such as the CALUX assay, have been used in many studies and the 
combination of its speed, relative simplicity, and ability to integrate the effects of complex 
mixtures also makes it a valuable additional tool for studies related to pollution in otters and their 
environment.  When applied with the right protocol for sample preparation and quality controls, 
this bioassay can serve as an example for the successful application of in vitro bioassays for 
bioanalysis of relevant contaminants related to otters.
