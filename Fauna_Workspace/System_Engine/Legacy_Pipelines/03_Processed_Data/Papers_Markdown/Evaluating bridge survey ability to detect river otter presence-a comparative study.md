Abstract: Many researchers use bridges as search sites to monitor freshwater otter species 
23 
along watercourses. Bridges enable rapid and easy access to their habitat but for most otter 
24 
species, little is known on whether these anthropogenic structures affect their distribution, 
25 
their marking preferences, and consequently, the ability of such surveys to detect their 
26 
presence. We investigated the bridge survey method using data gathered during four 
27 
winters of survey along the rivers and streams of Kouchibouguac National Park and 
28 
surrounding area in New Brunswick, Canada. Our results show that sign surveys using 
29 
bridges as search sites can have the same capability to detect river otter (Lontra canadensis) 
30 
occurrences than surveys using randomly distributed sites. Future surveys can be improved 
31 
by increasing search distance at bridge sites. This will increase detection rates and 
32 
safeguard against results under representing otter occurrence on the landscape, which could 
33 
prompt unnecessary conservation actions. Researchers choosing to increase search distance 
34 
are advised to augment survey efforts in order to maintain large sample sizes, ensuring 
35 
sufficient statistical power for tests aiming to detect trends in river otter occurrence.

Introduction 
38 
Population monitoring is an integral part of resource management and has 
39 
considerable importance for species at risk or those of commercial interest. Long term 
40 
monitoring programs required for proper management can be economically and logistically 
41 
demanding. Therefore, considerable efforts are made by scientists to develop and test novel 
42 
population monitoring methods (e.g. Kohn et al. 1999, Eggert et al. 2003), and to test the 
43 
accuracy of existing cost-effective ones (e.g. Ruiz-Olmo et al. 2001, Sharp et al. 2001). 
44 


 
3
Freshwater otters are inconspicuous animals in nature. Because of this, populations 
45 
of these species are most often monitored indirectly by searching for activity signs such as 
46 
tracks and faeces (e.g. Lodé 1993, Lee 1996, Shackelford & Whitaker 1997). Because these 
47 
species typically establish home ranges along rivers and streams (Melquist & Hornocker 
48 
1983, Reid et al. 1994), searches for signs of activity are concentrated along their shores. 
49 
These sign surveys consist of visiting predetermined sites where stretches of shoreline of 
50 
standardized lengths are scrutinized for activity signs. Ideally, these sites would be selected 
51 
at random. Because of accessibility constraints however, many sign surveys included 
52 
bridges and roads passing close to rivers and streams as sites to search for activity signs 
53 
(e.g. Liles & Jenkins 1984, Prigioni et al. 1986, Lodé 1993, Brzeziński et al. 1996). Some 
54 
surveys in North America explicitly use bridges as search sites (e.g. Clark et al. 1987, 
55 
Shackelford & Whitaker 1997, Bischof 2003). Their ease of access by vehicle enables cost 
56 
effective survey of numerous search sites over a large geographical area. 
57 
The rationale for using bridges remains limited, considering the fact that they 
58 
constitute anthropogenic disturbances that may influence the otters’ habitat use and 
59 
consequently, the potential for these searches to detect their presence in a given region. 
60 
Moreover, using bridges is a non-random method of selecting search sites and the outcome 
61 
of such surveys can be highly influenced by the ecology of the species (i.e. its reaction to 
62 
bridges and marking behaviour). In Europe, available evidence indicates that Eurasian 
63 
otters (Lutra lutra) do not avoid sites with bridges (Romanowski et al. 1996) and often 
64 
defecate near or under them (e.g. Reuther & Roy 2001, Elmerost & Bussenius 2002). 
65 
However, further inquiry is required to ensure that managers in other regions can rely upon 
66 
bridge surveys for population monitoring and management decision-making.  
67 


 
4
It is therefore essential that this way of selecting sampling sites be tested for other 
68 
species and in other regions before determining that it is a universally effective survey 
69 
method for otters. The objective of this study was to determine if sign surveys are less 
70 
effective at detecting North American river otter (Lontra canadensis) occurrence when 
71 
bridges instead of random sites are designated for conducting transect searches along 
72 
riverbanks. Almost nothing is known about North American river otter behaviour relative 
73 
to roads and bridges. The model developed by Dubuc et al. (1990) for predicting river otter 
74 
occurrence on Mount Desert Island, in Maine, did not retain the variables that were 
75 
accounting for dirt and paved roads in their study area. Mowbray et al. (1976) reported a 
76 
few findings of otter activity signs near roads. Based on this limited available information, 
77 
we hypothesized that using bridges as search sites would not adversely affect otter 
78 
detection rates, and that bridge sites would yield results similar to randomly chosen sites. 
79 
To compare bridges and randomly selected sites, we analyzed data collected during four 
80 
years of monitoring a North American river otter population by conducting long-distance 
81 
transects on rivers and streams crossed-over by several bridges.

Material and Methods 
84 
Study area 
85 
The study area covered Kouchibouguac National Park of Canada (KNPC) and its 
86 
vicinity (Fig. 1). Located in the province of New Brunswick, the park covered an area of 
87 
238.8 km2 and was representative of the Maritime Coastal Plains (Desloge 1980). The 
88 
topography was flat and contained eight major watercourses with numerous bogs and 
89 


 
5
swamps: Portage River, Carrigan Brook, Fontaine River, Black River, Rankin Brook, 
90 
Kouchibouguac River, Major Brook, and Kouchibouguacis River (Desloges 1980). The two 
91 
main rivers, Kouchibouguac and Kouchibouguacis, were tidal. Average annual temperature 
92 
was 4.8˚C, average freeze-free period was 177 days and annual precipitation averaged 979 
93 
mm (Desloges 1980). The park contained mixed forests dominated by balsam fir (Abies 
94 
balsamea) and birch (Betula spp.), as well as coniferous forests of black spruce (Picea 
95 
mariana) (Graillon et al. 2000). Speckled alder (Alnus rugosa) dominated the shores of 
96 
most of the smaller streams. The study area extended into regions outside the park along the 
97 
Portage, Kouchibouguac, Kouchibouguacis, and St-Charles rivers, as well as along the 
98 
Black River in the Black River Provincially Protected Zone of New Brunswick 
99 
(BRPPZNB) that was adjacent to the park (Fig. 1). Areas outside the park and the protected 
100 
zone were at various stages of succession, and included light residential areas and 
101 
agricultural lands. The study area contained 18 bridges, with six of them in KNPC and none 
102 
in the BRPPZNB (Fig. 1). Salt and small gravel were spread on roads of the region in 
103 
winter.

Field Methods 
106 
Documenting otter movements during winter is advantageous in many regards. 
107 
Detection probability is high because of the conspicuous tracks each animal leaves in the 
108 
snow, and all other activity signs will be linked to them. The homogeneous substrate left by 
109 
snowfalls also safeguards against possible biases linked to differential detection rates 
110 
(Conroy & French 1987, Romanowski et al. 1996). We conducted winter sign surveys of 
111 
otter activity from early January until the end of April for the winters of 2000, 2001, 2003, 
112 


 
6
and 2004. We monitored river otter activity in riparian habitats by conducting wintertime 
113 
transects along the shores of the eight main rivers and streams of the study area, as well as 
114 
tributaries associated to them. We respected a minimum delay of 12 h after snowfalls, 
115 
thereby leaving enough time for otters to manifest their presence and produce activity signs 
116 
on the fresh snow. We used light snowmobiles (Bombardier’s Tundra models) when river 
117 
width and ice thickness made it possible. We conducted transects as a team of two riders on 
118 
separate machines, riding single file at slow speed along both shores of rivers and stopping 
119 
to document river otter signs of activity. We accessed narrower sections of rivers and 
120 
streams by snowshoe. We sampled rivers in random order and alternated the order for each 
121 
survey period. We conducted continuous transects, with an entire day or two invested in 
122 
scrutinizing each river. A typical continuous transect would take an entire day to conduct, 
123 
during which we would strive to survey as many kilometers of the chosen river as possible. 
124 
Transects did not overlap each other within a given survey, but once all rivers and streams 
125 
were searched, we resurveyed them as many times as possible within the given winter 
126 
season. This maximized our ability to document river otter activity along the shores of the 
127 
major watersheds in the study area (Fig. 1). 
128 
We recorded coordinates (UTM, Grid #20, in meters, with a Garmin12 XL 
129 
Geographic Positioning System), date and time for the beginning and end of each transect, 
130 
as well as for all encountered otter activity signs. We calculated time (hour) elapsed since 
131 
last snowfall each time a new transect began or an otter activity sign was detected. For each 
132 
transect where bridges and otter activity were present, we then used topographical maps to 
133 
measure the straight-line distance (m) between bridges and the respective closest otter 
134 


 
7
activity signs detected. We measured strait-line distance both upstream and downstream of 
135 
bridges when activity signs were found on both sides of bridges.

Data analysis 
138 
For each field transect, by keeping track of the distance at which we found the 
139 
closest otter activity signs from respective bridges, we were able to compute the detection 
140 
rates that would have been obtained by conducting bridge surveys of different lengths (i.e. 
141 
length of shoreline scrutinized at search sites with bridges as starting points). We then 
142 
constructed a scatter plot of the cumulative percentage of known otter presences detected as 
143 
a function of the maximum distance from bridges searched. The relationship obtained was 
144 
described by plotting an accumulation curve defined as: 
145 
 
146 
[1] 
A(x) = ax / (b + x) 
147 
 
148 
where x is the maximum distance (m) of shoreline searched with bridges as the starting 
149 
point, A(x) is the cumulative number of instances with otter detection by bridge searches up 
150 
to the given distance, a is the parameter that determines the asymptote of the function, and 
151 
b is the parameter defining the rate at which the non-linear slope diminishes as x increases. 
152 
This function is commonly used when estimating population size from genotyped faecal 
153 
samples and is also known as a rarefaction curve (Kohn et al. 1999, Eggert et al. 2003). For 
154 
our data, we obtained a and b values by using the Levenberg-Marquardt estimation method 
155 
from the non-linear regression module in SPSS (version 8.0 for Windows). We set the 
156 
starting value at 1 for parameter estimations, with no restrictions and no limits imposed on 
157 


 
8
the number of iterations. Computations were terminated and final parameter values 
158 
accepted by SPSS when the relative reduction between successive residual sums of squares 
159 
was < 0.00000001.

In survey schemes where locations are searched for the presence-absence of a given 
161 
species, researchers can make two types of errors in the field. The first is to conclude that a 
162 
species is absent from a location when in fact it is present, and the second is to conclude 
163 
that the species is present when in fact it is absent. When conducting sign surveys, the latter 
164 
is practically impossible to make unless sympatric species produce similar activity signs 
165 
that can confound species identity. River otter activity signs are quite unique and are 
166 
unlikely to be confounded with those of other animals, except for regions of the world 
167 
where sympatric species of otters are found (e.g. Rowe-Rowe 1992). The type of error in 
168 
the field that often plagues otter surveys is thus failing to detect otters at a site when they 
169 
actually are present. Using the same accumulation curves, we computed the percentages of 
170 
known otter presences that would have gone undetected in our study area if bridge surveys 
171 
using different search distances had been used instead of long-distance riparian transects 
172 
that sampled whole rivers. 
173 
It is foreseeable that longer transect searches will result in more of the known otter 
174 
presences being detected. To investigate the bridge effect apart from that of transect length, 
175 
we created a companion set of random sites in order to investigate how results change when 
176 
random sites instead of bridges are used for transect searches. For each continuous transect, 
177 
on each side of a bridge where otter activity signs were present as detected by the given 
178 
transect, we selected a random site along the river for the companion dataset. For example, 
179 
to select a random site downstream from the bridge on Portage River, we would use the 
180 


 
9
random number tables in Zar (1999) to generate a linear random distance (m) downstream 
181 
from the bridge that would correspond to the location for the random site. For this 
182 
particular example, we would repeat the procedure until we would obtain a random distance 
183 
that places the given random site anywhere between the bridge and the river’s mouth, rather 
184 
than outright in the ocean. These sites are then taken to represent the center of randomly 
185 
distributed transect searches for activity signs. We repeated, on an individual basis, the 
186 
measurement of the distance between a random site and the closest sign of otter activity for 
187 
the data of each continuous transect done in the field. The analyses are thus based on 
188 
individual transect data. We compared results for scenarios using different transect lengths 
189 
at these randomly chosen sites in tandem with bridge survey scenarios by applying the 
190 
same non-linear regression analyses described above for bridge sites.  
191 
Because the data consist of direct, on site scrutiny of riverbanks, our study concerns 
192 
bridge surveys where riverbanks are inspected directly rather than observed at a distance 
193 
with binoculars by standing on the bridge. In order to determine if time elapsed between 
194 
snowfalls and continuous riparian transect searches influenced how far activity signs were 
195 
found from bridges, we applied simple linear regressions on our survey data. We 
196 
designated distance (m) between respective bridges and their closest river otter activity sign 
197 
as the dependent (i.e. response) variable and time elapsed (days) since last snowfall at the 
198 
time of sampling as the independent (i.e. predictor) variable.

Results 
200 
During the winters of 2000, 2001, 2003, and 2004, we conducted 92 continuous 
201 
riparian transects, totalling 1557 km of riverbank searches for river otter activity signs by 
202 
repetitively surveying the study area. The network we surveyed amounted to approximately 
203 
150 km of rivers and streams (Fig. 1). Transects averaged 6.18 km in length, with a 
204 
standard error of 0.63 km. We recorded water access holes, burrows, direct sightings, 
205 
faeces, and snow tracks. Faeces and snow tracks were the most prevalent signs of otter 
206 
activity on the landscape. Most river otter occurrences were confined to the protected areas 
207 
of KNPC and the BRPPZNB, with only 32 of the 643 documented signs of activity located 
208 
in the unprotected portion of our study area. Consequently, our results are mostly 
209 
associated to bridges within or in the vicinity of the mostly undisturbed, protected areas of 
210 
KNPC (Fig. 1). Of the 92 transects, 50 included bridges and had otters present. From these 
211 
50 transects, we made 61 measurements of the distance between bridges and their closest 
212 
otter activity sign. There were 26 measurements upstream and 35 measurements 
213 
downstream from bridges. There was no statistically significant difference in the mean 
214 
distance at which closest otter activity signs were found upstream compared to downstream 
215 
of bridges (Student’s test; t59 = 1.580, P = 0.120). Therefore, we merged data from both 
216 
upstream and downstream of bridges in order to conduct further analyses and thus, effective 
217 
sample size was n = 61. Consequently, we used 61 random sites where measurements were 
218 
made to compare results with bridge sites. Linear regression analysis indicated that time 
219 
(days) since last snowfall had no statistically significant influence on the distance at which 
220 
we found closest activity signs from respective bridges for continuous riparian transects 
221 


 
11
from our winter surveys (distance from bridges = 1410.977 – 96.593x, F1,59 = 1.106, P = 
222 
0.297, r2 = 0.018).

59, r2 = 0.990) and random sites (A = 70.389x / (805.110 + x), df = 59, r2 = 0.994) are 
225 
illustrated in Figure 2. They represent the cumulative percentage of known otter presences 
226 
that would have been detected as a function of the different search distance scenarios 
227 
applied to our data. We converted values of these functions into percentage values before 
228 
being plotted (Fig. 2) in order to facilitate comparisons. Figure 3 illustrates percentages of 
229 
known otter presences that would have gone undetected for different bridge and random 
230

increased, a higher percentage of known otter presences would have been detected (Fig. 2), 
233 
while the percentage of known otter presences that would have gone undetected diminished

Discussion 
245 
Our study demonstrated that sign surveys using bridges as search sites can have the 
246 
same capability to detect river otter presence than surveys using randomly distributed sites. 
247 
Our results also suggested that river otters in our study area did not actively avoid locations 
248 
with bridges and this is in accordance with Dubuc et al. (1990), who did not find 
249 
statistically significant effects of paved and dirt river crossings on otter occurrence. 
250 
Working with Eurasian otters, Durbin (1998) found that only one of five animals studied 
251 
with radio-telemetry avoided bridges. The river otter, as a semi-aquatic top-level predator 
252 
of riparian ecosystems, requires access to prey as well as adequate terrestrial shelter. North 
253 
American river otter occurrence on the landscape has been associated with several 
254 
characteristics offering shelter, such as overhanging vegetation along riverbanks and latter 
255 
succession stages of vegetation like mature forests (Newman & Griffin 1994, Bowyer et al. 
256 
1995, Swimley et al. 1998), silt and organic matter dominated soils (Reid et al. 1994), 
257 
ground debris like fallen trees or logs (Swimley et al. 1998), vertical banks (Reid et al. 
258 
1994, Swimley et al. 1998), as well as beaver ponds, bank dens, and lodges (Dubuc et al. 
259 
1990, Newman & Griffin 1994, Reid et al. 1994, Swimley et al. 1998). Melquist & 
260 
Hornocker (1983) observed that they could be found at sites with anthropogenic 
261 
disturbances, provided that these two essential habitat requirements (i.e. prey and shelter) 
262 
were met. Considering this, it is to be expected that the capability of bridge surveys to 
263 
detect otter presence will vary from one study area to another. Detection success will 
264 
depend on where bridges are located in relation to sites having habitat characteristics that 
265 
otters will use; the latter is where production of activity signs is most likely to occur. This 
266 


 
13
situation is equally relevant to surveys using randomly selected sites. The uniqueness of 
267 
each potential study area precludes recommendations of definitive search distances that 
268 
universally guarantee high detection rates. However, our research constitutes the first 
269 
demonstration that bridges do not diminish the odds of detecting North American river otter 
270 
presence when they are used as starting points to conduct searches.  
271 
 
Our results also show that current bridge surveys (Table 1) could be improved by 
272 
increasing the distance searched at each site. For example, by searching 600 m of 
273 
riverbanks from bridges in our study area, only 51 % of known otter presences in our data 
274 
would have been detected. This relatively low detection rate for North American river 
275 
otters could well have occurred for bridge surveys in other parts of North America, 
276 
considering the fact that population density estimated from the 2000 data was 5.8 groups of 
277 
otters per 100 km of river (M. Dumond & C.H. Bérubé, pers. comm.), and compares well 
278 
with the 5.0 groups per 100 km obtained by Melquist & Hornocker (1983) in Idaho. By 
279 
increasing search distance, more sites with otter occurrence will be detected but as 
280 
explained above, the level of increase in detection rates brought by extended searches will 
281 
depend on where the bridges are located in relation to habitat characteristics that otters use 
282 
and this will vary from one region to another. Even with some locations with otters going 
283 
undetected, bridge surveys can still be able to reflect temporal trends in river otter 
284 
occurrence on the landscape, if detection rates remain stable from one survey to the next. 
285 
We did not study this aspect of the performance of bridge surveys or other types of sign 
286 
surveys, as it would require a long-term study to evaluate the temporal stability of detection 
287 
rates. However, caution is advised if survey results are interpreted in the form of presence-
288 
absence data, as is often the case (e.g. Prigioni et al. 1986, Lodé 1993, Lee 1996, 
289 


 
14
Shackelford & Whitaker 1997). Temporal fluctuations in the number of positive searches 
290 
over time risk not accurately reflect trends in true population size when activity signs of the 
291 
surveyed species play a role in intra-specific communication and exist in a clumped 
292 
distribution on the landscape (Gallant et al. in press). Such presence-absence survey data 
293 
are best used for monitoring species distribution rather than population size.  
294 
 
Clark et al. (1987) found similar results when simultaneously conducting bridge and 
295 
scent station surveys, with 100 m being searched on both sides of bridges. This suggests 
296 
that very short searches might be sufficient to detect otter presence. Their study design 
297 
appears to be inadequate for validating bridge sign surveys however, because scent stations 
298 
were established at the end of the 100 m searches upstream and downstream of bridges. It is 
299 
likely that otters would have left faeces and other activity signs closer to these bridges and 
300 
on the 100 m search strips leading to the scent stations because otters were attracted to 
301 
these sites by the scent stations. For our winter study, linear regression analysis indicated 
302 
that activity signs were not closer or farther from bridges in continuous transects conducted 
303 
several days after snowfall in comparison to those conducted shortly after snowfall. 
304 
Therefore, the time at which we conducted transects after snowfalls was not a source of 
305 
bias. The presence of other anthropogenic disturbances such as houses and fields in our 
306 
study area could have limited our ability to determine if bridges had a negative impact on 
307 
otter detection rates. Because we only analyzed data from continuous transects with otter 
308 
detection and 95 % of detected activity signs were in the protected areas of KNPC and the 
309 
BRPPZNB, anthropogenic structures and disturbances other than bridges probably had a 
310 
negligible influence on our results because our data essentially came from these protected 
311 
portions of our study area. 
312 


 
15
Our results and conclusions are not directly transposable to bridge surveys 
313 
conducted on landscapes inhabited by Eurasian otters. Contrary to what we observed for 
314 
North American river otters, this species frequently defecates under bridges (e.g. 
315 
Romanowski et al. 1996, Reuther & Roy 2001) and most activity signs tend to be found 
316 
within 100 to 200 m from bridges (Romanowski et al. 1996, Reuther & Roy 2001, Elmeros 
317 
& Bussenius 2002). This means that short transect searches at bridges could result in high 
318 
detection rates for this species. However, the frequent findings of faeces under bridges 
319 
might be an artifact of the absence of riparian vegetation under bridges, hence facilitating 
320 
detection during seasons other than winter. Some surveys of Eurasian otters included sites 
321 
with poor vegetation cover (e.g. Macdonald & Mason 1983a, Lodé 1993). It is possible that 
322 
otters in such situations are attracted to the cover offered by bridges. Still, Romanowski et 
323 
al. (1996) recommended extending searches from 600 m to 1 km in regions with low otter 
324 
density. In their study, extended searches increased the number of positive sites by 27 %. 
325 
Elmeros & Bussenius (2002) also remarked that not all bridges have adequate substrate 
326 
under them for attracting defecation and marking by Eurasian otters. 
327 
For North American river otters, it can be argued that they may demonstrate 
328 
different defecation behaviour in summer as opposed to winter. However, during past 
329 
summer fieldwork on other research projects along the rivers and streams of KNPC (e.g. 
330 
Gallant et al. 2004), no otter latrines were ever found under bridges (D. Gallant, pers. obs.). 
331 
This difference could be due to most bridge sites in our study being in forested areas, but 
332 
our results suggest the possibility that these two species of otters differ markedly in their 
333 
behavioural response to bridges. Just as for other types of sign surveys, it is likely that our 
334 
surveys did not detect all locations used by otters in our study area. Because our surveys 
335 


 
16
used continuous transects and were conducted in winter, we judge that detection rates were 
336 
high due to the conspicuous tracks left by otters on the snow, which link all other activity 
337 
signs to them. However, it is unrealistic to consider that all locations without activity signs 
338 
on ice or snow are locations devoid of otters. It is possible that they remained in the water 
339 
under ice when passing through locations with bridges. Even if the North American river 
340 
otter population we studied do not seem to use bridges as sites to deposit faeces and were 
341 
not found as close to bridges as Eurasian otters, our similar results for bridge and random 
342 
sites clearly demonstrate that they do not actively avoid locations with bridges. 
343 
 
344 
Management implications 
345 
Our results show that the performance of surveys using bridges as search sites can 
346 
be similar to those using randomly selected sites. Our results showed that bridges do not 
347 
adversely affect the odds of detecting North American river otter presence. Management 
348 
decisions based on surveys that underestimate a species’ presence might prompt 
349 
unnecessary conservation actions. If avoidance of survey results that under represent North 
350 
American river otter presence on the landscape is important, our results (Fig. 2) suggest that 
351 
past bridge surveys (Table 1) would have probably required longer searches at each site in 
352 
order to maximize detection rates. This means that fewer bridge sites could have been 
353 
sampled with the same investment of time and labour. Sample size is an important 
354 
parameter defining the statistical power of tests applied to survey data (Strayer 1999, Lewis 
355 
& Gould 2000). Wildlife managers, researchers and conservationists using longer search 
356 
units in bridge surveys would benefit from increasing time and labour efforts in order to 
357 
maintain large samples (i.e. visits at many bridges), if statistical tests applied to bridge 
358 


 
17
survey data are to have sufficient power for detecting trends in river otter occurrence in a 
359 
surveyed area.
