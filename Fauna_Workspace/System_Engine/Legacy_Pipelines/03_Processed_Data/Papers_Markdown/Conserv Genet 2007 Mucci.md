BRIEF COMMUNICATION
Sex identiﬁcation of Eurasian otter (Lutra lutra) non-invasive
DNA samples using ZFX/ZFY sequences
Nadia Mucci Æ Ettore Randi
Received: 3 January 2007 / Accepted: 8 February 2007 / Published online: 15 March 2007
 Springer Science+Business Media B.V. 2007
Abstract
We developed a new PCR/RFLP system
targeted to amplify and cut a segment of the ZFX/ZFY
gene in non-invasive otter (Lutra lutra) samples. This
assay produced one sex-speciﬁc fragment in females
(XX
genotypes)
and
two
fragments
in
males
(XY genotypes), and is intrinsically more reliable than
alternative systems (e.g., SRY genes) that are based on
the ampliﬁcation of a single Y-linked fragment. The
new primer pair correctly identiﬁed the sex of 23 DNA
extracted from sexed otter tissues. This procedure was
used to sex unknown DNA extracted from otter scats.
A multi-tube approach led to identify the sex of 72/91
(79%) samples, using a minimum of two PCR repli-
cates. This procedure is currently used in non-invasive
genetic monitoring of Italian otter populations.

Introduction
Direct persecution, wetland pollution and reclamation
led otter populations (Lutra lutra) to strongly decline
and eventually disappear in the last few centuries from
most of their former range in central Europe and Italy
(Conroy and Chanin 2002; Ruiz-Olmo et al. 2002).
Recently otter populations started to expand again,
naturally or sustained by local reintroduction projects
(Jeffries et al. 1986; Mason and Macdonald 2004).
Non-invasive genetic methods (Waits and Paetkau
2005) are being increasingly integrated in otter moni-
toring projects, aiming to obtain information on dis-
tribution range, population size and genetic variability
(Dallas et al. 2003; Hung et al. 2004; Ha`jkova` et al.
2006; Prigioni et al. 2006). Accurate identiﬁcation of
gender is an additional piece of information, which
contributes to determine demographic composition
(sex ratio) and kinship relationships, and eventually
conﬁrm the identiﬁcation of multi-sampled individual
genotypes (Smith et al. 2006). Gender in otter (Dallas
et al. 2000) and other mustelids (Lynch and Brown
2006) has been usually identiﬁed by PCR-ampliﬁcation
of the chromosome Y-linked SRY gene (Palsbøll et al.
1992). This system, however, produces only male-spe-
ciﬁc ampliﬁcations. Null ampliﬁcations might originate
both from females or ampliﬁcation failures. Genes
mapping
in
both
male
and
female
genomes
(i.e., mitochondrial sequences) should be positively co-
ampliﬁed (Palsbøll et al.1992; Kamimura et al. 1997),
although false negative results could be not completely
excluded (Ortega et al. 2004). Moreover, SRY markers
might cause additional uncertainty in genotyping non-
invasive samples, which are characterized by highly
degraded DNA and frequent PCR failures (Fernando
and Melnick 2001; Ortega et al. 2004). Positive results
in non-invasive samples should be conﬁrmed by mul-
tiple PCR replicates, thus raising the laboratory costs
(Lynch and Brown 2006). Aim of this study is to de-
velop a procedure for reliable molecular sex identiﬁ-
cation
of
non-invasive
otter
samples
(scats
and
spraints) typing a gene that can be ampliﬁed simulta-

Methods
We ampliﬁed a portion of the zink-ﬁnger protein genes
(ZFX/ZFY; Luoh and Page 1994), which map in X and Y
chromosomes and that have been successfully used in
other mammalian species (i.e., Aasen and Medrano
1990; Be´rube´ and Palsbøll 1996; Garcı`a et al. 1997,
Fernando and Melnick 2001; Lucchini et al. 2002, Ort-
ega et al. 2004), using the following two-steps protocol:
1)
total DNA, extracted from 23 tissue samples of
sexed wild otters collected in Spain, Norway,
Germany, France and Italy, was ampliﬁed using
primers P1-5EZ and P2-3EZ (Aasen and Medrano
1990). PCR reactions were performed in 10 lL
volumes containing 25 ng DNA, 1.5 mM MgCl2,
0.15 lM of each primer, 400 lM dNTP, 10 mM
PCR buffer 10X and 0.05 U Taq polymerase
(Eppendorf), using a GeneAmp PCR System 9700
(Applied Biosystems) and the following thermal
cycle: 94C for 2 min; 40 cycles of 94C for 30 s,
55C for 30 s, 72C for 45 seconds; a ﬁnal exten-
sion at 72C for 10 min. The PCR products were
puriﬁed using ExoSap (Amersham), sequenced
with both forward and reverse primers in an ABI
Prism 3100 Genetic Analyser (Applied Biosys-
tems), aligned and analysed using the software
SeqScape 2.0 (Applied Biosystems). We obtained
an alignment of 394 bp, showing four polymorphic
sites in males (XY genotypes; GenBank accession
no
EF409420),
which
were
monomorphic
in
females (XX genotypes; GenBank accession no
EF409419).
2)
The alignment was used to design a new PCR
primer
(ZFXYRb
5¢–TTGTTCAGCTGTCT-
CATATTCACA–3¢),
with
its
5¢-nucleotide
annealing at pos. n. 112 of the otter ZFX/ZFY
sequence, which was labelled with 6-FAM, and
used with primer P1-5EZ to amplify a shorter
fragment of 180 bp including the ﬁrst male-linked
polymorphic site: C/T in males, C/C in females
(pos. n. 12 of the otter ZFX/ZFY sequence). We
used Bioedit v.7.0.1 (Hall 1999) to identify the
restriction enzymes BsmI that cut this polymor-
phic site. Isoschizomer Mva1268I (M-Medical) was
used to digest the ampliﬁed fragment, which was
automatically analysed with an ABI Prism 3100
Genetic Analyser and software GeneMapper v.4.0
(Applied Biosystems).
Results and Discussion
The genotyping procedure led to identify one fragment
(119 bp) in otter females and two fragments (119 and
178 bp) in males (Fig. 1a). All DNA samples collected
from known males and females were correctly sexed.
We applied this procedure to sex 91 non-invasive otter
faecal samples collected in 2001–2004 in southern Italy,

which were assigned to 37 distinct individual geno-

ten microsatellite loci originally developed by Dallas
et al. (2002). These samples were sexed using a multi-
tube approach (with four PCR replicates), and the
same procedure described above, except for the num-
ber of PCR cycles that was increased to 55. Also these
samples were split in two groups, one showing two
ZFX/ZFY fragments (119 and 178 bp; putative males),
and one showing a single fragment (119 bp; putative
females). Using the multi-tube procedure we sexed 72
samples (i.e., 79% of the total), 38 being females and
34 males, with a minimum of two PCR replicates. Ten
samples (11%) did not yield any PCR product, and
nine samples (10%) did not ampliﬁed consistently
across four replicates.
Aiming to check for unspeciﬁc ZFX/ZFY products,
we ampliﬁed DNAs extracted from prey remains (i.e.,
Leuciscus cephalus, Chondrostoma genei, Alburnus
spp., Barbus spp., Rutilus spp., Rana sp., Bufo sp.,
Austropotamobius sp.) that were found in some of the
otter scats. These DNAs produced some unspeciﬁc
ampliﬁcations with molecular weights clearly out of the
range of the otter speciﬁc fragments (119–178 bp),
except for the ampliﬁcation of one fragment 176 bp
long in DNA samples from Cyprinidae ﬁsh. This
fragment, however, is well identiﬁable as a peak very
close to the true otter fragment (Fig. 1b and 1c).
The new ZFX/ZFY primers described in this study
are more reliable than SRY markers, particularly in
non-invasive genetics, because they minimize the risk
of false negatives, which can frequently occur even if
another bi-parental marker is co-ampliﬁed (Ortega
et al. 2004). However, PCR failures and allelic dropout
are always frequent in non-invasive genotyping, and
PCR replicates should be carried out also to achieve
reliable sex identiﬁcations (Lynch and Brown 2006). In
our lab we use a multi-tube protocol based on four
PCR replicates for each marker and sample to geno-
type non-invasive otter samples (mainly scats; Prigioni
et al. 2006). Consensus genotypes are constructed and
their likelihood in the population is assessed using a
reliability analysis procedure developed by Miller et al.
(2002). DNA in otter scats and spraints is apparently
exposed to very rapid degradation processes (Dallas
et al. 2003; Ha´jkova´ et al. 2006), thus producing pro-
portions of PCR success much lower than in other
carnivores (e.g., 20–30% positive PCRs in otters vs.
60–80% in Italian wolves or Scandinavian brown bear;
Lucchini et al. 2002; Bellemain et al. 2005). Therefore
genotyping otter scats, including sex determination,
should rely on stringent quality control of the labora-
tory procedures.
