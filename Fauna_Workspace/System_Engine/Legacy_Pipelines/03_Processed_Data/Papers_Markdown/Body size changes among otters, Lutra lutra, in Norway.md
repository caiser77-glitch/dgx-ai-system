Abstract
Using museum data of adult specimens
whose sex, age, and locality are known, we studied
temporal and geographical body size trends among the
otter, Lutra lutra, in Norway. We found that body size
of the otters increased during the last quarter of the
twentieth century, and suggest that this trend is related
to increased food availability from ﬁsh farming and
possibly also to energy saving due to elevated sea
temperatures. Birth year and death year explained 38.8
and 43.5%, respectively, of the variation in body size.
Body size of otters was positively related to latitude,
thus conforming to Bergmann’s rule.

1 Introduction
Animals are exposed to various environmental factors
that may inﬂuence their body size, and many studies
have documented changes in body size of birds and
mammals that might be related to changed environ-
mental conditions. Among these factors are predation,
ambient temperature, interspeciﬁc competition and
food availability. For example, body weight of the
great tit Parus major in Britain changed in accordance
with predation pressure (Gosler et al. 1995); body
weight and wing length of the Galapagos medium
ground ﬁnch (Geospiza fortis) changed following two
drought periods of only 2–3 years each (Grant and
Grant 1995); skull size of the great Japanese wood
mouse Apodemus speciosus and the Alaskan masked
shrew Sorex cinereus increased during the second half
of the twentieth century, apparently due to an im-
proved diet (Yom-Tov and Yom-Tov 2004; Yom-Tov
and Yom-Tov 2005, respectively); and body size of
several species of passerine birds (Yom-Tov 2001;
Yom-Tov et al. 2006) and a rodent (Smith et al. 1998)
decreased within several decades, apparently due to
increased ambient temperatures and in accordance
with Bergmann’s rule.
Nutritional conditions affect growth and ultimately
body size (Henry and Ulijaszek 1996; Lindstrom 1999).
This is exempliﬁed by the observations that during the
last two centuries body height and weight in human

(presumably due to hunting pressure and a decrease in

among several species of carnivores in Israel have been
attributed to an improved diet (Yom-Tov et al. 2003;
Yom-Tov 2003).
Global mean surface temperatures have risen by
0.6C since the late nineteenth century (IPCC 2001).
Global climate change may affect animals in two
opposite ways: those sensitive to heat may decrease in
body size, as predicted by Bergmann’s rule; while
shorter and warmer winters may save energy spent on
maintenance and increase food availability and, con-
sequently body size. Bergmann’s rule states ‘‘In warm
blooded animals, races from warm regions are smaller
than races from cold regions’’ (Mayr 1970). Hence, it is
reasonable to assume that, among animals sensitive to
ambient temperature, the recent increase in ambient
temperature will be followed by a decrease in body
size. An additional aspect is that elevated ambient
temperature, particularly during winter and spring,
may enhance primary production and food availability
for animals, and the improved food availability may
consequently inﬂuence various life history parameters.
For example, an advance of 6 days per generation in
the timing of breeding of the North American red
squirrel (Tamiasciurus hudsonicus) in the Yukon re-
gion, Canada, has been attributed to a large increase in
the availability of food to female squirrels that fol-
lowed an increase in ambient temperature (Reale et al.
2003).
In addition, changes in harvesting of wild organisms
and cultivation practices, including ﬁsh farming, may
have changed food availability for wild animals. In
Norway, the annual production of salmon and trout has
risen from a 100 t in 1971 to about 600,000 t in 2004,
and a recent survey revealed that on average as much
as 24% of the catch of salmon and trout in Norwegian
coastal waters are escaped farm ﬁsh (Esmark et al.
2005). This increasing food source is available for ot-
ters and may inﬂuence marine predators.
The Norwegian otter population was at a historical
low in the 1960s and 1970s, but has increased consid-
erably in numbers since then (Heggberget 1998). The
main food items of the coastal otters in Norway are
marine ﬁsh (Heggberget 1995). It is reasonable to as-
sume that the increased availability of salmonids has
affected the feeding ecology and condition of otters.
Museum collections can be regarded as an archive
for past and present biodiversity and can thus be the
basis for analyses of temporal changes in whole eco-
systems or within single species or populations. The
importance of such collections increases with the
length of the time they cover. The abovementioned
changes in world climate and food conditions for wild
animals have been related to changes in body size of
animals in several parts of the globe (see above). In the
present study we have used a large collection of otter,
Lutra lutra, skulls from Norwegian museums and re-
search institutions to explore change in body size over
time.
The aim of the study was to examine if skull size
(and by implication body size) of the otter in Norway
changed during the second half of the twentieth cen-
tury, and if it did, to suggest the most likely environ-
mental factors that affected skull size.
2 Material and methods
We examined the skulls of 1,194 otters collected ran-
domly by hunters, or deposited through licensed culls,
or following accidental death on roads or drowning.
Otters were collected between 1975 and 2004 from
coastal and fjord areas between 60 and 71N. Most
skulls are deposited at the Bergen Museum, University
of Bergen, the Natural History Museum, University of
Oslo, and at the Norwegian Institute for Nature Re-
search, Trondheim, Norway. We used only skulls with
known year of capture and locality of capture. Age was
estimated from skull criteria (1 year olds) and annual
growth layers of a canine tooth (Heggberget 1984). A
healthy, upper canine was preferred, while the least
damaged of the four canines was selected in animals
with tooth damage. The tooth was de-calciﬁed and
freeze-sectioned longitudinally through the central
plane of the apex, at 16 l section thickness, and col-
oured in hematoxylin. Cementum incremental lines
(annuli) were counted by microscope, at 100· magni-
ﬁcation. Skulls and/or teeth from ten known-age Eur-

Several skull measurements had been taken by cal-

selected because this was the single available mea-
surement for the highest number of skulls. Otters grow
very fast during their ﬁrst year, but skull size continues
to increase, albeit at a much slower rate, also in older

In order to account for the possible effect of latitude
and ambient temperature, we corrected ZB for latitude

latitude. We used the mean latitude for the munici-

detailed location was not available for many of the
specimens.
We used multiple regressions to test for the effects
of sex (categorical variable), age and latitude on ZB.
At the second stage we tested the effect of birth year or
death year on skull size (and thus body size) by using
the residual of ZB corrected for sex, age and latitude
obtained from the above multiple regressions, by using
weighted regression (each of the year points is weigh-
ted in proportion to the number of records of that
year). We tested the relation between carcass weight
and ZB by regression in order to explore how good a
predictor ZB is for body size. Data on ﬁsh production
in Norway were taken from Statistics Norway (http://
www.ssb.no).
3 Results
Mean ZB and carcass weight of male and female otters
sorted by age are presented in Table 1. There was a
highly signiﬁcant relationship between ZB and carcass
body weight (R2 = 0.58, F1,1142 = 1590.6, P < 0.0001).
ZB was signiﬁcantly related to sex, age and latitude
(R2 = 0.53, F3,1190 = 452.3, P < 0.0001; Table 2). Males
were larger than females, and latitude was positively
related to ZB, as predicted from Bergmann’s rule. All
interaction terms were non signiﬁcant. Residual ZB
was signiﬁcantly and positively related to birth year
(R2 = 0.39, F1,1141 = 725.72, P < 0.0001; Fig. 1a) and
death year (R2 = 0.44, F1,1141 = 876.18, P < 0.0001;
Fig. 1b), indicating that body size increased during the
fourth quarter of the twentieth century.
The annual production of salmon and trout in Nor-
way has risen from a 100 t in 1971 to about 600,000 t in
2004, and residual ZB is signiﬁcantly related to ﬁsh
production (Fig. 2).
4 Discussion
We found that 58.2% of the variation in body weight is
explained by ZB. Considering the fact that body weight
is a very variable parameter that may change daily, we
conclude that ZB is a good predictor of body size in the
otter. Birth year and death year explained 38.8 and
43.5% of the variation, respectively. It is commonly
believed that conditions during the fast growth period
(ﬁrst year in the otter) have a major effect on adult size
(Henry and Ulijaszek 1996; Lindstrom 1999), and
consequently the birth year is expected to have a larger
effect on ZB than does death year, but we found that
both birth and death years are related to ZB. We
suggest that this is explained by the continuous growth
of the skull until the age of 3–4 years and the condi-
tions during these years inﬂuence skull size (mean age
of our sample was 2.63 years, 0.07 SE).
Several factors may be responsible for the observed
changes in body size. Studies of otters’ diet during
1978–1992 indicated that Gadidae, mainly young cod

diet of marine-feeding otters is not known. However, a
change in diet composition related to a change in

The total biomass of coastal cod in Norway de-
clined from about 300,000 to 60,000 t between 1994
and 2005 (Anon 2005). This decline in cod biomass, of
which only a fraction is available to otters, has been
greatly exceeded by the increase in production of
farmed salmonids. The number of ﬁsh farms along the
Norwegian coast producing salmon and trout in-
creased from 55 in 1971 to about 900 in 2004. The
annual production of salmon and trout has risen from
100 t in 1971 to about 600,000 t in 2004. Residual ZB
is signiﬁcantly related to ﬁsh production (Fig. 2). We
believe that ﬁsh farming can affect the availability of
food for otters in at least three ways: easy access to
ﬁsh inside the farms, easy access to ﬁsh that escape
from the farms, and easy access to ﬁsh of different
species that concentrate outside the farms due to good
food availability.
Otters prey on ﬁsh in farms. Predation appears to be
more common where and when sea surface tempera-
tures are low, i.e. in the north, where more than 90% of
the ﬁsh farms have claimed that this occurs, especially
in winter and early spring (Johansen and Eliassen
1999). Otter cubs along the Norwegian coast are born
mainly in summer and early autumn (Heggberget and
Christensen 1994). These cubs grow to nearly adult size
during their ﬁrst winter and the following spring. Thus,
if otters feeding on farmed ﬁsh tend to grow to be
larger, then a higher frequency of ﬁsh-farm feeding
may partly also explain the northward increasing size
of otters.

revealed that on average as much as 24% of the catch
of salmon and trout in Norwegian coastal waters are
escaped farm ﬁsh (Esmark et al. 2005). A large part of
the salmon along the Norwegian coast is accordingly
escaped individuals. We believe that escaped ﬁsh are
easier to catch than wild ﬁsh and might therefore be an
important food resource for otters.
Fish farms have been found to attract wild ﬁsh from
larger areas around the farms (Dempster et al. 2004;
Machias et al. 2005). Carss (1993) found that most of
the ﬁsh eaten by shags (Phalacrocorax aristotilis) at ﬁsh
farms in western Scotland were wild ﬁsh that concen-
trated around the cages. We therefore suggest that
otters are also attracted to farms due to high density of
wild ﬁsh around the farms.
Although the relationship between ﬁsh production
and body size (Fig. 2) is not a result of a controlled
experiment, we suggest that otters increased in size
mainly due to higher food availability from farmed ﬁsh
and wild ﬁsh around farms. The suggestion that
recruitment of otters in Shetland, close to the Norwe-
gian coast, is food dependent (Kruuk et al. 1991) and
that populations of otters in fresh water may also be
food limited (Kruuk 1995), provide further support for
our suggestion that abundant food supply is the cause
of the observed increase in body size. On the other
hand, an increase in food availability may increase
otter population size, and this may affect food avail-
ability. However, while the increase in ﬁsh production
was exponential, the increase in body size was linear
(Fig. 2). Thus, the increase in ﬁsh production may have
affected both individual body size and population size.
A population index calculated since 1987 and based on
Norwegian road-killed otters indicate that the coastal
otter population increased by about 10% per year
during 1987–1995 (Heggberget 1998), in accordance
with an improved food availability hypothesis. How-
ever, since then, the population index has decreased at
a similar rate (T. M. Heggberget, unpublished data).
This decrease coincides with a decrease in the coastal
cod population (Anon 2005). Clearly, more detailed
studies on temporal otter diet and food availability are
needed in order to clarify these relationships.
Another possible factor that may contribute to the
increase in otter size is the increase in both sea and
land temperatures during the twentieth century. The
heat conductivity of water is 23 times greater than that
of air (Schmidt-Nielsen 1983), and the aquatic envi-
ronment poses a thermal challenge for small, elongated
animals such as otters. Coastal otters spend many
hours daily swimming and hunting in cold water
(Kruuk and Balhary 1990). Due to their long body and
very little fat, the metabolic rate of mustelids (includ-
ing otters) is higher than expected from their body
weight (King 1989). For insulation otters heavily rely
on the air layer in their fur, which loses its insulating
quality very quickly in seawater (Kruuk et al. 1994).
The metabolism of the otter is strongly affected by
water temperature, and Kruuk et al. (1994) found that
variation in water temperature accounted for 55% of
the variation in oxygen consumption of otters. Using
Eq. 1 in Kruuk et al. (1994) we calculated that, within a
temperature range of 3–18C, a 1C increase in ambi-
ent temperature decreases oxygen consumption by 6.4
or 12.4% of resting metabolic rate. The January–
March mean surface water temperatures along the
Norwegian coast were within the range 2 and 7C since
1970 (Sjøtun 2004), and any rise in the mean temper-
ature should have an appreciable effect on the otter’s
metabolism. February and August sea temperature
data for the second half of the twentieth century at
seven localities distributed throughout the study area
showed no signiﬁcant linear trend during the study
period (Aure and Strand 2001). However, at the three
stations on the southern part of the coast (latitude 61–
64N), February temperatures between 1988 and 2000
were above the mean (of the period 1936–1990) in all
but 2 years, while between 1975 and 1987 February
temperatures were evenly distributed above or below
the mean. These data may indicate a trend of an in-
crease in sea temperature along the southern part of
the Norwegian coast. We suggest that global warming
resulted in energy saving that could be diverted from
metabolism to growth, thus explaining a part of the
observed body size increase among otters.
The effects of changing sea temperatures on two ﬁsh
communities in the North Sea near the British coast
have been studied by Genner et al. (2004). They
showed that within a region, populations of the same
species may respond differently to climatic change,
possibly
owing
to
additional
local
environmental
determinants, interspeciﬁc ecological interactions and
dispersal capacity. Hence, presently it is difﬁcult to
predict species-level responses within geographically
differentiated communities.
In conclusion, we found that in Norway, the otter
increased in size during the last quarter of the twenti-
eth century. We suggest that the increase in otter size
was due to increased food availability from ﬁsh farm-
ing, and possibly also from energy saving attributable
to global warming.
