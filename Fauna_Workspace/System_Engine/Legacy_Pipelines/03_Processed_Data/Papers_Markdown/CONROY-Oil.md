THE THREAT OF OIL CONTAMINATION ON EURASIAN OTTER (LUTRA LUTRA) 
POPULATIONS AND THE NEED FOR A PAN-EUROPEAN CONTINGENCY PLAN FOR 
THEIR REHABILITATION 
 
James W. H. CONROY 
 
Celtic Environment Ltd., Old Mart Road, Torphins, Aberdeenshire AB31 4JG, Scotland, UK  
 email:  jim@celticenvironment.wanadoo.co.uk  
 
 
Abstract.   Eurasian otters are found on many European coasts and can form important cohorts of national 
populations.  Many of the major tanker routes between Europe, the Gulf States and America run parallel to these 
coasts.  In recent years there have been several incidents in which coasts have been impacted by spilt oil and 
otters have been effected.  Details of the effects and specific incidents in European waters are discussed, and the 
need for a Pan-European contingency plan for otter rehabilitation explored. 
 
Keywords:  Eurasian otter, Lutra lutra, oil spills, wildlife contingency plan, Europe 
 
 
1   INTRODUCTION 
 
Over the past five decades there have been significant declines in populations of Eurasian 
otters (Lutra lutra) throughout much of their range, but especially in Western Europe where 
concern had been expressed about the survival of the species (REUTHER AND FESTETICS, 1980; 
MACDONALD AND MASON, 1994).  The causes of the decline are not fully understood, but 
industrial pollution by organochlorines and heavy metals, in particular mercury, as well as 
habitat loss and destruction of fish and invertebrate populations, have all been identified as 
contributors to the species’ decline (CONROY, YOXON AND GUTLEB, 2002).   
Today the otter is protected in most European countries by national law, while within 
the European Union the species is subject to internal legislation, under Annex II of Council 
Directive EC/92/43 on the conservation of natural habitats of wild flora and fauna - the 
Habitats Directive.  This requires member states to establish Special Areas of Conservation 
(SACs) that will incorporate sufficient area for the species to successfully live and breed.  The 
Directive not only forbids the killing of these animals, but also prohibits destruction or 
disturbance of their breeding dens and resting areas.  The Eurasian otter is also listed in 
Annex I of CITES (the Convention for the Trade in Endangered Species), considered 
vulnerable by the IUCN (International Union for the Conservation of Nature and Natural 
Resources) and is protected under the Bern Convention which also aims to conserve both 
species and their habitats, including breeding and resting sites. 
While recent reviews of the status of the species throughout its range have shown that 
there has been a significant recovery in many countries, there are still many regions where 
numbers continue to decline and the otter remains rare or extinct (CONROY, MELISCH AND 
CHANIN, 1998; CONROY AND CHANIN 2002a & b).  The Eurasian otter was for a long time 
classified as ‘vulnerable’ in the International Union for the Conservation of Nature and 
Natural Resources (IUCN) Red List of Threatened Species.  However, in the most recent issue 
of the Red List (2004), the status of the species has been lowered to ‘near threatened’ (IUCN, 
2004). 
Coastal otters are known to be at risk from oil spills. In this paper the importance of, 
and risks to, coastal Eurasian otters in areas of Europe likely to be affected by oil spills are 
reviewed.  The impacts of some of the more serious incidents are described and the rationale 
for the establishment of a pan-European contingency plan for otter rehabilitation given. 
 


 
2   THE IMPORTANCE OF COASTAL OTTER POPULATIONS IN EUROPE 
 
The Eurasian otter is found in both freshwater and marine habitats.  In many countries, coastal 
otters form an important component of the national population.  For example, HARRIS ET AL. 
(1995) estimated that the United Kingdom population in the mid-1980s was around 7,350 
adults, of which some 6,600 were found in Scotland.  On the Shetland Islands, the otter 
population in 1988 was estimated between 700 and 900 individuals (KRUUK ET AL., 1989), 
between 9.5 and 12.5% of the UK population, making the Islands’ population of national and 
international importance (CONROY, 1998).  In addition, three national surveys of the UK have 
shown that otters are most common on the coasts of the northern and western islands, and 
north and west mainland of Scotland (GREEN AND GREEN, 1980, 1987, 1997).  In Wales, G 
LILES (personal communication) reports that the coastal otters form an important part of the 
country’s population, with the majority living in the south west where the shoreline is most 
suited to their lifestyle.  It is also the coastline that is most at risk from tanker traffic entering 
the refinery at Milford Haven (EDWARDS AND SIME, 1998).  Thus, today, coastal otters in the 
U.K., but especially Scotland, form the main component of the British population.  The recent 
(fourth) survey of otters in England has shown that the recovery recorded in the previous 
surveys is continuing with over 34% of the sites visited showing evidence of otter presence, 
an increase of 12% from the last survey in the early 1990s (CRAWFORD, 2003). Otters are also 
widespread in Ireland (CHAPMAN AND CHAPMAN, 1982), where the population is also of 
international importance and many live on the coasts. 
Coastal otters are known to exist in Portugal, especially along the north and south west 
coasts, being especially common in Vincentine (Algarve) (TRINDADE, FARINHA AND 
FLORENCIO, 1998).  BEJA (1989) studied the distribution of the species along 120 kilometres 
of coast around Saint Vincent, and found evidence of presence at 36 of 37 (97%) sites visited. 
In Spain, J. RUIZ-OLMO (personal communication) estimated that no less than 5% of 
the country’s otter population live along several hundred kilometres of coastline.  The species 
is especially common in Galicia and Asturia, along the north and west coasts of the country, 
bordering the Atlantic Ocean and Cantabrian Sea (RUIZ-OLMO AND DELIBES, 1988). Over the 
past 25 years these coasts have experienced three major oil spills – Uriquiola [100k: crude], 
Aegean Sea [73.5k: crude] and Prestige [77k: crude] (NIJKAMP AND CONROY, 2003a).  Otters 
also frequent the shores of south west Andalucía, near the border with Portugal (RUIZ-OLMO 
AND DELIBES, 1988). 
Coastal otters in France also account for about 5% of the national population.  The 
species, however, tends to live in fragmented, isolated populations, the principal 
concentrations being along the shores and estuaries of north and south Brittany, and the 
offshore islands and brackish wetlands of the French Atlantic front (e.g. Noirmoutier and 
Oleroná) (L. LAFONTAINE, personal communication).  There have been four oiling incidents 
along these coasts in the past 25 years - Amoco Cadiz [223k tonnes: crude], Tanio [6 tonnes: 
No. 2 fuel oil], Bohlen [14k tonnes: crude] and Erika [20k: diesel oil]. 
Although the otter is currently extinct or very rare in Belgium and the Netherlands, the 
species was once common.  According to A DE JONGH (personal communication), otters used 
to travel along the Dutch coasts.  The species is currently being re-introduced to the 
Netherlands, and should this prove successful, there is every likelihood that these animals will 
once again make use of the coasts. A recent incident, involving the loss of a relatively small 
amount of fuel oil [170 tonnes], from a sunken car transporter, the Tricolor, resulted in the 
contamination of thousands of birds, highlighting that an oil incident need not be solely the 
responsibility of tankers, and that even small losses of oil in the wrong place and the wrong 
time can cause immense damage (NIJKAMP AND CONROY, 2003b). 


Coastal otters are the most important cohorts of the population in Norway.  This is 
confirmed by the fact that nearly all the animals killed on the roads in central and northern 
Norway are from coastal municipalities (T. HEGGBERGET, personal communication). 
Before numbers decreased in Sweden, the species was very common along the coasts 
of at least the southern part of the country, between Strömstad and Gävle.  At present the 
coastal situation is unclear because there have been no recent surveys of these areas.  
However, there are reports of coastal living otters from the archipelagos of Stockholm, 
Vastervik and Strömstad.  The otter population in southern Sweden remains very small, 
although there is now evidence that over the past 15 years, numbers are again on the increase 
throughout the country (SJÖÅSEN, 1996; T. SJÖÅSEN, personal communication). 
In general coastal otters are not seen regularly in Denmark and are therefore not 
thought to be an important part of the population (A.B. MADSEN, personal communication).  
They do, however, inhabit salt/brackish waters primarily on the north eastern part of Jutland, 
especially in the vicinity of Limfjord.  However, numbers continue to increase in Denmark 
and new areas are being colonised.  It is thus possible that in the future they may come to 
occupy coastal areas. 
 
 
3   THE EFFECT OF OIL ON OTTERS 
 
Exposure to oil has long been known to trouble several species of marine mammals 
(ENGELHARDT, 1983; GERACI AND ST. AUBIN, 1990).  Oil contamination affects otters in a 
number of different ways, both externally and internally.  The animals have relatively little fat 
and are therefore dependent on air being trapped between the two layers of hair in their fur for 
insulation and buoyancy (TARASOFF, 1974).  Any agent causing this insulating layer to 
become ineffective results in individuals becoming lethargic and, if not treated quickly, dying.  
Oil is such an agent; it can matt the fur, so affecting the animal’s ability to thermoregulate 
resulting in either hypo- and/or hyperthermia.  Hypothermia is a major cause of vascular 
congestion, gastrointestinal lesions, centrilobular liver necrosis, pneumonia and ultimately 
death, and even should an animal survive the hypothermic event, it may suffer impaired organ 
function as a result of ischemic hypoxia (WILLIAMS, O’CONNOR AND NIELSEN, 1995; 
WILLIAMS AND DAVIS, 1995). 
There have been few experiments on the effects of oil on otters.  Two sea otters 
(Enhydra lutris) were released into a pool partially contaminated by oil.  The animals 
appeared to avoid the oil, but surfaced and came into contact with it, and in each instance 
death occurred within 24 hours of contamination (SINIFF ET AL., 1982).  WILLIAMS ET AL. 
(1988) using sea otter pelts showed that heat conductivity increased about four-fold once the 
fur was coated with oil.  When about 20% of the body of the sea otter is covered with oil the 
heat exchange between the body core and surroundings increases by 1.8 (DAVIS ET AL., 1988). 
Oiled otters groom their fur more frequently, than unoiled individuals, leading to an 
increase in the amount of oil in the digestive tract with a subsequent increase in the problems 
associated with this (COSTA AND KOOYMAN, 1982).  Eating contaminated food can also lead 
to an increase in the ingestion of oil, causing a number of problems including emphysema, 
anaemia, and hypoglycaemia.  In addition, otters caught in freshly spilt oil are exposed to the 
highly volatile toxic compounds, such as hydrocarbons, which can accumulate in the blood 
and tissue. 
Fuller accounts of the effects of oil on otters and other marine mammals can be found 
in GERACI AND ST. AUBIN (1990), LOUGHLIN (1994) as well as WILLIAMS AND DAVIS, (1995). 
 
 


4   OIL SPILLS AND OTTERS IN AMERICA 
 
The effects of oil on marine living and coastal otters in America were clearly demonstrated by 
the Exxon Valdez spill in Alaska in March 1989 (see BAYHA AND KORMENDY, 1990; 
LOUGHLIN, 1994; LOUGHLIN, BALLACHEY AND WRIGHT, 1996). 
It was estimated that up to 4,000 sea otters were killed by the oil (DOROFF ET AL., 
1993).  An additional 357 animals were captured and taken into care.  Of these, only 197 were 
‘successfully’ rehabilitated and released, but many of these suffered increased mortality and 
reduced breeding performance.  For example, only 22 of 45 released otters fitted with radio 
tags, successfully survived their first winter (MONNETT ET AL., 1990; BALLACHEY, BODKIN 
AND DE GRANGE, 1994; ESTES, 1998). 
In addition to the sea otters, the Canadian river otter (Lontra canadensis), which in 
Alaska can also live on the coasts, suffered as a result of the spill.  Few river otter carcasses 
were found suggesting the immediate effect of the spill on this species was not too serious.  
Later research, however, found several differences in the otters’ behaviour, including changes 
in diet, home range size and increased haptoglobin levels in blood (DUFFEY ET AL., 1994).  
BOWYER ET AL. (1993) concluded that any major effects on the river otter populations caused 
by the oil spill may have been delayed.  A fuller account of the effects of this spill on the river 
otter can be found in BOWYER ET AL. (2003). 
 
 
5   THE THREAT OF OIL CONTAMINATION TO COASTAL LIVING OTTERS IN EUROPE 
 
All the European countries mentioned above have coasts that are adjacent to the main oil 
tanker routes between the Middle East and Europe and/or the North Sea oil fields and, Europe 
and North America.  Some of the coasts along the North Sea, especially Shetland and Norway 
are also potentially at risk from spills or blow outs from oil rigs or drilling activities currently 
operating in the North Sea (CONROY, 1992; HEGGBERGET AND MOSEID, 1995).  Also some 
countries have either oil terminals or refineries on coasts which are well used by otters.  For 
example, in 1998, the largest oil terminal in Europe at Sullom Voe, Shetland handled over 
300 tanker movements and shipped out over 31,000,000 barrels of North Sea crude oil.  This 
facility is situated at the heart of some of the Islands’ best otter habitat (CONROY AND 
FRENCH, 1985).

incidents where in excess of 1,000 tonnes of oil has been lost) on coasts with populations of

Otters were at risk in at least five of these incidents – Esso Bernicia, Braer, Sea 
Empress, Erika and Prestige.  In addition, two other oil spills - Torrey Canyon (119,000 
tonnes – crude on 18 March 1967 off the Scilly Islands, England and Haven (144,000 tonnes 
– crude on 11 April 1991) off Genova in Italy occurred in areas where the species might have 
lived on the coasts prior to its decline. 
 
Esso Bernicia 
Following the loss of some 1,200 tonnes of bunker C fuel oil from the Esso Bernicia at 
Sullom Voe, Shetland in December 1978, at least 13 otters died (RICHARDSON, 1979).  This is 
probably an underestimate because it relates only to carcasses found, a number of other otters 
were seen oiled, and it is highly likely that some animals died in their holts or in areas which 
were not searched.  The cause of death in five carcasses examined was found to be oil related 
– haemorrhagic gastroenteropathy (BAKER ET AL., 1981).   
 
Braer 
The volume of oil lost when the Braer ran aground on the south coast of Shetland in January 
1993 was great (85,000 tonnes), making it, at that time, one of the world’s (13th) largest oil 
spills.  The combination of events, including the weather during the spill and type of oil, were 
unique and meant that the impacts were not as serious as they might have been and the threat 
to the rich ecological habitats and species of the area were minimum (DAVIS AND TOPPING, 
1997). 
    Although there are large numbers of otters on Shetland, few are found along the southern 
part of the Islands (KRUUK ET AL., 1989), and because no serious slicks impacted the coasts on 
the northern and western parts of the Islands, where the species is more numerous, the effect 
of the spill on the otter population was minimal (CONROY, KRUUK AND HALL, 1997).  During 
the two weeks immediately following the incident, around 20 otters were seen along the 15 
kilometres of coastline around the site of the grounding.  Some were reported as being oiled, 
while some were observed swimming in oil sheens.  Six animals were found dead or dying, 
but none of the fatalities could be directly linked to the oil spill.  Three cubs were taken into 
care, their mothers apparently abandoning two, but there is no evidence to support the 
statement that their mother died from oil contamination (SCOTTISH OFFICE, 1993). Other 
effects included the disappearance of demersal fish, the main prey of otters, from the 
immediate vicinity of the wreck for at least eight months. 
 
Sea Empress 
In February 1996, the tanker Sea Empress ran aground near the mouth of Milford Haven, 
south west Wales.  In the weeks immediately after the incident, the coasts were surveyed for 
otters.  None were seen, but this does not mean that individuals were not affected by the spill.  
Coastal otters tend to live in holes, are usually solitary and are largely nocturnal, so it is 
possible that contaminated animals might have returned to the ‘safety’ of their dens and died.  
Such carcasses would never be found.  Also there was ample evidence from the presence of 
otter faeces that the species were frequenting the coasts (G. LILES personal communication).  
[It was unfortunate that no faeces were collected and analysed for the presence of oil 
contaminants.] 
 
Erika 
In December 1999, the tanker Erika broke in two and voided about 20,000 tonnes of diesel oil 
into the sea and polluted several hundred kilometres of shoreline of south Brittany, the fourth 
such incident in these waters in the past 25 years.  Invertebrates, fish and especially several 
thousands of seabirds were affected.  An extensive impact assessment programme of more 


than 35 research projects, funded by the government of France is being co-ordinated by the 
Institut National de l'Environnement Industriel et des Risques (INERIS) and Institut Français 
pour l'Exploitation de la Mer (IFREMER).  
Although no dead otters were recovered during the initial three incidents, this is not 
too surprising, because at that time, the threats of oil contamination to this species were not 
fully understood and numbers could have been very low or the species might have been 
locally extinct. Again no dead or alive oiled otters were found, but a few oiled seals were 
observed.  This does not mean that this oil spill had, nor will have, no impact on coastal otter 
survival, especially in terms of accumulation in the food chain. A study is currently being 
completed to determine the presence of coproporphyrins and vanadium (as Erika's oil 
'signature') in both otter spraints (from coastal areas) and marine mammal tissues 
(LAFONTAINE AND HASSANI, in progress). All the results will be presented and then published 
following a specific Colloquium at Nantes in November 2003 (L. LAFONTAINE (personal 
communication).   
 
Prestige 
On 13 November 2002, the tanker Prestige suffered hull damage near the coast of Galicia 
(Spain). It was towed to open sea where, on 19 November, it broke in two sinking in water 
some 3.5 kilometres deep, about 275 kilometres west of Vigo.  Prior to sinking, the tanker 
could have lost 20,000 tonnes of heavy fuel oil.  After sinking, oil, in amounts of ca 125 
tonnes a day, continued to leak through a series of holes. Later the French submarine Nautile 
sealed most of these and leakage was reduced to ca. 2 tonnes a day.  When the vessel broke, it 
was clear that bird and mammal populations were at risk along hundreds of kilometres of 
Galician coast, an area well known for its important coastal-living population of Eurasian 
otters.   
 
While the majority of interest was attached to the collection and rehabilitation of 
seabirds, other groups of animals were also affected by the spill.  According to RAFAEL 
ROMERO AND JORDI RUIZ-OLMO (personal communications), four otters were found dead on 
the coasts of Galicia between November 2002 and January 2003.  Of these, it is probable that 
the death of one (found some 40-50 kilometres north of the oiled beaches) was not related to 
the Prestige.  Three were oiled, the other was a cub, the cause of death at this stage is 
uncertain.  Between seven and 11 otters were observed, of these five had at least partially 
oiled fur, while a further two were seen in contaminated areas. One or two animals were 
reported to have died of starvation.  This could have been indirectly caused by the spill, 
animals having difficulty in obtaining food, the prey species largely disappearing from 
inshore waters.  A similar situation occurred in Shetland, where no demersal fish, the otters’ 
main prey, were caught in traps set in the immediate vicinity of the Braer (CONROY, KRUUK 
AND HALL, 1997).  An additional six otters were seen in oiled waters.  
 
 
6   OTHER INCIDENTS AND THREATS 
 
In Norway, where coastal otters live, animals tend to be found along all the coasts and on the 
offshore islands at very high densities.  They are, therefore, at risk from any significant oil 
spill.  HEGGBERGET AND MOSEID (1989) reported the case of at least one contaminated 
individual following the release of heavy fuel oil from the Deifovis off the coast of Helgeland.  
Over the succeeding month an otter with shabby fur was regularly seen.  It appeared to be 
avoiding the sea and may have been feeding on household waste.  Some weeks later such an 
animal was caught in the area with evidence of oil on its fur.  A post mortem revealed a strong 
degree of emaciation and moderate gastric catarrh, which could have been associated with an 


earlier ingestion of oil (GRIFFITHS, ØRITSLAND AND ØRITSLAND, 1987).  Use of a heat 
sensitive camera showed that the insulation of the oil affected area of fur was much reduced. 
In addition to direct oiling, otters are also threatened by eating contaminated prey.  For 
example, following a ship wreck on the coast of Norway in 1992, the incidence of bird in the 
diet of the otters increased by a factor of 7-8 (T. HEGGBERGET personal communication). 
 
Much of Sweden’s petro-chemical industry is located on the country’s west coast and 
these are potentially the coasts at risk of oil pollution because of the tanker traffic.  These are 
also the coasts where the expanding Swedish populations are likely to recolonise, and so too 
will be at risk to oil pollution (T. SJÖÅSEN personal communication). 
The major threat to otters in Denmark in the future would be along the east coast, 
between Fünen and Sealand, where the tanker routes are close to land (A.B. MADSEN personal 
communication).  
Another major problem, highlighted especially from Scandinavian countries, is the 
threat caused by vessels cleaning tanks while at sea.  The source of this pollution is usually 
difficult to identify, the first evidence being the appearance of dead sea birds on the shore.  
The impact of such chronic pollution on otters is difficult to assess, and may be more of a 
threat to small cubs rather than adults.  In a study on the impacts of such oil incidents on grey 
seals (Halichoerus grypus) EKKER, LORENSTEN AND RØY (1992) highlighted the risk to young 
seals.  Between 1982 and 1985, up to half the pups born on the Foran Archipelago in Norway 
were found oiled.   
The recent incident (February 2003) in France, Belgium and the Netherlands 
highlighted that risks are not only restricted to tankers and large spills.  As part of the salvage 
operation on the sunken Tricolor in the English Channel, 170 tonnes of oil were lost – the 
result was nearly 9,000 bird casualties in Belgium alone (NIJKAMP AND CONROY, 2003b).  For 
France, Belgium and the Netherlands the figure was 18,451 casualties. An overview of the

7   THE NEED FOR A PAN-EUROPEAN OTTER REHABILITATION PROGRAMME 
 
With the ever increasing movement of oil by sea, as well as the development of new oil fields 
offshore (e.g. the north east Atlantic to the west of the Shetland Islands) and the increase in 
otter numbers, populations are increasingly likely to be exposed to the threat of oil from spills 
caused by tankers either colliding or running aground, or from oil lost from offshore 
installations.

What can be done to reduce the threat to otters from spilt oil?  It is during the initial 
days following a spill that many of the most badly oiled animals are found.  It is therefore 
important to have a wildlife contingency plan in place to ensure that rehabilitation can begin 
as soon as possible after notification of the possible release of oil.  Unfortunately, there are 
relatively few such plans in existence in Europe.  CONROY (1992) produced one for Mobil 


North Sea Ltd., in which he detailed the effects of oil on wildlife, identified the key players 
and equipment, as well as protocols for rehabilitation.   
 
In Spain, there is currently no contingency plan to deal with oiled wildlife.  However, 
in the wake of the Prestige it is hoped that new rehabilitation facilities will be built in Galicia 
and that such a plan will be developed.  It will be important to ensure that the plan does 
include information on otters.  In France, the response appears to be ad hoc, dependent very 
much on the government and voluntary sectors.  In Scandinavia, the general policy is that 
contaminated animals are killed humanely.  This, however, may have to change in areas 
where otters are being re-introduced or the population is recovering naturally.   
 
What is needed now to protect the coastal otters of Europe from oil spills is a pan-
European plan for their rehabilitation.  The plan should be incorporated in a wider plan to deal 
with the care and rehabilitation of all oiled wildlife.  It should include the best protocols for 
the capture, cleaning, holding facilities and subsequent rehabilitation of the species, identify 
the main rehabilitation experts in Europe and have facilities available to make use of in the 
event of a spill anywhere on the continent where otters are likely to be impacted.  Part of the 
plan should also include a means of accrediting rehabilitation groups or individuals who wish 
to handle otters (much damage can be done if animals are not handled properly).  This species 
is very susceptible to stress and those dealing with these animals should be fully aware of this 
and be trained to reduce the effects of stress.  There already exist in Europe a number of otter 
groups as well as the IUCN Otter Specialist Group.  Initial contact should be made through 
these groups and/or the various countries’ national animal welfare agencies.  Links should 
also be made with similar groups in the United States of America where many of the lessons 
learned from the Exxon Valdez incident have been incorporated in contingency plans [see 
WILLIAMS AND DAVIS (1995)]. 
 
The advantages of a Pan-European approach are clear.  With every incident our 
understanding of the problems will increase, and protocols can be revised accordingly.  It will 
mean that each spill is not handled by separate agencies many of which will have little or no 
expertise in otter rehabilitation;  rather a co-ordinated team will be available to respond.  If the 
idea of such a plan is accepted, it could also help minimise the claims to insurance companies 
after the spill, by, for example, reducing duplication of effort. 
 
When discussing oil spills and wildlife, the most usual question asked is ‘What has 
been the affect of the spill on the wildlife?’ In a recent letter to Marine Pollution Bulletin, 
HEUBECK ET AL. (in press) addressed this question.  They suggested that the most suitable 
approach would be an Oil Spill Impact Assessment (OSIA).  The aim of such an assessment is 
to assess properly the damage done by the spill, in terms of recording the numbers of 
casualties and predicting possible effects at the population level.  In their letter, the authors 
detail what this involves.  Although relating to seabirds, the OSIA approach could readily be 
applied to otters and other groups of animals that are likely to be affected by spilt oil.  The 
principle difference between seabirds and otters is that few otter carcasses will ever be found. 
It will therefore be necessary to look at other means of assessing impact.  The simplest 
approach would be to collect fresh otter faeces (spraints) and analyse them for the presence of 
polyaromatic hydrocarbons, indicative of oil contamination.  The use of OSIA therefore must 
be recognised by the relevant authorities as being integral to impact assessment and be 
included as part of any wildlife contingency plan.  Thus when an incident occurs, the concept 
of the OSIA is not only recognised, but procedures are in place to ensure its immediate 
implementation when a response is required to deal with oiled wildlife. 
 
The Sea Alarm Foundation (www.sea-alarm.org) was established with a view to 
developing an integrated pan-European contingency plan to deal with all oiled wildlife.  The 
otter plan should be part of this.  Links with Sea Alarm Foundation should also be maintained, 
and co-operation between otter specialists and them further developed.
