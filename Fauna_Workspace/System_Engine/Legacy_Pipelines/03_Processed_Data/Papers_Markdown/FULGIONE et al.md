SOUTHERN ITALIAN POPULATIONS OF OTTER (LUTRA LUTRA L.), SPATIAL 
REQUIREMENTS AND HABITAT PROFITABILITY IN CILENTO AND VALLO DI DIANO 
NATIONAL PARK 
 
D. FULGIONE, M. CANNETIELLO, S. DI BELLO, F. FINAMORE, L. FUSCO, D. RIPPA & M. MILONE. 
 
Department of Zoology, University of Naples Federico II. Via Mezzocannone 8 80134 Naples. Italy.  
email:  fulgione@unina.it 
 
Abstract. Southern Italian populations of Eurasian otters are disconnected from the wider European range of the 
species. The decline of the Italian populations is attributed to several aspects of river management since good 
habitat has been shown to be essential for otters. With a view to increasing the general knowledge of otter ecology, 
and in order to detect the major factors influencing the occurrence of the species, we studied the spatial distribution 
of the otter using data on habitat characteristics from a study area in the Cilento and Vallo di Diano National Park 
(South Italy). Spraints are preferentially put on rocks, boulders, or half-submerged boulders. Landscape analysis of 
otter presence in the surrounding areas shows a positive correlation with fragmented landscape. The results suggest 
some aspects useful to managing the river and its surroundings to ensure good preservation and to make the spread 
of otter populations easier. 
 
Keywords: Eurasian otter, Lutra lutra, Italian populations, ecological profitability 
 
 
1   INTRODUCTION 
 
The Eurasian otter (Lutra lutra) is a piscivorous predator whose populations declined 
dramatically over much of its geographic range during the last century, as a result of persecution, 
destruction of habitats, contamination and the availability of fish prey.  The species is now rare or 
absent in many areas (MACDONALD AND MASON, 1983; FOSTER-TURLEY, MACDONALD AND 
MASON, 1990; MACDONALD AND MASON, 1990), although recent evidence strongly suggests a 
recovery in many parts of its range (CONROY AND CHANIN, 2000).  In southern Italy there is an 
important otter population whose needs for conservation increase when one consider that it looks 
separate and isolated from other European populations.  In this article we focus attention on the 
ecological requirements of the otter based primarily on sprainting behaviour data.  This 
information has been correlated with a spatial analysis of some aspects of the habitat as landscape 
pattern and substrate used for marking.  This work was developed in the Cilento and Vallo di 
Diano National Park. 
 
2   METHODS 
 
2.1 Study areas 
The study areas belong to the Cilento and Vallo di Diano National Park (Figure 1), Campania.  
We considered three confluences in the basin of the River Calore where otter presence was 
assessed from a previous study (REGGIANI ET AL., 2001).  The Calore is a typical Mediterranean 
river:  it is 68 km long with annual capacity 4-70 m/s and generally keeps its water flow during 
the dry season.  The main tributaries are the Sammaro, Ripiti and Fasanella.  In all the rivers 
considered, the river bed consists principally of stones and rocks with occasional little sandy 
shores, while mud and earth are above all in the lower branches.  The bank-side vegetation is 


dominated by Salix sp., Alnus glutinosa, Populus sp., as well as shrub vegetation such as 
Equisetum, Dipsacacea and Graninacea families.

2.2 Surveys 
In each confluence three observations areas were established: rip-sam [Sammaro-Ripiti]; rip-fas 
[Ripiti-Fasanella]; fas-cal [Fasanella-Calore] (Figure 1A).  Each contained three 300 metre-long 
transects, with a common starting point coinciding with the junction of the river.  Censuses were 
carried out in accordance with the method suggested by the “Otter Specialist Group” (REUTHER 
ET AL., 2000).  Monitoring was monthly from June 2002 to May 2003. 
 
2.3 Site description 
The landscape complexity was determined through the patches variability in a square of 1.5 km2, 
centred on the confluence. Landscape fragmentation was recognised on the vegetation map 
produced to using packages ArcView 3.1 (ESRI, 1998) and Fragstat 1.0 (MCGARIGAL AND 
MARKS, 1995). 
 
 
3   RESULTS AND DISCUSSION 
 
Otter spraints were found close to each confluence in variable amount (Figure 1): rip-sam = 66, 
rip-fas = 106, and fas-cal = 74 (significantly different χ2 test, P  = 0.0042).  The landscape at the 
confluence of the rip-fas shows an elevated number of patches of rather similar dimensions.  By 
contrast, the confluence of cal-sam, shows a landscape characterised by few patches of varying 
dimensions, the rip-sam is in intermediary between these two extremes (Figure 2).  The otter 
presence (based on the sparints number) seems to be positively related to the landscape 
fragmentation. 
The otter tends to deposit spraints on prominent visible points within a narrow series of 
structures in which the rock is the most common (Figure 3).  Second in importance are gravel and 
finally the beach. Cement blocks, although apparently offering the same characteristics and 
positions as rock, were poorly represented (3%). Occasionally spraints are found in the grass.  
The otter preferred natural rock found in the water.  There appeared to be a difference in the 
selection of sprainting sites used on gravel, with a preference for the riverbanks rather than islets 
in the centre of the riverbed. 
In this paper we analysed the distribution of spraints in the Italian range of the otter, 
relating it to the landscape ecological variables (KRUUK AND CONROY, 1987). 
The otter populations seem to follow precise patterns based on landscape variability and 
suitability of some elements of the environment.  This pattern must be kept in mind when 
environmental management aims to lead to the spread of the species within its southern Italian 
range.  We think that all possible actions, developed in the Cilento and Vallo di Diano National 
Park, with the purpose of facilitating the expansion of the otter, cannot ignore the ecological 
demands and environmental characteristics of this species.
