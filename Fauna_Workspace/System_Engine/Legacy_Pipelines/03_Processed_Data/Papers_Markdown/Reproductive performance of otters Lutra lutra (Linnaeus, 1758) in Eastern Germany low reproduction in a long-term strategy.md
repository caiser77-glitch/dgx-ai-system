Reproductive performance of otters Lutra lutra 
(Linnaeus, 1758) in Eastern Germany: low reproduction 
in a long-term strategy
SILKE HAUER1*, HERMANN ANSORGE2 and OLAF ZINKE3
1Institute of Zoology, Martin-Luther-Universität, Domplatz 4, D-06108 Halle/Saale, Germany
2Staatliches Museum für Naturkunde, Am Museum 1, D-02806 Görlitz, Germany
3Museum der Westlausitz Kamenz, Macherstraße 140, D-01717 Kamenz, Germany
Received 2 January 2002; accepted for publication 30 May 2002
The reproductive status of female otters (Lutra lutra) was determined from an examination of 518 carcasses collected
from Eastern Germany between 1950 and 2001. In Germany otters mate throughout the year. Signiﬁcant seasonal
differences are evident with a distinct peak in summer. Females were in breeding condition between the ages of three
and 15 years, with the majority between six and nine years. The mean litter size at birth was 2.36, which was the
lowest when compared with other inland populations. There were no signiﬁcant differences in litter size for regions,
seasons or age. Prenatal losses account for 26.31%. The mortality between birth and ﬁrst appearance of cubs fol-
lowing their mother is about 29%. An effective reproduction rate of 78.4% was deduced from the known age structure
of the sample and the proportion of breeding females. Reproductively active females show a signiﬁcantly higher body
condition than non-reproductive females. Following these investigations into breeding status, suggestions on repro-
ductive output and the life history of the otter are discussed.

INTRODUCTION
Because of the decline of the otter (Lutra lutra) in
Germany, the species has been the subject of research
over a wide range of topics,  especially distribution
trends (Ansorge, 1994; Binner, 1997; Blanke, 1999;
Hauer & Heidecke, 1999; Teubner et al., 1999) and
investigations of mortality causes (Zinke, 1991; Dolch
et al., 1998; Zinke, 2000; Hauer, Ansorge & Zinke,
2002). Whereas mortality characterises loss from a
population, reproduction reveals information about
increases in numbers and population dynamics. Data
on reproductive performance are thus essential to
monitoring population dynamics. Reproductive fea-
tures of otters are, furthermore, of special interest
because the majority of mustelids, including the
closely-related L. canadensis (Hamilton & Eadie,
1964; Mead, 1989a) are seasonal and photosensitive
breeders (Amstislavsky & Ternovskaya, 2000), with
Lutra lutra as the sole exception.
The key to evolutionary success is in the production
of offspring that survive or are better at breeding
(Macdonald, 1995). Thus the life histories of animals
are adapted to maximise the reproductive value of
present and future breeding. Although there are sig-
niﬁcant correlations between phylogeny and life-
history variables across different taxonomic levels of
carnivores (Gittleman, 1993), the basic life-history
and reproductive patterns are considered to be
extremely ﬂexible (Oftedal & Gittleman, 1989);
interspeciﬁc variation is predicted to be great and
phylogenetic effects weak. Correspondingly to such
adaptations, the data of Mead & Wright (1983) and
Ferguson et al. (1996) suggest a shorter gestation
period without delayed implantation for mustelids in

Many studies of otter reproduction have been car-
ried out in Europe, showing considerable differences
in reproductive timing and seasonality. Even though
female otters were reported to be continuously
polyoestrous (Gorman et al., 1978; Mead, 1989a) and
therefore able to give birth throughout the year, repro-
duction ranges from nearly seasonal on the Shetland
Islands (Kruuk et al., 1987) and in Sweden (Erlinge,
1967, 1968) to non-seasonal birth with peaks at differ-
ent times of the year (Sidorovich, 1991; Heggberget
& Christensen, 1994; Tumanov & Sidorovich, 1994;
Beja, 1996; Elmeros & Madsen, 1999). However, there
are relatively little data giving deﬁnitive reproduction
rates based on carcass examination, and only a few
studies have been published on otter breeding in
Germany (Stubbe, 1969; Stubbe, 1977; Uthleb et al.,
1992; Ansorge et al., 1997; Hertweck & Schipke, 2001).
Based on a considerable collection of scientiﬁc data, a
study with the aim of determining various parameters
of reproduction for otters living in freshwater habitats
from central Europe was initiated. We explore the
relationships between breeding patterns, age and the
inﬂuence of body condition on breeding status. Finally,
on the basis of present data, an attempt has been
made to quantify the reproduction rate for L. lutra in
eastern Germany and to integrate the otters’ repro-
ductive traits into the wildlife life histories.
MATERIAL AND METHODS
STUDIED MATERIAL AND STUDY AREA
A total of 518 female otter carcasses was examined.
They originated from the seven regions of eastern
Germany: BC (Baltic coast), MV (Mecklenburg),
BB (Lake district and table land of Mecklenburg/
Brandenburg), ON (Oder-Neiße-region), LL (Lower
Lusatia and Brandenburg heath and lakeland), UL
(Upper Lusatia), and EE (Elbe-Elster-region). For fur-
ther description of these regions see Hauer et al.
(2000). Most animals (71%) were collected as road
casualties. The carcasses considered for this analysis
were collected from 1950 to 2001 and were not evenly

BREEDING OBSERVATIONS
During post-mortem analysis, uteri of all otters were
examined for distensions or contractions of uteri
horns, embryos, and for placental scars. Reproductive
tracts were mostly frozen before examination but in
an unﬁxed state. Ovaries of 86 females were ﬁxed in
10% formaldehyde, sectioned to 12 mm and stained
in haematoxylin-eosin. These sections were examined
under a light microscope for corpora lutea and follic-
ular development. We differentiated between two
stages of corpora lutea (CL); thriving CL in relation to
the implantation of embryos (ﬁlling 75% of the ovary),
and regressing CL as derived from a thriving CL and
associated with placental scars (ﬁlling at least 10% of
the ovary).
Females with follicles ≥ 2.0 mm were deﬁned as in
oestrous.
Generally, all females were classiﬁed as mature at
≥2 years, and reproductively active females showed
either placental scars, embryos or thriving corpora
lutea.
Prenatal mortality was calculated from the differ-
ence between the number of CL and embryos.
BREEDING SEASON AND BIRTH FREQUENCIES
The season of birth was estimated by the evaluation of
placental scars of females and the age of cubs. For the
calculation of the birth time of juveniles, only cubs
with deciduous or incomplete permanent dentition
were used. Subsequently age determination on the
basis of dental development followed Heggberget
(1996) and our own experiences. To establish the birth
time of litters, colour of placental scars was assessed
according to Heggberget & Christensen (1994); very
dark and pigmented implantation sites were consid-
ered to be no more than two months old. Slight pla-
cental scars were estimated to be four months old.
Females with contracted uteri were assessed to have
given birth no more than two weeks previously.
The approximate time of birth for embryos was cal-
culated by crown–rump length following data of Hill &
Lauhachinda (1981).
Monthly birth frequencies based on pregnant and
recently parturient females were calculated according
to Heggberget & Christensen (1994). A birth month as
well as a death month was assigned to each female
that will give birth (embryos) or gave birth at that
time (placental scars). The birth month is related to
the expected birth time of the detected embryos or the
probably birth time after placental scars. Whereas the
death month is the real date on which the female was
found dead. For each birth month, the female index
was computed as the proportion of females which
would have given or had given birth in this month to
all mature females found in the respective death

The index of body condition was calculated with the

AGE DETERMINATION
Age in years was determined by counting the incre-
mental cementum lines in canines (or adjacent teeth).
Tooth roots were sectioned using a low-speed saw,
according Driscoll et al. (1985) and Ansorge (1995).
Otters in their ﬁrst year of life can be aged by skull
characteristics as described by van Bree et al. (1966)
and Zinke & Ansorge (1998). For some of the present
investigations age classes (AC) were deﬁned as AC 0
for individuals aged 1–2 years (immature), AC 1 for 3–
5 years, AC 2 for 6–9 years, and AC 3 for 10–15 years.
STATISTICS
To test for regional differences in birth season, a log-
linear analysis was performed based on a matrix of
three variables (region, season, reproductive status)
followed by contrast analysis according to Warnstorff
& Dörfel (1998), using their computer program KTA.
To test for signiﬁcance, expected frequencies were
compared with observed frequencies using the 2I-
value (minimum discrimination information statistic;
Kullback, 1959) at the level of a = 0.05, which is nearly
c2-distributed.
Statistical evaluation of the seasonal dependency of
relationships between body condition and reproduc-
tive status was made by factorial ANOVA and a posthoc
Scheffé test that controlled Type I errors, a = 0.05. The
same test was conducted to determine the inﬂuences
of region, age class and season on mean litter size.
Differences in uterus length of reproductive and
non-reproductive females were tested using a F-test,
a = 0.05. Testing of signiﬁcance between  the number
of embryos and the number of following cubs per
mother, respectively, were calculated by Mann–
Whitney U-test. Any relationship between body condi-
tion and cause of mortality was determined using a
t-test.
RESULTS
AGE
The youngest female showing evidence of having bred
(placental scars) was in her second year, while the old-
est was 15 years old (fresh placental scars).
Six- to nine-year-old otters formed the bulk of the
reproductively active females (Fig. 1). Newly mature
females, or young adults (3–5 years) formed approxi-
mately the same proportion of the studied population
as the oldest cohort (>9 years). Only 20.40% of all
females aged between three and ﬁve years showed evi-
dence of having bred, compared with 45.45% of the 10-
to 15-year-old cohort and 55% of the 6- to 9-year-old
cohort.

DISCUSSION
REPRODUCTIVE PERFORMANCE
Sexual maturity has been recorded in female otters
between 18 and 24 months old (Stubbe, 1969;
Heggberget, 1988; Kruuk et al., 1991; Tuman &
Sidorovich, 1994), while Shilo & Batenava (1993)
estimated the age of ﬁrst breeding in a captive L. lutra
to be 2.5 years, which coincides with the results in this
study where a two-year-old female had signs of recent
parturition. Results of follicular analysis as well as
the proportion of aged females with evidence of breed-
ing showed that early pregnancies seem to be an
exception. The main focus of reproduction in this
study is on females between ﬁve and nine years, an
age which can account for a maximum of 30% of
females in a population (Kruuk & Conroy, 1991;
Ansorge et al., 1997). Reproduction of a very old
female (15 years) as indicated by placental scars is
notable, however nothing is known about her breeding
success.
The mean litter size seems to increase slightly with
age, as would be expected, but this trend is not statis-
tically signiﬁcant. Few investigations of reproduction
in mustelids consider litter size and age of the females.
In the badger Meles meles, Leyssac (2001) found no
apparent difference in mean litter size between age
groups. For the stone marten, Martes foina, Ansorge &
Jeschke (2001) reported similar results as those in
this present paper. Furthermore, in a study of Filaroi-
des invasion on sables Martes zibellina a possible age-
related reproduction could not be proved because of a
possible correlation with the presence of the helmin-

thiasis (Monakhov, 1999). Litter size is known to
change in both directions with increasing age in
captive breeding American mink Mustela vison and
American marten Martes americana, as well as in the
ﬁsher Martes pennanti (Mead, 1996). However, age-
related litter size has not been found in L. lutra. In the
sea otter Enhydra lutris, however, Monson et al. (2000)
indicated clear age-speciﬁc reproduction on Kodiak
and Amchitka Islands where birth rates more than
double with increasing age. Similarly, Dockter et al.
(1987) found that number of corpora lutea in
L. canadensis increased signiﬁcantly with increasing
age. Although such differences were not obtained in
the present study, a larger sample size might well
show a signiﬁcant age-related reproduction for the
Eurasian otter.
Generally, less than 70% of mature females are
actively in breeding condition at any one time. For the
calculation of pregnant females a compromise had to
be made. The estimation was based on the assumption
of an annually even distribution of births, although we
could prove there was a summer peak. Thus, the cal-
culated 69.4% is an approximate value, but seems to
be at an appropriate level, and little is to be gained by
attempting to deﬁne more precise monthly or seasonal
pregnancy rates. Furthermore we did not found a sin-
gle correlation between region and season of birth.
Therefore, a comparison of pregnancy rates between
regions seems to be feasible.
The great differences between natural regions may
reﬂect different habitat conditions as well as differing
population densities of otters in general and mature
females in particular. Upper Lusatia is apparently the
region with the highest otter density in Germany with
1.0–1.14 otters km-1 in the centre of Pond district
(Ansorge, 1994), and this is also very high compared
with the Swedish inland population (0.17–0.56
otters km-1, Erlinge, 1967), but has the lowest propor-
tion of reproductively-active females in relation to
other Eastern German regions. On the other hand,
females of Upper Lusatia have by far the highest
mean litter size as measured by embryos or placental
scars. Postnatal mortality in this region, however,
seems to be the highest, assuming that the number of
cubs per female is nearly the same in all regions other
than Upper Lusatia, and these other regions have
lower litter sizes at birth. Thus, as one hypothesis, a
regulatory effect could be assumed via a higher popu-
lation density (in Upper Lusatia) resulting either in a
decreasing number of active breeding females or in a
decreasing litter size. In this context we analysed
whether there is a relationship between the propor-
tion of reproductive females and mean litter size
(Fig. 9). However, with the present material such a
relationship could not be proved. Whereas the lower
proportion of breeding females in Upper Lusatia could

[inland]: 2.5; Belorussia: 2.7; Portugal [inland]: 2.9;

higher than the values in nearly all coastal habitats:  cf

inland habitats other than Germany can be deduced
from the noticeably similar numbers of cubs raised.

BODY CONDITION
The differences in body condition at different stages of
the breeding cycle point to a ‘targeted preparation’ for
breeding, and the lack of seasonal changes in body
condition in reproductively active females corresponds
with the absence of a deﬁnite breeding season for
L. lutra. A higher body condition for reproductively
active females is of use for breeding success, to rear a
litter without loss and without profound disadvantage
to the mother. Elmeros & Madsen (1999) did not ﬁnd
signiﬁcantly higher body condition indices for breed-
ing females and link this to the fact that lack of vari-
ation in body condition is the result of stable food
resources. However, higher energetic demands are
imposed during the lactation period and weaning
(Oftedal & Gittleman, 1989), thus an increase in body
condition seems to be appropriate. That is in agree-
ment with the ﬁndings of Woodroffe & Macdonald
(1995) for badgers, where body condition was signiﬁ-
cantly higher in lactating females than in non-lactat-
ing females, but there were no differences between
pregnant and non-pregnant females. Kruuk et al.
(1991) reported mothers abandoning their cubs, sug-
gesting this was because the female was in poor con-
dition. From our data, poor feeding conditions can be
excluded as responsible for the observed small litter
sizes in Eastern Germany.
SEASONALITY
For most species the availability of resources, espe-
cially food, varies seasonally in temperate climates,
and consequentially births should be timed such that
the most energy expenditure (lactation and weaning)
coincides when food is abundant. Marked correlations
between the seasonal peaks of food abundance and
date of birth or peak time of lactation have been found
in otters that live mainly in marine habitats often
associated with strong ﬂuctuations of shoaling ﬁsh
(Heggberget, 1988; Kruuk et al., 1991; Heggberget &
Christensen, 1994; Beja, 1996), and also in some
freshwater or brackish water habitats (Beja, 1996;
Elmeros & Madsen, 1999). Although no parallel inves-
tigations of seasonal food availability were carried out
in this study, we can assume that food resources in the
inland habitat are relatively stable throughout the
year, as Elmeros & Madsen (1999) noted. Neverthe-
less, in Germany a seasonal birth peak in summer is
evident, possibly indicating optimal food and also cli-
mate conditions.
There are many hypotheses why Eurasian otters
are non-seasonal breeders. For example, Woodroffe
(1995) investigated badgers with delayed implanta-
tion and found that the photoperiod was the main
inﬂuence on the implantation of blastocysts at a
timescale of months, but that females were able to
calibrate their reproduction in response to their own
local conditions at a time-scale of days depending on
food availability and body condition. Tumanov &
Sidorovich (1994) found that the mating season of the
otter was determined by the physiological state of the
individual and the onset of maturity. Ben-David
(1997) found that the breeding period in mink Mustela
vison (Schreber, 1777) is determined by photoperiod in
a wide range in the males, whereas the breeding
period of the females can shift to a certain extent
within this range. Elmeros & Madsen (1999) found
a continuous mating preparedness for male otters
which facilitated reproduction timing by females,
and more so where otters are spontaneous ovulators
(Amstislavsky & Ternovskaya, 2000).
Two further explanations for non-seasonal breeding
pattern focus on the main reproductive output.
Because of an uncertain onset of maturity (Tumanov
& Sidorovich, 1994) or  variable length of depen-

Based on the results of this study, some reﬂections
on general life-history strategies could be made.
Although some of the otters (marine or coastal-living
especially) live in a somewhat unpredictable habitat,
the species is more characteristic of a stable environ-
ment. Therefore they represent a typical k-selected
species, which means long-living animals with a pro-
portionally lower metabolism, longer pregnancies,
smaller litters, slower growth and longer dependen-
cies of the offspring (Macdonald, 1995). Compared
with two other species of mustelids, Meles meles
(Linnaeus, 1758) and Mustela nivalis (Linnaeus,
1766), Eurasian otters show an intermediate situation
in terms of (1) the length of gestation (34–37 days

60–64 days 
L. lutra; 
345–365 days
M. meles); (2) litter weight, expressed as percentage
of female body mass (2.9% M. meles; 10.0% L. lutra;
21.8% M. nivalis); and (3) mean litter sizes vary from
5.8 (M. nivalis) to 2.5 (L. lutra and M. meles) (Mead,
1989b; Oftedal & Gittleman, 1989; Gittleman, 1993).
The period of cub dependence in L. lutra, (9–
13 months, Kruuk et al., 1991) is longest in otters and
much more than in other mustelids, and the litter
growth rate per metabolic body size is the lowest. This
is possibly due to the more inconsistent habitat condi-
tions for the otter compared for instance to the badger.
In conclusion the present data on the life-history and
reproductive traits of the Eurasian otter suggest a
pattern of long-term reproduction effort at a lower
level.
