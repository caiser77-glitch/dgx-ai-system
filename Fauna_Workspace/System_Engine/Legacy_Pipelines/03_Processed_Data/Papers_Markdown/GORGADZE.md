PRESENT PROBLEMS AFFECTING THE OTTER (LUTRA LUTRA) 
POPULATION IN GEORGIA 
 
Giorgi GORGADZE 
NACRES (www.nacres.org); PO Box 20; Tbilisi; Georgia (CIS) 
email:  giorgi.gorgadze@nacres.org  
 
Abstract. There are seven mustelid species in Georgia namely Eurasian otter (Lutra lutra), pine marten (Martes 
martes), stone or beech marten (Martes foina), badger (Meles meles), American mink (Mustela vison), least 
weasel (Mustela nivalis) and marbled polecat (Vormela peregusna), but data on these species is scarce. 
Preliminary population assessment studies have shown that the Eurasian otter and marbled polecat are the most 
endangered mustelids in the country. In the past, the otter was widespread throughout the country occurring on 
almost every river and lake. It is now supposed that the otter has already disappeared from many areas or 
formed isolated sub-populations as a result of different limiting factors - hunting and poaching, habitat loss and 
disturbance, over-exploitation of lakes and rivers, pollution and competitors.  
 
Keywords:  Eurasian otter, Lutra lutra, Georgia. 
 
1   INTRODUCTION 
The Caucasian region of south east Europe is one of the Global Biodiversity Hotspots and is 
distinguished by biologically rich areas with high diversity and a large percentage of endemic 
species. 
 
 
Georgia is situated in the central western part of the Caucasus and is bordered on the 
north by Russia, the south by Armenia and Turkey, the east by Azerbaijan, and the west by 
the Black Sea. The territory of the country is 69,500 km2; the total length of its borders is 
1,969 kilometres, of which 1,160 are terrestrial (MARUASHVILI, 1970).  
 
The country is characterised by a diversity of natural conditions, mainly due to a 
contrasting topographical relief: high, medium and low mountains, highland plateau and 
plains systems.  The primary topographic units are the mountains of the Greater Caucasus, the 
plains between the mountains, the mountains of the Lesser Caucasus, and volcanic mountains 
of southern Georgia (GULISASHVILI, 1964). 
 
There are a total of 25,075 rivers and streams in Georgia, with a total length of 54,768 
kilometres. Almost every river in east Georgia belongs to the Mtkvari system, which empties 
into the Caspian Sea. The majority of west Georgia’s rivers feed directly into the Black Sea. 
 
2   MAIN THREATS TO WILDLIFE IN GEORGIA 
The escalation of human impacts has caused serious demage to Georgian biodiversity over the 
last several decades. The main reasons which led to the decrease of wildlife populations are: 
(1) the division of wildlife into "useful" and "non-useful (harmful)" categories which has had 
especially negative influences on predators; (2) increased poaching, intensified by the socio-
economic crisis in the country which significantly affected species population numbers. 
Therefore some species have already disappeared from Georgia (e.g. leopard (Panthera 
pardus), Persian gazelle (Gazella subgutturosa)) and significant numbers are at the risk of 
extenction [e.g. striped hyena (Hyaena hyaena), lynx (Felis (lynx) lynx), and bezoar goat 
(Capra aegagrus)].

3   MUSTELIDAE IN GEORGIA 
There are seven species of mustelid in Georgia, namely Lutra lutra, Martes martes, Martes 
foina, Meles meles, Mustela vison, Mustela nivalis and Vormela peregusna.  However, no 
fundamental research has been done on this family in the country, and therefore data provided 
in the scientific literature is scarce. 
Preliminary population assessment studies carried out by NACRES in different parts 
of Georgia have shown that the Eurasian otter (Lutra lutra) and marbled polecat (Vormela 
peregusna) are the most endangered of the seven mustelid species found throughout the 
country. 
The Javakheti wetland ecosystem (south central Georgia) is the only area where 
scientific data proves the existence of marbled polecat populations in the country. The range 
of this single population in Georgia covers only one side of the Madatapha Lake. The species 
is included in the Georgian Red Data Book and is classified as ‘critically endangered’ (RED 
DATA BOOK OF GEORGIA, 1982). 
 
4   EURASIAN OTTER IN GEORGIA 
 
In the past, the otter was widespread throughout the country occurring at almost every river 
and lake (DINNIK, 1914; SATUNIN, 1915; RED DATA BOOK OF GEORGIA, 1982, GEORGIAN 
BIODIVERSITY COUNTRY STUDY REPORT, 1996; MATERIALS OF THE GEORGIAN BIODIVERSITY 
COUNTRY STUDY REPORT, 1997). According to the limited existing literature, while the otter 
population was not abundant its density was stable (JANASHVILI, 1963).  
Now the species is listed in the Red Book of Georgia under the category ‘Nearly 
Extinct Rare Species’. At the same time the USSR RED DATA BOOK (1984) assumes that there 
are three subspecies throughout Russia and the Caucasus and classifies Lutra lutra 
meridionalis, the subspecies present in Georgia, as ‘threatened, with rapidly declining range’. 
However, no DNA analysis on the Eurasian otter has been undertaken in this part of the 
world, and therefore, the actual presence and number of subspecies cannot be considered 
clear. 
In 1999, NACRES undertook the project “Wildlife Regional Study in the Caucasus”. 
According to the results of this project and scientific information provided by experts from 
Georgia, Azerbaijan and Armenia the otter can be considered a ‘Critically Endangered’ 
species according to IUCN Red List criteria (WILDLIFE DATA BANK OF THE CAUCASUS, 2002). 
It is supposed that, because of different limiting factors listed below, the otter has 
already disappeared from many areas or formed isolated subpopulations (GORGADZE, 2001). 
 
5   BASIC LIMITING FACTORS TO OTTER POPULATIONS IN GEORGIA 
5.1 Hunting and poaching 
Official data claimed the existence of ca. 20,000 individuals in USSR while the annual fur 
harvest accounted for 5,000 individuals through the existing bounty system on predators, 
Georgia harvested the highest number of animals compared to other Caucasus countries, viz.  
Armenia and Azerbaijan.  


 
3
 
After abolishing the bounty system in 1993, the fur harvest reduced. Nevertheless furs 
of recently killed animals are still regularly seen on the black market. Usually these animals 
are victims of fishery operations and killed through illegal methods (e.g. fixed nets). It is to be 
noted that local human populations largely depend on water resources and fishery. Fishermen 
and/or villagers see otters as competitors for fish, resulting in their persecution and death on 
every convenient occasion. The problem is largely determined by the lack of any 
compensation to the human populations for the perceived damage.  
 
5.2 Habitat loss and disturbance 
Unsustainable use of forest resources has increased dramatically over the last decade. Loss of 
riparian forest habitat has occurred through the otter’s range and has resulted in a high loss of 
living space for numerous other animal species as well.  
Unsustainable steppe grass artificial fires along the riparian forests in semi-arid lands 
of Georgia (important otter habitat in the south eastern part of the country) contribute greatly 
to this issue in the above-mentioned ecosystem.  
 
5.3 Over-exploitation of lakes and rivers 
Drainage of wetlands was common practice in Soviet times. Riverbeds were frequently 
changed, increasing the levels of disturbance along riverbanks.   A vivid example of this is the 
Javakhety wetlands (south central part of the country), where nearly 60 different sized lakes 
are present. There was a large-scale experimental drainage programme of the major lakes in 
the Javakheti region which began in the 1960s. This involved draining the wetlands for 
agricultural purposes or transforming them into fish farms. Special canals and dams were built 
to regulate water levels and this caused serious damage to shorelines. Dams were built to cut 
off some parts of the lake from each other (MANAGEMENT PLAN OF JAVAKHETI PLATEAU 
WETLANDS IN GEORGIA, 2001). During spring, the water rises above the dam and these parts 
are filled with water. In dry seasons, these sections are completely drained for fish. This kind 
of exploitation caused severe eutrophication. Shores are still cultivated every year. 
 
5.4 Pollution 
Water pollution is also a serious problem. Those rivers that flow near or through cities are 
seriously polluted with different industrial wastes. 
  
Illegal fish farming also pollutes rivers and lakes. Poachers empty bags of poisons into 
some rivers.  These dissolve slowly and shortly afterwards the rivers are full of dead fish. In 
many cases, this causes either a disappearance of the fish or they do not spawn in the area any 
more.  It has a similar effect on otters.  
 
5.5 Competitors  
According to some scientific data, American mink (Mustela vison) and raccoon (Procyon 
lotor) are considered competitors to the otter in Georgia. Both were introduced to the country 
during the 1960s. The introduction of American mink was unsuccessful but the raccoon 
population increased and covered relatively large areas. Again, no research on introduced 
species has been done in Georgia, thus their impact on the local species is still unclear.
