The eﬀects of traﬃc on the raccoon dog
(Nyctereutes procyonoides viverrinus) and other
mammals in Japan
M. Saeki 1, D.W. Macdonald *
The Wildlife Conservation Research Unit, Department of Zoology, University of Oxford, South Parks Road, Oxford OX1 3PS, UK
Received 28 May 2002; accepted 3 October 2003
Abstract
We investigated the extent of raccoon dog roadkills, and the factors inﬂuencing them, using data from the National Expressways
in Japan for 1987–1998. A high rate of raccoon dog roadkills occurred in western Japan, whereas a relatively low rate was typical of
the central region and of Hokkaido. Kills on the National Expressways increased as traﬃc and the length of the road network
increased between 1987 and 1998. The relationship between mortality and traﬃc volume was scale-dependent. The number of
roadkills was positively correlated with traﬃc volume at national level, but negatively correlated with the amount of traﬃc along
sections of an individual major road between exits. There were spring and autumn peaks, and possibly early morning and evening
peaks, of mortality. Mortality was higher for roads in a cutting, where the road was closer to water, and where the roadside habitat
was broad-leaved woodland. Coniferous forest was negatively associated with roadkills. The total numbers of roadkills on four
types of roads (National Expressways, National, Prefectural, and Municipal roads), was estimated to be between 110,000 and
370,000 in 1998. We suggest some preventative measures which might mitigate road casualties (while remaining attentive to human
safety). Roadkill data, after controlling for traﬃc volume, can be useful as a population trend index. Patterns for racoon dogs are
compared with those for other species.

1. Introduction
Traﬃc accidents involving wildlife are a serious
problem on developed and developing road networks
worldwide (Broekhuizen and Derckx, 1996; Coulson,
1997; Drews, 1995). In addition to their relevance to
human safety (Romin and Bissonette, 1996; US De-
partment of Transportation, 2000), traﬃc collisions are
a major component of mortality in many vertebrate and
invertebrate species (e.g. Kushlan, 1988; Seibert and
Conover, 1991; Drews, 1995; Fahrig et al., 1995). Roads
may obstruct seasonal migration (Van Gelder, 1973)
and may interfere with the recovery of rare or endan-
gered species (Kushlan, 1988; Philcox et al., 1999). De-
spite attempts to mitigate them with fences, overpasses,
underpasses or culverts, and road signs, no cost-eﬀective
methods to prevent adverse eﬀects of roads and road
traﬃc accidents are currently available (Trombulak and
Frissell, 2000).
Although roadkills may be a major cause of anthro-
pogenic mortality for wildlife, only a small number of
nationwide estimates of this source of mortality have
been conducted (Romin and Bissonette, 1996; Meijer
and Smith, 1999; Holsbeek et al., 1999; Clarke et al.,
1998). In Japan, large numbers of raccoon dog (Nyc-
tereutes procyonoides Gray) roadkills have been reported
on the National Expressways (Yanase, 1999), National

Raccoon dogs are native to Japan and are not cur-
rently considered threatened, although they are in-
creasingly coming into conﬂict with people (Saeki,
2001). They are culled as a pest, and for their fur and
sport (under licence); unlicensed culling also occurs, and
there are no management plans except for removal op-
erations. Two subspecies of raccoon dog are endemic to
Japan (Ward et al., 1987): N. procyonoides viverrinus is
found on the main island of Honshu, on two other
major islands (Shikoku and Kyushu) and many islets,
while N. procyonoides albus inhabits Hokkaido. There
are no population estimates for either subspecies nor for
the Japanese raccoon dog population as a whole. Japan
has frequently been accused of irresponsibility towards
wildlife conservation (Stewart-Smith, 1987; Potter, 1994;
Danaher, 1987; Cryanoski, 2000; Normile, 2000; Sae-
gusa, 2000). Numbers of mammals culled, including
raccoon dogs, have grown exponentially (from 12,997 in

1999) but there is no nationwide scheme or system for

monitoring
and
management
(Lawyers Association of Japan, 1999), and research on
native mammals is limited (Saeki, 2001). Given this
background, information on causes of mortality even in
an apparently common species such as the raccoon dog
is valuable, not only for the conservation of the species
in question, but also for raising awareness and under-
standing of Japanese conservation problems in general.
Furthermore, the copious data on raccoon dog road
deaths provide insight into patterns that may illustrate
considerations relevant to more scarce species, for which
data are inevitably sparse.

the 1950s. There are four basic categories of public roads
in Japan: National Expressways (covering 6402 km in

average of 83 km/h), under the responsibility of the Japan

averaging 28.5 km/h in 1997), administered by National

Expressways are 14 m wide, National Highways 9–10 m

registered vehicles grew 80-fold between 1955 and 1998,
reaching 73,688,389 (Ministry of Transportation, 2000),
while the total road length increased almost 10-fold dur-
ing the same period (Ministry of Transportation, 2000).
To investigate the extent of raccoon dog roadkills and
the factors inﬂuencing them, we analysed data on the
National Expressways in Japan (this being the only road
category for which detailed data are currently available).
We investigated spatial patterns in the data: for exam-
ple, are kills spatially clustered? do the same patterns
prevail in both carriageways? The answers to these
questions may aﬀect mitigation measures for raccoon
dogs, and other mammals with similar movement
characteristics. In the absence of alternative population
data for Japanese mammals, we examine the feasibility
of using roadkill data as an indicator of population
trends for a variety of species.
2. Methods
2.1. Collation of roadkill data
Data on roadkills on the National Expressways and
some toll roads (total 44 roads) are compiled annually
by the Japan Highway Public Corporation. Roadkills
were collected by corporation staﬀduring routine road
checks, usually carried out several times daily. Because
they record only corpses on roads, samples exclude any
individuals that are hit by a vehicle and die subsequently
oﬀthe road. Information recorded included species, lo-
cation (at a scale of 100 m segment), and date and time
of collection. When compiled, these data provide in-
formation on the following: (1) the number of roadkills
by species and road in each ﬁscal year between 1995 and
1998; (2) total number by species, in the ﬁscal years from
1987 to 1998 nationwide; (3) the total length of roads
and the traﬃc volume and traﬃc load in the ﬁscal years
from 1987 to 1998 nationwide; (4) for 1989, more de-
tailed data were available: the location and time of
collection of roadkills by species on the National Ex-
pressways (563.8 km) and the toll roads (63.5 km) in the
Kyushu area, where the highest number of roadkills of
raccoon dogs occurred; and (5) traﬃc volumes in 1989
between exits in the Kyushu area. In Japan, there are
tolls at all exit points, allowing traﬃc volume to be
measured at this scale.

2.3. Extrapolation of total numbers of raccoon dogs killed
on roads
We made three assumptions in estimating the num-
bers killed on roads nationally: (1) That there is a linear
relationship between roadkills and road length; (2) that
the kill-rate per unit length on Highways and Prefectural
roads was the same as measured for the Expressways;
and (3) that the kill-rate per unit length on Municipal
roads was approximately one sixth of that measured on
Expressways. The latter two assumptions were based on
the pattern of mortality rates observed for badgers (M.
meles) in Britain in relation to traﬃc ﬂow on four cat-
egories of road (Clarke et al., 1998). In that study, the
three largest road categories (traﬃc ﬂow 34,000 vehi-
cles/day) had similar kill-rates per unit length, but this
was approximately six times higher than that observed
for the lowest category of road (traﬃc ﬂow 530 vehi-
cles per day). As the traﬃc ﬂow rates for Expressways
and Municipal roads in this study were similar to those
reported by Clarke et al. (1998) we have assumed the
same pattern of mortality for raccoon dogs. While these
are diﬀerent species in diﬀerent places, many aspects of
the movement patterns of the two species are similar
(Saeki, 2001).
The estimates were obtained by prefecture (adminis-
trative area) and summed to provide total estimates as
follows: K ¼ Rdr for National Highways and Prefec-
tural roads; K ¼ Rdr=6 for Municipal roads; where K is
number of roadkills by prefecture, d is road length, r is
roadkill rate of each prefecture, calculated with roadkill
rates on National Expressways. Another assumption
was that the number of roadkills is proportional to av-
erage speed and nightly traﬃc volume. The formulae for
the latter estimates are: K ¼ RSdrn for National High-
ways and Prefectural roads and K ¼ Rdrn=6 for Muni-
cipal roads were used, where K is number of roadkills by
prefecture, d is length of road, r is roadkill rate, n is the
ratio of nightly traﬃc volume to that of the Expressways
by road category.

2.5. Statistics
In order to reveal the pattern of roadkills over 10
years from 1987 to 1998, a trend analysis was per-
formed. A regression analysis was also performed with
total roadkills (y), total road length (x1) and traﬃc
volume (x2), i.e. 100 million vehicle-km, per ﬁscal year.
For three Expressways in Kyushu (Miyazaki-do, Kyu-
shu-do, and Nagasaki-do), spatial distributions were
compared with a Poisson series using dispersion tests to
determine whether they occurred randomly in space
(Elliott, 1977). Greens coeﬃcient of dispersion (G) was
used to compare aggregation among roads and car-
riageways; unlike most indices based on the variance to
mean ratio, this is not sensitive to number of items (here
roadkills) or to the sample size (number of segments):
G ¼ (variance/mean ) 1)/(total ) 1) (Elliott, 1977). This
index is negative for distributions more uniform than
random, zero for random, and 1.0 for maximum possi-
ble contagion (where all observations are in the same
unit).
In order to investigate which factors inﬂuenced the
occurrence of roadkills on the Miyazaki-do road in
Kyushu, we used four predictors (variables): road fea-
ture, road-crossing type, roadside habitat type, and the
distance to water (Table 1). The number of observations
was 825 on each lane for presence or absence of road-
kills per 100 m segment with these predictors. Contin-
gency tables were used to test whether the presence of
individual road features within a segment aﬀected the
likelihood of detecting raccoon dog roadkills in that
segment. Using Chi-square goodness-of-ﬁt analysis, we
tested whether the presence of roadkills on 100 m seg-
ments of the Miyazaki-do was independent of road
features, or roadside habitat type. The expected values
were obtained from the ratio of 100 m segments that fell
into the seven road features and eight habitat types
(Table 1). Logistic regression was used to test whether
distance from water was a signiﬁcant predictor of
roadkills being recorded, where detection of kills was a
binary yes/no response. Contingency table analyses were
carried out using SAS (SAS Institute, 1989–1996), and
all other analyses using MINITAB.
3. Results
3.1. Spatial and temporal trends
Records for the National Expressway, calculated for
each prefecture in 1998, identiﬁed areas with the highest
mortality rates per km of road (Fig. 1). Raccoon dog kill
rates were highest in the Kyushu region of Japan, where
on average, 4.34 ± SE 0.46 (n ¼ 4 expressways) raccoon
dogs were killed for every km of Expressway (totalling
682.4 km) in 1998. This far exceeded the national av-
erage of 2.07  0.27 roadkills/km (n ¼ 41 Expressways).
There was a marked upward trend on the National
Expressways between 1987 and 1998 (r2 ¼ 0:96; Fig. 2;
F1;10 ¼ 259, P < 0:001).

3.5. Roadkills as a population index for Japanese mam-
mals
The average roadkill rate of raccoon dogs on the
Expressways by prefecture was signiﬁcantly related to
the average reported harvest density in each Prefecture
from 1995 to 1998 (F1;44 ¼ 46:7, P < 0:0001; Fig. 6). The
conﬁdence interval for the slope of this relationship is
0.92–1.66, and therefore consistent with 1.0, which
would indicate a simple proportionality.
For other species the form of the relationship was
varied. Evidence for non-linear relationships between
roadkills and traﬃc volume was observed for deer
(F1;7 ¼ 34:00, P < 0:001; Fig. 7a), domestic dog (F1;7 ¼
17:29, P ¼ 0:002; Fig. 7b), red fox (F1;7 ¼ 29:07, P <
0:001; Fig. 7d) and domestic cat (F1;7 ¼ 44:78, P < 0:001).
A marginal result was observed for the hare (F1;6 ¼ 4:24,
P ¼ 0:063; Fig. 7f). For the weasel, a straight line model
was an adequate ﬁt to the data (F1;8 ¼ 49:48, P < 0:0001;
Fig. 7c).

Average speed and traﬃc volume during the night,
particularly at dusk and dawn, could be more critical for
nocturnal species than the 24 h average of both. If roads
lie between resting/den sites and foraging sites, com-
muting humans and nocturnal animals are likely to co-
incide, increasing the probability of roadkills. In such
cases, local roads used for human commuting can be
dangerous to wildlife even with a relatively low traﬃc
volume. Inbar and Mayer (1999) found that roadkills of
the armadillo (Dasypus novemcinctus) in the USA corre-
lated with night traﬃc volume but not daytime traﬃc
volume, and more roadkills occurred in summer when
armadillos are more strictly nocturnal (diurnal activity
was observed in winter). They also found a relationship
between roadkills and climatic factors and soil pH, im-
plying that activity of the animals inﬂuenced the likeli-
hood of a collision with traﬃc. Rolley and Lehman
(1992) found that roadkills of the raccoon (Procyon lotor)
were not signiﬁcantly correlated with traﬃc volume, but
with the traﬃc speed. This may account for the obser-
vation in the current study that mortality per unit length
increased faster than proportionately with traﬃc density.
The hourly roadkill trend must be interpreted with
caution because the eﬀort (frequency) of checking roads
may have been neither constant nor random. However,
the morning peak (between 0600 and 0800) and the
evening peak (between 2100 and 2300) in percentage of
roadkills (Fig. 3b) seem to reﬂect a high rate of early
morning and early evening deaths.
In our study, two contrasting relationships were found
between roadkills and traﬃc: the national total of traﬃc
volume had a positive eﬀect on total roadkill rate by road,
whereas traﬃc by road sections between exits was nega-
tively related to roadkill rate. We speculate that the rea-
son for this is that traﬃc volume at a local scale may have
an eﬀect on whether animals attempt to cross; on the ﬁne
scale of sections of road between exits, animals may
choose to cross at the least busy point if they are able to
detect traﬃc volume discontinuities in some way, which
must occur to some extent between exits. Nonetheless,
total roadkills depend on traﬃc volume, so at a larger
spatial scale, kills are dependent upon probability of
collisions. Inbar and Mayer (1999) noted that the sam-
pling scale for traﬃc volume data might be important.
Although the factors inﬂuencing roadkills appeared
diﬀerent between lanes, the high rates at sites in a cutting
were consistent with observations in the Chiba prefec-

and none on the raised ground (M. Saeki, personal

roadkills and coniferous forests corresponds with the
results from our habitat selection study (Saeki and
Macdonald, in press). Crossing roads in a cutting and
avoidance of coniferous forests appear to be character-

diﬀerent results from the westbound lane of the Miya-

may diﬀer spatially and temporally with local circum-
stances. Alternatively, the eﬀects may frequently appear
stronger in one lane because the animals tend to en-
counter it ﬁrst. The lane the animals ﬁrst entered is
unknown, but the greatest hotspot (43–44 km-post) lay
between streams, and the road was mainly in a cutting.

4.3. Impact of roadkills
The impact of road mortality on mammalian popula-
tions has not been assessed in Japan. Indeed, without an
estimate of national populations, such an assessment
cannot be made. Nevertheless, if 110,000–370,000 rac-
coon dogs are killed each year and the number of roadkills
increases as traﬃc volume increases, the impact of road
mortality on the population cannot be ignored. However,
there was no evidence that the rate of roadkills was de-
clining as traﬃc density increased, as would be expected if
this mortality was having an eﬀect on population size. In
Britain, traﬃc casualties were considered to account for
30–66% of all adult and post-emergence cub mortality of
badgers (Skinner et al., 1991; Clarke et al., 1998), while at
the same time badger populations are certainly not de-
clining. Also in Britain, a male bias in roadkill records was
found in otters (Philcox et al., 1999).
4.4. Roadkills as a population index
Raccoon dog roadkills increased more rapidly than
did traﬃc density; the model illustrated by the line in
Fig. 4c predicts an increase of 46.6% over the interval
between 9.5 and 11 million vehicles (an increase of
15.8%). In contrast, roadkills of sika deer plainly had a
greater rate of increase than traﬃc volume (Fig. 7a).
Deer densities have been reported to have increased in
many parts of Japan (Kaji, 1995; Nakamura and Ono,
1999; Matsuda et al., 1999; Takayanagi, 2000). Simi-
larly, programmes to exterminate stray dogs, and the
increasing practice of keeping dogs on a leash, have ef-
fectively decreased free-ranging domestic dog densities
and this is reﬂected in the decrease in road kills with
traﬃc volume. Caution is required because density in-
crease does not necessarily mean total population in-
crease when concentrations occur locally, and new roads
may result in more roadkills than old ones.
Nonetheless, trends in roadkills may be a useful index
for short-term population trends, especially in con-
junction with traﬃc data. McCaﬀery (1973), for exam-
ple, found regional and temporal correlations between
deer harvest and roadkills in Wisconsin, USA. He ad-
justed roadkills for changes in annual traﬃc volume for
a long-term trend analysis. Population surveys of deer
further supported these results. Although reaction to
traﬃc change probably varies with species, it is still
useful for temporal comparisons within species when
traﬃc is measured at a scale appropriate to the species.
In contrast, harvest data – another widely used method
of estimating population trends – often include more
species-speciﬁc complications. For example, pelt price
and/or number of hunters, which may diﬀer by species
and year, often inﬂuence harvest eﬀort so an accurate
estimate of these eﬀects is diﬃcult. The number of reg-
istered hunters declined by 25% between 1987 and
1996, but appeared to be stabilising at the end of this
sequence (document available at www.env.go.jp/earth/
coop/coop/materials/08-ttmnce/08-ttmnce-51.pdf).
No
ﬁgures are available for illegal hunting. The observation
that roadkills were a reasonable predictor of harvest
density between 1995 and 1998 is therefore diﬃcult to
interpret.
We recorded a great deal of variation in the geo-
graphic distribution of roadkills. One of the factors may
have been regional density of animals. Density estima-
tion of raccoon dogs is diﬃcult and has not been con-
ducted at a large scale in Japan. If the relationship
between roadkill rate and density is elucidated, this
could be used for both roadkill prevention and moni-
toring populations, not only for racoon dogs, but also
for other species.
This rapid development of the road network, and the
increase in traﬃc, may have signiﬁcantly aﬀected pop-
ulations of raccoon dogs and other mammals. There is a
perception in Japan that the increase in numbers of
raccoon dogs killed on the roads reﬂects an increase in
their populations, but an alternative explanation might
be that roads are busier and traﬃc faster, and therefore
collisions with wildlife, including raccoon dogs, are
more likely to occur.
4.5. Preventative measures
Overpasses or underpasses accommodating other
roads and railways did not appear to have any value in
providing safe passage across the Expressway for rac-
coon dogs as there was no evidence for an eﬀect of these
road features (crossing in Table 1). Modiﬁcations of
these, as well as the installation of new crossings for
wildlife, may be needed. Mitigation measures may be
useful, for example, providing signs with information on
the locations and times of high risk. Trees and cover
alongside roads might encourage animals to escape to-
wards them, instead of lingering on the road. Lowering
speed limits between dusk and dawn in autumn may also
