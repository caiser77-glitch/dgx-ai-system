Abstract:  The Afon Dwyryd Otter Partnership was established to monitor the recovery of Lutra lutra on the 
Afon (River) Dwyryd, in Snowdonia National Park.  The Afon Dwyryd estuary is at the beginning of the Lleyn 
Peninsula, Gwynedd, Wales, UK.  Since its conception the Project has been collecting and analysing spraint 
from a number of locations along the catchment on a monthly basis.  A 12 month collection of spraint is now 
ready to be analysed.  Preliminary investigations have shown a number of marine prey species turning up in 
spraints some distance from the shore, A survey of the coast was conducted on various sites along the Lleyn 
Peninsula to try and elucidate further the otters' use of coastal sites, their foraging patterns, what marine species 
they are preying on, and the relative amounts of marine and freshwater prey in their diet. 
Fish were by far the main component of the diet (approximately 96% of spraints contained fish remains), 
amphibians were notably absent, with birds and mammals making up only approximately 4% of the total diet.  
Results show that the main prey item was eel, (Anguilla anguilla), at approximately. 32% of the diet, with 
salmonids (Salmo spp) and sea sticklebacks (Spinachia spinachia) around 15% respectively.  This shows that the 
otters are primarily feeding on species that occur in both the freshwater and marine environments, and are not 
therefore necessarily foraging significantly on the coast. 
 
Keywords:  Eurasian otter, Lutra lutra, diet, spraint analysis, Wales 
 
 
1   AIMS    
 
A coastal survey of the Lleyn Peninsula was undertaken to monitor coastal site use by the 
Eurasian otter (Lutra lutra) during its recent return to this region in Wales.  This project 
aimed to examine seasonal variation in otter diet, to determine the relative proportion of 
marine and freshwater species within that diet and to attempt to infer foraging reliance on 
marine species with respect to kelp and non-kelp dominated inshore sites. 
The Afon Dwyryd Otter Partnership was established to monitor the recovery of the 
Eurasian otter on the Afon (River) Dwyryd, in the Snowdonia National Park.  A 12 month 
collection of spraints, gathered during monthly surveys of designated sites throughout the 
catchment, is now ready to be analysed.  Preliminary analyses have revealed a number of 
marine species occurring in spraints at least 20 kilometres from the shore. 
 
 
2   METHODOLOGY 
 
2.1 Site selection 
A total of ten sites were selected, on both the north and south coasts of the Lleyn Peninsula;  
five contained extensive areas of Laminaria forests (kelp) in conjunction with a variety of 
freshwater features and other habitats, e.g. ditches and streams.  The five other coastal sites 
contained no Laminaria, but other features were comparable. 
 
2.2 Survey  
Otter field sign data, (e.g. couches, footprints etc.), were recorded and any spraints found 
collected during intensive surveys of ten 1km coastal squares during September 2002.  


Habitat and ecological features of actual or potential benefit to otters were also mapped, along 
with any site-specific characteristics that may prove detrimental to continued otter presence, 
e.g. high levels of human disturbance and the presence of crustacean traps, (JEFFRIES, GREEN 
AND GREEN, 1984). 
 
2.3 Spraint analysis  
Spraints were processed using the standard methodology (see CONROY ET AL., 1993).  All 
vertebrae and other body parts were separated and identified using a microscope (10x 
magnification) and spraint keys.  Species identification was confirmed in some cases using 
jaw bones, scales and otoliths where available.  Bird and mammal remains were also noted. 
 
 
4   RESULTS 
 
Of the ten 1km squares surveyed, eight (80%) contained positive field signs of otters, (HALL, 
2002a,b).  In total, 17 spraints were collected and analysed, (Figure 1).  Fish vertebrae 
accounted for ca. 96% of the total numbers of vertebrae recovered from these spraints.  The 
vast majority (32%) of these vertebrae were eel (Anguilla anguilla).  Salmonids (Salmo spp.) 
and sea sticklebacks (Spinachia spinachia) accounted for approximately 15% respectively of 
the total vertebrae recorded.   
 
The remaining vertebrae/bones were of avian, e.g. moorhen (Gallinula chloropus) and 
mammalian, e.g. water vole (Arvicola terrestris) origin.  Amphibian vertebrae were notably 
absent from the spraints.

5   DISCUSSION   
 
Despite the relatively low numbers of spraints analysed to date, it is clear from this 
preliminary work that the principal prey types of otters in this area are (unsurprisingly) eel 
and salmonid species.  Of particular interest, however, is the presence of an unusually high 
proportion of S. spinachia in the otters’ diet.  Although this marine fish is generally solitary 
for most of the year, the period of time immediately prior to the study (early autumn) 
coincided with the peak availability of this species as it congregates in kelp forests to spawn 
(WHEELER, 1969).  The subsequent analysis of a larger number of spraints collected at other 
times of year should hopefully reveal whether the presence of this species in the diet of otters 
in this area is highly seasonal in occurrence. 
 
The absence of any amphibian remains in the spraints can be attributed to a 
combination of factors, including season, spraint sample size and a prolonged period of 
drought prior to survey.  It is also worth noting the presence of A. terrestris in the mammal 
component of the prey remains, as it is a species of particularly high conservation concern at 
present. 
 
 
6   CONCLUSIONS   
 
This study shows that the otters on the Lleyn Peninsula are primarily feeding on anadromous 
species, therefore not necessarily foraging extensively on the coast.  The results of this 
baseline survey of the Lleyn Peninsula are extremely encouraging as they strongly indicate 
that otters are currently returning to their previous distribution in this region of Wales after a 
prolonged period of absence, (ANDREWS, HOWELL AND JOHNSTON, 1993). 
A detailed survey of specific sections of the Snowdonia National Park coastline has 
been completed in May 2003.  Twelve of the 13 sites surveyed were positive, (HALL, 2003).  
It is hoped that the Lleyn will be surveyed in May and the Park coast in September 2004.
