TOXICOLOGICAL STUDIES ON OTTERS IN BRITAIN – IS THERE A FUTURE?

, James W.H CONROY2* & David N. CARSS2

Abstract.  It is well documented that bans on the manufacture/use of dieldrin led to reductions in exposure and 
bioaccumulation in predatory birds in Britain but evidence for a similar decline in PCB accumulation is less 
clear.  Quantitative data on concomitant changes in organochlorine concentrations in the Eurasian otter (Lutra 
lutra) in Britain are relatively sparse and fragmented.  In the present paper, we collate and review the available 
data on dieldrin and PCB residues in otters in Britain to determine if concentrations have declined, whether 
current levels of accumulation still pose a threat, and whether there is a need for further toxicological 
monitoring.  Liver dieldrin concentrations were found to have declined in otters from south west England 
between 1988 and 1996.  Data on dieldrin residues in otters at a national scale are sparse but collation of 
published data suggests that there may have been a decline that was most marked before 1970.  However, 
detection of this trend is highly dependent upon data for just a handful of animals.  There is good evidence that 
liver PCB concentrations have declined over time in otters from regions as far apart as south west England and 
Shetland.  This probably reflects changes in animals across the whole of Britain although, as for dieldrin, a 
decline is hard to detect at a national scale.  Much of the decline in PCBs in otters appeared to occur 
particularly during the 1980s and possibly earlier.  Current dieldrin accumulation is not thought to generally 
pose an acute toxic threat to otters in Britain but the threat from chronic exposure is poorly understood.  
Similarly, the risks to individual otters and to populations from current levels of PCB accumulation are not well 
characterised and may vary regionally.  This overall poor level of understanding, together with a lack of 
knowledge about what other contaminants otters are exposed to, demonstrates the need for further toxicological 
studies in this species.  This is particularly needed in Scotland where there have been very high levels of PCB 
contamination in some otter populations but, paradoxically, a lack of recent chemical monitoring.  Such 
monitoring could form part of a wider programme designed to assess environmental quality and the health of 
aquatic habitats.  
 
Keywords: dieldrin, polychlorinated biphenyls, PCBs, Eurasian otter, Lutra lutra, liver concentrations 
 
 
1   INTRODUCTION 
 
The impacts of organochlorine pesticides, polychlorinated biphenyls (PCBs) and some heavy 
metals on Eurasian otters (Lutra lutra) populations in Britain and elsewhere is well 
documented.  In England, otter populations are thought to have crashed in the late 1950s/early 
1960s.  Organochlorine pesticides, in particular dieldrin, were implicated as the cause 
(CHANIN AND JEFFERIES, 1978; SIMPSON, 1998; SIMPSON, ET AL., 2000; JEFFERIES AND 
HANSON, 2002), although it is also argued that PCBs, rather than dieldrin, may have been the 
main agent (MASON AND WREN, 2001).  The crash in the otter population coincided with 
declines in the populations of several predatory bird species which were mediated by 
organochlorine pesticides, principally dieldrin and DDT (RATCLIFFE, 1980; NEWTON, 1986).  
Following the imposition of voluntary and then mandatory bans on the use of dieldrin and 
DDT, and on the manufacture of PCBs, otter populations have progressively, but not 
completely, recovered in England and Wales (LENTON, CHANIN AND JEFFERIES, 1980; 
STRACHAN, ET AL., 1990; STRACHAN AND JEFFERIES, 1996; CRAWFORD, 2003).  However, this 
recovery has been slower than that of affected predatory bird species, and in some cases has 
been assisted by introductions.    


 
There is clear evidence that the ban on the use of dieldrin led to subsequent reduction 
in exposure and bioaccumulation in predatory birds in Britain (NEWTON, HAAS AND 
FREESTONE, 1990; NEWTON AND GALBRAITH, 1991; NEWTON AND WYLLIE, 1992; NEWTON, 
WYLLIE AND ASHER, 1993; NEWTON, DALE AND LITTLE, 1999; SHORE, ET AL., in press-a).  
However, evidence for a similar decline in PCB accumulation in raptors is less clear.  Total 
PCB concentrations have declined significantly in the eggs of some terrestrial and marine 
predatory birds but similar reductions have not been observed for liver residues in kestrels 
(Falco tinnunculus) and the reduction in PCBs in sparrowhawks (Accipiter nisus) has been 
significant but slight (NEWTON, DALE AND LITTLE, 1999; SHORE, ET AL., in press-a). There has 
been a significant decline in total PCB concentrations in the livers of herons (Ardea cinerea) 
in Britain but this occurred predominantly during the 1980s and there has been little change 
since (SHORE, ET AL., in press-a).     
Otter numbers in Britain (England in particular) were relatively low during the second 
half of the twentieth century and few carcasses were recovered and assessed for 
contamination.  Thus, quantitative information on changes in dieldrin and PCB concentrations 
in British otters over the last 30 years is somewhat sparse and fragmented.  In the present 
paper, we collate and review the available data on dieldrin and PCB residues in otters and ask:   
 
(i)  have the declines in dieldrin concentrations observed in predatory birds also occurred in 
otters in Britain? 
(ii)  given the variable nature of declines in liver PCB concentrations in predatory birds in 
Britain, what evidence is there that PCB levels have declined in otters in Britain? 
(iii)  do current levels of dieldrin and PCB contamination pose a threat to otters in Britain?  
(iv) in the light of the reviewed information, is there is a need for toxicological monitoring of 
otters in Britain in the future?  
 
 
2   LONG TERM TRENDS IN DIELDRIN AND PCB CONCENTRATIONS IN OTTERS IN 
BRITAIN 
 
2.1 Dieldrin 
Surprisingly, there are few extensive time-trend data for dieldrin concentrations in otters in 
Britain.  Liver dieldrin concentrations were monitored over eight years (1988-1996) in otters 
from south west England and were estimated to have fallen from approximately 1 mg/kg to 
0.05 mg/kg wet weight (ww) during this period (SIMPSON, ET AL., 2000).   Collation of 
available data for liver dieldrin concentrations in otters from across Britain over a longer time 
period demonstrates considerable variation between studies (Figure 1).  This, in part, may 
reflect differences in the provenance of the samples and the time period in which they were 
collected.  It is also possible that the variation is in part due to differences in analytical 
methodologies (SHORE, WALKER AND WEINBURG, 2002) and in the age, sex and nutritional 
status of animals in the sample, all of which can affect the magnitude of liver residues in 
vertebrates (WIENBURG AND SHORE, 2004).  When the arithmetic mean liver dieldrin 
concentration from each study was related to the mid-point for the period over which samples 
were collected, a significant negative relationship between year and liver dieldrin 
concentration was detected (Spearman Rank Correlation Coefficient (r) = -0.627; P<0.05). 
The most marked reduction in liver concentrations appeared to occur before 1970 and was 
concurrent with that measured in herons and other freshwater piscivorous birds in Britain 
(NEWTON, WYLLIE AND ASHER, 1993; SHORE, ET AL., in press-a).  However, the significance 
of the decline in dieldrin residues in otters was highly dependent on the data for the small 
sample of five otters that had died between 1965 and 1969 (Figure 1) and there is little 


 
evidence for any decline after 1970 (Figure 1); the decline in liver dieldrin concentrations was 
no longer significant (r= -0.503; P >0.10) when the five otters from 1965-69 were excluded 
from the analysis.   Thus, in contrast to the data for otters from south west England, evidence 
for a national decline in dieldrin residues in otters is, at best, weak.

2.2  PCBs 
There has been a decline in PCB concentrations in otters in various countries in Europe 
(GUTLEB, 2002), but evidence for similar declines in Britain is somewhat equivocal.  Analysis 
of total PCB concentrations in the livers (or occasionally thigh muscle) of 32 otters collected 
in England and Wales between 1983 and 1992 suggested that tissue PCB concentrations in the 
species were declining linearly at a rate of 5.83 µg/g lipid per year and were predicted to 
reach an average concentration of 6 µg/g lipid by the end of 1994 (MASON, 1998).  However, 
the magnitude of this decline, and in fact evidence for any decline at all, was highly 
dependent on measurements made in ten otters that died between 1983 and 1985.  Six had 
tissue residues of 50 µg/g lipid or less (similar to concentrations measured in otters in later 
years), whereas four had much higher residues of between 100 and 250 µg/g lipid and skewed 
the estimated average concentration upwards.  Thus, as with dieldrin, the apparent decline in 
PCB residues over time was highly influenced by data for just a handful of animals. This is a 
general problem with small sample sizes and contaminant residues which are usually not 
normally distributed.  A subsequent analysis of liver total PCB concentrations in 214 otters 
from across England and Wales (BRADSHAW AND SLATER, 2002) did not detect any decline in 
liver total PCB concentrations between 1988 and 1999 (Figure 2), and the median liver total 
PCB concentration was more than twice the 6 µg/g predicted by MASON (1998).  The lack of a 
detectable decline in otters during the 1990s was consistent with the lack of any detectable 
change in liver PCBs in sparrowhawks, kestrels and herons over a similar time period (SHORE 
ET AL., in press-a).

There was a similar trend for specific PCB congeners in 56 otters from south west 
England that died between 1988 and 1996 and were analysed by SIMPSON ET AL. (2000).  Liver 
concentrations of congeners 118, 138, 153 and 180 declined significantly by some 14 - 17% 
each year over the eight year period.  However, it was notable that the decline in Aroclor 
(1260) matched total PCB concentration was less marked (7% per annum) and was not 
statistically significant (SIMPSON, 1998). 
The results of these different regional and national scale studies on temporal trends in 
liver PCB concentrations appear to conflict with each other to some extent.  However, they 
are all broadly consistent with the concepts that: (i) liver concentrations of individual PCB 
congeners have declined in otters in Britain; (ii) the annual rate of decline was faster in earlier 
(1980s and perhaps earlier) than later (1990s) years, and not linear as suggested by MASON 
(1998); (iii) the decline in liver total PCB concentrations may be less marked (and so less 
detectable) than that for individual congeners, and (iv) there may be regional variation in PCB 
accumulation by otters which reduces the power to detect temporal trends at large spatial 
scales.  Significant regional variation in PCB accumulation was reported for otters in Scotland 
that died between 1987 and 1992 (KRUUK AND CONROY, 1996) and in otters from Sweden 
between 1968 and 1999 (ROOS ET AL., 2001), although it was not detected in otters in England 
and Wales between 1998 and 2000 (BRADSHAW AND SLATER, 2002). 
To determine whether there was any further evidence that liver PCB concentrations 
have declined in otters in Britain, we collated all other published data on liver residues in 
British otters that we could find.  Earlier data for PCBs in otters from south west England 
were compared with that for the 56 otters analysed by SIMPSON ET AL. (2000).  The decline in 
liver PCBs in otters that occurred between 1988 and 1996 appeared to be part of a longer-term 
reduction (Figure 4).

Overall therefore, there is evidence that there has been a decline in liver PCB 
concentrations in otters in areas in Britain as far apart as south west England and Shetland, 
and probably, therefore, across the whole country.  This decline was evident during the 1980s 
and the first half of the 1990s, but may have begun earlier, as detected elsewhere in Europe 
(e.g., ROOS ET AL., 2001).  Whether this decline is ongoing is unclear as there was no apparent 
reduction in liver total PCB concentrations across the 1990s as a whole (BRADSHAW AND 
SLATER, 2002), although such trends may be difficult to detect when measuring total PCBs 
rather than specific congeners (SIMPSON, 1998 but see ROOS ET AL., 2001).  If liver PCB 
concentrations are still declining in otters in Britain, the pattern of earlier trends suggests that 


 
the annual rate of change is likely to be smaller (in absolute terms) currently than in earlier 
years, although the annual percentage decline may be similar.  Small temporal changes in 
liver PCB concentrations are likely to be difficult to detect in animals across wide 
geographical areas because of regional variations in exposure.  Furthermore, variation due to 
other causes, such as sex and cause of death (BRADSHAW AND SLATER, 2002), may also 
confound detection of temporal trends, unless sample sizes are large.  
 
 
3   DO CURRENT LEVELS OF DIELDRIN AND PCB CONTAMINATION POSE A THREAT 
TO OTTERS IN BRITAIN? 
 
The most recent published data on dieldrin and PCB concentrations in otters across England 
and Wales is from the 1988-2000 monitoring study of the Environment Agency (BRADSHAW 
AND SLATER, 2002). The geometric mean liver dieldrin concentration in otters during this 
period was 0.119µg/g ww and the maximum concentration, reported erroneously by 
BRADSHAW AND SLATER, (2002) as 20.2 µg/g ww, was 28.1µg/g ww (SIMPSON ET AL., 2000).  
Liver dieldrin concentrations associated with acute toxicity range typically up to 
approximately 50µg/g ww in various mustelids; the fox (Vulpes vulpes) is thought to be one 
of the more sensitive mammals and lethal dieldrin liver residues in this species are 1µg/g ww 
(JEFFERIES, 1969; JEFFERIES AND HANSON, 2002).  Thus, although liver dieldrin 
concentrations may still be high in a small number of otters in England and Wales, average 
concentrations are a tenth of those associated with acute toxicity in the “sensitive” fox.  
Evidence of any chronic impacts of dieldrin on otters is scant but animals suffering from 
retinal dysplasia were found to have significantly lower concentrations of vitamin A and 
higher concentrations of dieldrin than otters with normal retinas (WILLIAMS, SIMPSON AND 
FLINDALL, 2004).  Furthermore, it has been suggested that dieldrin may act synergistically 
with other toxicants such as PCBs (SIMPSON ET AL., 2000).  Thus, while current levels of 
dieldrin accumulation by otters in Britain are generally well below those likely to cause acute 
toxicity, the chronic effects that dieldrin may have, and how any such effects may impact on 
otter populations, are poorly characterised.  
Proposed “safe” and “critical” tissue concentrations for total PCBs of 10µg/g lipid and 
30µg/g lipid have been proposed for otters (MASON AND WREN, 2001); these are based 
largely on reproductive endpoints.  The median liver PCB concentrations in otters collected 
across England and Wales between 1988 and 2000 was 14.5µg/g lipid and the maximum 
concentration was 128µg/g lipid (BRADSHAW AND SLATER, 2002).  Median concentrations in 
different regions of England and Wales varied between 10.4 µg/g (north east England) and 
31.0µg/g (north west England), although inter-region variation across England and Wales was 
not statistically significant (BRADSHAW AND SLATER, 2002).  Thus, although liver PCB 
concentrations appear to have declined, more than half of the otters in England and Wales 
would appear to accumulate liver total PCB concentrations that exceed the proposed safe 
level.  Substantial numbers of animals (in one region more than half) also accumulate liver 
concentrations above the proposed critical limit.   
PCBs may also exert chronic effects on otters other than those directly affecting 
reproduction.  PCB accumulation in some cases has been associated with low vitamin A 
status in otters (MURK ET AL., 1998; SIMPSON ET AL., 2000) which, in turn, is associated with 
increased susceptibility to infections.  While there is no clear evidence of gross pathology due 
to vitamin A deficiency in otters, relatively few animals have been examined in detail by 
veterinary pathologists (SIMPSON, 1997; SIMPSON ET AL., 2000).  BRADSHAW AND SLATER 
(2002) noted that otters with enlarged adrenals and/or peritoneal adhesions of septicemia had 
higher liver total PCB concentrations compared with animals without these symptoms.  


 
Whether there was any direct link between PCB accumulation by otters and their pathology 
was unknown.  Such relationships can be confounded by associated factors such as starvation 
which can result in the internal remobilisation of PCB residues and elevate concentrations in 
the liver (WIENBURG AND SHORE, 2004).   
What impacts current PCB accumulation by otters may have on their populations in 
England and Wales is not clear.  Although there appears to be widespread exceedence of the 
proposed safe tissue concentration in otters from almost all regions in England and Wales, 
populations have expanded in many areas (LENTON, CHANIN AND JEFFERIES, 1980; 
STRACHAN, ET AL., 1990; STRACHAN AND JEFFERIES, 1996; CRAWFORD, 2003).  Proposed safe 
and critical concentrations may possibly be overly conservative but it is also possible that 
proposed safe limits for individuals cannot be extrapolated directly to populations because 
population dynamics are affected by a complex suite of density-dependent and independent 
factors.  It is also possible that current levels of PCB accumulation may be reducing the 
population growth rate of otters in England and Wales but not to zero, and so do not prevent 
expansion of the population.   
The possible impacts of PCBs on otters in Scotland are even less well understood than 
for animals in England and Wales.  This is because the last published data for PCB 
concentrations in otters in Scotland date back to 1992.  The geometric mean total PCB 
concentration for 116 otters sampled from across Scotland between 1987 and 1992 was 
27.4µg/g lipid (KRUUK AND CONROY, 1996), close to the proposed critical limit, but there was 
considerable variation between regions.  Some of the most contaminated otters were from 
Shetland and their liver PCB concentrations were roughly 1-2µg/g ww (Figure 3), which 
approximates to 25-50µg/g lipid weight using the conversion factor proposed by JEFFERIES 
AND HANSON (2002).   PCBs may have had sub-lethal effects on individual otters in Shetland 
(KRUUK AND CONROY, 1991) but whether there were impacts on populations is unclear.  
KRUUK AND CONROY (1996) found that otter population density increased on Shetland 
between 1987 and 1992, even though the geometric mean liver total PCB concentration 
exceeded 50µg/g lipid weight.  The authors argued that their findings cast doubt on the 
significance of the critical PCB tissue limit proposed for otters.  Interestingly however, the 
long-term residue data for Shetland otters indicate that liver PCB concentrations had fallen 
sharply prior to 1987 (Figure 3).  Thus, the increase in otter numbers on Shetland after 1987 
could be considered consistent with a reduction in PCB-mediated effects on survival and/or 
reproduction and a resultant increase population growth rate.  However, the quantitative 
relationship between tissue total PCB concentrations and population growth rate in otters is 
not defined and may vary with population density.  Overall, the current significance of PCB 
contamination for otters in Scotland remains poorly understood.   
 
 
4   IS THERE A NEED TO CONTINUE TOXICOLOGICAL STUDIES ON OTTERS IN 
BRITAIN?  
 
The available data suggest that dieldrin residues in otters are now generally below those likely 
to cause acute toxicity, although some uncertainty remains about the potential impacts of 
current chronic exposure.  Arguably, there is even greater uncertainty about the impacts that 
exposure to PCBs may have on otter populations in Britain. There is evidence that PCBs have 
declined in otters in Britain but it is not clear if this decline continued throughout and beyond 
the 1990s.  There is also scant information on spatial variation in PCB accumulation by otters 
and the likely relative importance of local versus diffuse sources of contamination.  Such 
knowledge is important in understanding the factors that may affect habitat suitability for 
otters, their ability to recolonise previously occupied areas, and why some areas may act as 


 
population sources or sinks.   The Environment Agency is, to date, still monitoring PCBs in 
otters in England and Wales.  These studies will enhance the ability to predict long-term 
trends and assess current risk.  In contrast, there is a complete lack of information about how 
PCB concentrations in Scottish otters have changed over the last 15 years, despite the 
prevalence of high liver concentrations in populations in some regions.  Assessment of current 
PCB concentrations in otters from Scotland is needed and should be accompanied by detailed 
pathological examination of carcasses so as to detect any chronic effects associated with 
contaminant exposure.  
While further measurement of total PCB concentrations in otters is required to 
quantify long-term spatial and temporal variation in accumulation (and its causes), such 
measurements are relatively crude and do not reveal the concentrations of the most 
toxicologically important dioxin-like congeners.  Thus, there is also a need to measure toxic 
equivalents (TEQs) in otters (VAN DEN BERG, ET AL., 1998).  This can be done by chemical 
quantification of relevant PCB congeners and/or through chemical-activated luciferase 
expression (CALUX) assays (GUTLEB AND MURK, 2002).  Measurement of toxic equivalents 
will allow a more refined assessment of the risk to otters from PCBs in Britain than has been 
possible to date as TEQ concentrations can be related directly to effects on retinoids and 
thyroid hormone homeostasis (GUTLEB AND MURK, 2002).  The ongoing studies by the 
Environment Agency will differ from their earlier studies on otters in Britain in that they will 
chemically quantify the dioxin-like individual congeners so that TEQs can be determined 
(SIMPSON, Personal communication) 
While attention with regards the effects of contaminants on otters has focused on 
organochlorine pesticides and PCBs, other contaminants and biologically active compounds 
may also pose a toxic threat.  Certain metals, such as mercury, can cause adverse effects, at 
least at the level of the individual (MASON AND WREN, 2001; GUTLEB, 2002).  Although 
average levels of mercury accumulation by otters in Britain would appear too low to be 
directly toxic (MASON, LAST AND MACDONALD, 1986; MASON AND REYNOLDS, 1988; 
KRUUK, CONROY AND WEBB, 1997; BRADSHAW AND SLATER, 2002), more subtle, sub-lethal 
effects may occur at lower exposures.  Furthermore, otters recolonising habitat close to 
industrially developed areas may suffer more severe exposure than typically measured in 
animals from rural environments as there can be strong gradients of mercury bioaccumulation 
away from industrial point sources (LÓPEZ ALONSO, ET AL., 2003).  Spatially resolved data on 
mercury accumulation by otters is sparse. 
Although there is some information on the levels of mercury and some other toxic 
metals in otters in Britain, data for other compounds, including those which are being emitted 
in increasing amounts into the environment, are completely lacking.  Environmental inputs of 
platinum group metals are rising due to their use in catalytic converters and the therapeutic 
use of cisplatin as an anti-cancer treatment (WHO, 1991, 2002).  The exposure of wildlife to 
these metals, and possible associated effects, are largely unquantified.  There is also concern 
about widespread and increasing contamination of biota with polybrominated diphenyl ethers 
(PBDEs) which are used as flame retardants, are structurally similar to PCBs, and may cause 
adverse effects in mammals (TANABE, 2004).  The exposure of otters to other classes of 
contaminant, including agricultural and industrial biocides and waste products, 
pharmaceutical and veterinary products are also all mostly unquantified.  Mass spectrometric 
analysis of sparrowhawk livers from birds that died in the 1990s revealed the presence of at 
least 40 unknown compounds (SHORE, ET AL., in press-b) and the exposure of otters to 
contaminants is equally poorly characterised.  Identification and quantification of exposure to 
unknown persistent and non-persistent chemicals is clearly important.  The recently 
discovered drastic role of dichlofenac in causing population crashes in vultures in India and 


 
Pakistan (OAKS, ET AL., 2004) have again demonstrated the capability of biologically active 
compounds to drastically affect top predator populations.  
In conclusion, lack of knowledge about the range of contaminants to which otters are 
exposed, and the resultant impacts this exposure may have, means that there is a continued 
conservation need for toxicological monitoring in this species.  Furthermore, measurement of 
chemical exposure in top predators is a way of: (i) obtaining an integrated measure of 
spatially and temporally variable contamination, and (ii) interpreting the impacts of this 
contamination in a biologically meaningful way.  The otter is one of the best bio-indicators in 
freshwater systems of the risk posed by contaminants, particularly persistent organic 
contaminants (CARSS AND SHORE, 2003).  Thus, chemical and/or biomarker analysis of either 
carcasses or spraints should form a key component of any monitoring programme for riparian 
systems.  Coupling chemical and population monitoring of otters to chemical monitoring of 
sediments, waters and fish would provide a powerful tool for assessing the environmental 
quality and health of aquatic habitats. This, and the conservation requirement for better 
characterisation of the chemical risk to otters, is why there is a need for future toxicological 
monitoring in this species.
