MONITORING THE RE-INTRODUCTION OF THE OTTER IN THE NETHERLANDS BY 
TELEMETRY AND GENETIC AND HORMONAL ANALYSIS OF SPRAINTS 
 
Hugh JANSMAN, Dennis LAMMERTSMA & Freek NIEWOLD 
 
Alterra - Centre for Ecosystems,  P.O. Box 47, NL-6700 AA Wageningen, The Netherlands 
email: HughJansman@wur.nl: Dennis.Lammertsma@wur.nl: Freek.Niewold@wur.nl  
 
 
In 2002 the first Eurasian otters (Lutra lutra) were re-introduced into The Netherlands. 
According to IUCN (1995) guidelines for re-introductions, it is recommended to monitor this 
process, which is the responsibility of Alterra. The objective of the monitoring is to become 
well informed about the status of the animals, which allows the establishment of a viable 
population with a minimum of released animals. The monitoring will show if there is a need 
to adjust the release plan early in time as any problems arise.  
Fifteen otters, six males and nine females, were released in 2002, originating from 
Latvia (6), Belarus (5), Czech Republic (2; rehabilitated otters) and Sweden (2; EEP breeding 
programme).  The otters received a transmitter and a transponder.  Three types of transmitters 
were used, ranging from 67x30 millimetres, 70 grams to 56x40 millimetres, 50 grams. The 
pulse frequency was ca.20 biebs per minute at 30MHz. The range differed between 700-1,100 
meters, depending on the habitat, and the lifetime of the batteries was, on average, 11 months 
(with a maximum of 23 months). Telemetric monitoring was by car, boat and bike in National 
Park ‘De Weerribben’ (total area ca. 45 km2) and took about nine hours per day. If an otter 
was lost for a few days a plane was used to search large areas in a few hours (LAMMERTSMA 
ET AL., 2003). 
Besides telemetry, a non-invasive method to monitor the otters was developed.  This 
was based on genetic typing of DNA extracted from spraints (molecular scatology). By DNA 
typing, individual otters can be identified and sexed by a genetic profile (fingerprint) 
consisting of several highly variable microsatellites. By doing so the population structure and 
dynamics can be studied as well as the kinship structure and genetic drift. The hormonal 
monitoring of otter spraints is based on excretion products of hormones. The analyses can be 
added to the information derived by genetic monitoring, which allows the tracing of the 
reproduction status of an animal. Thus it is also possible to monitor any disturbances in this 
status, due to, for example, environmental pollution or other stress factors. The genetic and 
hormonal monitoring with spraints has as important advantage in that there is no need to 
disturb the animals. However, the DNA and hormones in faeces are susceptible to breakdown 
by bacteria and UV light. As a consequence spraints need to be fresh (<12 hours old) when 
gathered and only approximately <30% of the spraints can be analysed successfully 
(JANSMAN, CHANIN AND DALLAS, 2001).  
After the batteries of the transmitters have failed, this non-invasive monitoring method 
will take over. In the future it can even be used to reconstruct genetic processes as genetic 
drift and gene flow. The total of monitoring techniques has already proved their use. With 
telemetry the surveillance behaviour, and settlement, could be followed. In the autumn of 
2003, levels of the female pregnancy hormone progesterone were found to be elevated in 
some of the analysed spraints. In January 2004 in collected spraints, two new DNA profiles 
were found which could be related to two otter ‘couples’ from which both the father and the 
mother gave half of their DNA to their offspring. Therefore not only was reproduction proved 
by this spraint analysis, but the parents could be determined as well. The practical experience 
of this non-invasive technique, parallel to the telemetric surveillance data gathered during this 
re-introduction project, will be of great value for other monitoring and conservation projects 


of otters and other animals in the future. Progress reports of the re-introduction project can be 
found on www.otter.alterra.nl.
