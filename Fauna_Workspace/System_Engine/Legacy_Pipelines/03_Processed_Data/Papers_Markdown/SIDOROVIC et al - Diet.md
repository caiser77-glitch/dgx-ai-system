DIETARY PREFERENCE OF OTTERS LUTRA LUTRA IN RELATION TO AQUATIC PREY 
SUPPLY IN SMALL WATERCOURSES IN BELARUS 
 
V.E SIDOROVICH, I.A. SOLOVEJ AND G.G. JANUTA  
 
The Vertebrate Predation Research Group, Institute of Zoology, National Academy of Sciences of Belarus, 
Akademicheskaya str. 27, Minsk-220072, Belarus. email: vadimsid@mustbel.open.by 
 
 
Keywords:  Diet, Lutra lutra, spraint analysis,  
 
 
1. INTRODUCTION 
 
There has been a considerable number of studies on feeding and the general diet of otters 
(MASON AND MACDONALD, 1986; KRUUK, 1995; JEDRZEJEWSKA ET AL., 2001 and references 
therein).  There are, however, fewer studies which analysed the feeding habits and dietary 
structure of otters in relation to the composition and abundance of prey species. (KRUUK AND 
MOORHOUSE, 1990; WEBER, 1990; HEGGBERGET AND MOSEID, 1994; KRUUK, 1995; BIRZAKS, 
OZOLINŠ AND ORNICANS, 1998).  In Belarus, such studies have not been undertaken, while 
there is only a single publication dealing with this topic in eastern Europe (BIRZAKS, 
OZOLINŠ AND ORNICANS, 1998).  To address this question we were particularly interested in 
investigating the small watercourses which constitute the main part of the aquatic ecosystem 
network and harbour most of the otter population in Belarus. 
 
 
2. STUDY AREAS, MATERIAL AND METHODS 
 
The study took place on the upper reaches of the Lovat River (Gorodok District, Vitebsk 
Region, north eastern Belarus), the Svolna Catchment (Rossony District, Vitebsk Region, 
central north Belarus), and the Volka Catchment (Volozhin District, Minsk Region and Ivie 
District, Grodno Region, central Belarus). 
Studies of the composition of the otters’ diet and the availability of the aquatic prey in 
12 small watercourses of different types (five small rivers, three brooks, two canalised small 
rivers, and two drainage canals) were undertaken during 2001-2002.  The small watercourses 
chosen for the study were typical of Belarus.  A total of 2,658 spraints were analysed, while 
the aquatic prey (fish, crayfish (Astacus astacus), edible frogs (Rana esculenta) hibernating 
common frogs (Rana temporaria) and aquatic beetles was determined along 76 sections of 
stream and 77 fragments of beaver ponds partly inundated the streams.  The stream lengths 
investigated ranged from 60 to 210 metres, while the beaver pond fragments were between 15 
and 420 m2.  We investigated otter diet and the aquatic prey stock at the same time during the 
warm (June-early October) and cold (late October-December) seasons. 
The method used for estimating the species diversity, density and biomass of the 
water-dwelling prey of otters was based on netting.  Several nets were adapted for the size, 
flow rate and other parameters of small rivers (SIDOROVICH ET AL., 2003; in press).  Other nets 
were developed to census the aquatic prey in beaver ponds and their fragments of different 
configuration, depth and presence of a large littoral part overgrown by macrophytes and other 
parameters (SIDOROVICH ET AL., 2001; unpublished.).  In the warm season brown frogs, (R. 
temporaria and R. arvalis) were censused on 10 x 1 metre plots located on the stream 
banksides (PIKULIK ET AL., 2001). 


Otter spraints were washed and the contents identified microscopically according to 
published keys (fish scales, teeth, and vertebrates: GALKIN, 1953; ZHUKOV, 1965; MARZ, 
1987; STEINMETZ AND MULLER, 1991; amphibian bones: BOHME, 1977; MARZ, 1987).  The 
presence of insects, crayfish and molluscs was determined by exoskeleton remains; reptiles, 
by their bones and skins; birds, by feathers and mammals by hairs and teeth.  The number of 
different prey individuals found in the spraints analysed was taken to be 100% in calculations 
of percentages of prey occurrence in the diet (%OC).  The diets, calculated as percentages of 
occurrence of various prey categories, were transformed into a ratio of prey biomass 
consumed (%BC) by means of average weights of each prey species or category. 
To assess the otter preference of particular prey species, Ivlev’s selectivity index [D, 
modified by JACOBS (1974)] was calculated: D=(r-p)/(r+p-2rp), where “r” is a fraction of a 
given prey species or category among all prey species in the dietary composition of otters 
(calculation based on %OC in the spraints analysed) and “p” is a fraction of the prey species 
or category in the prey association.  D ranged from minus one (total avoidance of a species) to 
zero (selection proportional to occurrence) to plus one (maximum positive selection).  Also, 
Spearman correlation (rs) (SOKAL AND ROHLF, 1995) was used for statistical analysis of the 
data obtained. 
 
 
3.   RESULTS 
 
 Table 1 summarises our observations on the otter diet from the 12 small watercourses 
separately in the warm and cold seasons.  On these small watercourses, otters fed mainly on 
three prey categories - fish, crayfish and frogs, which comprised 87-98% (average 94%) of 
the biomass consumed.  In the warm season the frequency of fish in the diets varied from 36- 
79% (average 60%); crayfish, from 0–33% (average 7%); frogs, from 5–47% (average 23%); 
and aquatic beetles, from 1–20% (8%). From four to 15 (total 20) fish species were found in 
the otter diet at the small watercourses investigated.  Basically pike (Esox lucius), roach 
(Rutilus rutilus), dace (Leuciscus leuciscus), and spiny loach (Cobitis taenia), as well as, in a 
part of the small watercourses, perch (Perca fluviatilis), crucian carp (Carassius carassius), 
common bream (Abramis brama) and mud loach (Misgurnus fossilis) dominated the fish 
eaten by otters.  In the warm season edible frogs which live continuously in aquatic 
ecosystems contributed only a small part amongst amphibian prey consumed:  on small rivers 
and brooks, about 2% and drainage canals 27%.  The majority of other amphibians predated 
by otters were brown frogs.  Aquatic beetles in the diet were represented by rather big species 
such as Dytiscus marginalis, D. latissimus, D. dimidiatus, D. circumcinctus, D. circumflecsus, 
and Hydrophilus piceus.  Other prey categories made only a small contribution to the otter 
diet.  In the cold season many more frogs (mostly the common frog) were eaten by otters, 31-
59% (Table 1). 
 
The aquatic prey stock in the same 12 model small watercourses was also estimated 
(Table 2).  In the warm season, there were 0.2-4.8 kilogrammes, (average ca. 1.4 kg) of 
aquatic prey per 100 metre stretch of river.  In terms of biomass, fish were the dominant 
category in the census catches, 43-83% (mean 65%).  From three to 16 (total 21) fish species 
were caught in the different watercourses.  Pike, roach, perch and spiny loach occurred most 
frequently in the majority of the streams, while in a part of them, verkhovka (Leucaspius 
deliniatus), mud loach, dace and crucian carp were the most common fish recorded.  In

High overlap between the frequency of occurrence of different prey categories in the otter 
diets and the composition of the prey in each of the 12 watercourses studied was found - the 
Pianka’s index = 0.50-0.98 (mean 0.88).  Statistically significant positive correlations 
between the proportions of different prey categories in the otter diets and their proportion in 
the prey were also found (Table 3).  The calculated values of the Ivlev’s selectivity index, also 
given in Table 3, suggested that pike, burbot and crayfish were preferred by otters.  
Cyprinidae, Percidae and Cobitidae were not so preferable as pike and burbot, and the 
selectivity index for them varied between small watercourses investigated.  Amongst the 
common species roach, common bream, white bream (Blicca broerkna), crucian carp, ide 
(Leuciscus idus) and mud loach were preferred, while rather small species such as the 
verkhovka, bleak (Alburnus alburnus), spiny loach and stone loach (Nemacheilus barbatulus) 
were taken by otters in proportions which were much lower than those in the living fish 
community.  As with small fish, frogs and aquatic beetles were mostly taken by otters less 
often than they occurred in the habitats (Table 3).

 4.   DISCUSSION 
 
In inland habitats, otters prefer to feed on fish and crayfish, and if the latter is abundant, this 
prey is consumed by otters at an even higher proportion than fish (JEDRZEJEWSKA ET AL., 2001 
and references therein).  The data obtained relating biomass of these preferred prey in small 
watercourses showed that it is too scarce to provide enough for a dense population of otters.  
Indeed, taking into account that wild living otters need about one kilogramme of food per day 
(TUMANOV AND SMELOV, 1980), even a coarse approach shows, for instance, that if we 
suppose that otters consume all the fish and crayfish available in such a small river, each otter 
needs to occupy solely more than 20 kilometres of stream.  Otters actually populate such 
rivers more densely; usually two to four individuals per ten kilometre stretch (SIDOROVICH, 
1992, 1997).  On small watercourses, otters need to feed, in part, on other less profitable prey, 
such as frogs.  Therefore, on small watercourses in conditions of limited stocks of fish and 
crayfish, otters tend to consume different prey in relation to their availability.  Generally 
following this opportunistic feeding strategy on small watercourses, otters demonstrated some 
preference for crayfish and bigger species of fish, whereas frogs were consumed in fairly high 
proportions, but this was still lower than their frequency in the prey association.
