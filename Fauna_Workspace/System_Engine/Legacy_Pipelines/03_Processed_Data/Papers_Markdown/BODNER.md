IS COMPENSATION THE RIGHT APPROACH TO OTTER PROBLEMS? 
 
Michaela BODNER 
 
RAMSAR OEG, A-3943 Schrems, Austria 
email bodner@ramsar.schrems.at 
 
 
Abstract: Throughout Europe, otters live in so-called ‘cultured’ landscapes.  This means that most areas of the 
continent have been, and still are, strongly influenced by human activities, such as agriculture, producction of 
hydroelectric energy, recreation, road-building, etc. Natural habitats have become man-made habitats.  Otters 
throughout Europe, to a variable but generally high extent, predate on fish.  In areas where fish either have a 
traditional or commercial value, or both, like fish farming, the foraging strategies of this predator may lead to a 
conflict about who is permitted to eat fish.  In particular in fish farming areas, otters, like other fish predators, 
will be seen as unwelcome competitors, reducing the farmers´ profits by eating fish that could otherwise be sold.  
This problem cannot be solved to everyone’s satisfaction as both fish farmers and otters have a right to exist.  So 
a compromise is needed.  One possible solution seems to be paying compensation to the farmers.  Give them the 
money for the fish the otter has eaten and he is satisfied.  Or is he? And can the conservationist also be satisfied?  
In (Lower) Austria, fish farmers receive compensation for loss due to otter predation.  Is this an example to be 
followed?  What other options do we have?  Do we need any at all ? 
 
1   INTRODUCTION  
 
Following a serious reduction in their populations throughout much of Europe in the 
past two centuries, otters (Lutra lutra) are beginning to recover and have started recolonising 
several regions in Europe (e.g. United Kingdom, Germany, Austria, and others). A 
precondition of this recolonisation is the existence of viable otter populations, i.e. populations 
that have the potential to allow the animals to expand and re-occupy areas where the species 
became extinct in past years.  It is therefore evident that such core areas for recolonisation are 
vital for the future positive development of otter populations throughout Europe.  To follow a 
general principle of species conservation, we have to protect those populations that still 
naturally exist, thus these core areas deserve high priority in conservation attention and 
activities. For otters, several of these core populations are located in the principal fish farming 
areas of Europe (e.g. Czech Republic, Hungary, Austria, Germany) and fish is the main prey 
of otters throughout Europe. 
 
2   A CONFLICT OF INTERESTS 
 
The existence of a population of otters, thriving or not, foraging on fish, in a fish farming area 
will lead to a conflict of interests. 
Fish farmers want to produce fish that they can sell on the market.  They act within 
economic necessities and work towards optimising their economic benefits.  ANY factor that 
reduces the fish harvest, and thus their profit, will be seen as a threat, regardless of whether 
this factor has a real negative affect or is just a theoretical possibility.  This is true for bad 
weather, poachers, fish diseases, as well as other animal species that prey on fish, such as the 
otter.  
Conservationists want the otter to recolonise lost areas.  They act towards a natural 
recovery of otter populations, and an increase in the species distribution areas.  
In summary, fish farmers want as few otters as possible in ‘their’ fish farming areas, 
and conservationists want as many otters as possible in fish farming areas as core locations for 
the species’ recovery. 


Clearly fish farmers, without wishing to do so, support otters by providing an extra 
source of food.  Fish ponds, stocked with all kinds of fish species in densities much higher 
than in natural water ecosystems, will clearly attract species, such as the otter which prey on 
fish especially as fish in-man made ponds are, in most cases, easily available to the predator.  
The fish farmer will clearly perceive this as loss of income. On the other hand, fish ponds or 
fish tanks are usually part of the open landscape, and are not located in greenhouses or sheds, 
and, as such, fish farms are not entitled to an “otter-free” environment.  Otters, and other 
animal species, are an integrated part of the landscape which the fish farmer uses for his 
business. 
As each area only exists once, from what has been said so far, it is evident that these 
conflicting interests have to co-exist.  The issue of eliminating the otters or closing down the 
fish farming industry is not an option.  The big question is how the co-existence can work and 
who has a say in this?  Who decides what constitutes “enough otters”? The fish farmer who 
wants to protect his production or the conservationist who wants otters to spread? Or do we 
have to decide at all? 
 
3   OPTIONS 
When discussing this conflict of interests, how can we act at all, what possibilities are there?  
After working on this topic for more than ten years and holding many discussions with a wide 
variety of people who meant well or not so well for the otter, the following ideas and 
possibilities have been suggested: 
 ‘No problem attitude 
 Legal killing 
 Preventive measures 
 Compensation 
 Insurance 
These options deserve a closer look: 
 
‘No-problem attitude’ 
This approach is based on the assumption that the conflict of interests described above is not 
always perceived as a problem.  Indeed, this attitude may have advantages: as conservationists 
we have to ask ourselves if we might not in fact create the problem by admitting its existence.  
Thus we reinforce the opinion that the otter is a ‘problem species’.  A very strong argument in 
favour of this approach is usually that, if the otter has full legal protection, as it has in most 
European countries, we should not have to give specific financial support to individuals to 
enable them to obey the law.  On the other hand, the situation where fish farmers get the 
impression that their problems are ignored or neglected or that they, worst of all, are not taken 
seriouosly, might easily lead to illegal killing of otters.  Fish farmers could try to get rid of the 
problem in their own way if no-one else help them.  Once this starts, it is very hard to get 
back to a co-operative way of co-existence between otters and fish farmers. 
 
Legal killing 
Legal killing aims to regulate the number of otters in a certain area, and in this case, 
regulation means the reduction of otter numbers.  In the long-term, this approach will only be 
successful if the species numbers are reduced considerably, otherwise vacant territories will 
be quickly re-occupied by other individuals.  The measure for the “success” of these activities 
would be a decrease in the complaints of fish farmers.  Clearly, systematic attempts at legal 


killing will stop the population from spreading, so, at present this is an option not to be 
considered by conservationists. 
 
Preventive measures 
Theoretically, preventive measures aim at preventing otter predation on fish stocks.  Such an 
approach should be to the utmost satisfaction of the farmers.  Moreover, no direct interference 
with the otter population is necessary, two clear advantages of this approach. However, not 
many measures are known to be successful, and even those that are may not be applicable 
everywhere, depending, as they do, on pond size, fish stock, land ownership and other factors. 
 
Compensation 
Paying compensation for damage to crops and harvests caused by wild animals is a difficult 
issue.  From a nature conservation point of view, a positive aspect might be an increase in the 
acceptance of the otter and, as a result, support for otter populations in core areas.  On the 
other hand, there are serious drawbacks to such an approach:  costs are unknown in advance 
and will tend to increase. The biggest problem, however, is how to assess damage if part of 
the harvest is missing.  Even if it is known that the otter has been there, how do you separate 
the damage caused by this species from heron and/or cormorant damage, poaching, etc.  
In the Austrian province of Lower Austria, for the past 20 years, fish farmers have 
been compensated for otter damage to their stock in ponds.  The compensation is worked out 
in a case-by-case basis, i.e. the damage is assessed for each fish pond for each year.  Any 
damage that is accepted as having been caused by the otter is paid for. 
Our experience has shown that this approach has succeeded in increasing the 
acceptance of otters by the local people (even in areas where fish farming is not a commercial 
operation, but where there has been a strong tradition). 
 
Insurance 
It is acknowledged that the conservation of wild and/or endangered species is in the interest of 
the public.  This being the case, it is the responsibility of the public to address any problems 
or conflicts that arise regarding these species.  So one might genuinely argue that it is not fair 
to make the “victims” of otter predation carry the full costs of any damage.  Insurance might 
come up as a solution. 
However, insurance is designed principally to cover the unexpected or unpredictible.  
One cannot seriously claim that it is unexpected for otters to eat fish. So, is insurance 
completely out of the question?  
It is reasonable to assume that insurance would not really be cheaper than paying 
damages, as the insurance companies would not want to lose money in this deal. So, 
eventually, premiums would have to increase to cover the costs of damages. 
What might be possible is that the fish farmers take sensible precautions against otters 
entering their ponds. Then, the fish stocks could be insured against any remaining and 
minimised risk. 
 
4   FINAL REMARKS 
When discussing otter predation on commercially valuable fish, paying compensation is a 
possible means of reducing the conflict of interests between nature conservation and the fish 
farming industry.  It is not possible to argue that this is the best or right or only way to address 
this situation, and indeed I think there is no right or wrong way.  Sometimes, solutions in 
nature conservation are also a case of trial and error, where we try to find solutions and then 
have to wait and discover if they do in fact work or not.  I strongly want to encourage anyone 
who has to deal with this conflict to discuss and think and talk and compare how others have 


addressed the problem and then find their own approach.  I clearly do not think there is such a 
thing as a general solution that works everywhere, but it is very important that the problem is 
addressed.  The recovery of otters in many areas in Europe strongly depends on the attitude of 
people to, and perception of, this animal.  Such an approach must also be accepted by fish 
farmers and anglers. So, for the future of otters in Europe, solutions have to be found that 
support the right of the otter to exist.
