PREPARATIONS FOR THE RE-INTRODUCTION OF OTTERS IN THE NETHERLANDS 
 
Addy W.J.J. DE JONGH 
 
Stichting Otterstation Nederland, Spanjaardslaan 136, 8917 AX Leeuwarden, The Netherlands 
email:  a.dejongh@aqualutra.nl  
 
 
Abstract: In 1988 the otter was declared extinct in the Netherlands. Recovery plans resulted in large scale 
measures to improve and re-connect former otter habitats. After other causes of extinction had been removed, a 
re-introduction programme, according to the IUCN guidelines, was planned and carried out in 2002. Four 
captive and orphaned otters from Sweden and the Czech Republic, and 11 wild otters from Belarus and Latvia 
were used in the releases.  
 
Keywords: Eurasian otter, Lutra lutra, re-introduction, Netherlands 
 
 
1   HABITAT RECOVERY 
 
The Dutch otter population was declared extinct in 1988.  In the 1990s some signs of otters 
were found in the province of Limburg (SMIT, 1991), but these incidental observations did not 
alter the national status of the population.  It was still considered as being extinct. 
From 1986 until 2002 much effort was put into the restoration of former otter habitats 
in the north of the Netherlands. This resulted in a remarkable improvement in the water 
quality with regards to its pollution by nitrates and phosphates, heavy metals and 
organochlorides as PCB’s.  Wetlands were considerably enlarged and reconnected.  Near 
roads, canals and bridges, fauna passages and steering fences were built.  The need for the 
defragmentation of nature became the national policy for three Ministeries.  For the Dutch 
wetlands, this resulted in the design of the so called Marsh-axis, which diagonally crosses the 
country from the north east of the country to the south west, and comprises all re-connected 
former and newly developed wetlands in the lowest parts of the country.  Agreements were 
reached with muskrat (Ondatra zibethicus) catchers, because in the Netherlands there is 
intensive control of this pest species.  The controlling agencies agreed to the use of adapted 
trapping methods.  In the last stage of the programme an agreement with local fishermen was 
also obtained.  Since then, fyke nets have to be supplied with stop grids to prevent otters from 
drowning in the re-introduction area.  The fishermen receive a financial compensation for 
proven losses in their catches.  The Dutch government has planned to make the use of 
stopgrids mandatory for the whole country. 
 
 
2   PREPARATIONS FOR RE-INTRODUCTION 
 
The spectacular recovery of otter habitats led to the idea of re-introduction of the Eurasian 
otter (Lutra lutra).  A feasibility study, in which a Population and Habitat Viability Analysis 
(PHVA) had been incorporated (KLOP ET AL., 1998), showed there was a good chance for the 
establishment of a new population.  All causes of extinction in this area had been removed.  
At that time the nearest core areas of good otter populations were more than 350 kilometers 
away.  Natural recolonisation from these populations was not expected to happen in the near 
future. 
Good preparations for the re-introduction of otters in the Netherlands have been made 
following I.U.C.N. guidelines (VOCHTELOO, DE JONGH AND SMIT, 1997).  However, from an 


international point of view, mistakes were made by not informing neighbouring countries and 
the Re-introduction Advisory Committee of the I.U.C.N. Otter Specialist Group about the 
intended re-introduction.  
It was decided to start the re-introduction using wild otters from Belarus and Latvia.  
In Belarus agreements were signed with the national government, the local hunting 
association of Gorodok and the Academy of Sciences in Minsk.  In Latvia there were similar 
agreements with State Forest Service and Gauja NP.  CITES agencies in both these countries 
and in the Netherlands delivered the necessary permits. Finnish, German, Belarussian, Latvian 
and Dutch veterinary control agencies helped in providing the right procedures and papers.  
 
 
3   CATCHING 
 
In 2002, in order to catch otters in Belarus and Latvia soft catch leghold traps were used and 
in Latvia some boxtraps were also set.  Results for the first catching period (April to June) 
there were disappointing.  Only three of six wild otters caught were fit to be transported to the 
Netherlands.  The others were too young, too old or not fit enough.  Together with four otters 
from captivity these were released on 7 July 2002.  The goal for 2002 was to catch 12 otters 
with a 1:1 sex ratio.  An extra catching session in September and October yielded a further 
eight wild otters considered suitable for re-introduction. 
No otters were caught in the box traps, although footprints near and in the entrances 
showed that otters became increasingly more interested in these devices. For a description of 
these traps see KRUUK (1995).  On several occasions people demolished the boxes, while in 
one instance a box was stolen. 
The use of trap transmitters proved to be very important.  Otters caught in traps which 
were connected to such transmitters could be released fairly quickly (after 10–20 minutes), 
which resulted in less stress of the animals.  Catching without transmitters could result in a 
much longer period of captivity (at the most one night) in the trap.  All traps were checked 
daily in the early morning.  As a result of the experiences gained during the catching of otters 
in Latvia and Belarus it is strongly advised to use trap transmitters at all times.  New, long 
range trap transmitters have now been developed which also make it possible to be notified of 
a catching event on a mobile phone (DE JONGH AND DE JONGH, 2004). 
During the second session three otters died as a result of their sedation.  Only recently 
has it become clear that the choice for immobilisation with Domitor and Ketamine was not 
right.  Evidence from the most recent literature shows that Domitor is very dangerous to use 
in animals that might experience stress, hypothermia and fatigue (DE JONGH AND DE JONGH, 
2004).  The wild otters caught in traps suffered mainly from stress.  The animals, having been 
much longer in a trap, also suffered from the other ailments. 
These sedatives can be hazardous not only to wild animals, for example a significant 
number of collie dogs showed a high mortality after sedation with Domitor (DE JONGH AND DE 
JONGH, 2004).  Additionally one otter caught in Belarus had to be killed because of a bad 
wound on its leg. 
At the catching site, the trapped otters were checked according to a standard protocol 
to determine if they were fit for re-introduction, e.g., body weight, length and condition 
(KRUUK, 1995) were measured.  An attempt to age the individuals from the condition of their 
teeth, proved impractical. H. KRUUK AND T. HEGGBERGET (personal communications) 
confirmed this to be true for otters more than one year old.  In the field only other body 
characteristics (such as the presence of testes in the scrotum of males and nipples for females) 
could give some clues about the age classification of an otter as a subadult or adult. 
 


In some cases the check-up could not be performed under the best of conditions, 
because of problems with the sedation.  Stress, hypothermia and fatigue resulted in these 
animals being only lightly sedated.  
 
 
4   TRANSPORT 
 
The otters which were fit for re-introduction were transported to enclosures 12x12 metres, 
where they were held before being transported to the Netherlands.  In Belarus the enclosures 
were situated at the research centre of Dr. Vadim Sidorovich in Zadrach on the border with 
Russia.  In Latvia the otters were held in Gauja NP, Ligatne.  The enclosures were built 
according to the EEP husbandry guidelines for Lutra lutra (MELISSEN, 2000).  
Here the otters were observed and treated with Stronghold® (selamectin, Pfizer) 
against ecto- and endoparasites and vaccinated against rabies.  Some days after arrival in the 
centre one otter in Latvia showed a small wound on its leg, probably as a result of a bite from 
another animal.  This animal was treated several times by a veterinarian using a crush crate.  
The wound fully recovered afterwards. 
When the time came for their transport to the Netherlands the otters were caught from 
their sleeping dens in the enclosures and put in large, slightly adapted, varikennels.  In the 
kennels hay was provided as nest material.  There was also a bowl to provide drinking water. 
At the airports of Minsk and Riga, the animals were given a final visual check by the 
local veterinary agency and customs, after which they were taken to a waiting aircraft.  The 
otters coming from Latvia were flown into the EC via Helsinki; the otters coming from Minsk 
arrived in Frankfurt.  On arrival, the animals were again checked visually by veterinary 
agencies and customs.  All necessary paperwork concerning CITES and health certificates 
were also checked.  After this, the animals were flown to Amsterdam, where after a final 
document check the animals were transferred to Burgers’ Zoo in Arnhem.  Here a tiny animal 
recognition transponder was injected under the skin of their right shoulder and a VHF 
transmitter was surgically inserted in their abdomen.  After some recovery, the animals were 
released into the re-introduction area “the Weerribben” in the Province of Overijssel. 
 
 
5   CRITICISM FROM ABROAD 
 
In 2002 and 2003, the Director of the German Aktion Fischotterschutz, Claus Reuther, 
claimed in the Dutch press that otters are already very close to the border of the Netherlands.  
He stated that he expected that otters would recolonise the Netherlands within ten years in a 
natural way.  If the statements of Aktion Fischotterschutz can be confirmed by sound field 
data and population dynamic modelling, then a fair estimate can be made of the speed of 
natural recolonisation.  If this shows to be less than ten years for the Netherlands, the 
necessity of the Dutch re-introduction has to be reviewed.  If the results show otherwise, a 
continuation of the re-introduction is justified. 
Re-introductions of otters can be very beneficial and can help to reconnect fragmented 
otter populations in Europe (JEFFERIES ET AL., 2005).  Neither the I.U.C.N. nor its Otter 
Specialist Group is principally against re-introductions of Lutra lutra.
