Abstract
The investigation of the 52 largest Austrian rivers with catchment areas >500 km2 (Danube River excluded)
provides a national estimate of the ecological status of Austria’s rivers and an example of the current status of
European alpine rivers. Emphasis is placed on evaluation criteria, such as morphological character, instream
structures, longitudinal river corridor, lateral connectivity and hydrological regime compared with original con-
ditions. This assessment and evaluation of nearly 5000 river kilometers identiﬁes the remaining river stretches with
high habitat quality as well as those stretches that have been altered by systematic channelisation or hydropower
development. Altogether, about 80% (3900 km) of the stretches in Austria’s ‘larger’ rivers are moderately to heavily
impacted by human activity and thus no longer correspond to their original channel form and dynamics. Impound-
ment, water diversion and hydropeaking each impact the hydrology of approximately 20% of the above stretches.
Only about 20% of all investigated river stretches can still be classiﬁed as having high or good habitat quality and as
retaining essentially natural functions. Using natural conditions as a reference, the most severe losses have occurred
in reaches that were originally braided or meandering. Analysing the amount of unaltered river stretches according
to biocoenotic regions, 37% (523 km) of the originally rhithral (trout) region (1416 km) and 18% (480 km) of the
originally hyporhithral (grayling) region (2739 km) remain today in high or good habitat quality. Only a few intact
river stretches (totaling 43 km) can be found along formerly potamal river reaches (731 km). These data underline
the rarity and, therefore, national importance of the remaining unspoiled aquatic habitats. The results presented
here form a basis for water resource management and nature conservation strategies, as well as for restoration
programs. In particular, these investigations help to fulﬁll the requirements of the EU-Water Framework Directive
by assisting to delineate and characterise reference sites of different river types.
Introduction
The morphology, longitudinal and lateral connectiv-
ity, as well as bed load- and discharge regime of
most running waters in Central Europe are severely
disturbed. In their general overview of the alpine re-
gion, Martinet & Dubost (1992) reported that only
10% of the river reaches can be classiﬁed as ‘near-
natural’. Based on Müller’s (1991) vegetation studies,
only fragments of the large braided river systems have
been preserved. This development is by no means re-
stricted to the densely populated regions of Central
Europe. Only 2% of 5.2 million kilometers of U.S.A.
rivers were classiﬁed as relatively pristine (Benke,
1990). In Australia, a national habitat evaluation of
rivers and streams revealed a relatively high degree of
impairment through logging, grazing and construction
of reservoirs for drinking water and irrigation (Stein
et al., 1998). Dynesius & Nilsson (1994) produced a
rough estimate of the proportion of rivers that have
been degraded through fragmentation or ﬂow regula-
tion in the northern third of the world (North America
north of Mexico, Europe and the Republics of the
former Soviet Union). Of the 139 largest river systems


344
(deﬁned as rivers with an average annual discharge
of >350 m3 s−1), the hydrological regime of 77% is
classiﬁed as moderately or strongly altered.
Channel straightening and stabilisation, daming,
water diversion and hydropeaking, bedload retention
and wastewater inputs often severely alter the aquatic
fauna and ﬂora. This is reﬂected in an increasing loss
of species, as well as other negative community level
impacts. From an ecological perspective, the remain-
ing river stretches with largely intact morphological
and hydrological conditions are, therefore, essential
components of water resource management, partic-
ularly within heavily impacted systems. Efforts to
secure and renew drinking water reserves increas-
ingly focus on such river sections (Stanford & Ward,
1992). These areas also function as refuges for species
whose habitats have been changed or destroyed, and
provide a source of organisms recolonizing adjoining,
formerly impacted habitats that have been improved
by remediation or mitigation.
Future programs directed at protecting, restoring
or using aquatic habitats will, therefore, require basic
nationwide inventories or surveys of intact river eco-
systems. The results of such investigations form the
basis for management strategies, including identiﬁc-
ation of priority areas for restoration, conservation,
environmental protection and biotope networks. At
the same time, these data produce an underlying grid
system for detailed future investigations of abiotic
and biotic features, exchange processes and ecological
functions of near-natural reference stretches (Naiman
et al., 1995).
National inventories of river quality can also
provide a valuable input into future guidelines for
Europe-wide water management policies currently un-
der discussion by identifying the remaining intact
river stretches with ‘high or good water quality’
(EUROPEAN COMMISSION, 1999). Therefore, the
objective of this paper is to describe the results of such
an inventory for all Austria’s rivers with catchments
>500 km2. The Danube, as the largest river in Aus-
tria, was excluded from these investigations due to its
unique hydrological and morphological dimensions.
Habitat quality assessment methods
A survey of internationally applied assessment
methods
In comparison with water quality studies, the large-
scale survey and evaluation of physical habitat com-
ponents is a relatively young discipline. Methods
designed to document and evaluate the physical integ-
rity of aquatic habitats over entire catchments were
not developed until the 1980s. In German-speaking
countries, initial efforts have concentrated on the mor-
phology and structural features of the river bed and
riparian zone (LÖLF, 1985; Böttger, 1986; Patzner,
1986; Werth, 1987; Spiegler et al., 1989; Rose, 1990;
Bauer, 1992; Konold, 1992; Bohl et al., 1992; LAWA,
1993). Results of such studies were mostly published
as maps primarily referencing structural components
of the rivers (LFU, 1994). These methods were ex-
panded to assess entire river/ﬂoodplain systems, as
it was done for example in Germany in the frame-
work of a nationwide investigation on aquatic habitats
(Binder & Kraier, 1999). The ‘modular concept for
comprehensive assessment and evaluation of streams
in Switzerland’ (Bundi et al., 2000) is another example
of an application of large-scale habitat assessment
systems, complemented by ﬁve biological modules.
In North America, physical habitat assessment has
increasingly become a standard element in govern-
ment monitoring programs. Abe et al. (1992) report
that most state water quality agencies in the U.S.A.
have initiated investigations to qualitatively address
physical habitat integrity. Especially in Ohio, qual-
itative evaluation of habitat features is already an
integrated part of surface water monitoring (Ohio
EPA, 1988; Rankin, 1995). The U.S. Environmental
Protection Agency’s Environmental Monitoring and
Assessment Program conducts quantitative habitat as-
sessments at all randomly selected sites, as well as a
landscape characterisation of those sites’ catchments
(Kaufmann & Robinson, 1998; Hughes et al., 2000).
Physical habitat inventories of streams are also con-
ducted by the U.S. Forest Service and U.S. Bureau of
Land Management.
Similar approaches are registered in the U.K.,
where the river corridor survey (NRA, 1992) and the
subsequent river habitat survey (RHS, Raven et al.,
1997, 1999) are being systematically carried out to
assess the state of river habitats nationally. SERCON
is a further, conservation-oriented evaluation program
combining physical, chemical and biological features
of rivers and their ﬂoodplains (Boon et al., 1997;
Boon, 2000).
Method for identifying Austria’s running water
habitats with high and good quality
The methodological approach to evaluate and identify


345
intact aquatic habitats was formulated in the frame-
work of a preliminary methodological study (Muhar et
al., 1993); it builds on well-known ecomorphological
assessment approaches, especially those applied in
German-speaking countries, as mentioned above, e.g.
by LÖLF (1985), Werth (1987), Spiegler et al. (1989),
Konold (1992) and Bohl et al. (1992). It focuses on
surveying numerous fundamental elements of ecolo-
gically healthy watersheds as, for example, described
by Naiman (1992) and Muhar & Jungwirth (1998).
In addition to considering established morphological-
structural parameters, emphasis is placed on hydrolo-
gical conditions, as well as their modiﬁcation through
water diversion, hydropeaking or daming; anthro-
pogenic changes related to associated wetlands and,
therefore, lateral connectivity are also included. The
evaluation also gives consideration to criteria affecting
the dynamics of running waters (erosion and sediment-
ation processes, ﬂooding), as well as to longitudinal
connectivity. Finally, the collected data on the indi-
vidual river habitats are related to the original river
type, as promoted both in the legal regulations of Aus-
tria’s Water Rights Act and in the current version of
the EU’s Water Framework Directive.
The evaluation and identiﬁcation of largely intact
aquatic habitats is based on the conditions that charac-
terised Austria’s rivers and streams prior to systematic
channel regulation measures (end of 19th to mid 20th
century) and the construction of large hydroelectric
stations and chains of dams. In contrast to modiﬁca-
tions caused by local ﬂood protection measures, clog
drivers, mills, channel adaptation for navigation etc.
before 1800, these systematic impacts lead to large-
scale alterations of whole river systems. For the major
European rivers, Petts (1989) sets the date for the
dramatic alterations of their hydrological and geomor-
phological characteristics at 1850, although anthro-
pogenic changes caused by weirs, improvements for
navigation etc. have been signiﬁcant over a longer
timescale.
By setting this date, the current evaluation refers
back to a scenario of ‘naturally developed’ Central
European cultural landscapes in which the funda-
mental functions of most river systems were largely
intact and system-inherent processes could still run
their natural course. In Austria, a national land sur-
vey map was developed in 1806–1850, providing a
high-quality physical reference for all rivers.

Results
Characteristics of Austria’s larger running waters
The 52 investigated rivers with a total length of
4915 km are distributed relatively homogeneously
over Austria’s different landscape types. Included are
the larger rivers of the Bohemian Massif, those along
the eastern margin of the Alps, as well as the main
alpine river systems.
The size and distribution of the rivers are clearly
reﬂected in stream order and average annual discharge
(mean annual ﬂow prior to major hydroelectric plant
construction). Stream order classes range from 3 to 8,
but stream order 5 and 6 make up 60% of all invest-
igated systems (Figure 2). Although the focus of the
study was to cover the largest rivers of Austria, the
investigated river stretches represent a wide range of
mean annual ﬂow values. This is due to the fact that the
upper sections (beginning with stream order 3) of all
investigated rivers have also been included (Figure 3).
Channel morphology broadly integrates a range of
features such as ‘adjacent wetlands’ (including differ-
ent types of waterbodies within the ﬂoodplains, ﬂood-

Regarding the original ﬁsh communities, approx-

3300 km; based on the situation prior to system-

can be characterised as rhithral or dominated by
rhithral faunal elements (Epirhithral to Hyporhith-

and transition, deﬁnition of ‘ﬁsh regions’ after Illies,

Typically, in Austria’s alpine rivers the grayling re-

mentioned above.
Some 750 km, or 15% of the total, were originally
potamal in nature (Epipotamal to Metapotamal; bar-
bel to bream region) and can typically be found in the
large basin landscapes in eastern Austria bordering the
Pannonian Basin.
Anthropogenically altered rivers
The mapping results and evaluation of the 52 rivers
reﬂect the current status of the investigated running
water habitats (Figure 5): Nearly 80% (3869 km)
correspond to the category ‘moderately to heavily im-
pacted’ and can, therefore no longer be deﬁned as
reﬂecting the original morphological and hydrological
character.
Based on their physiographic/hydrologic charac-
teristics, virtually all river systems that formerly inﬂu-
enced their valleys and ﬂoodplains through ﬂooding,
as well as erosion and sedimentation processes, have
been altered by channel straightening and/or stabilisa-
tion measures.
The dynamic processes within such river systems
have been largely inhibited by ﬂood prevention meas-
ures and hydroelectric plants. Intensive land use along
valley ﬂoors, which often extends directly up to the

Surveys of Austria’s wetland vegetation (Lazowski,
1997) conﬁrm these results: the overwhelming major-
ity of near-natural riverine forests has been replaced by
agricultural or residential areas and is now restricted to
isolated sites. The large-scale damage caused by river
regulation has another visible repercussion: a wide
range of highly diverse aquatic systems is lost when
riverine wetlands shrink. According to Gepp et al.
(1985), a mere 1/10 of Austria’s original backwaters
are preserved today. Of these, only 1/3 are considered
to have relatively intact hydrologic dynamics.
Running waters, such as the constrained channel
rivers in V-shaped valleys, which ‘take up’ less sur-
rounding land, tend to be more heavily impacted hy-
drologically/hydraulically. Such impacts in the stud-
ied stretches were further differentiated into altera-
tions related to impoundment, water diversion and
hydropeaking (Figure 6).
The riverine character of 16% of Austria’s ‘larger’
rivers has been altered by impoundments. Addition-
ally, 19% contain diversions, whereby the original bed
is either dry or residual ﬂow is far less than the average
monthly low-water discharge. Hydropeaking-related
phenomena through reservoir systems occur in 14% of
the investigated stretches. Many of these stretches with
altered hydrology are additionally impaired through
river straightening or stabilisation measures.
Overlaying these data with the results of the mor-
phological ﬁeld investigations delineates all those river
stretches which remain morphologically intact but hy-
drologically altered either by water diversion or hy-
dropeaking and which, therefore, cannot be put into
the high or good habitat quality class. Impoundment,
which totally restricts ﬂuvial dynamics of rivers and,
therefore, has a low restoration potential, is omitted
from this analysis. The results delineate 380 km or
8% of the total river length as being of ‘potentially
high or good habitat quality’, comprising all morpho-
logically unaltered river stretches with residual ﬂow or
alteration through hydropeaking.
River stretches with high and good habitat quality
The spatial distribution of river stretches with high
and good habitat quality is shown in Figure 7a. Ap-
proximately 6% (284 km) of all investigated river
kilometers are classiﬁed as having high habitat quality
(see ‘Methods for identifying Austria’s running water
habitats with high and good quality’ and Figure 5).
This quality class contains a total of 61 river stretches
divided among 25 rivers, with an average length of
4.7 km. About 30% of the total length of rivers with
high habitat quality are longer than 10 km (Figure 7b).
Additionally, 206 river stretches with a total length of
762 km (15%) represent good habitat quality, out of
which 23% are longer than 10 km (Figure 7c). The
average length of these stretches (3.7 km) is very short,

The evaluation of morphological character clearly
reveals the rareness of intact river stretches of speciﬁc
river types (Figure 8). Today, only few meandering
stretches (with a mere 16 km having high habitat qual-
ity) reﬂect largely undisturbed habitat conditions both
in the immediate river area and in the associated wet-
lands. Along with unimpaired, braiding river sections
(10 km), these stretches belong to those rare areas
where the river and the adjoining wetlands continue
to form an intact functional unit: at least episodic-
ally, river dynamics still shape the entire system. In
these running waters, lateral connectivity is a critical
habitat feature that is also essential for healthy ﬁsh
assemblages (Jungwirth et al., 2000).
Constrained rivers belong to the less degraded mor-
phological river type, with a total of 41% of the
stretches evaluated having high (234 km) and good
(202 km) habitat quality.
The ﬁsh-based assessment highlights the tremend-
ous loss of intact rivers originally corresponding to
the potamal and hyporhithral region (Figure 9). Of the
stretches with rhithral coenoses, 18% (480 km) have
remained morphologically and hydrologically largely
undisturbed, providing intact aquatic habitats for the
hyporhithral ﬁsh fauna. These rivers formerly domin-
ated alpine landscapes in Central Europe. Originally
2730 river kilometers (55%) of Austria’s larger rivers
belonged to this region. Thus, healthy river habitats of
the typical, dominating biocoenotic regions have been
quantitatively diminished. Unimpaired rivers with a
potamal fauna are currently represented by very few
river stretches (total only 43 km).
However, these data show the current proportion
of river stretches with high or good habitat quality,
respectively, the loss of intact stretches within the dif-
ferent ﬁsh regions only in a simpliﬁed way. We are cur-
rently unable to quantify the degree to which the above
impacts have also led to shifts in the ﬁsh assemblages.
Nonetheless, numerous examples demonstrate that
altered hydrological and morphological conditions in
running waters can reduce the number of ﬁsh species,
shift the dominance relationships of these species, as
well as reduce density and biomass (Karr et al., 1987;
Schmutz, 1996). River straightening tends to trigger
so-called ‘rhithralisation’ effects in which the habitat
features of a river are reinforced or altered in the direc-
tion of rhithral characteristics. Fish-ecological invest-
igations clearly document this phenomenon (Zauner,
1993; Zauner & Schmutz, 1994). Impoundments tend

Absolute priority must be given to protecting river
stretches with high habitat quality. While current law
mandates the preservation of such aquatic systems,
practical experience shows that legal regulations are
often insufﬁcient. Future strategies must go beyond the
‘classical’ measures foreseen under nature conserva-
tion laws and must increasingly integrate aquatic con-
servation concerns into regional planning and stream
management concepts.
The river status documented here reveal a critical
need for measures designed to improve the ecological
integrity of impaired rivers. From the geomorpholo-
gical perspective, stretches changed by straightening
and bank stabilisation have a high regeneration poten-
tial, provided that their bedload balances have not been
severely changed and that dynamic processes remain
or can be restored. Priority should be given to avoiding
any measures that would further reduce the ecological
integrity of these stretches/reaches and to strengthen-
ing efforts to incorporate such systems into ongoing
stream management schemes or restoration programs.
The long-term goal is the systematic, nationwide im-
provement of hydromorphological conditions, which
play a key role in regenerating running waters and their
biocoenoses.
Yet, the restoration potential of those stretches
impacted by hydroelectric stations, is in most cases
relatively low, especially where chains of power sta-
tions/impoundments block the development of ori-
ginal dynamics. This holds true for the current situ-
ation in Europe, whereas in the U.S.A. several dams
have been removed in order to improve the ecological
status of rivers. The prognosis for those river stretches
affected by hydropeaking or water diversion is better,
both from the legal standpoint and based on the poten-
tial success for improving ecological integrity using
state-of-the-art technology. The strategy here would
mainly be to minimise hydrological impacts (dampen
hydropeaking through compensation reservoirs and/or
coordinated reservoir operations, increasing residual
water ﬂow, etc.; see Moog, 1993 and Moog et al.,

The EU’s Water Framework Directive (Council
Directive, EUROPEAN COMMISSION, 1999) de-
termines the goals of an EC-wide water policy. One
main objective is to improve the ecological quality
of degraded running waters and to preserve high or
good ecological quality. This Directive, formulated
by representatives of all EU member states, deﬁnes
both the qualitative and quantitative ecological status
of running waters in terms of biological, chemical,
hydromorphological and toxicological criteria on a 5-
point scale (‘high’, ‘good’, ‘fair’, ‘poor’ and ‘bad’
water quality). The deﬁnitions applied in the present
paper for river stretches with high and good habitat
quality correspond to the respective categories of the
Water Framework Directive. In addition to biological
and chemical investigations, assessments, like the
present study, that identify and document natural river
stretches with original river typology are therefore a
ﬁrst step toward fulﬁlling the EU guidelines.
