Effects of forest structure on the distribution
of southern ﬂying squirrels (Glaucomys volans)
in urban parks
ERIN M. KEEFE
WILLIAM M. GIULIANO

Abstract.
Although southern ﬂying squirrels (Glaucomys volans) are present in many urban parks, squirrel-
habitat relationships in such areas are poorly understood, limiting conservation efforts. Our objective was to
quantify the effects of forest structure at multiple scales on ﬂying squirrel distribution in New York City (NYC)
parks. From 18 June–24 August 2001, we captured ﬂying squirrels in 5 New York City parks and measured forest
structure at the ground, shrub, understory, and overstory levels at trap sites. To examine the importance of forest
structure to squirrels, we compared (1) habitat variables between trap stations where squirrels were captured and
not captured and (2) habitat variables between parks with and without documented squirrel populations. Our data
suggest that areas with forests containing smaller trees at higher densities, dense woody ground cover, and little
herbaceous ground vegetation may not provide the food, cavities, ease of movement, or allow predator detection
necessary to be used by or support ﬂying squirrel populations. Other factors, such as the size and density of
stumps and snags may be important to non-urban squirrel populations, but not populations in NYC parks. Because
NYC parks contain almost all of the remaining woodlands within the City, forests within these areas should be a
conservation priority.
Keywords:
Glaucomys volans, habitat, parks, southern ﬂying squirrel, urban
Introduction
Vegetation structure is an important determinant of habitat occupancy by and distribu-
tion of many wildlife species, with selection of structural habitat characteristics occur-
ring at multiple scales (e.g., microhabitat and landscape; Morrison et al., 1998). Although
well-studied, the importance of forest structure to southern ﬂying squirrels is equivocal (e.g.,
Bendel and Gates, 1987; Healy and Brooks, 1988), and little is known about ﬂying squirrel-
habitat relationships in urban areas. Information is needed to properly manage habitats for
squirrels in urban environments because urban forests may differ in their management and
conditions from those studied in suburban and rural areas. Our objective was to examine the
importance of forest structure at multiple scales in urban parks to southern ﬂying squirrel
distributions by comparing (1) habitat variables between sites where squirrels were cap-
tured and not captured (i.e., microhabitat) and (2) habitat variables between parks with and
without squirrel populations (i.e., landscape-level).

Methods
This study was conducted in the Forever Wild Areas (FWA) of 5 NYC parks, managed by
the City of New York Department of Parks and Recreation, including Blue Heron (91 ha;
FWA: 68 ha), Central (341 ha; FWA: 28 ha), Forest (218 ha; FWA: 111 ha), Inwood Hill
(79ha;FWA:55ha),andVanCortlandt(464ha;FWA:231ha)(ﬁgure1).ForeverWildAreas
contain the majority of multilayered (“natural”), contiguous forest within NYC (Profous
and Loeb, 1986; A. Brash, City of New York Department of Parks and Recreation, personal
communication), were primarily forested, and contained numerous footpaths. Important tree
species in each park included: sweet gum (Liquidambar styraciﬂua), maples (Acer spp.),
and oaks (Quercus spp.) at Blue Heron; cherry (Prunus serotina), oaks, and sycamore
(Platanus occidentalis) at Central; oaks and cherry at Forest; oaks, tulip tree (Liriodendron
tulipifera), and shagbark hickory (Carya ovata) at Inwood Hill; and oaks, maples, cherry,
and sassafras (Sassafras albidum) at Van Cortlandt. Important shrubs included: viburnum
(Viburnum spp.), cherry, and spicebush (Lindera benzoin) at Blue Heron; maples, cherry,
and oaks at Central; cherry and maples at Forest; oaks, maples, and sassafras at Inwood
Hill; and maples and honeysuckle (Lonicera spp.) at Van Cortlandt. Each park had high
public use throughout the year and is surrounded by extensive urban development.
From 4–15 June 2001, survey points and transects were established within each Forever
Wild Area. Transects, oriented east-west, were established every 200 m from the northern
to southern end of each FWA. A survey station was established every 100 m along each

Because of a limited number of traps, they were rotated among transects in each FWA each
week (each station was surveyed for 4 trap nights; 161 total trap stations; 48 total transects).
Traps were baited with peanut butter and sunﬂower seeds and checked daily, with captured
animals identiﬁed to species and released.

We used analysis of variance (n = 106), blocked to remove the effect of inter-park
differences, to compare habitat at stations where squirrels were captured to those stations
not capturing squirrels, in parks with squirrel populations. Discriminant function analysis
(DFA; a form of logistic regression) was performed to determine which combination of
variables best discriminated between sites where squirrels were captured and those not
capturing squirrels, and determine the relative importance of each habitat variable (Hosmer
and Lemeshow, 2000; McGarigal et al., 2000).
Methods described by Noon (1981) and McGarigal et al. (2000) were used to reduce
multicolinearity problems and the number of variables considered in each DFA model.
All DFA models were ﬁt using a stepwise forward procedure with Tolerance = 0.001,
F-to-enter = 0.15, and F-to-remove = 0.15. Since the order in which variables enter the
model can effect ﬁnal model selection, and there is no accepted method of determining the
order of variable entry into a model (SYSTAT, 1999; McGarigal et al., 2000), we entered
variables into the model based on effect size (Cohen, 1988) in univariate comparisons (i.e.,
the variable with the largest effect size entered ﬁrst and smallest last). We assumed effect size
was positively associated with biological importance, regardless of statistical signiﬁcance.
The relative importance of each variable in the ﬁnal model was assessed by examining
standardized canonical discriminant functions (SCDF; i.e., variables with higher SCDF
values made greater individual contributions to the discriminating power of the model;
McGarigal et al., 2000).
Analysis of variance (n = 5) was used to compare each variable between parks with
(i.e., Inwood Hill, Forest, and Van Cortlandt) and without (i.e., Blue Heron and Central)
ﬂying squirrel populations present. Discriminant function analysis could not be performed
to determine which combination of variables best discriminated between parks with and
without squirrels present due to an insufﬁcient sample size (McGarigal et al., 2000).
Model assumptions were tested for all analyses (Zar, 1999; McGarigal et al., 2000).
When data failed to meet one or more of the assumptions, they were rank transformed prior
to analysis (Conover and Iman, 1981; Conover, 1999; Zar, 1999). SYSTAT software was
used to conduct all statistical analyses (SYSTAT, 1999), and we considered tests signiﬁcant
at P ≤0.10, rather than the more typical P ≤0.05, to reduce the likelihood of making a
TypeIIerror(Zar,1999).ForDFA’s,wealsoexaminedcanonicalcorrelationsandjackknifed
classiﬁcation matrices to assess the ﬁnal model (SYSTAT, 1999; McGarigal et al., 2000).
Results
Forty-eight ﬂying squirrel captures at 36 different sites in Forest (8 captures at 6 of 25
possible sites), Inwood Hill (18 captures at 12 of 21 possible sites), and Van Cortlandt (22
captures at 18 of 60 possible sites) parks were recorded, with a success rate of 0.06 cap-
tures/trap night. Captures of non-target species included eastern chipmunk (Tamius striatus)
at Forest and Van Cortlandt parks, and gray squirrels (Sciurus carolinensis), Peromyscus
spp., and Rattus spp. at all parks.

Compared with parks where ﬂying squirrels were not captured, parks supporting squirrels

Discussion
Microhabitat selection by ﬂying squirrels most likely reﬂects the needs for food and cover.
Larger overstory trees were probably important as sources of food and roosting and nesting
cavities for ﬂying squirrels. The parks in this study were dominated by oaks (i.e., Quercus
spp. were always one of the three most abundant species at a site in each park; Greller et al.
(1979), Ruffe (1981), Weinstein (1984), Profous and Loeb (1984, 1986), Loeb (1986), and
Huckstep (2001) noted that oak species were the dominant tree species in all of our study
areas), which provide a preferred, high-quality food (Muul, 1968; Bendel and Gates, 1987;
Harlow and Doyle, 1990; Thomas and Weigl, 1998) and are critical to squirrel productivity
and population persistence (Van Voorhees, 1976; Weigl, 1978; Loeb, 1993; Taulman et al.,
1998; Brady et al., 2000). Further, larger trees typically have greater numbers of cavities,
which are necessary for thermoregulation, nesting, and roosting cover (Sawyer and Rose,
1985; Bendel and Gates, 1987; Fridell and Litvaitis, 1991; Stapp et al., 1991; Taulman,
1999). Larger trees also may be selected because they provide more surface area, which
makes it easier for squirrels to land after a long “ﬂight” (Holmes and Austad, 1994).
Bendel and Gates (1987) suggested that sites with dense understory, shrub, and woody
ground layers may be detrimental to ﬂying squirrels by impairing predator detection, re-
ducing growth of herbaceous ground cover (i.e., a potential food source), and hindering
movements, via gliding, to the ground and among trees. However, Soneshine and Levy
(1981) concluded that understory vegetation was unimportant to ﬂying squirrels. Further,
it has been suggested (Bendel and Gates, 1987; Taulman, 1997) that the presence of un-
derstory, shrub, and ground cover may be important to ﬂying squirrels as escape cover.
Our ﬁnding that squirrels selected sites with increased deciduous ground cover partially
supports the conclusion that understory vegetation may be important as escape cover.
Flying squirrels in NYC parks also selected sites with smaller stumps and snags occurring
at lower densities. Numerous studies (Bendel and Gates, 1987; Fridell and Litvaitis, 1991;
Taulman, 1999) have suggested that larger, more abundant snags are important to ﬂying
squirrel survival and recruitment. However, larger more abundant snags may inhibit predator
detection, through visual obstruction, and provide avian predators with perches from which
to hunt. Our data indicate no difference in stump and snag density between sites used and
not used by ﬂying squirrels, supporting the conclusion that ﬂying squirrels are not limited
by this habitat component in these urban parks.
At the landscape-level, parks supporting ﬂying squirrel populations had forests with lower
tree densities and deciduous canopy cover than other parks, suggesting more open forests
may facilitate gliding between trees, improve predator detection and avoidance (Bendel
and Gates, 1987; Holmes and Austad, 1994), and permit increased growth of herbaceous
ground cover as a result of increased light penetration (Payne and Bryant, 1994). As a food
source, herbaceous ground cover (Dolan and Carter, 1977) may be important to squirrel
productivity in parks or during years with reduced mast production. Furthermore, because
ﬂying squirrels forage on the forest ﬂoor, parks with reduced deciduous and dead woody
ground cover may beneﬁt squirrels by improving predator detection and facilitating glides
to the ground (Bendel and Gates, 1987). Alternatively, shrub and ground cover may provide
concealing cover from predators (Bendel and Gates, 1987; Taulman, 1997). Our ﬁndings

that lower deciduous ground cover and tree densities were important forest features suggests
that gliding ability and predator detection may be of primary importance to squirrels. This
conclusion is supported by Bendel and Gates (1987) and Holmes and Austad (1994) who
suggestedpredationasthemostimportantfactorlimitingsquirrelpopulations,butincontrast
to Weigl (1978), Taulman et al. (1998), and Brady et al. (2000) who found that food
availability was the most important factor for squirrels. Such contrasting ﬁndings suggest
that there may be dissimilar limiting factors in different areas.
Several studies indicate that snags, as sources of cavities, are important to ﬂying squirrel
populations (Bendel and Gates, 1987; Fridell and Litvaitis, 1991; Taulman, 1999). However,
recent studies have found that cavities in live trees are used as much as cavities in snags
(Taulman, 1999), and cavities may not be a limiting factor for ﬂying squirrels (Brady et al.,
2000). Our ﬁnding that snags were not important to squirrels in urban parks supports this
conclusion.
Contrary to our microhabitat analyses, we found that decreased deciduous ground cover
may be important to ﬂying squirrels at the landscape level. Interscale differences in habitat
relationships and differences between our ﬁndings and those of the aforementioned studies
may indicate that (1) ﬂying squirrels select structural habitat components at different scales,
(2) different requirements of urban ﬂying squirrels compared with non-urban animals, (3)
the need for multiple cover types within squirrel home ranges, (4) that southern ﬂying
squirrels are habitat generalists (Muul, 1968; Stone et al., 1996), adapting to various forest
conﬁgurations, or (5) analysis at the landscape level may be too broad to make speciﬁc
habitat assessments.
Although forest structure is important to ﬂying squirrel distributions in urban parks, other
contemporary and historical factors may affect current squirrel distributions. The abundance
and distribution of trails, roads, buildings and other human structures, forest openings and
ﬁelds, park age, human activity, and the surrounding urban matrix may be important to
squirrel distribution and abundance in parks. While we did not examine the importance
of these factors directly, park-level data suggest that they do not have a signiﬁcant impact
on squirrels as the 2 parks without squirrel populations (i.e., Central and Blue Heron)
exhibit the extremes of each of these factors, i.e., of the parks studied, Central Park is
the oldest and has the most trails, roads, buildings, forest openings and ﬁelds, and human
activity, and Blue Heron Park the least (Thomas and Thomas, 1983; A. Brash, City of New
York Department of Parks and Recreation, personal communication). Further, although
southern ﬂying squirrels have not been examined speciﬁcally, other studies (Van Druff and
Rowse, 1986; Dickman and Doncaster, 1987) have suggested that the structural complexity
of vegetation within a patch, such as a park, is more important to urban small mammal
populations and communities than the surrounding urban development and activities.
Anecdotal evidence suggests that squirrels were once present in all of our study areas,
however, they have not been observed in 2 of our study areas (i.e., Blue Heron and Central
parks) in several years (A. Brash, City of New York Department of Parks and Recreation,
personal communication). During a study in 1994–1995 of small mammal communities
in Inwood Hill, Forest, and Van Cortlandt parks (i.e., our 3 study areas containing ﬂying
squirrels), Huckstep (2001) also found southern ﬂying squirrels present, but did not assess
the importance of habitat features to squirrel distribution.

Increased predation, inbreeding depression, disease epidemics, competition from other
rodents and generalist birds, or other stochastic events may have led to population extinc-
tion in 2 of our study parks. While we cannot directly address all of these possibilities
with our data, the presence of several potential competitors (i.e., eastern chipmunks, gray
squirrels, and Rattus spp.) in parks both with and without ﬂying squirrels present sug-
gests that this may not be a signiﬁcant factor. Although data on southern ﬂying squirrels
in urban systems are limited, research in non-urban forests on the European ﬂying squirrel
(Pteromys volans) indicated that habitat fragmentation and connectivity are important to
squirrel population persistence (Reunanen et al., 2000). Similarly, we believe there was no
chance for gene ﬂow, recolonization, or rescue effects (Gotelli, 2001) and may have led to
the current population status, because our urban populations are completely isolated (i.e.,
many city blocks apart) from other populations. However, longer term studies that include
more parks and examine the movements of squirrels is needed to address these questions
fully.
While other factors may affect the distribution of southern ﬂying squirrels in urban parks,
forest structure appears to be important. Our data suggest that areas with forests containing
smaller trees at higher densities, dense woody ground cover, and little herbaceous ground
vegetation may not provide the food, cavities, ease of movement, or predator detection
necessary to be used by or support ﬂying squirrel populations. Other factors, such as the
size and density of stumps and snags may be important to non-urban squirrel populations,
but not populations in NYC parks.
To enhance habitat for ﬂying squirrels in NYC parks, we suggest (1) maintaining a va-
riety of forested cover types and structures, (2) preserving large mast producing trees as
a source of food and cavities, and to facilitate gliding, and (3) reducing tree and woody
ground cover in some areas to improve predator detection, increase the growth of herba-
ceous ground cover, and ease movements. With these habitat improvements, it may be
possible to reintroduce southern ﬂying squirrels to parks where they are not currently
found.
Although the City of New York Department of Parks and Recreation has promoted tree
planting and conservation for decades along streets, on private property, and in City parks
and other properties (e.g., New York Botanical Garden and Bronx Zoo), relatively little
contiguous woodland exists in the City (Ward, 1915; Barlow, 1971; Profous and Loeb,
1986; McDonnell, 1988). New York City parks cover >12,000 ha (∼13% of the City) and
contain the majority of woodlands in the City (Profous and Loeb, 1986; A. Brash, City of
New York Department of Parks and Recreation, personal communication). Thus, maintain-
ing functional forests in the parks will be critical to providing habitat for ﬂying squirrels
in this urban area. However, within park woodlands, forest structure differs greatly. Much
of each parks’ woodlands lack the structure of more natural forested areas (i.e., they have
scattered large trees with lawns beneath; Profous and Loeb (1986)). It is generally only
within FWA’s that more natural woodlands with multiple vegetation layers and increased
structure can be found (Profous and Loeb, 1986; A. Brash, City of New York Depart-
ment of Parks and Recreation, personal communication). Therefore, within the City, FWA’s
should be a conservation focus for protecting forest-wildlife such as the southern ﬂying
squirrel.
