Functional response of the least weasel, Mustela ni7alis ni7alis
Janne Sundell, Kai Norrdahl, Erkki Korpima¨ki and Ilkka Hanski
Sundell, J., Norrdahl, K., Korpima¨ki, E. and Hanski, I. 2000. Functional response of
the least weasel, Mustela ni6alis ni6alis. – Oikos 90: 501–508.
We investigated the functional response of the least weasel (Mustela ni6alis ni6alis) in
a series of experiments conducted in large outdoor enclosures (0.5 ha). Radiocollared
Microtus voles were released in the enclosures at different densities (4, 8, 16 and
100/ha) three days before the release of a radiocollared weasel. During the three-day
experiment every vole killed was replaced with another one as soon as possible to
retain constant prey density. The results demonstrated type II functional response
with the predation rate reaching 50% of the asymptotic rate at a vole density of 15
individuals per ha. More voles were killed at the highest densities than would be
expected from the known energy demands of weasels. Female and male voles were
killed in proportion to their abundances in the enclosures, and no difference in
predation risk was detected between voles released in the enclosure before the weasel
(residents) and during the experiment (transients).

The total response of predators to changing prey den-
sity has two components, the numerical and functional
response. The numerical response is a function of natal-
ity, mortality, emigration and immigration. In princi-
ple, the numerical response is relatively easy to estimate
by observing the numbers of predators and their prey in
natural populations, though in practice obtaining reli-
able demographic and abundance data is time-consum-
ing and difﬁcult for many species. The functional
response indicates how an individual predator responds
to changes in prey density via the full range of be-
haviours involved in foraging.
Differences in the predators’ responses to changing
prey density have major consequences for predator-
prey population dynamics. Fast numerical response
generally has a stabilizing effect on the dynamics, whilst
a slow (delayed) response tends to destabilize the dy-
namics (e.g. May 1973). Holling (1959) classiﬁed the
functional responses into three types. In type I func-
tional response, the predation rate increases linearly to
the maximum level. The type II functional response,
with a rapid initial increase in predation rate with prey
density, is thought to characterize specialist predators,
whilst the S-shaped type III response is typical for
generalist predators.
To determine the shape of the functional response in
natural populations is difﬁcult and often some indirect
method is needed. For vertebrate predators, the func-
tional response has traditionally been studied by col-
lecting
scats
or
pellets
and
by
examining
the
proportions of different prey types in them relative to
their abundances in the environment. This approach
has many problems, for example obtaining unbiased
scat samples is difﬁcult (Korpima¨ki and Krebs 1996).
In the case of highly specialized predators with surplus
killing, the proportion of a particular prey item in scats
may remain relatively constant at very different prey
densities even if predation rate changes. Whenever pos-
sible,
the
functional
response
should
be
studied
experimentally.

feeding on small rodents (King 1989, Korpima¨ki et al.

weasel based on a series of experiments conducted in
large outdoor enclosures.
Materials and methods
There
is
pronounced
sexual
dimorphism
in
small
mustelids, females being the smaller sex (King 1989).
Because of the great size difference, the sexes have
somewhat different diets (Erlinge 1975), which might
affect their functional responses. In this study, we focus
on the functional response of the female least weasel.
Female least weasels are probably more efﬁcient preda-
tors of small rodents than males; they have smaller
home ranges (Nyholm 1959, Erlinge 1974, King 1975),
they can reach into most burrows and nests of small
rodents, and they also do all the hunting for the
nestlings. For these reasons females are the more rele-
vant sex with respect to the population dynamics of
voles.
The experiment involved manipulation of prey den-
sity in three 0.5-ha enclosures from 12 October to 20
November 1998. Enclosures were made of vole and
weasel-proof wirenet. A roof of nylon netting prevented
the entry of avian predators (Klemola 1999, Klemola et
al. 2000). The enclosures had been erected in old agri-
cultural ﬁelds at Alajoki, Lapua, in western Finland.
Old ﬁelds is the typical habitat for weasels and their
main prey, Microtus voles, in this part of Finland (e.g.
Korpima¨ki et al. 1991, Klemola et al. 1999). The habi-
tat is rather homogeneous inside the enclosures, vegeta-
tion consisting primarily of different grasses (e.g.
Elymus repens, Phleum pratense, Deschampsia cespitosa
and Calamagrostis spp.) and thistle (Cirsium ar6ense).
Monthly average temperatures varied from −4.4 to
+4.0°C during the experiment and the ground was
covered by the ﬁrst snow in the last replicates of the
experiment.
We placed sibling voles (Microtus rossiaemeridion-
alis) in the enclosures at densities of 4, 8, 16 and 100
voles/ha. The sibling vole is a common species and it
constitutes a major part of weasels’ diet, along with the
ﬁeld vole (M. agrestis), in the study area (Korpima¨ki et
al. 1991, Norrdahl and Korpima¨ki 1995). The experi-
mental voles originated from a laboratory colony main-
tained at the University of Helsinki (founder voles had
been captured near the study site). The voles were
acclimatized for at least one week before the experiment
by keeping them out of doors and feeding them mainly
with grass. Voles were distributed evenly in the enclo-
sures with a sex ratio of 1:1.
Three days after the release of the voles, a weasel was
released in the enclosure. We used six young female
least weasels in the experiments (mean weight 47.5 g,
range 44–49 g). Five of them were born in a captive
breeding colony at the Lammi Biological Station and
one was captured near the study site. Each experiment
lasted for three days, after which the weasel was
captured.
All weasels carried a radiocollar (Biotrack Ltd., UK;
type TW-4, ca 2.2 g), hence their activity, space use and
hunting behaviour could be monitored. There were four
radiotracking periods per 24 h, during sunrise, sunset,
around midday and midnight. Timing of the tracking
periods was not exactly the same during all the experi-
ments,
because
the
daylength
becomes
noticeably
shorter in the autumn. Thus the sunrise and sunset
tracking periods became closer to each other towards
the end of the experiments. Every tracking period lasted
2 h, except the midnight period, which consisted of only
one ﬁx after the ﬁrst two experiments. In other periods
there were nine ﬁxes per period, except in the ﬁrst two
experiments, where the number of ﬁxes was 13. In every
ﬁx the animal was located and it was characterized as
active or inactive.
At the densities of 4, 8 and 16 voles/ha, all voles had
radiocollars (Biotrack Ltd., UK; type TW-4, ca 1.1 g)
with temperature sensors. With this sensor, it was possi-
ble to observe the death of the vole within few minutes.
If the vole was killed between radiotracking periods,
the time of the death used in the analyses was estimated
as the median between the last time observed as alive
and the time observed as dead. The killed vole was
replaced with another similar vole as soon as possible
to keep density and sex ratio constant. All released
voles were mature and their mean weight was 28.2 g
(range 23–42 g). Visibly pregnant females were not
used. Voles were located at least once in every radio-
tracking period and twice or more often if the weasel
was active. The experiments were replicated four times,
so that three weasels experienced all three densities and
the other three were chosen randomly for different
densities. Only one experiment could be done with the
density of 100 voles/ha. This experiment was conducted
in the same way as the experiments with smaller densi-

ties, but voles did not have radiocollars. In this experi-
ment we released one extra vole per day to roughly
compensate for the killed ones. We presumed that the
weasel killed at least one vole per day, which would be
enough for its energy demands (East and Lockie 1964,
Gillingham 1984). After three days the experiment was
discontinued and the weasel and all the voles that were
alive were captured (capturing all the voles turned out
to be very time-consuming and it took 15 d to
complete).
The activity data were collected and analysed in two
ways. Observations on activity made during the radio-
tracking periods provided quantitative data. On the
other hand, in every tracking period and between the
tracking periods the weasel was scored either as active
or inactive; in this manner we obtained qualitative data
on activity. The weasel was thought to be active be-
tween tracking periods if it had moved or it had killed
a vole. An activity index was calculated by summing up
individual observations. For example, if the weasel had
been active in every period (20 out of 20) during the
experiment, its activity index was 20. In the pooled data
for weasels, if all weasels had been active in all morning
periods the pooled activity index would be 39. Because
periods between radiotrackings had different lengths,
the activity indices were compared relative to the actual
lengths of the respective periods. The ‘home ranges’ of
voles and weasels were calculated with the RANGES V
software (Natural Environmental Research Council,
Wareham, UK). We used the minimum convex polygon
(MCP) method, which is adequate for making rough
estimates of space use in this kind of enclosure
experiment.
Mortality of voles at different densities (excepting the
density of 100 voles/ha) was analysed in terms of ‘vole
lineages’, which consisted of the vole originally placed
in the enclosure (before the release of the weasel) as
well as any ‘replacement’ voles (following the death of
the previous individual in the lineage). Death of the
vole released before the weasel and possible deaths of
the replacement voles were included in the same lin-
eage. The overall death rate was assumed to be a
Poisson process. The data were analysed using a gener-
alised linear model, with Poisson errors and a log link
function. Density was assumed to be a continuous
variable and replicates were nested within density. We
also tested whether predation rate was equal for the
original versus replacement voles (type of vole, a cate-
gorical variable) by comparing lifetimes, assuming them
to be exponential and right censored (when the experi-
ment was ﬁnished).

Predation rate
More voles were killed at the higher densities during the
experiment (Fig. 4); the largest number of voles was
killed at the density of 100 voles/ha, 11 voles in three
days. The average numbers of voles killed at the densi-
ties of 4, 8 and 16 voles/ha were 2.8 (91.03 S.E.), 4.5
(90.7) and 6.5 (91.5) in three days, respectively. At
the density of 4 voles/ha, one weasel died during the
experiment. Unfortunately, it had lost its collar, and
hence the carcass was never found and the cause of
death could not be determined (it apparently did not
escape from the enclosure, because no tracks could be
seen – there was a thin snow cover on the ground
during the experiment).

Surplus killing introduces yet another difﬁculty into
the determination of the functional response. A weasel
needs to consume one vole per day on average to satisfy
its daily energy requirements (East and Lockie 1964,
Gillingham 1984). The density of 100 Microtus/ha cor-
responds to the average peak density of voles in western
Finland around the experimental enclosures (Korpi-
ma¨ki and Norrdahl 1991), while the maximum densities
in abandoned ﬁelds may reach 200 voles/ha (K. Nor-
rdahl unpubl.). We observed surplus killing even at the
moderate densities used in the present experiment (\3
voles killed per three days, Fig. 4). Jedrzejewska and
Jedrzejewski (1989) found that weasels killed more
bank voles (Clethrionomys glareolus) in their enclosure
experiment when temperature was decreasing. Weasels
killed on average 1.8 voles/d when the temperature was
above 5°C, the kill rate increased to an average of 2.8
voles/d at temperatures from 5 to −5°C, and decreased
again to a lower level when temperatures remained
below −5°C. These ﬁgures indicate slightly higher
rates than was observed in the present experiment,
which is possibly due to the bigger body size of the
southern subspecies of the weasel, smaller size of the
bank vole compared to the sibling vole, and abnormally
high vole densities in the small enclosures (50 m2) of
Jedrzejewska and Jedrzejewski (1989). The estimated
predation rate was also slightly higher in the study of
Jedrzejewska (1989) than we observed in this study.
We recorded instances of apparent indirect mortality
caused by predation in the experiments. Voles tended to
form pairs of a female and a male, which used the same
nest, even if they were released in different locations in
the enclosure. In two cases the weasel entered into the
nest of a pair of voles and killed one vole. The voles
that had escaped ran to the other side of the enclosure.
They were found dead a few hours later without any
bite marks of the weasel. The voles probably died due
to a combination of the shock caused by the chase by
the weasel and harsh weather conditions. It is unknown
whether this phenomenon is common in natural condi-
tions or whether it was an artefact of the experimental
procedure. The rather short times that the voles spent
in the enclosure may have prevented them from estab-
lishing escape routes to other safe nest places. However,
according to Steen (1994), long-distance dispersers of
the root vole (Microtus oeconomus) survived as well as
residents in the new surroundings after the ﬁrst risky
couple of days. Steen’s (1994) results indicate that three
days in the enclosure before the release of the weasel is
probably enough for voles to get used to their new
environment. In both cases of indirect mortality, voles
had been placed into the enclosure before the weasel. In

Discussion
We found that the least weasel has a type II functional
response, which is not surprising because it is a special-
ist predator and in any case there was no alternative
prey available in this experiment. The value of the
half-saturation constant (14.5) estimated in this study is
considerably larger, and consequently the functional
response is less steep, than the one estimated by
Turchin and Hanski (1997). They used the proportion
of small rodents in weasel scats in their estimation, and
worked on the bigger subspecies, the common weasel
(Mustela ni6alis 6ulgaris), from southern Sweden (using
data collected by Erlinge 1975). The common weasel is
ecologically substantially different from the smaller
least weasel. In southern Sweden, vole populations are
generally non-cyclic and alternative prey for the com-
mon weasel is available also in winter.
Differences in the observed functional responses can
be explained by many factors apart from the subspe-
cies, including sex (Turchin and Hanski 1997 used
pooled data for the sexes), the availability of alternative
prey, presence of potential weasel predators and com-
petitors, and the structure of the landscape. However,
these factors should have produced the opposite differ-
ence in the results, a steeper response in our experi-
ment, as we studied the sex most specialized in voles in
a structurally simple environment in the absence of
alternative prey, predators and competitors. We suspect
that problems of using scat data collected over long
periods of time may have biased the results of Turchin
and
Hanski
(1997)
towards
a
steeper
functional
response.

potentially a great impact on the shape of the func-

Many studies have found breeding females of small
rodents to be more susceptible to mammalian predation
than other functional groups in the population (Cush-
ing 1985, Manhagen 1991, Klemola et al. 1997, Korpi-
ma¨ki
and
Norrdahl

whereas

and

avian predation (e.g. Korpima¨ki 1985, Mappes et al.
1993, Koivunen et al. 1996a, b). In our study both sexes
were killed in proportion to their abundances in the
enclosures, but it should be recalled that we did not use
breeding females in the experiment to eliminate extra
variation in the voles and for ethical reasons.
More surprisingly, we did not ﬁnd any differences in
the mortality rates of transient and resident voles. This
agrees partly with the results of Jedrzejewska (1989). In
her experiment, ‘preference’ by weasels ranged from
clear preference of transients through random capture
of both transients and residents to slight preference of
residents, but transient voles were killed more often
than expected by chance during their ﬁrst day in the
enclosure. Our experiment was not speciﬁcally designed
to detect possible differences in the mortality rates of
different types of voles, and our test was not very
powerful because of the relatively small sample size.
The functional response probably varies in time and
in space, being different in different prey and predator
assemblages and in different landscapes. However,
there are reasons to suspect that the functional response
might vary less in the least weasel than in many other
predators. The least weasel is a highly specialized vole
predator and it turns to alternative prey, if any is
available, only at the very lowest densities of the main
prey (Korpima¨ki et al. 1991). In winter the proportion
of voles in the diet of the least weasel never drops
below 65% in the present study area, and most of the
voles eaten are Microtus voles (Korpima¨ki et al. 1991).
The least weasel is a skilful vole predator. It can hunt
voles in their tunnels and in the subnivean space.
Because of these unique characteristics, the functional
response obtained in this study can be assumed to be
representative for most situations in central and north-
ern Fennoscandia, where alternative prey for weasels is
scarce most of the time in most years, except in the
short midsummer season.
Using captive-born animals as prey and predator
may have biased our results to some extent. However,
the least weasels are true natural-born killers (East and
Lockie 1965, King 1989), and we did not ﬁnd any
difference in the hunting efﬁciency between the individ-
ual captured near the study site and the captive-born
weasels. In voles, the escape reactions to weasel are
probably mostly inherited, hence laboratory-born ani-
mals may not differ much from wild-born conspeciﬁcs
in this respect. On the other hand, it is not clear
whether one week is enough for voles to become accli-
matised and adapted to a new diet consisting mainly of
grass. The area of the experimental enclosures (0.5 ha)
is most probably large enough for voles in this kind of
short-term experiment, but for weasels in low vole
densities the limited area will force the predator to hunt
more intensively within a small area, and therefore
perhaps ﬁnd voles more effectively. If vole densities in
nature are low in large areas, the weasel may seek vole
patches with higher density. On the other hand, in the
case of breeding least weasel females the home range is
limited to a couple of ha, and the size of the enclosure
in this experiment would not be unrealistically small.
In summary, individual weasels responded to changes
in prey density by killing more voles at higher densities
according to a type II functional response. This re-
sponse was not due to increased activity or change in
the space use but to better hunting success at higher
densities. At high vole densities, weasels killed more
voles than was needed to satisfy their daily energy
demands. From the point of view of an individual
Microtus, the observed functional response implies
higher probability of survival at higher densities.
