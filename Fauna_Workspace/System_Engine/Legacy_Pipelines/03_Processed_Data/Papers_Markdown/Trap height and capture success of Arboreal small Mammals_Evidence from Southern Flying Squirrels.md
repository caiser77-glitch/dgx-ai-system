 ABSTRACT.-Studies of small mammals often use live traps, but the effect of different trap-
 ping heights on capture effectiveness of arboreal mammals has not been directly addressed.
 We compared the capture success of three trap heights ("low" = 2 m, "medium" = 4.5-5
 m, and "high" = 8-8.5 m) in capturing arboreal mammals. Southern flying squirrels (Glau-
 comys volans) constituted 86% of the captures. Low traps captured approximately half as
 many squirrels (15) as either medium (30) or high traps (28), and individual squirrels were
 recaptured at a single trap height more often than expected based on chance. Traps at the
 three heights exhibited significantly heterogeneity in initially capturing individual flying
 squirrels (i.e., previously unmarked squirrels). We reject the hypothesis that trap height does
 not affect capture success of southern flying squirrels in southern forests during the summer.
 We discuss the general importance of trap height in studies of mammalian community ecol-
 ogy and conservation.
 INTRODUCTION
 Choosing an effective, unbiased trapping method is important for collecting reliable in-
 formation on small mammals. Studies have demonstrated that capture rates vary consider-
 ably between pitfall and live traps (Peterson, 1980; Szaro et al., 1988), pitfall and snap traps
 (Brown, 1967; Mengak and Guynn, 1987), snap and live traps (Wiener and Smith, 1972),
 various types of snap traps (Wiener and Smith, 1972; West, 1985), and various types of live
 traps (Morris, 1968; Slade et al., 1993). Despite numerous comparisons of trapping meth-
 ods, the effectiveness of techniques for trapping arboreal and semiarboreal mammals has
 rarely been addressed. The most notable omission is comparison of capture rates among
 traps placed at different heights in trees (but see Engel et al., 1992), despite some evidence
 that trapping in trees may increase trapping success of arboreal and semiarboreal species
 (Gentry et al., 1968; Sawyer and Rose, 1985). Gentry et al. (1968) captured 42% (5 of 12)
 of golden mice (Ochrotomys nuttalli) in live traps placed in trees, although they sampled
 for only 704 trap nights in trees and 6912 concurrent trap nights on the ground. Live
 trapping of southern flying squirrels (Glaucomys volans) at ground level was abandoned in
 favor of placing nest boxes 3.5-4.5 m high in trees by Sawyer and Rose (1985), because of
 low capture rates.
 Southern flying squirrels are nocturnal arboreal mammals widely distributed in eastern
 North America (Dolan and Carter, 1977). Although many studies have addressed the be-
 havior and ecology of southern flying squirrels, researchers have employed a variety of
 capture techniques including the use of different trapping heights (Table 1). The lack of
 standardization in trapping height coupled with different capture rates among studies

 STUDY AREA
 The study site was a 13-ha, 10 X 10 grid on the Savannah River Site (SRS) near Aiken,
 South Carolina. The SRS is on the Upper Coastal Plain and is bordered on the SW by the
 Savannah River. Since arboreal activity of flying squirrels may be influenced by habitat
 composition, we include a habitat description. The grid included four forest habitat types,
 ranging from bottomland hardwoods (18%), a mesic (Whipple et al., 1981) or transitional
 hardwood forest zone (17%), upland hardwoods (60%) and pine (5%). The trapping area
 was bordered on the W by an abandoned borrow pit, with early successional vegetation.
 The location of this borrow pit precluded the establishment of one corner grid point, so
 the trapping grid consisted of 99 points.
 The habitat was composed of a relatively even-aged forest established in 1937, with the
 exception of the pine area, which was established in 1957. The bottomland component of
 the grid was a yellow poplar (Liriodendron tulipifera)-swamp gum (Nyssa sylvatica var. bi-
 flora)-dog hobble (Leucothoe axillaris) community (Jones et al., 1981). Many tree roots in
 this portion of the grid were buttressed, since they were subject to periodic flooding. Dom-
 inant tree species in the mesic zone were water oak (Quercus nigra) and pignut hickory
 (Carya glabra). Post oak (Quercus stellata) was co-dominant in some areas. Dense areas of
 switch-cane (Arundinaria gigantea) were present in the understory. The overstory of the
 pine stand was composed exclusively of slash pine (Pinus elliottii). The largest section of
 the grid (60%) was dominated by upland oak-hickory hardwoods (Workman and McLeod,
 1990).

 Standard Sherman live traps (22.9 X 7.6 X 8.9 cm) were placed on trees using three
 nails. Two bottom nails were parallel to the ground and served as a platform, while the
 third nail was just over the trap. The trap was held in place with a piece of wire extending
 diagonally over the side of the trap from the top nail to a bottom nail, so that the longer
 side of the trap was parallel to the ground. Traps were baited with a mixture of whole oats,
 peanut butter and molasses. Trapping took place continuously from 22 July to 8 August
 1992. All traps were rebaited every 5th day and/or after an animal was captured. Traps
 were checked daily from 0600-1030 h.
 Traps containing mammals were removed from the tree, flying squirrels were removed
 from the trap, and marked with paired, numbered, metal eartags (Monel #1) for individual
 identification. Uniquely marking squirrels allowed us to assess the response to variable trap
 heights by individuals. Data on trap height and grid location were recorded for all captures.
 Mammals other than squirrels were released unmarked.
 We tested for heterogeneity in new captures (unmarked animals) among trap heights
 using a single classification goodness of fit test (G statistic) and compared computed values
 of G to a x2 distribution. We calculated expected captures by assuming equal capture prob-
 abilities among trap heights. To test for capture independence we calculated the number
 of squirrels expected to be caught by chance at a single trap height based on the total
 observed number of squirrels captured more than once and compared these expected
 values to the observed values.
 RESULTS
 Four mammalian species were captured during the study (Table 2). As expected, most
 captures were of southern flying squirrels. All species were captured at more than one trap
 height with the exception of golden mice (Ochrotomys nuttalli), which were only captured
 at the medium trap height (Table 2). High and medium traps captured a similar percentage
 of flying squirrels (38.4% and 41.1%, respectively), but low traps captured approximately
 half as many squirrels as the other heights (20.5%; Table 2). We did not test for differences
 in capture success of these data, since the data include recaptures and individual squirrels
 tended to be captured at a single trap height (see below), violating the assumption of in-
 dependence.

 DISCUSSION
 The "catchability" of arboreal small mammals is an important issue for community ecol-
 ogy and mammal conservation. For example, the use of elevated trapping revealed arboreal
 activity and the degree of habitat use of brush mice (Peromyscus boylii), deer mice (P
 maniculatus) and Stephen's wood rats (Neotoma stephensi) (Holbrook, 1979). Furthermore,
 if ground trapping excludes arboreal species (e.g., Golley et al., 1965) or underestimates
 their abundance (e.g., Gentry et al., 1968; Sawyer and Rose, 1985), resulting estimates of
 species richness, evenness and heterogeneity (reviewed in Krebs, 1989) would be biased.
 Studies addressing the conservation of mammals in areas of concern should, therefore,
 include arboreal trapping to gain complete and unbiased results (see Laurance, 1994).
 Although incidental captures of flying squirrels occur at ground level (e.g., Healy and
 Brooks, 1988; Barry et al., 1989), flying squirrels were not captured during a previous 12-yr
 ground-level trapping study (86,000 trap nights) on the Savannah River Site, even though
 appropriate habitats were included in the sample (Golley et al., 1965). Since we did not
 include ground traps in our study, we can only infer from our data and Golley et al.'s (1965)
 study that ground trapping would be less successful than trapping in trees and that capture
 success would decrease with increasing distance away from the closest tree. A study of flying
 squirrels in Wisconsin found that traps in trees were far more successful than ground traps

 Medium and high traps were similar in capture success of flying squirrels (Tables 2 and
 3), but investigator access to the medium trap height is markedly more convenient. There-
 fore, we recommend trapping flying squirrels at our medium trap height (4.5-5 m). Im-
 portantly, we reject the hypothesis that southern flying squirrels are equally likely to be
 captured at these three trap heights. We suggest conducting a preliminary investigation of
 trapping success at varying heights similar to ours when investigators are embarking on
 flying squirrel research.
