CURRENT THREATS AND FACTORS ALLOWING HIGH POPULATION DENSITY OF 
OTTERS LUTRA LUTRA IN EASTERN EUROPE: REVIEW OF THE PUBLISHED RESULTS 
AND NEW FINDINGS 
 
Vadim E. SIDOROVICH 
 
The Vertebrate Predation Research Group, Institute of Zoology, National Academy of Sciences of Belarus, 
Akademicheskaya str. 27, Minsk-220072, Belarus. email: vadimsid@mustbel.open.by 
 
 
Keywords:  Eurasian otter, Lutra lutra, population density, threats, Eastern Europe 
 
 
1   INTRODUCTION 
 
The countries of Eastern Europe (eastern regions of Russia, Belarus, Latvia, Lithuania, 
Eastern Poland, and Estonia) have for a fairly long time been in the same economic regime 
and historically have similar environments.  They also provide similar habitat conditions for 
otters, but with human pressure on the species.  Well preserved habitats (rivers and lakes), a 
dense habitat network, with a high carrying capacity supported fairly high density otter 
populations (OZOLINŠ AND RANTINŠ, 1992; SIDOROVICH, 1992, 1997; OZOLINŠ AND PILÃTS, 
1995; BRZEZIÑSKI AND ROMANOWSKI, 1996; ROZHNOV AND TUMANOV, 1996; ULEVICIUS AND 
BALCIAUSKAS, 1996; SIDOROVICH ET AL., 1997).  On the other hand, first and foremost 
trapping has markedly reduced otter numbers even in good habitat conditions, where the 
species was quite common a decade ago (OZOLINŠ AND RANTINŠ, 1992; SIDOROVICH, 1992, 
1997; ROZHNOV AND TUMANOV, 1996).  Currently, there are other threats acting on the otter 
populations in Eastern Europe.  This paper describes these current threats and the factors 
which allow high population density of otters in Eastern Europe.  To do this, data is used from 
the author’s study on otters in different parts of Belarus, adjacent regions (Pskov, Tver, 
Smolensk) of Russia and in Eastern Poland (Bialostok region, Hainowka district) as well as 
information from the published literature (KIILI, 1991; OZOLINŠ AND RANTINŠ, 1992; OZOLINŠ 
AND PILÃTS, 1995; ULEVICIUS AND BALCIAUSKAS, 1996; ROZHNOV AND TUMANOV, 1996). 
 
 
2   METHODS 
 
The methods used to census otters is based on snow tracking and have been described in 
previous publications (SIDOROVICH, 1992, 1997, SIDOROVICH, JEDRZEJWSKA 
AND 
JEDRZEJWSKA, 1996) especially (SIDOROVICH AND MACDONALD, 2001).  The method used for 
estimating species diversity, density and biomass of the water-dwelling prey of otters was 
based on net equipment.  We created several nets adapted for the size, flow rate and other 
parameters of small rivers (SIDOROVICH ET AL., 2003, in press).  Other nets were developed to 
census the aquatic prey in beaver ponds of different size, configuration, depth, presence of a 
large littoral part overgrown by macrophytes and other parameters (SIDOROVICH ET AL., 2001, 
Unpublished data).  The length of the stream sections investigated varied from 60-210 metres, 
and the beaver pond fragments investigated were between 15-420 m2.  In the warm season 
frogs were counted on transects in the variety of river banks   (PIKULIK ET AL., 2001).  
 
 


3   RESULTS AND DISCUSSION 
 
In the 1990s, in contrast to Western Europe, where the otter populations largely declined, the 
countries of Eastern Europe seemed to hold healthy populations of this endangered species 
(KIILI, 1991, OZOLINŠ AND RANTINŠ, 1992; SIDOROVICH, 1992, 1997; OZOLINŠ AND PILÃTS, 
1995; BRZEZIÑSKI AND ROMANOWSKI, 1996; ROZHNOV AND TUMANOV, 1996; ULEVICIUS AND 
BALCIAUSKAS, 1996; SIDOROVICH ET AL., 1997). 
In Belarus, during the 1990s, in areas with protected fauna (different kinds of 
numerous reserves), the otter density varied to a maximum of eight, but, on average, 
constituted 1.7-4.4 individuals per ten kilometres of river, depending on habitat quality, river 
depth and width, flow rate, density of the flood plain water body in the old riverbed, and 
beaver construction activity, primarily, damming (SIDOROVICH, 1992, 1997).  In 1997-2001 in 
the adjacent regions (Pskov, Tver, Smolensk) of Russia, areas characterised by low hunting 
pressures on otters, the species density was also very high – up to nine, on average 4.1-2.2 
(s.d.) otters per ten kilometres of river (n = 13).  According to ROZHNOV AND TUMANOV 
(1996), in 1990 about 28.9 otters inhabited 100 km2 of forested area and about 16.9 otters 
were recorded per 100 km2 for the total north western region of the European part of Russia, 
and about 18.6 (8.9) in the central region.  In Eastern Poland (Bialowiez Reserve), we found 
between 1.1-5.0 (average 2.5) otters per ten kilometres of river (SIDOROVICH, JEDRZEJWSKA 
AND JEDRZEJWSKA, 1996).  In Lithuania, ULEVICIUS AND BALCIAUSKAS (1996) found that 
density ranged from 1.8-5.7 (mean 2.9-3.7) otters per ten kilometres of river.  In Latvia, on 
average 1.5-3.1 otters were registered per ten kilometres of water course (OZOLINŠ AND 
RANTINŠ, 1992).  These data suggest that in Eastern Europe the potential (in respect to the 
habitat carrying capacity) density of otters comprises mostly of 2-5 individuals per ten 
kilometres of river. 
Amongst the main threats to otters inhabiting non-protected areas, deliberate poaching 
with trap or gun, as well as rather frequent capture during beaver trapping, were demonstrated 
by the otter specialists in Eastern Europe (OZOLINŠ AND RANTINŠ, 1992; SIDOROVICH, 1992, 
1997; ROZHNOV AND TUMANOV, 1996).  For instance, in Belarus during the 1990s in the 
hunting areas, the average density of otters on different river types varied from 0.9-2.8 
individuals/10 kilometres, these values were 1.6-2.1 fold lower than those in the reserves 
(SIDOROVICH, 1997).  The differences are statistically significant.  
In those years, as well as several decades before, the other significant threat to the 
otter populations in Eastern Europe was drainage, reclamation of wetlands and canalising of 
rivers, resulting in a large-scale and pronounced deterioration of otter habitats, and a large 
decline in the carrying capacity of the canalised rivers and drained floodplains (SIDOROVICH, 
1992, 1997).  Usually, even after several years of recovery in the drained river valleys, otter 
numbers decreased on average 2.6-3.9 fold in an area with protected fauna, while greater 
declines were recorded in the hunting areas – 4.1-10.0 fold (Figure 1).  Drainage reclamation 
in forested areas impacted less on the otter population due to construction activity of beavers 
markedly improving the destroyed habitats over a period of three to five years after the 
deterioration (SIDOROVICH, 1990).  Reductions in the numbers of otters affected by drainage 
reclamation of wetlands were more pronounced in the hunting areas, because there were many 
more vacant riverine habitats available than in the reserves densely populated by otters.   
In the 1990s, otter deaths in fyke and other types of fishing nets were also recorded.  
There was quite a strong control on the use of such nets by organisations responsible for 
nature protection.   However, a special study of this threat was not undertaken and, I assume 
that such otter deaths were not common.  
Since the late 1990s, drainage reclamation of swamps and river valleys has largely 
stopped in Belarus, because of lack of financial support.  The canals previously created were  


 
 
 
gradually inhabited by beavers (Castor fiber), and with their damming and digging of 
numerous burrows the habitat conditions for otters in the drained river valleys have improved.  
As a result, the negative factors caused by draining became less dramatic.  Since the late 
1990s, at least, in Belarus and the Baltic countries, trapping of otters and other fur-bearing 
animals has, on the whole, also declined, due to people losing interest in fur clothes.  
Moreover, trapping appeared to be more expensive and this turned the majority of trappers 
away from this business.  After the fur trapping and draining of river valleys declined in 
Belarus, an increase in otter population density, first of all, in the hunting areas might be 
expected.  What actually happened was a further decline in otter number from the hunting 
areas as well as the other areas which still have fairly good otter habitats, regularly used by 


large numbers of people.  In such areas, by counting otters on 24 rivers and their tributaries, 
up to 3.3 (average ca. 1.1-1.0) otters/ten kilometres were registered.  This is nearly half what 
it was before.  On six model rivers inspected regularly (almost each winter), otter density also 
decreased by a factor of 1.9-3.3 (mean 2.4) when the winter of 2002-2003 was compared to 
that of 1997-1998 (Figure 2a).  At the same time, there was no evident trend in the otter 
numbers using the model rivers draining the reserves (Figure 2b).  There, the species was at 
the same level with a fairly high population density.  
 
 


 
 
 
From information in Belarus and data from papers published for Western Europe 
(JEFFERIES, GREEN AND GREEN, 1984; MADSEN, 1991; REUTHER, 2002), I assumed that the 
most likely cause was a sharp increase in the frequency of otter deaths in fishing nets, 
primarily fyke.  To study this, a special questionnaire was sent to locals and expeditions in 
Belarus over the period from December 2002 to May 2003.  Preliminary examination of the 
results convinced me that fyke usage was the next step in the decline of the otter.  Either the 
state control against fishing net usage had become less effective, or people’s interests in 
fishing had greatly increased.  In effect, fishing with nets largely increased, and, in turn, otter 
deaths in fishing nets became more frequent.  Of 156 respondents with expert knowledge of 


the ‘fyke-otter’ problem, 140 (90%) confirmed the possible dramatic negative effect of 
fishing with nets on otters.  They responded that the use of fishing nets, especially fyke nets, 
had increased approximately 5-40 fold, and confirmed that during the last two years, from two 
to 30 (mean 14.6) otters died in fishing nets in the localities where they live.  The locality 
square is approximately 70-300 km2.  Thus approximately 1-9, (average about 3) otters died 
in fishing nets per 100 km2 per year.  Extrapolating the census data on a unit area, showed that 
between five and 38 (mean ca. 9) otters live per 100 km2.  So, currently about one-third of the 
otters drown in fishing nets.  Taking into account that the respondents did not have complete 
information about all otter deaths, I assume that the number drowned is plausibly higher - at 
4-5 otters per 100 km2.    
  
Such mortality, ca. 50%, is enough to cause the decline in otters.  Thus, I think that, at 
least in Belarus and adjacent regions of Russia the main current threat to the otter population 
is drowning in fyke nets and other fishing nets which recently became used much more 
frequently.  
The second question to examine is the environmental factors supporting such a high 
density of otters in Eastern Europe on areas where neither hunting nor fishing markedly 
reduced the population.  A long-term study (1983-2002) on otters in different parts of Belarus, 
in Eastern Poland and the adjacent regions of Russia (SIDOROVICH, 1990, 1992, 1997, 2000; 
SIDOROVICH, JEDRZEJWSKA AND JEDRZEJWSKA, 1996) suggest that in Eastern Europe the main 
factors favouring high density of otters are as follows: 
 
a.  A dense network of rivers and other aquatic ecosystems, the majority of which are 
still in fairly natural conditions;  
b. A high diversity and biomass of fish in the large and medium-sized rivers, as well 
as in lakes; 
c.  An abundance of common frogs (Rana temporaria) concentrated in streams for 
hibernating; 
d.  On small watercourses, widespread and highly developed beaver construction 
activity which leads to a substantial increase in the carrying capacity of these 
habitats. 
 
I will analyse the significance of these factors using the detailed materials obtained in Belarus 
from the long-term study on otters.  
  
3.1 Importance of the dense river network   
In Belarus, with an area of some 207,600 km2, there are 20,800 rivers with a total length of 
ca. 90,600 kilometres, and about 10,000 lakes which cover an area of 1,500 km2 
(KUPRIJANOV, 1966, JAKUSHKO, 1988).  Excluding brooks up to two kilometres long and 
canals, the average density of the river network ranges from 0.3-0.7 kilometre/km2.  Between 
70-76% of the river network consist of small watercourses with lengths up to 25 kilometres 
such as small rivers, brooks and a variety of canals.  In areas where neither hunting nor 
fishing substantially impact the otter population (mainly in reserves), and so represent quality 
in relation to otter habitat, we collected data of otter presence, along with density at small 
watercourses of length up to 25 kilometres (Figure 3).  According to the data obtained, the 
majority of these small watercourses (62%; n = 124) were inhabited by otters all year round, 
while only at 5% (n = 172), mainly brooks one to three kilometres in length, otter signs were 
not recorded.  Extrapolating the above data relating the otter distribution and density in 
reserves to the whole area of Belarus, it may be concluded that otter habitats are available in 
nearly every area of 10 km2 which can harbour at least one or several otters year round.   
 


 
 
3.2 High diversity and biomass of fish   
In large and medium-sized rivers more than 100 kilometres long as well as in lakes, fish 
diversity and biomass are fairly high.  In such aquatic ecosystems located in the catchment 
areas of different large rivers, from 17-38 fish species were recorded, and the fish biomass 
was also fairly high (ZHUKOV, 1965, 1988).  In the water-abundant rivers the officially 
registered fishing bag varied from year to year, averaging from 130-250 kilogrammes/ 
kilometre (maximum 580 kilogrammes/kilometre), and in lakes - 16-23 kilogrammes/hectare.  
The mean percentage of fish harvesting constituted about 71%, but intensive fishing does not 
occur everywhere.  Even taking into account the fishing rate, ca. 60 kilogrammes/kilometre 
are available to otters after fishing.  But the water-abundant rivers having abundant fish 


populations comprise only a small part of the river network (ca. 11%), and lakes are ice-
bound during winter.  So, such habitats with a high fish biomass are not necessarily available 
to otters particularly in the cold season.

In the small river, 20 fish species were caught, but in the particular stretches censused only 
from four to 11 fish species (average, 5.7 fish species in fast flowing parts, 9.8 in slowly 
flowing parts: 7.4 in moderately flowing parts) were recorded.  The fish species richness 
gradually increased with the increasing river size (average width multiplied by average depth) 
(rs = 0.84: P < 0.01), and with the decreasing flow rate (rs = -0.76: P < 0.01).  The same trend 
was found in the fish biomass (respectively rs = 0.72: P < 0.01 and rs = -0.76: P < 0.01).  The 
differences in fish species diversity and total biomass revealed between the three flow types 
on the Lovat are statistically significant (t = 1.9-4.7: P ≤ 0.05).   In the moderately and slowly 
flowing parts of the river, pike (Esox lucius), roach (Rutilus rutilus) and perch (Perca 
fluviatilis) contributed 78-80% to the fish biomass estimated, whereas loach (Nemacheilus 
barbatulus) and Gobitis taenia, roach, gugeon (Gobio gobio), pike and burbot (Lota lota) 
were the main fish biomass components (76%) in the river fragments with fast flow rate 
(Table 1).  Crayfish were most abundant in the moderately flowing parts of the Lovat, while 
in the parts of the river with slow flow rates they were slightly less abundant.  The least 
biomass of crayfish was registered in fast flowing waters.  
 
3.3 Abundance of common frogs and their concentrations during hibernation in streams  
Concerning prey availability and otter numbers in the model small river, the question of 
whether the fish biomass is high or low arises.  Accepting that wild otters need about one 
kilogramme of food per day (TUMANOV AND SMELOV, 1980), it is not much.  If we suppose 
that otters consume all the prey available in the small river, each individual would need to 
occupy about 21 kilometres of river.  This estimate does not take into an account possible fish 
migration to spawn in the river during spring.  Such migrations from the water-abundant 
rivers and glacial lakes to small watercourses are fairly common in Belarus (ZHUKOV, 1965, 
1988).  This, however, does not add significantly to the prey biomass because this period of 
higher fish abundance in rivers lasts no more than two months.  These relatively accurate 
results suggest that, on average, about four otters usually inhabited ten kilometres of the Lovat 
(SIDOROVICH AND MACDONALD, 2001), however, a substantial part of the prey available to 
otters was not taken into an account in the above calculations.  Which prey group was this? It 
is the common frog, which is numerous along the riverbank in the warm season and 
concentrates in such small rivers to overwinter (PIKULIK, 1985; SIDOROVICH, 1997; 
SIDOROVICH ET AL., 2001).  Indeed throughout Eastern Europe, a large part of the otter diet 
consists of common frogs (see JEDRZEJEWSKA ET AL., 2001 for references).  For example, 
from spraints collected on the River Lovat, the proportion of frogs (mostly Rana temporaria) 
in the otter diet varied seasonally from 30-42% (SIDOROVICH, 2000).  In addition to fish and 
crayfish which may be scarce in a given river, the common frog is plentiful in nearly all otter 
habitats in Belarus.  According to PIKULIK (1985), in Belarus during the warm period, 
common frog density was very high and ranged from several to 3,325 individuals per hectare, 
on average ca. 70.  On the banks of streams along the upper reaches of the Lovat River, up to 
22 (average from 0.4 to 2.3) common frogs were counted on 10 m2 plots during the warm 
season (SIDOROVICH ET AL., 2001).  This species hibernates in streams creating concentrations 
of thousands of individuals.  As a result of a detailed census undertaken along a 200 metre 
section of the Lovat  (flow rate - about 0.6 m/sec, mean depth - 0.6 m, and mean width - 5 m) 
in December 1995, 79 kilogrammes of common frogs, i.e. about 395 kilogrammes per 
kilometre of the river were caught in nets.  In late October and November 2000, we did a 
census of water dwelling-prey in three brooks flowing into the Lovat, which were fairly 
densely dammed by beavers (SIDOROVICH ET AL., 2001).   On average about 60 kilogrammes 
of common frogs per kilometre of the brook were captured.  A great difference five to 379 


fold, (average 132) in the common frog biomass in the beaver ponds and adjoining non-
flooded parts of the small streams was found.  
 
 
 
 
Undoubtedly, without an adequate supply of common frogs, otters could not populate such a 
high proportion of small watercourses. Also, overwintering concentrations of these 
amphibians in small watercourses seems to affect the timing of otter breeding in Eastern 
Europe.  One of the periods, when female otters frequently give birth is autumn (SIDOROVICH 
AND TUMANOV, 1994: Figure 4).  In this season otter breeding seems to coincide with reduced 
prey availability due to water freezing over and low air and water temperatures.  This could be 
regarded as a strange phenomenon.  In Belarus, the autumn breeding of otters was more 
frequently observed in fast and moderately flowing streams (≥0.3 m/sec) than in slowly 
flowing streams and static waters (Figure 4b).  The phenomenon of autumn breeding by otters 
and its difference in different aquatic ecosystems could be explained by the wintering 
concentrations of common frogs which are especially common in fast flowing streams 


(PIKULIK, 1985).  Also, when compared to the warm period, in winter otter access to the 
aquatic ecosystem is much reduced by a factor of two to 25 (average 14) due to water freezing 
over.  On account of these two factors, a two to ten fold or even higher increase in prey 
availability was calculated.  Thus, in Eastern Europe under the conditions of high common 
frog abundance, otters can inhabit small watercourses all year-round, and breed successfully 
in the cold season. 
 
 
 
3.4   Beaver construction activity   
Another important factor which helps support the fairly high density of otters on the small 
watercourses in Eastern Europe is the construction activities of beavers.  It is well known that 
otters benefit from ponds, burrows and lodges created by beavers.  In a variety of small 


watercourses, statistically significant positive correlations were found between otter and 
beaver settlements densities (rs = 0.77 - 0.82: P < 0.01) as well as with the rate of construction 
activity of beavers (abundance of burrows and lodges rs = 0.86 - 0.92: P < 0.01; square area of 
ponds rs = 0.65 - 82: P < 0.01) (SIDOROVICH, 1992, 1997).  Taking these correlations into 
account, two hypotheses appear: otters benefit from beaver construction activity, or the 
correlations are indirectly determined by the similar influence of other factors on both otters 
and beavers.  With regards to the second hypothesis, we could find no such factor.  Along 
small watercourses the distribution of both species was well correlated with the proportion of 
forested bankside.  Undoubtedly, this affects beaver distribution, by directly providing food 
for beavers.  There is no other explanation, however, as to how this factor might provide 
substantially higher food supply for otters in order to increase its population density.  There is 
no doubt, that it may be a marked difference in aquatic prey biomass between shallow streams 
with forested banks and largely open banksides (completely open stream banks are rare in 
eastern Europe).  It is likely that higher food supplies comes with damming activity by 
beavers. Results from North America (GARD, 1961; HANSON AND CAMPBELL, 1963) 
confirmed that the abundance of fish and other prey of otters increase in beaver ponds.  
In Belarus, data confirming the first hypothesis were also obtained.  In the cold 
season, the aquatic prey biomass in the beaver ponds was up to 13 (mean 3.6) kilogrammes 
per 100 m2, in the warm season up to 3.9 (mean 0.6) kilogrammes per 100 m2.  On average it 
is higher than the biomass of aquatic prey censused in the 100 m2 of the small watercourses.  
In the cold season it was eight fold higher; in the warm season, 15 fold higher.  Nevertheless, 
parts of beaver ponds studied were less productive than the adjacent non-flooded section of 
the stream: in the cold season - 36.4% of the ponds investigated (n = 11); in the warm season - 
13.0% (n = 23).  The total biomass of aquatic prey in beaver ponds, however, was much 
higher than that in a comparable length of a neighbouring stream inundated by the pond: in 
the cold season - up to 180, (average 58) fold higher (n = 11 ponds investigated); in the warm 
season - up to 1, 403 (average 411) fold higher (n = 23).  
Concerning fish species richness and biomass, the following data were obtained.  In 
non-flooded parts of 12 small watercourses investigated, a total of 21 fish species were 
recorded (up to 16 [mean 6.4] species in the particular stream).  In the beaver ponds, however 
only 16 fish species (up to 10 [mean 4.5] species in a particular pond).  In the cold season, the 
biomass of fish in the beaver ponds was up to 1.8 (mean 0.5) kilogrammes per 100 m2, and in 
the warm season - up to 3.8 (mean 0.5) kilogrammes per 100 m2.  The total biomass of fish in 
beaver ponds was higher than that in a comparable neighbouring stream section of equal 
length inundated by the pond: in the cold season - up to 220 (average 112) fold higher; in the 
warm season - up to 223 (average 76) fold higher.  Taking into an account that usually about a 
third part of small watercourse valleys were flooded by beavers, it is a high increase in prey 
supply.  In the valleys of the model watercourses from 18-72% (mean 33%) of the stream 
stretches were inundated by beavers, and the water area increased 27-188 (mean 59) fold.  
Rough estimates of the rate of prey biomass increase after the beaver damming in the model 
streams gave 35-187 (mean 76) fold compared to a hypothetical situation without beavers. 
 
Very often otters used both abandoned and active beaver burrows and lodges (Figure 
5) as a sheltered rest site, and/or sometimes a den.  During my long-term study on otters in 
Belarus, 19 otter litters with blind cubs were found in beaver burrows and lodges.  Also, there 
is a very important role of beaver burrows and lodges in the cold season in the conditions of 
freezing over, when they frequently provide access for the otters to the water environment. 


 
 
As a result of all habitat improvements by beavers in Belarus, the numbers of otters along 
small watercourses increased.  A good example of this trend was the beaver expansion in 
central Belarus, in the Volka small river catchment the species extended into an area of 500 
km2 (31 by 16 km) of the large Naliboki Forest which has about 380 kilometres of small 
watercourses such as small rivers and forest drainage canals (excluding very small ones).  
There, in the winter of 1982-1983, 19 beaver ponds and 22 otter locations (hereafter, an otter 
location means the particular place inhabited by either a lone otter or family group of otters) 
were recorded (Figure 6).  In the winter of 1997-1998, during a similar survey, 187 beaver 
ponds and 52 otter locations were found (Figure 7), and in the 2002-2003 winter, the 


populations increased again with 447 beaver ponds and 57 otter locations being recorded 
(Figure 8).  So, during twenty years a three-fold increase in otter numbers was reported which 
coincided with a 24 fold increase in the development of damming by beavers. 
 
 
 


 
 
 
In conclusion, I would like again to highlight that currently in Belarus, and seemingly in 
Eastern Europe on the whole, otters inhabit a lot of well preserved rivers and lakes which still 
have fairly high species diversity and aquatic prey biomass including plenty of common frogs 


concentrating in streams for hibernating, as well as increased habitat from highly developed 
construction activity of numerous beavers.  A severe threat to the otter population, however, 
came from fishing especially with fyke nets which appeared to have become more intensive 
during the last decade.
