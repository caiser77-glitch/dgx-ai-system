STATUS, HABITATS AND NEW CONFLICTS

Abstract: Otter distribution and population trends in the 1970s and 1990s are compared for Austria, the Czech 
and Slovak Republics, Germany, Hungary, Poland, and Switzerland.  During the 1970s the distribution of otters 
was reduced and population trends declined throughout these countries with the exception of Hungary, where it 
was stable.  In the 1990s the ranges of the otters were expanding, with the exception of Switzerland, where they 
became extinct. In Czech Republic, Poland, Slovakia and Hungary, otter surveys have been carried out on a 
larger, more systematic scale than in either Austria or Germany, where in some cases even population borders 
have not been properly identified.  Throughout Central Europe, otters now make use of various man-made 
habitats indicating that the species is more adaptable towards habitat changes and disturbance than generally 
or formerly assumed.  Besides conflicts due to otters predating in fish farms, new conflicts have developed in 
rivers, where anglers perceive the otter as a pest (licences to reduce otter numbers were requested, but not 
given) and illegal killing occurs.  In addition to the regionally significant road casualties, the low acceptance of 
otters by anglers and fish farmers appears to be the most severe threat for the species in the near future. 
 
Keywords: Eurasian otter, Lutra lutra, Central Europe, distribution, population trend, status, habitats, new 
conflicts 
 
1   INTRODUCTION 
Otters (Lutra lutra) declined dramatically in numbers and distribution throughout Central 
Europe during the 20th Century.  As a result, the species became a high priority for 
conservation. This is reflected in most national or provincial laws, Red Data Books and the 
European Union’s Directive on Fauna, Flora and Habitats (92/43 EWG) – the Habitats 
Directive, where the species is listed in Appendices II (species for which special areas of 
conservation have to be declared) and IV (species which deserve special protection 
throughout their range). However, since about 1990 clear signs of recovery of the species are 
recorded throughout most of Europe (CONROY AND CHANIN, 2002). 
This paper focuses upon the situation in Central Europe, namely Austria, the Czech 
Republic, Germany, Hungary, Slovakia and Switzerland.  It reviews the distribution in the 
1970s and 1990s and the respective population trends, identifies some of the problems 
connected with such a review (variable methods and data quality), characterises habitat 
utilisation and outlines a new field of conflict. 
 
2   DISTRIBUTION AND POPULATION TRENDS 
The observed decline of otters before and after World War II was quantified, neither in 
distribution nor in numbers, but it led to a banning of hunting within all states of Central 
Europe.  However, the species was not on the agenda of conservationists until the second

international otter colloquium held in Göttingen in 1979, where a first attempt was made to 
describe the actual distribution and status in Central Europe and beyond (REUTHER AND 
FESTETICS, 1980).  In the summary and most country reports, otter distribution was scored 
into absent, very rare, rare, common, very common and unknown (Figure 1).  Definitions of 
the various status categories were not given, hence the differentiation between, e.g., ‘very 
rare’ and ‘rare’ was based upon the experts’ opinions and there was no general consensus 
about underlying otter numbers for any of the categories (except absent).  Results were based 
upon questionnaires, expert opinions and signs found in the field.  They represented in most 
cases the situation from 1970–1979.  In this paper, they are compared with survey results 
from 1990 – 2003.

Austria 1979: KRAUS (1980) identified a small area with rare occurrence of otters in northern 
Austria on the border with the former Czechoslovakia and a very small distribution area in the 
Northern Limb Stone Alps (status again very rare and rare).  For the rest of the country no 
information was available and the status was hence scored as ‘unknown’.  In those days, the 
population trend was clearly negative. 
 
Czechoslovakia 1979: In the former Czechoslovakia, otters were considered very rare in most 
of Moravia, Bohemia and Silesia (what is today the Czech Republic), in the central part of the 
state the species was absent, but in the south they were still classified as rare and common 
(KUCERA, 1980).  In the eastern part of Czechoslovakia, what is today Slovakia, otters were 
more common, particularly in the north, but absent in the south and south west (HELL, 1980).  
Both authors gave the official numbers of otters: 73 individuals for Moravia and Bohemia and 
208 for Slovakia. KUCERA (1980) mentioned that the real number might be higher due to the

animal’s elusive life style and estimated the total population at between 330-350 individuals.  
However, there was a consensus that the species was declining. 
 
Germany 1979: The status of the otter in Germany was reported by several authors during that 
otter colloquium: HEIDEMANN (1980) for the most northern province (Schleswig Holstein), 
REUTHER (1980a) for Lower Saxony, HODL-ROHN (1980) for the Bavarian Forest and STUBBE 
(1980) for the German Democratic Republic.  These were also those provinces, where otters 
had been (assumed to be) present at least in some areas.  HEIDEMANN (1980) reported a clear 
decline during the previous ten years as indicated by field signs and estimated about 300 
animals and according to other data just 50 pairs of otters (juveniles excluded).  Data from 
REUTHER (1980a) for Lower Saxony were based on questionnaires backed up by field 
observations.  Otter presence ranged from frequent (common) in central areas, to very rare in 
the north and absent in the south.  They occupied 9–18% of their natural range and REUTHER 
(1980a) estimated the total number at 200-400 individuals.  HODL-ROHN (1980) declared the 
population in the Bavarian Forest to have become extinct during the previous ten years apart 
from some migratory animals from Czechoslovakia or Austria.  However, the summary figure 
of REUTHER AND FESTETICS (1980) indicated otters to be ‘very rare’ in north eastern Bavaria 
(Figure 1).  STUBBE (1980) presented results based on questionnaires for Eastern Germany.  
In 68 districts the species was found to be common, in 34 rare and in 90 absent.  The total 
estimated population was 600 ± 200 individuals.  They occurred mainly in three areas: the 
Upper Lusatian fish pond area (Oberlausitz), in the lake district of Mecklenburg and in the 
south east of the district Frankfurt/Oder. 
 
Hungary 1979: The data of NECHAY (1980) were based on questionnaires and non-systematic 
field surveys, which were carried out frequently in connection with damage caused by otters 
at fishfarms.  In 1974, otters received full protection in Hungary.  Then the population was 
considered stable.  In the following years, until 1979, numbers increased in south west 
Hungary, but decreased slightly in the north eastern part of the country, with the overall result 
of an increase in the population from 700–900 to about 1,500 individuals. 
 
Poland 1979: PIELOWSKI (1980) produced an otter distribution map for Poland without 
explaining the method upon which the data were based.  Animals were present throughout the 
country but more rare in the south west and central part of Poland.  He reported a decline in 
numbers, but also mentioned that the otter might be more common than generally expected, 
because most hunters and fishermen were not able to read the signs any more and many areas 
were not checked by mammalogists.  WLODEK (1980) focused on north west Poland (West-
Pommern) and his data indicated quite a numerous population.  In one district it was stable, in 
others slightly declining and in one, extinct.  For the whole of West-Pommern (30.511 km2), 
he estimated 296 ± 100 individuals. 
 
Switzerland 1979: REUTHER (1980b) reported single individuals (10-15) in isolated areas of 
Switzerland. The population trend was obviously negative. 
 
The presentations of the 1979 otter colloquium and the continuous decline in the 
following years identified the need for more systematic field surveys to properly describe the 
distribution of the otter and to enable the quantification of changes after repeated surveys.  
The survey approach of MACDONALD (1983, 1990) which has become known as the “IUCN 
method” was used by many authors, but often in a modified way (ROMANOWSKI AND 
BRZEZŃSKI, 1997).  The efforts to develop and standardise the method culminated in elaborate

a) the survey area is clearly defined (e.g. province, catchment area)

represented also as the number of positive sites out of all sites checked, but are not

Austria 2003: Apart from single field observations, otter surveys have only been carried out 
since 1990. Of these, only two were conducted in a systematic manner and covered an entire 
province, Lower Austria (KRANZ ET AL., 2001) and Upper Austria (KRANZ, POLEDNIK AND 
POLEDNIKOVÁ, 2003).  These and smaller scale surveys indicated an increase in otters 
numbers in many provinces, but a quantification was not possible. 
 
Czech Republic 2003: In addition to a number of regional surveys, the Czech Republic was 
surveyed systematically in the late 1990s (KUCEROVA, ROCHE AND TOMAN, 2001), 40% of the 
11 x 12 km grid squares showed otter presence, 60% were negative.  The positive sites 
included a successful re-introduction area in the north east (KRANZ AND TOMAN, 2000).  The 
increase in otter distribution is certain, but similar to Austrian provinces, it cannot be 
quantified on the state level due to a lack of repeated surveys. 
 
Slovak Republic 2003: The first countrywide survey was carried out between 1996 and 2002 
(URBAN, 2003) and revealed that of 429 12 x 11 km squares, 69% had otter signs, 16% were 
negative and 15% were not surveyed.  The increase in distribution is again certain and was 
also quantified by regional surveys, but no numbers can be given to quantify it on the state 
level. 
 
Germany 2003: REUTHER ET AL. (2002) give the most recent summary for the distribution of 
otters in Germany. Three provinces, Schleswig Holstein, Mecklenburg Vorpommern and 
Brandenburg, were covered by a systematic survey in the 1990s.  Most of Lower Saxony was 
covered by two systematic surveys in the same period.  Other provinces, such as Saxony, 
Saxony-Anhalt, Thüringen and Bavaria have not been surveyed systematically and the 
borders of the populations have not been identified, e.g., in Bavaria surveys concentrated only 
on the Bavarian Forest and an area along the River Inn.  However, the repeated surveys of 
most of Lower Saxony revealed an increase of otter distribution from 0.9% to 6.1% of the 
area.  In the majority of other provinces, there are also signs of recovery, but it is not possible 
to quantify it. 
 
Hungary 2003: The most recent update of otter distribution is given by MIKLOS (2002).  The 
field data are based on 10 x 10 km squares and also on repeated questionnaires.  Otters are 
present in about 80% of the country.  They continue to be absent in some northern areas near 
the border with Slovakia. Overall, according to MIKLOS (2002) and other Hungarian 
colleagues (LANSZKI, personal communication) the population appears to be stable.  However, 
in Austria, otters are now recolonising along rivers coming from Hungary, indicating an

Poland 2003: From October 1991 until May 1994, BRZEZŃSKI ET AL. (1996) conducted a 
systematic survey of the whole country, except for 1.7% of suitable habitat.  It was based on

surveys in earlier times, it is difficult to judge the dynamics of the species in Poland, however, 
they suggest that the otter has extended its range and is no longer considered endangered in 
that country.

3   GENERAL COMMENTS ON SURVEYS AND POPULATION TRENDS 
The descriptions of distribution and status of otters in 1979 were a good first attempt, but self-
criticism and methods were not well developed.  It is remarkable how easily several otter 
density categories (common, very common etc.) were applied and accepted and maps drawn, 
some parts with significant errors, such as the absence of otters in the Trebon Region in 
southern Bohemia, where otters were never absent.  Concrete estimates for population sizes 
were given, but it is now evident that many of these were guesses.  Nowadays, methods are 
more developed and more standardised, self-criticism is widespread and actual numbers for 
population sizes are usually missing. 
The knowledge of otter distribution in Central Europe has significantly increased since 
the 1990s.  However, there is a remarkable difference between countries: for the Czech and 
Slovak Republics and Poland a clear picture can be drawn, based on state-wide systematic 
surveys.  The data were collected in a systematic way and appear reliable.  For Hungary, there 
also exists a state-wide picture of the otter distribution, but it was based on several surveys 
and the method and reliability is not always clear.  In Austria, larger systematic surveys only 
began in 1999, and three provinces (Styria included) have now been completely surveyed.  
Two provinces with otter presence, Salzburg and Burgenland, were partly surveyed, but 
Carinthia remains unvisited.  In western Austria (Tyrol and Vorarlberg), no otters have been 
recorded.  In Germany the database is even worse than in Austria.  Good data are available 
only for a few provinces. 
 
Generally it is an advantage to extend surveys at least to the relevant legal unit.  It 
gives the possibility to quantify the distribution there, to undertake appropriate conservation 
actions, to have a basis for administrative decisions, such as issuing licences to reduce otters 
in an area and to properly identify the status for Red Data Books. Poland, the Czech and 
Slovak Republics, as well as Hungary are centralised countries and have already conducted a 
nationwide survey.  In contrast, Austria and Germany (as well as Switzerland) are federal

states.  There, most governmental authorities have no particular interest in surveys, NGOs do 
not have the money, and enthusiasts are missing. 
However, sometimes it is not possible to cover the entire legal unit (e.g. province) due 
to a lack of money or because the areas would be too large and the expected occurrence of 
otters very low.  In such cases it would be very important to properly identify at least the 
borders of existing populations.  The case of upper Austria (Figure 4) indicates that a 
population border could not be identified by a single stripe of 10 x 10 km squares, without 
otter presence, but by a stripe of at least 20 kilometres (2 squares).  In 1999, in Upper Austria, 
a survey was conducted in the north of the country which extended southwards until a 
continuous line of squares without otter presence was found.  Two years later, the first 
province-wide survey was conducted for upper Austria.  In 2001 there was still the ten 
kilometre wide belt of otter absence, but in the adjacent squares further south, animals were 
found.  It is unclear, whether they were there already in 1999, and missed, or that 
recolonisation occurred in the years between the two surveys.

by a stripe of two or even just one 10 x 10 km squares.  This will hinder a quantification of 
future distribution changes.  In Germany, this is the case in Bavaria, Saxony, and Saxony-
Anhalt, for Austria, in Salzburg, Burgenland and Carinthia. 
The distribution of otters given in the Atlas of European Mammals (MITCHELL–JONES 
ET AL., 1999) shows a non-fragmented population in the eastern part of Central Europe with 
some gaps of distribution.  However, this map was based on a 50 x 50 km grid and included 
all available positive records since 1970 unless modified by published data on extinction.  The 
result is an overestimate, both because of the long period used, the grid size and the high 
mobility of otters.  Such approaches and maps may be meaningful for small mammals, but 
when animals are larger and capable of moving over large distances without building up a 
population, the scientific significance, and the value for conservation purposes, of such maps 
is limited.

4   HABITAT UTILISATION 
It is not the intention of this paper to describe, or even quantify, the otters’ use of habitat in 
Central Europe, but to emphasise some general aspects and the perception of conservationists 
in this context.  In the recent past, the species was thought to be the most sensitive semi-
aquatic species in respect to disturbance, habitat destruction and pollution (e.g. REUTHER AND 
FESTETICS, 1980; MASON AND MACDONALD, 1986).  It was believed that the more pristine the 
river, the better for the otter.  However, HEIDEMANN (1980) recognised and described that 
from the 13 otter occurrences in Schleswig-Holstein, seven came from man-made habitats 
which indicated that even in those days such habitats were used by otters.  Nowadays, the 
animals are increasing in most areas and the hypotheses of decline have to be reconsidered.  
Water pollution of rivers and streams has improved and this change may be one factor 
responsible, at least in part, for the comeback of the otter.  A couple of decades ago many 
Central European rivers were so polluted that no, or hardly any, fish could live in them;  one 
of the many examples was the River Mur in Austria, where otters were absent in those days.  
Today, they have recolonised the river from downstream.  However, there are other pollutants 
which may directly affect the otter such as PCBs etc.  Their concentrations in the aquatic 
environment have also decreased and may be partly responsible for a pan-European recovery 
of otters.  Many conservationists and early otter researchers even believed that otters needed 
clear water in terms of visibility in order to successfully hunt (see REUTHER AND FESTETICS, 
1980 for a review);  however, this was disproved, for example, by the constantly good status 
of otters in south west Hungary, where waters are permanently muddy. 
The case for disturbance and habitat destruction is more difficult.  Otters are elusive, 
and in many instances their presence is not recognised by people, but that does not necessarily 
mean that they are particular sensitive to disturbance.  Breeding females are definitely more 
sensitive than other otters towards disturbance (KRANZ, 1995b), but apart from them, there are 
examples from all over Europe demonstrating that otters are quite tolerant to human presence, 
buildings or from traffic as far as noise is considered.  Habitat destruction is, per definitionem, 
destructive and therefore negative for otters.  However, it was frequently assumed that a 
change of habitat is equivalent to habitat destruction.  This may be the case where natural 
habitats are altered and/or replaced by man-made habitats.  It may well be that such changes 
decrease the habitat quality, but could also, in fact, improve the quality.  Such examples 
include fishponds in Central Europe which degraded streams by damming, changing the water 
chemistry (oxygen, temperature etc.) and reducing the amount of water itself in the stream 
(increased evaporation from pond surface area).  However, these fishponds increased in most 
cases the carrying capacity of the habitat for otters (KRANZ ET AL., 2002) compared to the 
natural stream conditions.  In Saxony, there are examples, where centuries old fishpond areas 
were replaced in the second half of the 20th century by open mining (KRANZ AND TOMAN, 
2000).  This caused the sudden and absolute destruction of a traditional man-made otter 
habitat, but when these open mines were abandoned and refilled with water, they were 
quickly recolonised by fauna and flora including the otter.  There are today many other even 
more surprising occurrences of otters in highly modified or man-made habitats such as 
ornamental ponds within golf courses (Figure 5), quarries and within industrial complexes 
(Figure 6) (KRANZ AND TOMAN, 2000).

One may argue that these are just sinks, where otters appear due to an increased population 
pressure from other habitats, but they would not live there without the constant supply of 
invaders from source habitats.  This question has not been tested and would be a major and 
expensive investigation.  However, there are many indirect indications, that this may, on 
occasions, be the case, but more frequently it is unlikely to hold true. 
The urbanisation of otters, their capability to make use of artificial habitats and their 
tolerance towards disturbance, which was recognised throughout Central Europe, may be the 
result of several factors: 
a) The knowledge of habitat use and otter behaviour has significantly increased which is 
partially due to the use of radio-telemetry devices (GREEN, GREEN AND JEFFERIES, 
1984).  In other words, otters have not changed, but human perception has.

b) The behaviour patterns of otters changed; in the recent past they learnt to live close to 
humans and to make use of artificial habitats; a trend which was recognised also in 
other Mustelids, such as Martes foina and Meles meles (KRUUK, 2002). 
c) Increased population pressure is responsible for some individuals making use of sub-
optimal habitats. 
 
5   NEW CONFLICTS 
Increasing otter numbers and the increasing use of man-made habitats makes conflicts with 
fish farmers inevitable (BODNER, 1998; KRANZ, 2000; KRANZ ET AL., 2002).  Several 
mitigation measures are applied to keep otters away from ponds and in some countries 
compensation systems have been introduced (e.g. in the Czech Republic, lower Austria, 
Saxony).  However, the conflict is far from being resolved.  There is considerable evidence of 
illegal killing in several Central European countries. 
A new conflict arises with anglers on rivers.  They perceive otters as causing 
significant losses in particular to oligotrophic trout and grayling rivers which are of particular 
interest.  Such conflicts occur in northern Austria, southern Bohemia and the Bavarian Forest 
(H. MAU, W. POOST, personal communications).  The most recent and significant evidence of 
this conflict was seven requests in upper Austria between 2000 and 2001 for licences to kill or 
live trap at least 15 otters in a region of 2,800 km2 called Mühlviertel (KRANZ ET AL., 2003).  
In order to meet the requirements of the Habitats Directive (92/43 EWG), fish ecology, otter 
ecology and the anglers’ economy were assessed with the resulting recommendation not to 
provide such licences.  However, it became very evident, that in the light of increasing otter 
numbers throughout most of Europe and the high numbers in the concerned areas, arguments 
based upon conservation lose their significance and in future ethical aspects have to be 
emphasised in context with such otter reduction requests.  One such aspect is that in Central 
Europe, otters may give birth all year round and any reduction has to take this into account.  
Therefore, shooting of otters cannot be an option in most cases, because it is not selective 
since it is not reasonable to identify the sex nor status of females in the field. 
 
6   CONCLUSIONS 
The knowledge of otters in Central Europe has increased significantly during the last 13 years 
and there are also clear signs of a population recovery.  There is no obvious straightforward 
explanation for this change from dramatically declining 20 and 30 years ago to this increase 
recorded almost everywhere.  A decrease of pollutants may be one relevant factor, but may be 
only part of the story.  Habitat improvement appears unlikely to play an important role in the 
comeback of the otter, since it took place only on a very limited scale and the same is true for 
the intensification of fish farming, which does increase the habitat carrying capacity, but 
otters also thrive far from fish farming areas.  Long-term population cycles of otters might be 
worth taking into account in future considerations. 
Otters are increasingly becoming considered a pest species causing damage in both 
fish farms and rivers.  This conflict highlights the need for otter management which may 
include requests to reduce otter numbers.  This in turn makes it important that up to date 
distribution data are available to support the relevant legal unit (e.g. district or province) in 
their decisions.    Populations also have to be understood in terms of ecological units 
irrespective of country borders, which in turn require the co-operation and standardisation of

survey efforts on an international scale.  In addition, it is necessary to quantify any population 
trends, at least in terms of distribution area, while data on relative otter densities and absolute 
otter numbers might also be required.  The increasing conflicts make it evident that the 
knowledge about otters and the relevant human ability to identify the population and its 
impact on prey populations now has to be applied in practical conservation work.  Since 
conflicts are not only about ecological facts, but are also about human perceptions and socio-
economics, this has to be taken into account.  An EU financed project with the acronym 
FRAP aims at developing a procedural framework for action plans to reconcile conflicts 
between large vertebrate conservation issues and the use of biological resources using 
fisheries and fish-eating vertebrates as a model case.  Such an approach is urgently needed to 
increase the low acceptance of anglers and fish farmers towards otters. Presently, besides road 
casualties, illegal killings are considered to be the most severe threat for otters in many areas.
