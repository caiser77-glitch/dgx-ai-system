An evaluation of ﬁeld and noninvasive genetic methods
for estimating Eurasian otter population size

Abstract
Successful conservation and management of
rare and elusive species requires reliable estimates of
population size, but acquisition of such data is often
challenging. We compare the two most frequently used
methods of assessing abundance of Eurasian otter (Lutra
lutra) populations, noninvasive genetic sampling (NGS)
based on genotyping of faeces and ﬁeld surveys using snow
tracking. In a 100-km2 oligotrophic otter habitat with linear
water bodies, both methods yielded very similar estimates
(10–12 individuals). However, in a 100-km2 ﬁshpond area,
consisting of a complex network of rivers, ﬁshponds,
channels and marshes, genotyping of faeces revealed the
presence of a higher number of individuals (46–50 geno-
types) than the snow survey (38 individuals). NGS data
analysed by capture-mark-recapture (CMR)-based software
CAPWIRE provided even higher estimates, being twice the
number assessed through snow tracking (76–81 individuals,
CI95% = 49–96 and 55–89). Our results suggest that the
performance of both NGS and snow tracking is comparable
in simple linear habitats, but in complex habitats with very
high otter density a combination of genetic and ﬁeld
methods, or CMR analysis using genetic data, is recom-
mended. We emphasise that to obtain reliable estimates
using NGS it is necessary to follow strict protocols for
detection and elimination of genotyping errors. Based on a
literature review and our experience, we suggest improve-
ments that may increase the success rate and efﬁciency of
NGS for otters.

Introduction
Reliable estimates of population size are fundamental to the
successful management and conservation of any species.
Biologists need accurate data on distribution, abundance
and population trends in order to make informed decisions
and recommendations to conservation policy makers. There
are a variety of direct and indirect abundance estimation
methods in common use; however, estimation of population
size can be very difﬁcult for elusive, nocturnal, far-
ranging, rare and endangered species. As regards carni-
vores, traditional ﬁeld methods are often based on distance
sampling, including direct counts, transect sampling or sign
surveys (Buckland et al. 1993; Gese 2001; Wilson and
Delahay 2001), or on capture-mark-recapture (CMR)

These methods, however, do not provide accurate and
reliable estimates of abundance or population trends
(Kendall et al. 1992; Mills et al. 2000; McDonald 2004)

of the animals, they may pose a risk of injury or even death

left behind by the animal, such as faeces, urine, hairs,
feathers, sloughed skin, etc., allows individual identiﬁcation
without the need to disturb or even see the animal
(Kohn and Wayne 1997; Taberlet et al. 1999; Taberlet and
Luikart 1999).
The Eurasian otter (Lutra lutra) is a typical species for
which abundance is very difﬁcult to assess. Over most of
its distribution range, Eurasian otters are night active,
secretive, rarely observed and very difﬁcult to trap, mark
and recapture. For this reason, otter research has tended to
rely either on indirect techniques, such as spraints (otter
faeces), tracks and holt surveys (e.g. Conroy and French
1987; Kruuk et al. 1989; Sidorovich 1997; Reuther et al.
2000); or on direct methods, such as visual censuses and
the use of remote cameras or infrared counters (e.g. Ruiz-
Olmo et al. 2001; Garcia de Leaniz et al. 2006; Guter et al.
2008); or on a combination of several methods, such as
radio-tracking combined with labelling of faeces with the
65Zn radioisotope (e.g. Kruuk et al. 1993). The application
of these methods, however, is often hampered by technical,
biological, environmental or ethical conditions, such as the
need for special equipment (e.g. radiotransmitters, cam-
eras, infrared or radiation counters), presence of snow or
mud of the appropriate quality (needed for tracking or
footprint
measurement),
diurnal
or
crepuscular
otter
activity (for direct observations), or physical capture of the
otter (for radiotransmitter implantation and 65Zn injection).
In areas with suitable weather conditions, snow tracking is
considered the most appropriate method for estimating
otter population size (Sulkava 2006). In other areas, track
and visual censuses may be used, as suggested by Ruiz-
Olmo et al. (2001). However, the reliability and accuracy
of these methods has often been questioned (e.g. Reuther
et al. 2000; Ruiz-Olmo et al. 2001; Chanin 2003; Janssens
2006; Kruuk 2006), thus development of noninvasive
genetic sampling (NGS) has been strongly welcomed by
otter researchers and conservationists.
Over the latter half of the 20th century, the otter was
considered one of the most endangered of European
mammals, classed as highly vulnerable or vanishing
(Foster-Turley et al. 1990), or even extinct in some coun-
tries (e.g. The Netherlands and Switzerland). The high
conservation priority of this species was reﬂected in its use
as a symbol of the Bern Convention (Convention on the
Conservation of European Wildlife and Natural Habitats).
In recent years, however, otter populations have started to
recover in most European countries (Conroy and Chanin
2002; Mason and Macdonald 2004) and, as their range
increases, the historic conﬂict between ﬁsh farmers/anglers
and the otter is once again arising. In some countries,
especially in Central Europe, ﬁsh farmers even regard some
regions as being ‘‘overpopulated’’ (Kranz 2000). Conse-
quently, environmental and conservation bodies are facing
increasing pressure for a reduction in otter numbers
through legal culls. For such a ‘‘conﬂict species’’ there is an
urgent need for reliable methods of population size
assessment. Without adequate methods, monitoring and
quantiﬁcation of species recovery may be biased, resulting
in incorrect policy decisions and management actions.
The otter is a very suitable species for NGS. They
defecate frequently, as spraints are also used for scent
communication, e.g. for marking territories or signalling
the use of resources (Chanin 1985; Kruuk 1992). A large
number of samples, therefore, are available for analysis.
Moreover, otter spraints are usually deposited at conspic-
uous sites (Kruuk 1995, 2006; Reuther et al. 2000), thereby
facilitating collection of samples. Further, as the otter is a
semi-aquatic mammal maintaining a limited home range
centred on water, the survey area for collection of samples
can be more thoroughly investigated. This is a distinct
advantage compared with species that can range over vast
areas, such as elephants, wolves, coyotes or bears, for
example, in which sampling usually has to be limited to
transects or points (Lucchini et al. 2002; Eggert et al. 2003;
Prugh et al. 2005; Bellemain et al. 2007).
Unfortunately, despite relatively easy sampling in the
ﬁeld, subsequent genetic analysis of otter faeces, using
nuclear microsatellite loci, is challenging. Reported suc-
cess rates, though highly variable, are generally low,
ranging from 14% to 73% (Jansman et al. 2001; Dallas
et al. 2003; Hung et al. 2004; Ha´jkova´ et al. 2005; Janssens
et al. 2008; Kalz et al. 2006; Prigioni et al. 2006a, b;
Arrendal et al. 2007; Ferrando et al. 2008; Lanszki et al.
2008). High levels of genotyping errors are typical for
NGS, especially when polymerase chain reaction (PCR)
success rates are not very high, a ﬁrst indication of low
quality and/or quantity of extracted DNA. If these errors
are not identiﬁed early in the procedure, and analyses
adapted appropriately, the counting of genotypes is likely
to overestimate the number of individuals in a population
(e.g. by up to 5.5 times; Creel et al. 2003). These problems
have recently been widely investigated and there now
exists a number of methods to reduce their occurrence to a
negligible rate. These include optimisation of sampling and
laboratory methods, regenotyping of the samples (i.e. the
multiple-tubes approach), use of quantitative PCR (qPCR)

genotyping error rates and their incorporation into sub-

Petit 2004; McKelvey and Schwartz 2005; Roon et al.

taking place on Eurasian otters has increased signiﬁcantly,
and there are also a few recent studies on some of the less
well-known otter species (e.g. Garcia et al. 2007; Trinca
et al. 2007; Koepﬂi et al. 2008). There has been, however,
no evaluation of existing studies and methods for otters as
yet, though such reviews have been found very useful for
other species, such as bears (Paetkau 2003; Bellemain et al.
2005; Solberg et al. 2006).
The aim of this study was to compare and evaluate two
different methods frequently used for estimating the pop-
ulation size of Eurasian otters: (1) a ﬁeld method based on
snow tracking, and (2) the noninvasive genetic approach
based on DNA analysis of spraints. Both methods were
tested in two differing otter habitats, and special attention
was paid to estimating the error rates associated with
sampling, methodology and protocols used. The ultimate
goal of this paper, based on our experience and a literature
review, is to provide recommendations that will allow the
efﬁcient and reliable use of either of the two methods for
otters.
Materials and methods
Study area
Two study areas were selected, each of 100 km2. The ﬁrst
was a mountainous/submountainous area located in the
Slovensky´ raj National Park (NP) in Slovakia (hereafter
referred to as Slovensky´ raj or SR); the second was a
ﬁshpond region located in the Trˇebonˇsko Protected Land-
scape Area and Biosphere Reserve (PLA & BR) in the
Czech Republic (hereafter referred to as Trˇebonˇsko or TR).
The Slovensky´ raj site consisted of an oligotrophic river
and its two main tributaries, with a total watercourse length
of ca. 50 km. The Trˇebonˇsko site included a wide, slow-
ﬂowing lowland river and a high number of, mostly large
(average size 50 ha; Roche and Roche 2004), eutrophic
ﬁshponds connected by a complex system of channels,
brooks and marshes. Both sites were known to support
resident otter population that was part of a relatively con-
tinuous
surrounding
population.
Trˇebonˇsko
area
was
characterised by very high otter density derived from high
food availability both in ponds and rivers and the habitat
structure, which was very suitable for otters (Sˇimek 1997;
Roche and Roche 2004).
Noninvasive genetic method
Faecal sampling
Very fresh spraint and anal jelly samples (from the previ-
ous night) were collected at both study sites in the cold
months (October–May) of 2003 and 2004. The location of
each sample was recorded using a global positioning sys-
tem (GPS) unit. Samples were categorised as spraint
(containing prey remains and almost no jelly), spraint with
jelly (containing prey remains but a high amount of jelly)
or jelly (excretion of anal scent glands or intestine mucus,
without prey remains). While still in the ﬁeld, 1–4 subs-
amples, each of ca. 200 mg, were taken from each sample,
and were placed in different preservation agents (for testing
and optimisation of methods see Ha´jkova´ et al. 2006).
Tubes with samples were stored in a cool-box until arrival
at the laboratory and then transferred to a freezer (-20C).
For longer storage, preserved samples were stored at
-80C.
DNA extraction and typing
DNA was extracted using the QIAamp DNA Stool Mini
Kit (Qiagen, Hilden, Germany), or the Invisorb/PSP Spin
Stool DNA Plus Kit (Invitek, Berlin, Germany), following
the manufacturer’s protocols with slight modiﬁcations
(increase in ﬁnal elution time). Ten microsatellite loci were
assayed: Lut701, Lut715, Lut717, Lut733, Lut782, Lut818,
Lut832, Lut833, Lut902 and Lut914 (Dallas and Piertney
1998; Dallas et al. 1999, 2000). In addition, the Lut-SRY
marker (Dallas et al. 2000) was used for sex identiﬁcation.
In the ﬁrst round, six microsatellites and the SRY marker
were genotyped for individual identiﬁcation, yielding
acceptable probability of identity values (Table 1). As it
was planned that genotypes would be included in larger
datasets used for population genetic studies (Ha´jkova´ et al.
2007), the samples were subsequently genotyped for four
additional loci, resulting in multilocus genotypes composed
of ten loci for each identiﬁed individual. When the indi-
vidual was represented by several samples, those of the
highest quality were used in preference for the second
round of genotyping. Nine microsatellite loci were ampli-
ﬁed as singleplex, while Lut914 was used as a positive

Reliability of DNA results
We used only tetranucleotide microsatellite loci to reduce
the occurrence of stutter bands and the resulting ambiguity
in scoring allele size that often occurs with dinucleotide loci.
In the ﬁrst step, two PCRs were carried out for each locus
and subsample (i.e. 2–8 PCRs for each sample/locus; sam-
ples consisting of 1–4 subsamples). According to these
results, further ampliﬁcations were performed, strictly fol-
lowing the multiple-tubes approach (Taberlet et al. 1996),
when the consensus genotype was determined after a mini-
mum of six positive PCRs for homozygotes and three for
heterozygotes. An exception, i.e. a decreased number of
positive PCRs (2–4 PCRs per locus), was considered sufﬁ-
cient only for a few samples with very high DNA quality
(95–100% PCRs positive and no allelic dropout recorded at
ﬁrst loci analysed). In some cases, samples had a very high
proportion of negative PCRs and up to 12–16 positive PCRs
were required to obtain a reliable consensus genotype, or the
sample had to be discarded. We could allow such a high
number of PCR repetitions as we had several subsamples
from each spraint sample, providing sufﬁcient amounts of
DNA extract, and we could also choose the best working
subsamples to obtain reliable complete genotypes. Special
attention was paid to samples whereby a multilocus geno-
type was likely to establish a new identiﬁed individual.
Stringent precautions were taken to avoid contamination
when working with spraint DNA, such as separation of pre-
and post-PCR procedures, use of ﬁlter tips and use of neg-
ative controls at each step.
GEMINI software (Valie`re et al. 2002) was used to
calculate the probability of identity (PI), i.e. the probability
that two individuals drawn at random from a population
would have identical multilocus genotypes. We calculated
both random PI, i.e. theoretical expected PI based on allele
frequencies from noninvasive genotypes obtained (herein
PI), and PI for siblings, which is a conservative upper
bound for the probability of identity (herein PIsibs)
(Paetkau and Strobeck 1994; Taberlet and Luikart 1999;
Waits et al. 2001). Frequency of allelic dropout (ADO) was
computed as the ratio of the number of observed ADO at a
given locus on the number of positive ampliﬁcations of
individuals determined as heterozygous according to their
consensus genotype at this locus (Broquet and Petit 2004).
Ambiguous peaks that could be confused with a real allele
(e.g. unspeciﬁc ampliﬁcation, PCR or electrophoresis
artefacts, or occasional contamination) were counted as
false alleles (FA). Values of ADO and FA were calculated
for each locus across all samples and for each sample
across all loci. Pairs of genotypes that matched all but one
or two loci (1-MM and 2-MM pairs) were identiﬁed using
the GenAlEx 6 program, menu Multilocus, submenu
Matches (Peakall and Smouse 2006).
Population size estimation
As otters are semi-aquatic mammals, their presence is inti-
mately linked with water and range sizes are relatively
small. We tested the hypothesis, therefore, that it would be
possible to catch all individuals using a thorough search for
spraints along all suitable water habitats at the study sites
(i.e. without the use of specialised programs or equations).
Two different estimates of population size (N) were com-
pared: (1) the number of unique genotypes recorded among
all spraint samples collected at the study site; and (2) the
population size estimate produced by a capture-mark-
recapture-based program for NGS, CAPWIRE (Miller et al.
2005). This software accommodates data with multiple
observations of an individual within a single session, and
also accounts for capture heterogeneity. This appears to
work well, especially for small populations (\100 individ-
uals; Miller et al. 2005). The likelihood ratio test (with
P \ 0.1) was used to choose between the even capturability
model (ECM) and the two innate rates model (TIRM). As the
samples were collected in the ﬁeld over 2 years (thus vio-
lating the general assumption of population closure), we also
used the CAPWIRE program for estimates of population
size from short periods of intensive sampling. For the SR
site, sampling was undertaken over three terms: (1) Febru-
ary–May 2003 (25% spraint samples collected), (2)
November 2003–January 2004 (69% samples), and (3) April
2004 (remaining 6% samples). At the TR site, intensive ﬁeld
sampling took place over December of 2004, when most of
this site was thoroughly checked and seven surveyors col-
lected 58% of the total number of spraint samples within
5 days. Only a small part of the study site was not searched
as it had been sampled earlier in the year (February–April
2004). These data were analysed in CAPWIRE separately.
The rest of the samples originated from localities also
included in the December 2004 sampling, thus they were not
used in these discrete calculations.
Snow tracking method
The method we used relies on the freezing of most water
bodies, presence of fresh snow, and a large number of
experienced ﬁeld surveyors checking all water bodies in
the study area over the shortest possible period (usu-
ally \ 1 day). The tracking took place the day after fresh

snow so that only movements of otters over the previous
night and morning were recorded. The surveyors followed
fresh tracks, took measurements of their size, and noted the
direction of movement. All data were entered onto indi-
vidual maps of the search area and then carefully
examined, combined and evaluated to estimate the number
of individuals recorded. In some cases, it was also possible
to infer their sex (e.g. female with dependent cubs) and age
category (adult/subadult/juvenile) based on size of tracks.
Snow tracking at the Slovensky´ raj site was conducted over
3 days in February 2003 and at the Trˇebonˇsko site over
1 day in January 2004. More details on the Trˇebonˇsko
snow tracking survey can be found in Roche and Roche
(2004). Although snow surveys were planned to take place
at both sites each year between 2001 and 2005, only on
these two occasions were the snow conditions suitable and
logistic organisation of snow tracking possible.
Results
Population size estimates using noninvasive genetic
sampling
Quality of samples and genetic markers
A total of 198 samples were collected from the SR site and
250 from the TR site. Most of the samples were character-
ised as spraints (n = 98 in SR and 142 in TR), followed by
anal jelly samples (n = 67 in SR and 62 in TR) and spraint
with jelly (n = 33 in SR and 46 in TR). Approximately 60%
of samples were successfully genotyped: 63% (125 samples)
from the SR site and 55% (137 samples) from the TR site.
PCR success rate (calculated only for positive samples) was
71%. One positive sample at each site had to be discarded
due to contamination, indicated by the repeated occurrence
of three alleles at several loci. The highest success rate
(82%) was obtained for anal jelly samples, followed by 58%
for spraints with jelly and 46% for spraint samples. The
quality and/or quantity of extracted DNA varied greatly,
with 3% of samples providing 100% positive PCRs and no
ADO, while over the whole dataset there were 2.5% of cases
with 100% ADO at a locus and 56% cases without any ADO.
The mean frequency of allelic dropout was 18.0% among
loci (SD = 0.03) and 18.8% among samples (SD = 0.22).
Though mean values were relatively consistent among loci
(Table 2), single values for particular samples and loci
varied greatly, ranging from 0% to 100% (the mean across
all samples and loci was 17.8%). Microsatellites with longer
alleles did not have a higher average ADO rate (Spearman
rank correlation r = 0.43, P = 0.214), but note that the
allele length range of loci in our study was relatively narrow
(138–211 bp; Table 2). False alleles were recorded with a
frequency of 2.9% (SD = 0.05 among samples). The aver-
age number of positive PCRs per locus and sample was 4.6
(range 2–16). For probability of identity (PI and PIsibs)
values see Table 1.
Number of individuals
At the SR site, successfully genotyped samples yielded 13
different genotypes, i.e. individuals SR1 to SR13. Geno-
types of all individuals were complete except for one
individual with one locus missing. No genotypes differed
by just one locus, i.e. there were no 1-MM genotypes.
There was one pair of 2-MM genotypes, but the individuals
were of different sex, thus it was a mismatch at three loci.
Seven individuals were identiﬁed as males and six as
females. The number of positive samples per individual
ranged from 1 to 23 (mean 9.6, SD 7.32). Two individuals
were identiﬁed based on a single sample.
Fifty different genotypes were obtained from the TR site,
corresponding to individuals TR1 to TR50. One pair of
genotypes matched all loci but one (i.e. 1-MM), but the
individuals differed by sex. Another six pairs of genotypes
were 2-MM, though three of these differed also by sex. Of the
totalnumber of 50individuals, 29were identiﬁed as males and
21 as females. The number of positive samples per individual
ranged from 1 to 13 (mean 2.8, SD 2.58), with approximately
half of the otters (26) identiﬁed from a single sample.

are obtained for each locus and sample (Taberlet et al.
1996). Another efﬁcient solution is the use of qPCR, which
estimates target DNA concentration; thus, researchers can
adjust the typing protocol according to the quality of the
samples, or select only the highest quality samples for
further analysis (e.g. Morin et al. 2001; Wandeler et al.

Hung et al. (2004), in using NGS to investigate the
abundance and spatial organisation of otters on the island
of Kinmen, found a very high number of otters on two
small streams: 38 individuals for two 2.3- and 6.2-km-long
streams, i.e. a density of 1.5–1.8 otters per km, or 0.8–1.1
resident otters. These values are probably the highest
found so far, even higher than that assessed by visual
observation in Shetland (0.5–0.7 otters per km of coast;
Kruuk et al. 1989). Such a high density may have been
caused by the fact that these animals were feeding in the
nearby sea, not only in the streams (Kruuk 2006) or, as
Hung et al. (2004) suggest, that the NGS was able to
identify a larger fraction of the population than conven-
tional means. There are, however, some indications that
the high density reported in this study may be a result of
genotyping errors. The authors calculated the upper limit
of probability of identity (PIsibs) as being 0.8%, and the
real observed PI value as 0.57% (Hung et al. 2004).
However, despite these values, their dataset contained four
dyads of identical genotypes, matching at all seven
microsatellite loci and differing only by sex. The authors
used the multiple-tubes approach with modiﬁcations that
signiﬁcantly reduced the number of repetitions. They
assigned the genotype of the heterozygote after the ﬁrst
PCR when there was no sign of ambiguity, while three
independent typings with the same allele were required to
conﬁrm the homozygote genotype. The authors reported
an occurrence of allelic dropout in 30.7% of PCRs, with a
range for single loci from 19.6% to 46.3% (Hung et al.
2004). Using qPCR and empirical data, however, Morin
et al. (2001) calculated that four repetitions of each PCR
are necessary to reach the 99% certainty level for samples
with template concentrations of 101–200 pg (the category
with a mean ADO of 26%), while seven replications are
necessary for 26–100 pg templates (mean ADO of 42%),
thereby conﬁrming the recommendations of Taberlet et al.
(1996). In the light of these facts, the high density of otters
on Kinmen may be an overestimation resulting from
uncorrected genotyping errors.
Kalz et al. (2006) used genetic typing and hormone
analysis of spraints to estimate population size in a lake
region of Germany. They reported a 24% success rate for
wild-collected spraints but no dropout alleles, based on
tests with captive otters. This contradiction is not discussed
by the authors. Papers of Prigioni et al. (2006a) and
Lanszki et al. (2008) do not include any information on the
quality of samples nor any measures to mitigate genotyping
errors. All further studies on otters (Arrendal et al. 2007;
Ferrando et al. 2008; Janssens et al. 2008), however, report
error rates thoroughly, the ﬁrst also including the testing of
some factors that could affect success and error rates. As
found by Ha´jkova´ et al. (2006) and Janssens (2006), tem-
perature is one of the main factors affecting ampliﬁcation
success of spraint DNA and, therefore, sampling at low
temperatures is always to be recommended. Further, as
already suggested by the earliest NGS studies on otters
(e.g. Coxon et al. 1999; Jansman et al. 2001), and studies
on other species (e.g. wolf, Santini et al. 2007; or brown
bear, Murphy et al. 2007), success rates are greatly
improved by the use of very fresh samples. Although
Arrendal et al. (2007) found that older frozen spraints also
worked relatively well, this may only be so in countries
with long-term cold weather. All studies discriminating
different types of otter faeces (i.e. ‘‘normal’’ faeces and
‘‘jelly’’) report much higher success rates for anal jelly
samples and viscous spraints, which are probably a mixture
of faeces and jelly or intestine mucus (e.g. Coxon et al.
1999; Ha´jkova´ et al. 2006; Janssens 2006; Lampa et al.
2008).

Comparison of number of genotypes and CAPWIRE

In this paper, we present genetic estimates of population
size in two different ways: (1) as the number of different
genotypes identiﬁed, and (2) as a point estimate using
CAPWIRE software. The genetic estimate of otter popu-
lation size at the SR site was 11 individuals (assuming
closed population), or 13 individuals (complete study
period). Analysis using the CAPWIRE software yielded the
same point estimate as the number of genotypes identiﬁed.
At the TR site, a total of 50 different genotypes were
recorded during the entire study period, or 46 (the sum of
34 and 12 genotypes from two sampling sessions from
different parts of the study area) assuming closed popula-
tion. CAPWIRE point estimates, however, were much
higher, at 81 individuals from the complete study period, or
76 individuals, obtained as the sum of 56 and 20 from the
two corresponding sampling sessions.
The lack of difference between the number of geno-

the SR site indicates that the sampling at this site was very
thorough. This is supported by the high number of

temporarily entering the area to utilise better feeding sites,
migrating individuals or those otherwise not ‘‘defending’’

spraints, while resident ‘‘defending’’ otters may have
deposited more.

all the rivers, pools, streams and most of drainage channels,
it was not possible to check all water bodies and all
potential sites used by otters due to the complexity, and
occasionally inaccessibility, of this wetland ecosystem.

Comparison of snow tracking and genetic estimates
of population size
Our snow survey method corresponds in many ways with
the home-range mapping method of otter snow tracking
used by Sulkava (2006, 2007) in Finland. A very similar
method has also been used to study otters in Sweden (e.g.
Erlinge 1967), Poland (Sidorovich et al. 1996), Belarus
(Sidorovich 1997) and the Czech Republic (e.g. Sˇimek
1997; Polednı´k et al. 2008). In Northern Europe, the
weather conditions (i.e. long winters with permanent snow
cover and most of the water bodies frozen) provide an
excellent opportunity to study otter abundance and move-
ments using snow tracking surveys.
When comparing genetic and snow tracking estimates of
population size in our study, counts were very similar for
the SR site (11–13 ind. using the NGS and 10–12 ind. from
the snow survey). At the TR site, genetic estimates of N
(both number of genotypes and the point estimate from the
CAPWIRE program) were higher than the number of otters
identiﬁed by snow survey (46–50 genotypes and 76–
81 ind. by CAPWIRE estimate, compared with 38 ind.
from snow tracking). Upon reanalysis of the maps and data
from snow tracking, however, the minimum number of
otters estimated was 38 individuals, with a potential max-
imum of 44–46 individuals. Although we tried to adjust our
sampling effort according to the complexity of study sites,
i.e. to increase sampling effort for TR site, we are aware
that both snow tracking and genetic sampling were much
easier, and thus more thorough, at linear water bodies of
the SR site than for the highly complex TR site. Therefore,
our conclusion concerning comparison of NGS and snow
tracking is similar to that regarding the number of geno-
types versus the CAPWIRE analysis estimate: in linear
habitats with ‘‘normal’’ otter densities, both snow tracking
and NGS can yield very similar estimates of population
size, while in complex ﬁshpond habitats with very high
otter densities, the estimate obtained by snow tracking can
be lower. It should be noted, however, that even if we
analysed data separately from shorter periods to better meet
the assumption of population closure, the length of such
closure was much higher in NGS as compared with snow
surveys carried out within 1–3 days. The outcome of the
genetic method, therefore, may be affected much more by
potential overestimation of true N due to migration (i.e.
presence of nonresident individuals) than snow tracking.
Alternatively, NGS can also underestimate population size,
as faeces from not all individuals will be found or suc-
cessfully genotyped, although this bias could be minimised
by the CMR approach. With snow tracking, both under-
and overestimation are equally possible, depending on the
experience of surveyors and the suitability of weather
conditions.
The same census methods, snow tracking and NGS,
were compared for otters in Sweden (Arrendal et al.
2007). With the NGS census, they detected the presence
of almost twice as many otters as with snow tracking, and
mark-recapture estimates based on the genetic census
(using CAPWIRE software) indicated that the real num-
ber of otters could be even higher. Their methods (both
NGS and snow tracking), however, are not comparable
with ours due to the much larger size of study area:
2,500 km2 compared to our 100 km2. For this reason,
they could not check the whole study area, only selecting
110 sites where both snow tracking and collection of
spraints for genotyping were carried out. In our study, we
were able to search the whole study area thoroughly, both
by NGS and by snow survey (with some restrictions at the
TR site, as described), thus, we were able to collect
spraints from a larger proportion of the population for
genotyping. Further, we conducted the snow surveys
under conditions that enabled us to count only fresh
tracks, i.e. to obtain more accurate estimates of the actual
population size. Our results from the SR site showed that
two different census methods gave very similar results.
The results from the complex and highly populated TR
site, however, were more similar to the aforementioned
ﬁndings of Arrendal et al. (2007), i.e. that snow tracking
gave a lower estimate of N than the genetic method and
that CAPWIRE analysis indicates that the real number of
otters can be even higher. This is in agreement with the
conclusions of Ruiz-Olmo et al. (2001), who found that,
in areas with high otter density, it is possible to under-
estimate the real number of individuals using footprint
censuses. Concerning the genetic method, previous studies
have shown that the sampling intensity inﬂuences the
precision of the estimate: the higher the number of
recaptures, the narrower the conﬁdence interval (e.g.
Miller et al. 2005; Solberg et al. 2006; Puechmaille and
Petit 2007).
In a study of brown bears (Ursus arctos) that compared
the performance of three different methods of estimating
population size, CMR-based NGS was found to perform
the best. The method based on observation of females with
cubs underestimated true N, while the traditional CMR
observation of bears from a helicopter performed better,
but was more expensive than the genetic method (Solberg
et al. 2006). In otters, however, snow tracking is much
easier, cheaper and less time-consuming than NGS.
Financial costs for snow tracking may be about EUR 600–
1,000; while genetic analysis of 100 spraint samples may
cost around EUR 10,000 (costs for collection of samples
in the ﬁeld not included), i.e. between 10 and 17 times
more. The costs of NGS depend on the number of samples
to be analysed. It is recommended that approximately
2.5–3 times the number of faecal samples are collected as

provides more additional data, including reliable sex
identiﬁcation, relatedness, genetic variability and structure
(Kohn and Wayne 1997; Taberlet et al. 1999). The main
advantage of the genetic method, however, is that it can
provide information on gene ﬂow, dispersal, effective
population size, kinship and spatial structure, which are
highly important demographic parameters with strong
relevance for conservation management. The main meth-
odological problem linked with NGS is the existence of
genotyping errors that, unless corrected, may strongly bias
the results. Concerning snow tracking, it has to be
emphasised that it is a more subjective method, substan-
tially dependent on surveyor experience and conﬁdence. It
is also strongly dependent on weather conditions, with
relatively strict requirements (e.g. most water surfaces
frozen, allowing the tracking of individuals across water
bodies, and fresh snow of suitable quality). As snow sur-
veys can only take place in winter, and as it has been
shown that otters may temporarily move into better
feeding sites during hard winters (e.g. Erlinge 1967;
Roche and Roche 2004), it is possible that winter surveys
may not show the ‘‘normal’’ number in some areas. Fur-
ther, radiotracking has indicated that otters may not move
much on the surface in very severe winter periods, pre-
ferring to stay underground or under the snow surface,
perhaps for several days (Kranz and Knollseisen 1998).
Although low temperatures are favourable for NGS
(Ha´jkova´ et al. 2006; Janssens 2006), extremely cold
weather is not required and the method can be used also at
other times of the year. Another problem may be the
existence of several ice layers on water bodies, or air gaps
under ice, which arise due to ﬂuctuating water levels. Otters
swimming and hunting under the ice will leave no tracks
and may be missed, especially in the case of snow tracking
which takes place over a very short period. Snow surveys,
of course, cannot be undertaken in hot countries; and it
appears that their use may be increasingly problematic also
in Western and Central Europe due to changes in the global
climate. Unfortunately, as low temperatures also seem to be
the most favourable for NGS, spraint genotyping is also
more challenging in countries with a warm climate (but see
Ferrando et al. 2008).
Conclusions and recommendations
We compared and evaluated two different methods for
estimation of population size in Eurasian otters, a ﬁeld
method based on snow tracking and a noninvasive genetic
approach based on spraint DNA analysis, using empirical
data and published studies. The noninvasive genetic
method was superior, providing more reliable estimates,
together with additional data, but the method was very
time-consuming and costly; its use is, therefore, limited to
smaller areas. Snow tracking is much cheaper and less
complicated, though its use is presently becoming more
problematic due to a lack of suitable snow conditions in
many countries. Taking into account the above discussion,
we suggest that snow tracking should be used in preference
in areas of lower otter density and with linear habitats,
especially for cases aimed only at population size esti-
mates. For other situations, we agree with the suggestion of
Arrendal et al. (2007) on the complementary use of both
methods, which has also been found to be successful for
other species (e.g. Canada lynx; McKelvey et al. 2006).
Noninvasive genetic sampling should be employed in
smaller areas, e.g. in different ‘‘model’’ otter habitats where
more detailed information could be obtained, while snow
surveys can be used for larger areas whenever the weather
allows. NGS may potentially overestimate actual N due to
migration, unless the sampling period is very short, while
snow tracking may underestimate N if tracks of different
individuals are not distinguished or if some individuals do
not appear on the surface under extreme weather condi-
tions. Although there are a few other ﬁeld methods, such as
mud-tracking and counting of individuals using direct
observation or remote cameras (e.g. Smallwood and
Fitzhugh 1993; Cutler and Swann 1999; Ruiz-Olmo et al.
2001; Wilson and Delahay 2001), their use is generally
even more conﬁned to special conditions or locations. We
recommend, therefore, the complementary use of both ﬁeld
and genetic methods, while taking into account their
respective strengths and weaknesses, and to focus on
measures that will increase their efﬁciency and reliability.
In this respect, we would like to emphasise that the
technical difﬁculties associated with low quality and
quantity of DNA extracted from faeces (Taberlet et al.
1999) should not be underestimated. NGS projects should
follow rigorous standards and guidelines, both during ﬁeld
collection and laboratory analysis (Waits and Paetkau
2005). The quality of data needs to be thoroughly moni-
tored in order to detect and eliminate genotyping errors
and, therefore, calculations of error rates and probability of
identity values should be meticulously reported in all
studies based on NGS (Waits et al. 2001; Bonin et al. 2004;
Broquet and Petit 2004; McKelvey and Schwartz 2004;
Pompanon et al. 2005). Special software, such as GIMLET
Conserv Genet
123


(Valie`re 2002) and GEMINI (Valie`re et al. 2002) may be of
great help. However, the correct setting of threshold values
is crucial as subsequent procedures and simulations are
automated. Thus, despite the advantages of these programs,
they may provide biased results when input data are not
sufﬁcient or threshold values not properly chosen. Pilot
studies, with a sufﬁcient number of PCR repetitions, fol-
lowed by simulations, are strongly recommended before
starting larger projects (Taberlet et al. 1999; Solberg et al.
2006; Valie`re et al. 2007).
In order to increase the efﬁciency of genotyping of otter
faeces, several approaches are possible: (1) proper collection
and storage of samples (e.g. Ha´jkova´ et al. 2006; Lampa
et al. 2008); (2) the use of qPCR to assess the amount of
template DNA in faecal extract (e.g. Morin et al. 2001;
Deagle et al. 2006), which allows selection of samples with
sufﬁcient otter DNA concentration and assessment of the
number of PCR repetitions needed; and (3) multiplexing of
several loci, which decreases time and ﬁnancial costs of
analysis. Eventually, the use of multiplex preampliﬁcation,
i.e. a two-step PCR approach which may improve the
ampliﬁcation success rate and reduce genotyping errors (e.g.
Piggott et al. 2004; Hedmark and Ellegren 2006) is also
suggested, as there is some indication that this method may
also be helpful for otter faeces (Lampa et al. 2008). Imple-
mentation of these measures may signiﬁcantly reduce
ﬁnancial costs of NGS and thus make noninvasive genetic
techniques more available.
