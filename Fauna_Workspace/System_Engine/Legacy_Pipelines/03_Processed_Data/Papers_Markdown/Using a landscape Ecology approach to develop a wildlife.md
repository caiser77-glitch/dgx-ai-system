Using a Landscape Ecological 
Approach to Develop a Wildlife 
Habitat Model in Korea
| KEI | 2004 | WO-03S |
Paik Ho  Rho  


Abstract 
 
 
Owing to its variety of topographic features and climatic conditions, 
Korea has extremely diverse habitat types, including coastal wetlands, 
estuaries, islands, paddy fields, croplands and woodlands, which provide 
nesting and feeding sites for migratory birds and carnivorous predators 
(e.g., Siberian tiger).  These habitats have become deteriorated and 
fragmented as a result of extensive socio-economic development and road 
construction; therefore, many endemic species are faced with extinction in 
Korea as a consequence of habitat loss, degradation and fragmentation.  
The objective of this study was to explore the process of developing a 
habitat model to quantify and protect suitable habitats for the Korean 
fauna and flora. 
I applied four major steps in deriving a wildlife habitat model: (1) 
overview and designation process of protected areas to preserve wildlife 
habitats, (2) review of management strategies for protecting wildlife 
species and their habitats, (3) introduction of the habitat suitability index 
(HSI) model, which was developed in the United States and is now 
applied globally, and (4) application of a case study to evaluate the 
preliminary habitat model.  All procedures described above were 
integrated to develop GIS-based habitat models for Korean wildlife, with 
particular consideration for spatially-explicit habitat variables derived 
from the National Geographic Information System (NGIS) and the 
Environmental Information System (EIS). 
Biotic and abiotic features must be incorporated into habitat models.  
Data for few biotic factors and most abiotic factors are available in 
developing the habitat model for Korea.  Although the Korean 
government has conducted three extensive surveys to assess biotic 
characteristics, only the ‘National Survey of the Natural Environment’ was 
used to construct a nationwide GIS dataset for wildlife species in Korea.  
Abiotic characteristics such as rainfall, temperature, topography and soil 
have strong impacts on the habitat preferences of each species.  The 
spatial patterns of abiotic features were elucidated with NGIS and EIS; 
therefore, the GIS-based habitat model used in this study emphasized 
most abiotic factors. 
Given the paucity of biotic database as well as insufficient knowledge of 
the habitat requirements of wildlife species, I implemented several 
preliminary procedures for habitat model development.  Habitat models 


                                                    
for bullfrog (an invasive alien species) and mallard (a winter visitor) were 
developed as case studies to examine whether the preliminary habitat 
model and developing procedures are realistic under the current situation.  
When data are lacking, expert opinion and a literature review should be 
used to identify habitat variables and habitat preferences.  If more time is 
available, field surveys and experimental observations could also be used 
to derive the HSI model. 
In conclusion, I suggest the following procedures to develop a GIS-based 
habitat model for the Korean fauna and flora: (1) organizing a three- to 
five-member panel of experts in landscape ecology, conservation biology, 
plant biology, animal behavior and wildlife management; (2) acquiring 
ecological knowledge through a literature review, Delphi, field surveys 
and other methods by selected panelists; (3) defining spatial and temporal 
scales based on the study areas and life histories of the species of interest; 
(4) selecting appropriate habitat variables based on life requisites of 
wildlife species; (5) developing a suitability index curve; (6) evaluating the 
habitat model against real-world observations; (7) producing a habitat 
suitability map for individual species; and (8) modifying and improving 
the habitat model by adding or removing habitat variables and changing 
suitability index curves.

Chatper 1.  Introduction 
 
 
1. Background and objective 
 
Various topographical features are manifest in the peninsular Korea, 
surrounded by coastal wetlands, estuaries, sand dunes and small offshore 
islands along the coast and covered by forests with three-fourths of its 
land.  The climate is characterized with marked seasonal changes in 
rainfall and temperature.  A wide variety of topographic and climatic 
conditions have created extremely diverse habitat types, resulting in 
abundant wildlife species and ecological systems (MOE. 2004). 
Coastal wetlands and small islands provide breeding, staging and 
wintering sites for migratory birds, in particular essential habitats for 
internationally threatened species (e.g., Spoonbill, Red-crowned Crane) in 
the western coastal regions of Korea.  Large carnivores like Siberian tiger 
were historically recorded present in the mountainous landscapes for their 
foraging and shelter from severe weather.  Preserving these habitats are a 
vital role globally as well as nationally to promote biological diversity 
because they serve as breeding, resting and wintering sites for rare species. 
However, during the previous forty-year period, land use and 
management measures have followed development-oriented.  Due to the 
drastic increase of human population and urbanized area, natural areas 
have been fragmented or lost through the process of land transformation 
that accompanies the consequent increase in residential, commercial and 
industrial area.  Many suitable wildlife habitats have not been efficiently 
protected from the pressure of development during the last four decades. 
As rates of wildlife species and their habitats continue to decrease at an 
alarming pace, many of the Korean fauna and flora are being faced with 
extinction (Won and Smith. 1999).  Appropriate actions to prevent habitat 
loss and fragmentation are needed for sustaining and restoring natural 
areas.  Among the conservation actions, designating legally protected 
areas of wildlife habitat is a typical management strategy to conserve 
suitable habitat for wildlife species.  Suitable habitats of species interested 
must be determined through the application of habitat model. 
To date, few habitat models have been developed and little is known 
about the habitat requirements of wildlife species in Korea.  Habitat 
models are necessary to delineate which area is a suitable habitat for 
wildlife species. These models are gradually used in wildlife management

to decide habitat suitability of unobserved area, especially useful to 
delineate protected area for wildlife species and determine appropriate 
location to build wildlife overpass in the fragmented landscape.  
However, only a few wildlife species such as wild boar, river otter and 
ring-tailed peasant were studied on habitat requirements. 
The overall objective of this study is to understand which approaches are 
recommended to develop habitat models under the current status on data 
collection and related knowledge on wildlife species and their habitat in 
Korea.  Specific objectives were to (1) identify current status of data and 
legal systems related to wildlife management in Korea, (2) review 
management activities that apply habitat evaluation procedures used in 
other countries, and (3) suggest development procedures to build a habitat 
model from a landscape ecological perspective. 
 
2. Materials and Methods 
 
Governmental documents including laws and regulations are firstly used 
to understand current status of wildlife management in Korea.  Scientific 
papers, annual reports and research notes related to wildlife species and 
their habitat were obtained and classified into wild fauna and flora.  I also 
reviewed spatially-explicit habitat models developed globally, which have 
been built rapidly in these days with the advances in Geographic 
Information System (GIS) database and remote sensing technology. 
GIS dataset such as the National Geographical Information Systems 
(NGIS) and the Environmental Information System (EIS) were effective in 
deriving habitat models by obtaining abiotic characteristics including 
topography, climate, soil and other factors affecting habitat quality of 
individual organisms.  Satellite image and aerial photographs were also 
used to evaluate habitat requirements of wildlife species.  The spatial 
distribution, relative abundance and seasonal variation of wildlife species 
should be measured to derive biotic characteristics of the habitat models. 
To find appropriate procedure for habitat model development, different 
approaches were proposed and evaluated with case studies.  Based on 
the literature review, preliminary habitat procedures were proposed.  
Habitat models for mallard and bullfrog were developed based on the 
preliminary procedures, and assessed with five-member panels of experts 
in landscape ecology, plant ecology, wildlife management and natural 
resources policy.  Under the current situation of GIS data and appropriate 
studies for habitat model, development of habitat suitability index model

Chapter 2. Theoretical Review 
 
1. Habitat, Biotope and Niche 
 
Most studies on wildlife habitat had conducted without defining habitat, 
or incorrect concepts were used in Korea.  Habitat can be defined as all 
biotic and abiotic factors in a localized area necessary for survival, growth 
and reproduction of the wild fauna and flora.  Some wildlife species have 
overlapping habitats, such as wild boar, wasp, Korean hare and red 
banded odd-tooth snake, all of which may live in a woodland habitat.  
However, habitat is frequently confused with vegetation association (e.g., 
Quercus mongolica dominated the deciduous forest).  In particular, habitat 
management strategies by the Ministry of Environment (MOE) concentrate 
on vegetation association. 
Often synthetic characteristics are used in descriptions of habitats, instead 
of the single factor. For example, in the case of the scaled quail, a set of 
brushes for roosting is an essential minimum factor of the habitat, another 
is the openness of the ground where this bird feeds.  In this case its 
habitat is synthetically defined as “a set of brushes with open ground”. 
Biotope is used as being synonymous with wildlife habitat (Udvardy. 
1959). Biotope was originally developed in Germany, and now applied in 
Japan, Sweden, Hungary and Korea.  The creation and conservation of 
biotopes and their planning are proceeding rapidly.  Unlike the habitat 
which provides a place to live for individual organism, biotope is the 
habitat of biotic community.  In other words, wildlife species has a 
habitat but it does not have a biotope.  Biotope concepts have usually 
applied in the urbanized area.  In Korea, Seoul and Seong-nam were 
selected cities as case studies to classify and develop biotope type.  
Wildlife habitat in urbanized area might be consisted of gardens for 
individual homes, playgrounds and the rooftops of building in cities for 
the propagation of wildlife species (Song. 2004).  Biotopes may be 
positively created by using space in the green belts. 
Confusion of the habitat concept is also apparent in references to the 
ecological niche.  Ecological niche refers to essential components of the 
environment utilized by the wildlife species, and so habitat and niche are 
compatible.  Habitat is the place where an organism lives, whereas niche 
is the role of an organism in the community.  Wildlife species at the same

habitat can not have the same niche because they require different 
resources to consume.  If habitat is the organism’s home address, an 
ecological niche would be the organism’s job or life style. 
 
2. Factors affecting Wildlife Habitat  
 
A. Resource and Condition 
 
Resource and condition are two fundamental factors affecting wildlife 
habitat, but few researches on wildlife habitat based on resource and 
condition have been documented in Korea.  Condition is an abiotic 
environmental factor which varies in space and time, and to which 
organisms are differently responsive (Rho. 2003).  Examples include 
temperature, relative humidity, pH, salinity, stream flow velocity and the 
concentration of pollutants. 
Conditions can be viewed with respect to Lebig’s Law of tolerance where 
there is a range of condition that a plant or organism can tolerate or 
survive in.  Outside this range, the organism will be reduced or extinct.  
Decreasing the range of the condition will cause a change in existence from 
survival to growth and finally to reproduction which has the most limited 
range of conditions (Morrison. 1992). 
All things consumed by wildlife species are resources.  For example, 
carbon, all the nutrients, space and light may be resources for a plant.  
Unlike condition, resources represent quantities that can be reduced by the 
activity of the organism.  These resources when at low abundance will 
reduce the amounts of plants or if two of more plants are utilizing the 
resource, and increase the competition for this resource. 
 
B. Habitat Requirements 
 
As described above, wildlife habitat is affected by biotic and abiotic factor. 
Four major components which are food, cover, water and space are 
needed to represent habitat suitability of wildlife species.  The survival 
and distribution of wildlife species is highly dependently on food 
availability, and any management practice that maintains or improve the 
diversity and availability of food will increase the potential population 
size of wildlife species.  For carnivorous and predatory species, food 
availability and diversity depends on the species richness and relative 
abundance of prey. 


5 제목 
Wildlife species require cover for day-time loafing, hiding, escaping, 
breeding and night-time roosting.  Cover is any variation in the habitat 
that provides protection from weather or predators.  In particular, 
reproduction requirements are important for habitat selection by wildlife 
species.  Important reproductive variables include egg development by 
hens, egg laying, incubation, hatching, re-nesting, and brood rearing.  
Reproductive success, representing the quality of reproduction cover, can 
be evaluated by clutch size, number of nesting attempts, nesting success, 
brood survival, and chick survival (Rho. 2003). 
Availability of water can be a critical component of wildlife species, 
especially in desert and semi-arid areas.  During the hot summer time, 
wildlife species need water not only to replenish daily water loss but also 
to cool off their body.  However, water is abundant in Korea, so water is 
not limiting factor affecting wildlife habitat (Park and Lee. 2002). 
Besides availability of food, cover and water requirements, space 
requirement is necessary to evaluate habitat quality of wildlife species.  
The spatial structure of habitat types plays an important role in the 
abundance and spatial distribution of wildlife species.  For example, 
habitat selection by wild birds is closely related to landscape metrics such 
as patch size, edge density, core area, and fractal dimension. 
Many wild species are adversely affected by human activities. For 
example, Korean agricultural development and over-using herbicides for 
agriculture have profoundly influenced many bird species.  Among these 
is the Barn swallow, a summer visitor that has suffered dramatic declines.  
The population of tree sparrow, which is residents in Korea, is also 
decreasing with the changes in agricultural land use and harvest 
management.  
 
3. Habitat Suitability Index Model 
 
Wildlife habitat suitability index (HSI) models provide habitat 
information for evaluating impacts on wildlife habitat resulting from land 
use changes.  The impetus for this HSI models was the Habitat 
Evaluation Procedures, a planning and evaluation techniques that focuses 
on the habitat requirements of wildlife species. Habitat models provide a 
numerical index of habitat suitability on a 0.0 to 1.0 scale, based on the 
assumption that there is a positive relationship between the habitat 
suitability and environmental features (US Fish and Wildlife Service. 1980a, 
1980b, 1981).  When possible, models are included that are derived from 


                                                   장제목 6 
site-specific population and habitat data.  To date, 157 HSI models were 
developed by the U.S. Fish and Wildlife Service. 
Development of the HSI models was based on food, water, hiding, 
resting, roosting and reproduction requirements with little consideration 
of spatial requirements. The use of spatial arrangements has continuously 
been increased in habitat model development and modification. For 
instance, interspersion attribute, contagion, edge density and fractal 
dimension are easily applied and used dramatically during the last few 
decades. Both spatial and aspatial characteristics were used in this study to 
build a spatially explicit habitat suitability model for wildlife species. 
 
Chapter 3. Wildlife Habitat Management in Korea  
 
About 200 species of wild fauna and flora are determined as endangered 
species in Korea, the capture of which is strictly regulated on the basis of 
the Wildlife Protection Act. Few wildlife protected area had been 
designated for preserving these endangered species and their habitats. 
Designation of the habitat protected area requires precise identification of 
the current status and spatial distribution of a wildlife species through the 
extensive survey and monitoring. In this aspect, this chapter is to describe 
the overview of major management strategies related to wildlife habitat in 
Korea.  The government establishes wildlife protection measures, such as 
conducting nationwide extensive surveys, designating wildlife protected 
area, and monitoring wildlife species and habitat condition. 
 
1. Nationwide Survey 
 
In order to identify wild fauna and flora inhabiting Korea and estimate 
population change over the long-term period across the country, three 
extensive surveys were conducted: (1) National Survey on the Natural 
Environment, (2) Wildlife Population Census, and (3) Winter Migratory 
Bird Census.  Although these three surveys differed in the way data were 
collected (i.e., sample size, observation method, quality assessment), they 
are assumed to be appropriate for evaluating wildlife population in Korea. 
The Ministry of Environment (MOE), in accordance with the Natural 
Environmental Preservation Act, undertakes the “National Survey on the 
Natural Environment (hereafter, refer to as NSNE)” every ten years.  Two 
nationwide surveys were carried out over the periods of 1987-1993 and 
1997-2003, to collect information on the current status of the natural

resources including topography, landscape, vegetation, abundance and 
spatial distribution of wild animals and plants throughout the country. 
Many experts in bird, reptile, amphibian, mammal, invertebrate, plant 
and geography come together to acquire information on the current state 
of Korean natural environment.  This nationwide survey was conducted 
on the 206 territorial zones and 145 coastal zones during the survey period 
(Table 1). As in the second NSNE survey, species abundance and spatial 
distribution of wild animals and plants were used to produce the current

and “Rated Digital Map on Greenness” were derived from the first NSNE 
survey, and now created into “Digital Map on Ecological Soundness” and 
spatial distribution map of each wildlife species using the second NSNE

2. Protected Areas 
 
Protected areas in Korea were classified into two categories: 1) domestic 
protected area, and 2) internationally protected area for wildlife 
conservation. Preservation of wildlife species and their habitat is a subject 
of common concern for all mankind, contributions at a global level leads to 
its enhancement.  These efforts include international cooperation with 
monitoring migratory birds and developing international management 
strategies.  Furthermore, designation of internationally protected area 
was established by international organizations cooperated with each 
country to manage wildlife habitats.  Examples of the protected area are 
the Biosphere Reserves, Ramsar Wetlands and Migratory Waterbird 
Networks for Crane, Shorebird and Waterfowl in the Asia-Pacific region. 
The High Moor, Youngneup of Mt. Daeam and Woopo Wetland had been 
designated as the Ramsar Wetlands in 1997 by the “Convention on 
Wetlands of Internationally Importance Especially as Waterfowl Habitat”, 
in order to conserve the wintering, staying or breeding area for waterfowls 
such as swans, geese, ducks, egret and heron.  The Man and the 
Biosphere Programme by UNESCO has established the Biosphere Reserve 
to coordinate biodiversity conservation with sustainable use. Two 
Biosphere Reserves have been specified in Korea on each of both Mt. Halla 
of the Jeju Island and in the Mt. Seorak.

3. Constraints Related to Wildlife Survey and Protected Area 
 
In the current situation of data collection and analysis on wildlife habitat, 
designation of new protected areas is difficult because we don’t know 
which area is most suitable for wildlife species interested.  Since the 
protected areas are designated without the surveys on wild flora and 
fauna in and around the areas, many of them do not have data on wildlife 
species.  Therefore, it is unknown that a wildlife protected area covers 
suitable habitat of wild fauna and flora.  In order to establish the 
protected areas, habitat model must be developed to delineate habitat 
suitability map.  A draft plan for the establishment is accepted if the 
proposed boundary of protected area contains the most suitable and 
critical habitat. 
Little is known wildlife habitat data throughout the country: therefore, it 
is very difficult to develop habitat model.  Among the 3 extensive surveys, 
only the NSNE survey have been used to develop GIS database such as 
spatial distribution of wildlife species and habitat conditions.  Even 
though other two nationwide surveys, Wildlife Population Census and 
Winter Migratory Bird Census, are very useful for habitat model 
development, spatial database for these surveys did not construct until 
now.  Ecological studies on habitat requirements are needed to identify 
habitat use and preference of each species, which is fundamental to build

Chapter 4. Case Study: Building Habitat Model  
 
1. Species and Study Area  
 
A. Selection of Species  
 
Mallard and bullfrog were selected as the target species for case studies 
to develop wildlife habitat models. Mallard duck, a winter migratory bird 
species, is dominant species in the western coastal region of the Korea 
during the winter season with Baikal teal.  Mallards and Baikal teal arrive 
in a great mass from their breeding grounds.  In general, migratory 
mallard use the same migration routes to travel between their breeding 
and wintering grounds.  The birds are assumed to be ancestor of most 
domestic ducks.  According to the Winter Migratory Bird Census, 
abundance of the mallard has declined over the survey periods from 1999 
through 2003 (MOE and NIER. 2004). 
Bullfrog, which is an amphibian, was introduced from the North America 
to Korea in 1911 for food.  At that time, many people believed that the 
bullfrog was one of the most delicious foods so that introduction and 
reproduction of the bullfrog were tried by scientists with the government’s 
support. This frog is relatively large and are voracious predators of a 
variety of prey including small fishes, other frogs and snakes.  Bullfrog is 
now causing serious problems in Korea such as preying on native wild 
animals and plants to extinction. Since 1997, the Korean government has 
tried to get rid of bullfrog, and registered the species as nuisance invasive 
alien species under the Natural Environment Conservation Act by the 
Article 6 of “Enforcement Ordinance of the Act”(MOE. 2004).

Among the 3 extensive surveys conducted in the Korea, the NSNE was 
used for bullfrog to identify spatial distribution of the species.  Different 
survey methods including inquiry, questionnaire investigation, field 
survey, capture were selected as effective methods in the NSNE survey.  
According to the survey, the bullfrog was widely distributed near the 
water throughout the country, especially most abundant in southwestern

2. Habitat Use and Data Availability  
 
A. Habitat Use  
 
Many characteristics affecting bullfrog habitat use were recognized by 
literature review and expert opinion.  Bullfrogs are usually found on or 
near stream, but move a number of meters into the water when water 
temperature is higher than air temperature in the fall.  The species spend 
much of their time sitting on the riverside and prefer to stay at more 
complex shape of streamline for food diversity and availability. 
Standing and slow flowing waters are required for all stages (i.e., egg, 
larvae, and mature) of bullfrog life history.  During the cold winter, both 
larvae and mature bullfrog spend in soft mud at the bottom of permanent 
wetlands or standing water.  Mature bullfrogs require standing water but 
can migrate to other ponds if water level drops considerably.  Constant 
water level is beneficial to bullfrog because drops in water level result in 
exposed banks so that no shelters for protecting from severe weathers and 
predators like human. These species generally inhabit larger and deeper 
bodies of water than other frogs. Eutrophic waters may be preferred 
because of the abundance of algae as food for larvae, abundance of aquatic 
vegetation as cover for larvae and adults, abundance of prey animals 
living in and feeding on the aquatic vegetation, and mud bottoms for 
hibernating and hiding (Graves and Anderson. 1987). 
During the middle of the day, mallard ducks spend much of their time on 
water surface or in shoreline near the estuaries and lakes. Habitat use by 
mallards is partially dictated by the availability of foods that can be 
broadly classified as invertebrates, mast and agricultural grains 
(Heitmeyer 1985). Resting was a major time expenditure of mallard on 
water surface and riversides, and feeding occurred occasionally on the 
ground near the water.  During the daytime, feeding and resting 
activities increased as time passed toward dusks.  Major disturbances 
were related to the human activities such as fishing, transportation, 
tourism and culture.  More aspects on habitat use of mallard ducks are 
described in Allen (1986). 
 
B. Data Availability  
 
In order to develop habitat model based on habitat use described in the 
above section, habitat variables are necessary to satisfy life requisites of 


                                                   장제목 16 
bullfrog and mallards.  Habitat variables for developing habitat model of 
bullfrog are classified into 4 life requisites: (1) food, (2) winter cover, (3) 
reproduction, and (4) spatial requirements (Graves and Anderson. 1987).  
Habitat variables for food components are “distance from streamline of 
water > 1.5m deep”, “% canopy cover of aquatic vegetation in the littoral 
zone”, “edge density between open water and territorial land”, and “water 
transparency”.  For winter cover requirements, “maximum water depth 
greater than maximum ice depth” and “% silt in the bottom material of 
stream” are applied.  Reproduction requirements are described as 
“stream flow velocity”, “pH”, “water temperature” and “frequency of 
water level fluctuations (i.e., riffle and pool)”.  Finally, “distance to 
permanent water (e.g., reservoir)” is used to evaluate spatial requirements. 
The Korean governments have constructed the National Geographic 
Information System (NGIS) and Environmental Information System (EIS) 
in the last few decades.  Owing to the GIS dataset including NGIS and 
EIS, as well as high-resolution satellite images and aerial photographs 
available, 8 of 11 habitat variables related to the bullfrog can be obtained 
with GIS data, while remaining 3 variables can be measured by field 
survey and/or experimentation (Table 5).  Both GIS data and field survey 
are required to describe habitat variable of water depth and ice depth 
comparison.  Water depth is obtained for each geographic location which 
measures the depth of water, while ice depth is directly measured by field 
survey.

3. Development of Habitat Model 
 
The procedures of habitat model development are as follows: 1) acquiring 
ecological knowledge on target species, which is already described in 
Section 4.2.A, 2) collecting habitat variables related to life requisites of the 
species, described in 4.2.B, 3) deciding suitability ratings for food, water, 
cover and spatial requirements based on suitability index curves, 4) 
calculating combined suitability index by adding each suitability ratings, 
5) producing habitat maps, and 6) evaluating habitat model against real-
world observations (Rho. 2003, Schamberger and O’Neill. 1986). 
Under the current situation of data availability and ecological knowledge 
on habitat requirements, habitat models for bullfrog and mallards in Korea 
were developed.  Few is known on habitat and ecology of the bullfrog 
and mallard in Korea, 6 experts in landscape ecology, avian ecology, 
environmental policy, plant ecology and wildlife management take part in 
developing and reviewing the draft HSI models. 
After defining life requisites and habitat characteristics of the selected 
species, abiotic features related to habitat variables were obtained using 
the NGIS and EIS data. As a result, habitat models were developed based 
on the environmental factors such as topography, soil moisture and 
texture, land cover, vegetation, water quality and water level.  For 
example, habitat suitability ratings of mallard were assigned for food, 
cover, water and spatial requirements, according to the land-cover type 
(e.g., wetland, agricultural area).  Next, suitability ratings for feeding and 
cover were combined to calculate suitability index for mallard and used to 
portray the habitat suitability across the study area.  GIS software is used 
to produce habitat suitability map. 
 
Chapter 5. Guidelines to Develop Habitat Model  
 
1. Selecting Criteria for Wildlife Habitat Models  
 
Biotope and habitat suitability index model are typical approaches for 
developing and managing wildlife habitat.  As described in Section 2.1., 
Biotope is more appropriate for habitat of community, which is widely 
used in urbanized area.  In general, abiotic features and human activities 
are fundamental to classify biotope type (Song. 2004).  On the other hands, 
habitat suitability index model is initially developed to evaluate the 
habitat of a single species (U.S. Fish and Wildlife Service. 1980, 1981).  In 


19 제목 
particular, HSI models are recommended to use in natural area, instead of 
urbanized area, in order to identify suitable habitat of wildlife species. 
In the HSI model development, either conceptual- or empirical-based 
approach was used to obtain habitat requirements of species.  For 
endemic and dominant species, which are relatively easy to get real-world 
data, empirical approaches were appropriate.  Examples are the linear 
and logistic regression of species abundance and presence/absence against 
environmental variables.  The observation of wide-ranging species can be 
time consuming job; therefore, this empirical approach is useful when 
more time is available.  Under the situation of little or no empirical data, 
conceptual-approach including Delphi is useful to develop the suitability 
index curves based on professional judgment (Crance. 1987).  Together 
with the literature review, Delphi is frequently used to develop habitat 
models for rare or threatened species. 
 
2. Procedures to Develop Habitat Model  
 
Procedures for quantifying wildlife habitat based on the HSI model are 
consisted of the following three steps: (1) data collection and other 
processes to prepare for model development, (2) model development, and 
(3) evaluation of the model.  These procedures are applied differently 
depending on the current status of the data availability and ecological 
knowledge related to the species of interest.  When habitat data and 
requirements are lacking, data collection steps are more important than 
other two steps.  However, evaluation of the model is needed in the 
United States, where 157 HSI models were developed in the early 1980s. 
Preparation steps for the model development followed three steps.  
Firstly, which species and study area are selected for developing habitat 
model?  It is very difficult to choose representative species, from across 
and within each region, which really are hard than any other procedures.  
To decide the representative species and study area, a panel of expert 
related in landscape ecology, wildlife management, plant ecology and 
animal behavior were organized.  Secondly, appropriate approaches 
should be used based on the selected species.  As described above, 
empirical approaches including field observations and statistical methods 
are suggested for endemic and/or dominant species.  Thirdly, panelists 
examine the ecological knowledge on wildlife habitat of the species, 
especially summarized life requisites and habitat requirements of the 
selected species based on knowledge and experience of experts including 


                                                   장제목 20 
local experts. 
HSI development procedures are as follows: (1) obtaining habitat 
variables for satisfying species’ needs on food, cover, water, reproduction 
and space, (2) selecting function to relate between habitat variables and 
suitability index, (3) assigning weights of each components, (4) calculating 
total suitability index values by combining all components, and (5) 
generating habitat suitability maps based on the suitability index. 
Identification of protected areas which need protection and restoration of 
wildlife habitat can be carried out through the habitat suitability map 
derived from the HSI models.  Evaluation steps of the model will be 
described at the following section. 
 
3. Evaluation of Habitat Models  
 
Evaluating the performance of the draft habitat models can be conducted 
by generating a scatterplot of the suitability index value against real-world 
observations.  The 3 extensive surveys described in section 2.1 can be 
used as real-world observations for model testing, but these observations 
did not applied in this study.  Species occurrence from the NSNE was 
only useful to supplement other field survey data, because they oriented 
community-based observation, instead of organism-based observation.   
Until now, few draft models have been developed.  So, evaluation of 
habitat models is not critical in Korea right now. 
 
Chapter 6. Conclusion 
 
In the current situation, this research suggests the following procedures 
in order to develop a GIS-based habitat model for the Korean fauna and 
flora: (1) organizing a three- to five-member panel of experts in landscape 
ecology, conservation biology, plant biology, animal behavior and wildlife 
management; (2) acquiring ecological knowledge through a literature 
review, Delphi, field surveys and other methods by selected panelists; (3) 
defining spatial and temporal scales based on the study areas and life 
histories of the species interested; (4) selecting appropriate habitat 
variables based on life requisites of wildlife species; (5) developing a 
suitability index curve; (6) evaluating the habitat model against real-world 
observations; (7) producing a habitat suitability map for individual 
species; and (8) modifying and improving the habitat model by adding or 
removing habitat variables and changing suitability index curves.
