THE LAST TWO DECADES

Abstract: Results from otter surveys conducted during the last two decades show that the population has started 
to increase during the last 10 years both in numbers and geographic distribution. The main increase in otter 
numbers is found in the central and northern parts of Sweden. There are still areas, mainly in the south west, 
that show few signs of otters. In Sweden pollutants (e.g. PCB) and habitat destruction are proclaimed as the 
main reason for the decrease in the otter population. Results show that the levels of PCB in Sweden are lower in 
the northern parts of the country. This could be a contributing factor to the more rapid increase in the north and 
very high levels of PCB in the south west could be one reason for the absence of otters.  
       
Keywords: Eurasian otter, Lutra lutra, Sweden, otter distribution  
   
 
1   INTRODUCTION 
 
Eurasian otters (Lutra lutra) were once common throughout Sweden, with between 1,000-
1,500 animals hunted annually during the late 1940s.  The population, however, started to 
decline in the late 1950s (ERLINGE and NILSSON, 1978).  Two important reasons for the 
decline may have been the release and distribution of polychlorinated hydrocarbons (PCBs) 
and the destruction of otter habitats (OLSSON AND SANDEGREN, 1989b; OLSSON AND 
SANDEGREN, 1992; KIHLSTRÖM ET AL., 1992; SJÖÅSEN, 1995). PCBs were banned in Sweden 
in the 1970s and since then the concentrations have decreased substantially in the 
environment (SJÖÅSEN ET AL., 1997).  
A large number of otter habitats have been affected by water control projects, ditching 
and draining of small water courses and wetlands, roads and acidification (ERLINGE, 1972; 
SJÖÅSEN, 1995).  The acidification may have contributed to the decline of the species by 
decreasing their food supplies. Since the 1980s thousands of lakes and rivers have been limed 
in order to restore a natural ecosystem. 
Due to the decline in otter numbers, the population was monitored during the 1980s 
and the Swedish otter population was estimated to be 500-1,000 animals, with the main cohort 
being   found in the central and northern parts of the country, while the population in the 
southern part was estimated to be only 50 individuals (OLSSON, SANDEGRAN AND ROSENDAL, 
1984, OLSSON, SANDEGRAN AND SJÖÅSEN, 1987; OLSSON AND SANDEGREN 1989a, OLSSON 
AND SANDEGREN 1986).  During the 1990s the population was continuously monitored and 
the results showed that it increased in both numbers and geographic distribution (ISAKSON 
AND NORRGRANN, 1990; ISAKSON, 1991; MORTENSON AND OLSSON, 1992; LARSSON, 1993; 
MORTENSEN AND MOHLIN, 1993; STÅHL, 1994; ARONSON, 1996; FORSBERG, 1996; HAMMAR, 
1996; SJÖÅSEN, 1996; GRANSTRÖM, 1998; LÖNN, 2001; BISTHER 2000a & b).  The total 
number of otters in Sweden in the year 2002 is estimated at 1,600 animals (SJÖÅSEN AND 
BISTHER, in preparation).  However the results shows a slow population increase and there is 
a great need for action against the distribution of other pollutants (e.g. PBDE) and against the 
continuing destruction of otter habitats. 
 


 
2   CONCLUSIONS AND DISCUSSION 
 
The majority of the surveys were conducted by using standard survey methods. Some were also 
conducted during winter by snow tracking. Because two different survey methods were used 
the results were calculated as areas with and without otter signs (Figure 1; Table 1).  The 
survey efforts (e.g. total area surveyed) were larger during the first period (1983-1992) 
compared to the second (1993-2002).  However, the results indicate that the distribution and 
density of otter signs increased from the first to the second period (Table1). The density of 
otter signs in two continuously surveyed counties was compared with the country as a whole, 
and showed an increase comparable to the general increase in Sweden.

Even though the population shows a slow increase, the total number of otters in Sweden today 
is just slightly larger than the numbers that were killed annually 50 years ago. 
The majority of otters found dead in Sweden are killed either by traffic or in fishing 
gear. There is only one known record of an otter killed by another carnivore and that was by a 
lynx (Lynx lynx) (ARONSON AND NILSSON 1998).  The number of dead otters sent to the 
Museum of Natural History for analysis has increased during the last ten years (Figure 2).

Compared with the distribution of otters in Sweden, there is a probability that the levels of 
PCBs effects the distribution of otters and also acts as a time factor in which the population is 
able to re-establish itself, specially in the southern parts of the country (ROOS ET AL., 2001).  
The levels of both PCB and DDT in freshwater sediments is ten times higher in the most 
polluted areas in the south west of Sweden than in the least polluted areas in northern 
Sweden.  The highest concentrations of PCBs were found in the coastal areas in the south 
west, with concentrations of 10-40 ng/g dw PCBs in 13 out of 17 lakes.  The lowest 
concentrations in the southern Sweden were found in the north eastern parts. Most of the 
northern parts of Sweden showed very low concentration with levels ranging between 0.5 and 
2.5 ng/g dw PCBs in most of the lakes (SÖDERSTRÖM ET AL., 2002).  Results from surveys
