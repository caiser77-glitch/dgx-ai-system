Radio-collars are removed easily by most 
mustelids because of their sleek body form 
(Kruuk 1978, Taylor and Lloyd 1978, Mel-
quist and Hornocker 1979, Messick and Hor- 
nocker 1981, Voigt and Lotimer 1981, Birks 
and Linn 1982).Marine mustelids, such as the 
sea otter (Enhydra lutris), pose further limi- 
tations on the use of radio-transmitters. Be-
cause radio-frequency transmitters are not au- 
dible when submerged, transmitting packages 
must be affixed so that they are exposed when 
the animal is on the surface. Otters spend con- 
siderable time on the water surface, usually 
on their back with head, limbs, flippers, and 
most of the ventral surface exposed; thus, the 
neck, ankle, and flippers were considered po- 
tential transmitter attachment sites. Addition- 
ally, successful surgical implantation of trans- 
mitters in other mustelids (Melquist and 
Hornocker 1979, Melquist et al. 1981, Eagle 
et al. 1984) prompted us to try this technique 
with sea otters. In this paper, we evaluate var- 
ious transmitter attachment sites on sea otters. 
We consider the effects on animals and the 
types of data that can be obtained. 
METHODS 
From 1975 to 1982, 168 otters were captured in 
floating tangle nets in Prince William Sound, Alaska, 
and instrumented with 164 MHz pulsing radio-trans- 
mitters (Cedar Creek Bioelectronics Lab, Bethel, Minn. 
55005). Transmitters were sealed in Scotchcast no. 5 
(3M, St. Paul, Minn. 55144) and fitted around the neck, 
around a rear ankle, through a hind flipper, or surgi- 
cally implanted. Otters captured from 1979 to 1982 
were immobilized with fentanyl (0.05 mg/kg) and 
azaperone (0.20 mg/kg) before handling (Williams et 
al. 1981); immobilization was reversed using narcan 
(0.01 mg/kg). All radio-transmittered otters were 
marked with colored tags in a hind flipper for per- 
manent identification. 
Neck collars were fitted on 3 adult males. One male 
was restrained during collar attachment whereas 2 were 
immobilized to enable more precise fitting. Collars were 
made of 13-mm neoprene impregnated fabric belting 
covered by surgical tubing, and were fastened using a 
brass and steel link to facilitate corrosion and thus 
eventual release from the animal. Radio-collars weighed 
125 g (<0.4% of body weight) and had an expected 
transmitting life of 1 year. Transmitting antennas were 
encased within the collars. 
Six males were restrained and fitted with transmit- 
ters on their ankles. Ankle-mounted transmitters were 
constructed like radio-collars, but were smaller, 
weighed 65 g (<0.3% of body weight), and had an 
expected transmitting life of 150 days. Two otters with 
ankle transmitters were held in captivity and observed 
for 15 days; the other 4 were released immediately. 
Transmitters were attached to the hind flippers of 
110 males, 27 females, and 7 pups. Most of these were 
immobilized for handling and released immediately 
afterwards. Otters weighed 9-39 kg and transmitters 
weighed 22-54 g; smaller transmitters were made for 
smaller otters (all transmitters <0.3% of body weight). 
Transmitters were mounted across the middle digit of 
a flipper using either 2.5 cm or 3.2 cm teflon coated 
steel bolts and steel nuts, or teflon encased wire. These 
bolts or wire were inserted through holes punched in 
the webbing on either side of the digit, and passed 
through a neoprene pad on the underside of the flipper 
(Fig. 1). Transmitters were expected to fall off in about 
a year due to corrosion of the nuts on the bolts or the 
nuts on the linkage holding the wire. Expected trans- 
mitting life ranged from 40 to 300 days. All flipper- 
attached transmitters had a 24-cm aircraft cable whip 
antenna. 
We surgically implanted transmitters in 15 otters (3 
males, 4 single females, and 4 female-pup pairs) 
weighing 9-34 kg. Transmitting units measured 6.8 X 
4.8 X 1.8 cm and weighed 70 g (0.2-0.8% of body 
weight), including an outer covering of Marlex mesh 
(Bard Implants, Billerica, Mass. 01821). Coil or loop 
antennas were encased completely within the package. 
Tip switches that disconnected the battery from the 
transmitter when tilted, as during a dive, were used 
on 5 transmitters to extend estimated battery life from 
12 to about 15 months. 
Surgery was conducted in a 7.5-m open boat. All 
otters were immobilized. Transmitters were placed

Flipper Attachments 
No mortalities were attributable to flipper- 
attached transmitters, but some injuries re-
sulted from otters pulling at transmitters with 
their mouths or forepaws. Flipper attach-
ments with short (2.5 cm) bolts fit snugly over 
the middle digit on many otters and thus 
sometimes caused the digit to break when ot- 
ters attempted to remove transmitters; 9 of 21 
otters (43%) observed after detachment of flip- 
per transmitters with short bolts exhibited a 
broken middle digit. Longer bolts or wire that 
enabled a looser fit over the digit were used 
to reduce injury. No broken digits were ob- 
served with these attachments, but 10 of 12 
otters (83%) observed after transmitter de-
tachment had slits in the webbing on either 
side of the digit; apparently the bolts or wire 
cut through the webbing as the looser-fitting 
transmitter was pulled over the digit. 
Only 30% of transmitters (12 of 33 ob- 
served) remained attached until corrosion of 
the connective link. On a third of these (4 of 
12), the nut on 1bolt corroded before the oth- 
er, causing the transmitter to remain attached 
by a single bolt, which eventually tore through 
the webbing leaving a slit on 1 side of the 
digit. Attachments with wire fastened by a 
single connective link (Fig. 1) alleviated this 
difficulty. 
Observations of otters occasionally pulling 
at flipper transmitters indicated that they were 
a source of annoyance or discomfort to some 
individuals. Many otters held the transmit- 
tered foot out of the water, using it for pro- 
pulsion only when frightened, feeding, or 
swimming rapidly. Although otters without 
transmitters also frequently swam with a foot 
held out of the water, transmittered otters al- 
most invariably swam with the transmittered 
foot out (42 of 43 observations) as opposed to 
the other foot marked with small plastic tags. 
We used flatter, more streamlined transmit- 
ters in 1981 than in previous years, but noticed 
no differences in behavioral reactions to the 
modified design. 
Transmitters typically were removed by ot- 
ters within 3 months (Table I), and the exter- 
nal antenna usually was broken off within 3 
weeks (range 7-60 days). Broken antennas re- 
duced the reception range of transmitters from 
1 1 3  km to <5 km for otters that held their 
flippers vertical and exposed in normal resting 
posture. For feeding otters, maximum range 
was lower because even when otters were on 
the surface consuming food, the rear flippers 
were held horizontally just below or level with 
the water surface; in this posture transmitting 
antennas projected slightly above water level 
but transmitters without antennas often were 
completely immersed, severely hampering 
signal reception (range <lo0 m). 
Surgical Implantations 
Subcutaneous implantations of transmitters 
may have contributed to the death of a female 
and her pup. The female separated from her 
pup, was unusually inactive the day following 
surgery, and died the next day. A large sub- 
cutaneous contusion appeared associated with 
the transmitter, but an otter found dead with- 
out a transmitter had a similar injury. Histo- 
pathological examination indicated that the 
female died from pneumonia incurred prior 
to implantation (T. Williams, pers, commun.). 
The pup was not considered old enough to 
survive on its own. It appeared sluggish a week 
after separation from its mother and was found 
dead 5 days later; it had lost 1.4 kg (15% of 
body weight) and had removed the surgical 
sutures, exposing the transmitter. We could

effects on animals.

Collars, bracelets, and implants were more 
suitable than flipper transmitters for collection 
of locational information because their range 
was less variable, enabling us to rely on a fixed 
search radius when attempting to locate in-
dividuals. However, only flipper transmitters 
with intact antennas provided sufficient range 
for monitoring activity from fixed stations us-
ing event recorders. 
Lifespan 
Sizes of otters and position of attachment 
dictated the sizes, and thus maximum life ex-
pectancy, of transmitters used in this study. 
However, few external transmitters remained 
attached until expiration of their power cells, 
so transmitter lifespans were shorter than an-
ticipated. Therefore, we recommend against 
the use of external transmitters for studies re-
quiring lifespans of > 3  months. 
Implantation of transmitters precluded re-
moval by otters. Thus, transmitter lifespan was 
related directly to battery size. We used trans-
mitters with an expected life of 1 year (al-
though transmitters did not actually last that 
long). We believe that larger, longer lasting

Ease of Attachment 
Flipper transmitters were the easiest to at- 
tach and fit properly to animals restrained but 
not immobilized with drugs. All external 
transmitters were attached in <lo min to im- 
mobilized otters. Surgical implantation re-
quired greater expertise and 10-20 min more 
time than external transmitter attachment, but 
did not require special working conditions, 
housing, or holding facilities. 
Effects on Animals 
All transmitter attachments resulted in some 
adverse effects on animals. Neck collars are 
potentially the most dangerous. Aside from the 
single mortality reported here, Loughlin (1977) 
and D. Costa (pers. commun.) also observed 
sea otter mortalities due to neck collars at- 
tached too tightly. Neck collars can be used 
safely only on otters with unusually small 
necks. Loughlin (1977) successfully radio-col- 
lared females and sniall males, but not large 
males. Neck collars with harnesses have been 
used on other mustelids (Kruuk 1978, Lindzey 
1978) to improve retention without constrict- 
ing the neck, but we believe a harness would 
restrict an otter while grooming. 
Ankle bracelets caused severe swelling in 2 
of 6 otters, but this might be avoided if brace- 
lets were attached to immobilized otters, en- 
abling greater care in fitting them properly. 
Flipper transmitters were least likely to 
cause fatal injury. Ribic (1980) concluded that 
behavioral reactions to flipper transmitters by 
otters in California were slight and probably 
unimportant with respect to energy expendi- 
ture. Our study indicated that effects may be 
more pronounced, ranging from preferential 
use of the foot without the transmitter to per- 
manent physical injury of the flipper with the 
transmitter. We could not determine whether 
flipper-attached transmitters affected the sur- 
vivorship, movements, activity, foraging hab- 
its, and social relationships of the otters we 
studied, although we believe such effects were 
minimal. Otters with transmitters and otters 
with flippers damaged by transmitters swam 
distances >30 km at speeds over twice the 
previously recorded maximum for sea otters, 
dove to depths near the recorded maximum, 
established and defended breeding territories, 
mated, and raised pups (specific details in 
Garshelis 1983). Several otters with injuries 
from flipper transmitters, including broken 
digits, survived at least 3 years. 
Subcutaneously implanted transmitters may 
have contributed to 2 deaths and the small 
bulge caused by these transmitters enabled 
some manipulation by otters; no effects of in- 
traperitoneal transmitters were noticed. How- 
ever, we did not assess long-term effects of 
implanted transmitters. Other researchers have 
observed few long-term deleterious effects of 
transmitters implanted intraperit~neall~in 
other mammals (Smith and Whitney 1977, 
Melquist and Hornocker 1979, Eagle et al. 
1984). 
SUMMARY 
Radio-transmitters were attached as a collar 
around the neck (n = 3), a bracelet around a 
rear ankle (n = 6), through the webbing of a 
hind flipper (n = 144), or surgically implant- 
ed, either subcutaneously (n = 10) or intra-
peritoneally (n = 5), in sea otters in Prince 
William Sound, Alaska, from 1975 to 1982. 
Flipper-attached transmitters had the greatest 
range (>13 km) and thus enabled automatic 
recording of activity using event recorders at 
remote locations; however, otters commonly 
damaged the transmitting antenna within 3 
weeks and detached the unit within 3 months, 
causing some permanent deformation of the 
flipper. Neck collars and ankle bracelets also 
were removed prematurely by otters and 
sometimes caused fatal injuries. Surgically im-
