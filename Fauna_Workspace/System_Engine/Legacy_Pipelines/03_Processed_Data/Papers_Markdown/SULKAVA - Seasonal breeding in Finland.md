SEASONAL BREEDING OF OTTERS (LUTRA LUTRA) IN CENTRAL FINLAND 
 
Risto T. SULKAVA  
 
University of Joensuu, Department of Biology.   
Present address: Aaponkaari 3, FIN-42800 Haapamäki, Finland. Phone: +358 14 733014.  
email: risto.sulkava@pp.inet.f i 
  
 
Abstract.  Otter breeding was studied in central Finland between 1985 and 2002. The number of litters and cubs 
was calculated from snow tracking at the same time as other otter studies (e.g. determining the number of 
individuals in a river system). It was possible to identify tracks of female and young otters in autumn or early 
winter. Clear seasonality in breeding of otters was found, all cubs being born between May and September.  The 
density of otters per kilometre of shoreline and river bank was low (0.24 individual/10 km), when all shores of 
lakes and rivers were calculated. However, if only possible feeding areas (areas with open water or tunnels 
under the ice accessible to otters) in winter were taken into account, the population was very dense (about 5.2 
individuals/10 km) in 2002.  The otter population increased markedly between 1985 and 2002. However, the 
most rapid increase stopped in 1993. The otter population has most probably reached the local carrying 
capacity in central Finland. Winter is a “bottleneck” time, and probably there is not enough food and feeding 
areas for a more numerous population. The mean number of cubs in early winter was 1.51 per litter (n = 119).  
The yearly mean number of cubs per litter depends on the density of population. There was also small “source-
sink” system in the population of otters in central Finland. A few river systems produced most of the cubs, and 
enables population growth to other river systems.  
 
Keywords: Eurasian otter, Lutra lutra, breeding, cubs, litter size, population, snow tracks 
 
 
1   INTRODUCTION 
 
The Eurasian otter (Lutra lutra) has previously been described as a species with no specific 
breeding season (e.g. MASON AND MACDONALD, 1986). In northern Europe, however, 
seasonality of breeding has been reported (e.g. ERLINGE, 1967). Later, seasonality in breeding 
was found in many northern or continental climate areas, such as Shetland (KRUUK, CONROY 
AND MOORHOUSE, 1987, 1991; KRUUK, 1995), Norway (HEGGBERGET AND CHRISTENSEN, 
1994) and Russia (TUMANOV, 2002). However, some breeding seasonality was also found in 
the Netherlands (WIJNGAARDEN AND PEPPEL, 1970), Germany (REUTHER AND FESTETICS, 
1980) and Belarus (SIDOROVICH, 1997). In captivity the otters have non-seasonal breeding 
behaviour (TROWBRIDGE, 1983; REUTHER, 1991). In a recent review of otter breeding 
CONROY AND BACON (2004) suggested that in most European countries there was a degree of 
seasonality. 
The population of otters in central Finland has been studied since 1985 using snow 
tracks. Observations concentrated on population biology, feeding ecology and methods to 
estimate otter populations (SULKAVA AND SULKAVA, 1989; SULKAVA AND STORRANK, 1993; 
HÖGLUND, 1996; SULKAVA, 1996, 2003; LUDWIG ET AL., 2002; STORRANK, SULKAVA AND 
LIUKKO, 2002; SULKAVA AND LIUKKO, 2003). The aim of this work is to describe the timing 
of reproduction in the otter population in central Finland. Breeding features may also describe 
the state of the population, and, for example, tell about possible competition between 
individuals. I also refer briefly to other new results for otter breeding in our studies in central 
Finland.   
 
 
2   STUDY AREAS, MATERIALS AND METHODS 
 
The study area covers about 1650 km2 in central Finland (62015´N, 24025´E) and includes 
three main water systems, as well as many small rivers, streams and lakes (Figure 1). Most of 


the streams are less than five metres wide, while very few rivers are more than 20 metres 
wide. The total length of the rivers and streams in the study area is about 750 kilometres, of 
which some 506 kilometres are more than two metres wide. Only about eight kilometres of 
the streams are more than 20 metres wide, with 46 kilometres 5-20 metres wide. The total 
water area of rivers (more than two metres wide) is about 180 hectares. There are about 460 
lakes and ponds in the study area and the total length of shorelines of lakes and ponds is about 
1,400 kilometres. Water covers about 10% of the surface area (total 17,065 hectares). 
Dystrophic waters are typical in the study area, but some oligotrophic and eutrophic waters 
are also found. 
 
 
Figure 1: Map of the study area and its location in Finland. The size of the study area is about 1650 km2. Small 
rivers and streamlets dominate the study area. 
 
 
Data from the Finnish Meteorology Institute (METEOROLOGICAL YEARBOOK, 1997) 
shows that the temperature generally drops to below -200C, sometimes as low as -350C, in the 
study area in winter. The long-term mean temperature is -2.30C in November, -9.80C in 
January and -4.90C in March.  During that time, all lakes and most rivers are ice-covered. 
Snow remains on the ground from at least the end of November to late April in normal 
winters. The mean depth of snow is five centimetres in November, 35 centimetres in January 
and 51 centimetres in March. The mean thickness of ice in lakes is about 15 centimetres in 
November, 30 centimetres in January and 50 centimetres in March. 
The numbers of litters and cubs were studied by snow tracking in autumn and early 
winter. The size of snow tracks of a female and her cubs are different at the beginning of 
winter in central Finland i.e. between October and December (SULKAVA, 1993; unpublished 


data). Later in the winter identifying a litter is still possible, if a group is followed for a couple 
of days. This is because all known litters were born between May and September in the study 
area, and female and cubs stay together for at least most of the following winter. If a litter was 
born in late autumn or winter, the snow tracks would be easy to find and identify, but this 
never occurred. The situation of breeding time is similar to other areas in similar latitudes. 
Cubs are born in summer, and the family group stay together over the following winter, as for 
example in Sweden (ERLINGE, 1967) and in Shetland (KRUUK, 1995). More details of the 
methodology of snow tracking are described elsewhere (SULKAVA, 1993, 1995, 2003;  
SULKAVA AND  LIUKKO, 1999). 
It was not possible to determine the actual date of birth in this study, but this could be 
estimated by the sizes of the first tracks measured in the snow in autumn or early winter. I 
have a reference collection of footprints-size from a young captive otter of different ages and 
size. I also compared this knowledge with more data on development, weight and length of 
otter cubs in captivity (REUTHER, 1999). However, I do not know how reliable this method is, 
because otters in the wild may not develop as fast as in captivity. Therefore, I did not try to 
determine an exact birth date. Nevertheless, finding the season when birth occurred was 
possible. In some cases, it was possible to find approximately birth-time at between one or 
two months intervals. CONROY AND BACON (2004) have also discussed the problems of using 
captive data in field observations. 
 
    
3   RESULTS 
 
Several litters are born in most years in the study area. A total of 119 litters and 182 cubs 
were found between 1985 and 2002. Only for those cubs alive in late autumn or beginning of 
winter was it possible to calculate their time of birth. Seasonality in the timing of breeding in 
central Finland was clear, all litters (n = 119) being born between May and September. The 
birth-time of 25 litters was calculated more accurately, at one-month intervals (Figure 2). It 
was also possible to determine the approximate birth times of 50 litters at two or three month 
intervals (Figure 3). It seems that there are also some differences in birth-time in the summer, 
with more litters probably being born at the beginning and end of that season. However, the 
data were too few to be exact enough to study these kinds of differences statistically.

During 2002, the otter density in the study area was about 0.24 individuals per 10 
kilometres of shoreline (if all 2,150 kilometres of lake and river shores were taken into 
account). In winter, however, there are only some rapids and estuaries of large lakes without 
ice cover in the study area. The length of these open areas varied between months and 
between years. There are also some streams with tunnels under the ice in all winters. So it is 
not possible to calculate exactly the length of possible feeding areas for otters in winter. 
However, all lake shores (total of 1,400 kilometres) are impossible feeding areas at this time. 
In rivers, more than half their lengths are totally ice-covered throughout the winter. In the 
middle of winter approximately 100 kilometres of rivers are possible feeding areas for otters. 
The density of otters in these winter feeding areas was about 5.2 individuals per 10 kilometres 
in 2002. The mean density of otters was 0.7 individuals per 10 kilometres of river-bed. 
 
 
4   DISCUSSION 
 
Seasonal breeding is typical for otters, when the availability of food varies largely between 
seasons (CHANIN, 1985; KRUUK, 1995). Scarcity of food and/or few possible feeding areas 
make it impossible for otters to breed during winter in central Finland. The summer is the 
most favourable time for cubs to be born. Large water areas are open, and a large area of 
shoreline is available to the otters. The population density per kilometre of shoreline is very 
low, and plenty of food is available to all individuals. Spring is the time when food is easy to 
find and readily available, because it is the spawning season for many fish species and 
amphibians. Breeding in May to June, immediately after the harshest time of the year, is 
perhaps favourable for cubs because they are well grown before the following winter. 
Salmonids, however, spawn in the autumn, while amphibians hibernate in the bottom of rivers 
and lakes at this time. The proportion of amphibians in the diet of the otters increases rapidly 
in autumn (SULKAVA, 1996).  
Winter is a bottleneck for otter populations in Finland, and in central Finland it is 
dense for the area available for possible feeding at that time (5.2 individual/10 km), but low 
for all shores (0.24/10 km). The mean density per kilometre of river was 0.7. In Scotland, the 
corresponding density was approximately 0.7 individuals per 10 kilometres (KRUUK ET AL., 
1993) and in Karelia 0.6-1.3 individuals per 10 kilometres (TUMANOW, 2002). During the 
1960s the density of otters was 1 adult/4-6 kilometres of river, in southern Sweden (ERLINGE, 
1967, 1968). The density of otters is high also in more southern latitudes. For example, in 


Belarus it varied between 1.7 and 5.9 individuals per 10 kilometres of water course (3.7-10.7 
individual/100km2), depending on the size and type of river (SIDOROVICH, 1997; SIDOROVICH 
AND PIKULIK, 2002), and 2.2 individuals per 10 kilometres in Poland (SIDOROVICH, 
JEDREZEJEWSKA AND JEDREZEJEWSKA, 1996) and 1.5-3.1 in Latvia (OZOLIĥŠ AND RANTIĥŠ, 
1992). In fishpond areas of the Czech Republic, the density was 4.8-6.2 individuals per 100 
km2 (SIMEK AND SPRINGER, 1998). In marine conditions on Shetland only one animal/10 ha 
water was reported along an 80 metre wide strip of water (about 1 animal/km of shoreline) 
(KRUUK ET AL., 1989; KRUUK AND MOORHOUSE, 1991). 
The female and cubs usually remain together for more than eight months, most of the 
following winter in central Finland. This seems to be approximately the same time in other 
areas of similar latitudes. In Shetland the cubs stay with their mother for 9-13 months 
(KRUUK, CONROY AND MOORHOUSE, 1991), about one year in Sweden (ERLINGE, 1967) and 
up to 16 months in part of mainland Scotland (KRUUK 1995).  
In central Finland, the otter was quite rare at the beginning of the study period in 1985. 
After that there was a clear increase in the population (SULKAVA AND LIUKKO, 1999; 
SULKAVA, 2003; SULKAVA, SULKAVA AND SULKAVA, 2003). The population increased from 
22-52 individuals in our study area between 1985 and 2002 (SULKAVA, SULKAVA AND 
SULKAVA, 2003). In central Finland the end of hunting and killing at fishpond areas may have 
had greater effects on otter numbers than the reduction in the levels of toxic chemicals or 
other pollutants for the recovery of the species. The otter populations have declined 
throughout most of the Western Palaearctic in the 19th century (e.g. FOSTER-TURLEY, 
MACDONALD AND MASON, 1990; MACDONALD AND MASON, 1994). However, during the last 
decades a recovery has been observed in some areas (eg. KRANZ, 1994; KAUHALA, 1996; 
SJÖÅSEN, 1996; BRZEZINSKI AND ROMANOWSKI, 1997; CONROY AND CHANIN, 2002; REUTHER, 
2002). 
In our studies in central Finland the mean number of cubs was 1.51 per litter (n = 119 
litters) (SULKAVA, SULKAVA AND SULKAVA, 2003). Along the sea coast of Norway the mean 
litter size was 2.5 during pregnancy, but only 2.0 after cubs became mobile outside the 
breeding den (HEGGBERGET AND CHRISTENSEN, 1994). A number of embryos (2.75 per 
pregnancy) died before birth, with a figure of 2.12 for Germany (ANSORGE, REINHARD AND 
ZINKE, 1996). This is probably the case in Finland. We found only those cubs which were 
alive in autumn. 
The three best river systems (of 16 river systems) produced 51.0% of all juveniles in 
the years 1985-2003 (n = 154 cubs) and the six best river systems produced 81.2% (SULKAVA, 
SULKAVA AND SULKAVA, 2003). It is interesting that the growth of the otter population was 
equal in all river systems studied, although only a few systems produced most of the litters 
and cubs. The best river systems produced most cubs, so enabling the population to grow and 
possibly move to other catchments. There is, therefore, some kind of “source-sink” system in 
the population of otters in central Finland. Most litters and cubs were born in few river 
systems. Later some of these moved to other catchments, where it might not be possible to 
produce many new cubs. 
In Finnish winter conditions the competition for food, and possible feeding areas, must 
be hard. When the population is dense, little prey is available in winter, and litters are small. 
We found a clear density dependence in offspring production in the otter population in central 
Finland (SULKAVA, SULKAVA AND SULKAVA, 2003). KRUUK, CONROY AND MOORHOUSE 
(1991) and KRUUK AND CONROY (1991) found a strong correlation between the numbers of 
cubs and the availability of prey in Shetland. They also observed that females can deliberately 
reduce the litter size by neglect as they can abandon individual cubs if there is not enough 
food available (KRUUK, 1995). Perhaps this is the case in central Finland, too. We calculate 
the number of cubs in late autumn or in early winter, and do not know the number of cubs in 


summer, or when the birth-time was. However, the yearly mean number of cubs per litter 
depends on the density of the population (SULKAVA, SULKAVA AND SULKAVA, 2003). The 
density dependence in offspring production showed clear intra-specific competition in the 
population of otters in central Finland. Although there is much water in the study area, the 
carrying capacity is quite low and there is a bottleneck is winter, when nearly all waters are 
covered by ice. 
It is easy to think that lakes are useless for otters in winter in central Finland because 
of ice. However, the reason that there are more otters, more litters and more cubs in some 
particular river systems in central Finland is that there are lakes, and especially larger lakes 
(SULKAVA, SULKAVA AND SULKAVA, 2003). The river-bed length or water area in rivers did 
not correlate with the number of litters or cubs, but there were significantly more large and 
medium-sized lakes in those six river systems who produced most litters and cubs in our 
study area in central Finland (SULKAVA, SULKAVA AND SULKAVA, 2003). One of the most 
probable reasons for this is warmer water in larger (and also deeper) lakes in autumn and 
winter. Warmer water keeps estuaries (outflows) and river-beds open for a longer distance 
than is possible in other cases. These open areas without ice are essential feeding areas for 
otters in winter (SULKAVA, SULKAVA AND SULKAVA, 2003). However, there may also be other 
possible explanations and there is a need for further studies.
