Abstract
Natural nest-site selection was investigated in 50 radiocollared southern ÂŊying squirrels Glaucomys volans
during spring and summer, 1994Âą1996, in the Ouachita National Forest of Arkansas. Squirrels nested in
226 trees in a variety of habitats at ÂŪve study areas. Contrary to previous reports describing southern ÂŊying
squirrels as habitat generalists, in this study squirrels showed selection in both the habitat and tree type in
which nests were placed. Where it was available, mature pineÂąhardwood forest was selected for nesting.
Young (< 15 years old) and immature (15Âą40 years old) pine plantations and harvested areas were avoided
as nesting habitats. At harvested study areas, squirrels nested in protected riparian mature forest strips
(greenbelt) along, and 10Âą20 m either side of, intermittent creeks and in adjacent mature forests. Squirrels
constructed only outside nests in pine trees. In mature pineÂąhardwood forest, pines were used for outside
nests more frequently than hardwoods; in greenbelt habitat, pines and hardwoods were chosen equally for
outside nests. Both outside and cavity nests were found in hardwoods; standing dead trees (snags)
contained only cavity nests. Snags were selected over hardwoods for cavity nesting in both mature pineÂą
hardwood forest and greenbelt habitat. All hardwood species and all decay classes of snags were used for
diurnal nesting in greater frequency than expected. Considering both cavity and outside nest-site selection,
pines were used less than expected. Results suggest that mature forests are optimal ÂŊying squirrel nesting
habitats and should be retained adjacent to harvested areas to provide resources to squirrels abandoning
stands after disturbance. Within harvested areas, nesting habitat can be substantially improved through the
retention of overstory hardwoods and snags, as well as protection of mature forest strips along drainages.

INTRODUCTION
Southern ÂŊying squirrels Glaucomys volans are forest-
dependent rodents common throughout the eastern and
central United States from the gulf coast to the Cana-
dian border. Known habitat requirements include forest
structure for gliding travel (Bendel & Gates, 1987),
snags (standing dead trees) and other natural cavities
for nesting (Muul, 1974; Weigl, 1978; Doby, 1984;
Gilmore & Gates, 1985; Sawyer & Rose, 1985), and
hard mast as primary foods (Harlow & Doyle, 1990).
Flying squirrels are nocturnal mammals that construct
arboreal nests in natural and artiÂŪcial cavities, as well as
in the open on tree limbs. Nests are used as diurnal
refugia, as well as refugia and feeding sites during
nocturnal activity periods. Since ÂŊying squirrels do not
excavate their own tree cavities, den trees and snags are
critical habitat components (Muul, 1974; Weigl, 1978;
Doby, 1984; Gilmore & Gates, 1985; Sawyer & Rose,
1985; Bendel & Gates, 1987).
Due to their occurrence in a wide variety of hardwood
and pine-hardwood forest types, ÂŊying squirrels have
been described as habitat generalists (Muul, 1968). The
frequency of ÂŊying squirrel use of nest boxes has been
shown to be equal in adjacent maple and hemlockÂąbirch
forests (Gilmore & Gates, 1985). However, Goertz,
Dawson & Mobray (1975) found ÂŊying squirrel use of
cutover pineÂąhardwood forest to be greater than mature
hardwood forests, and Sonenshine & Levy (1981)
reported higher squirrel frequencies in dense swampy
forest compared with adjacent upland mature hardwood
forest. In an experimental enclosure, wild-caught ÂŊying
squirrels nested in a box placed in a hardwood tree
setting signiÂŪcantly more often than in a box placed in a
coniferous forest habitat (Weigl, 1978). Even captive-
raised squirrels nested in the artiÂŪcial hardwood habitat
67% of the time (Weigl, 1978).
