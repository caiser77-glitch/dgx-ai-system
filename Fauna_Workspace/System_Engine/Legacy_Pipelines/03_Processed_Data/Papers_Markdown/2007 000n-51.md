SPACE-TIME ANALYSIS OF THE RECOLONISATION OF HYDROGRAPHICAL NETWORKS 
BY THE EURASIAN OTTER (LUTRA LUTRA L.) IN THE CEVENNES

Abstract   The National Cevennes Park covers about 3000 km² of mid-mountainous landscape in the south east 
of the Massif Central (France). It is criss-crossed by flow-variable hydrography and divided into the Atlantic 
(1525 km²) and Mediterranean (1685 km²) catchment areas. The Eurasian otter disappeared from this region in 
the 1960s. Since the 1990s there has been evidence of its return in some rivers. Since1991, the population has 
been monitored by systematic surveys of the main watercourses. 
The aim of this study is to perform a space-time analysis of 13 years of records (3,000 field 
observations, 279 survey-sites), using a GIS (Geographical Information System). First, mapped times series 
bring out a progressive recolonisation of the Atlantic and the north of Mediterranean basins by otters tracks and 
spraints (faeces). Survey-data are aggregated together by sub-catchment areas because biases and missing 
values make the interpretation of individual data more complex. Eight types of basins are then defined according 
to the time dynamics of patrol effort and otter signs observations. Finally, swimming recolonisation distances 
are calculated from hypothetical catchments of origin. Results of a linear regression model between time and 
distances fit with a dispersal rate of 2 km/year observed along three rivers. The spatial structure of the 
population, the dispersal corridors and the seasonal use of the landscape by otters should be more extensively 
studied. 
 
Keywords:  Eurasian otter, Lutra lutra, recolonisation, GIS, Cevennes, space-time analysis   
 
 
1   INTRODUCTION  
 
The national Cevennes Park (south of France), created in 1970, covers about 3,210 km² of 
mid-mountainous landscape where the ecological, cultural and historical resources are 
protected and managed (PNC, 1999). The rivers are subject to extreme flows, varying 
annually from very low water levels to violent floods (SABATIER, 1994). The Park lies in two 
catchment areas: the Mediterranean (Rhone) and Atlantic (Garonne) basins, occupying 
respectively 1,685 and 1,525 square kilometres of the Park, separated by a south west/north 
east watershed (PNC, 1999) (Figure 1). 
Otters (Lutra lutra) disappeared from Cevennes probably during the 1960s 
(FONDERFLICK ET AL., 1995), intensive hunting, water pollution and habitat destruction being 
the main causes of its extinction (BOUCHARDY, 1982; DESTRE ET AL., 2000). The species has 
been protected in France since 1972. Otters remained extinct in the Cevennes throughout the 
1970s, but subsisted in Aubrac, e.g. along the Colagne River, about 20 kilometres from 
National Park north west boundaries (DEFONTAINES, 1999). Their presence was again found 
in some rivers (Lot, Tarn) of the Cevennes in the 1980s (FONDERFLICK ET AL., 1995; DESTRE 
ET AL., 2000).   
Thirteen years ago, the National Cevennes Park began a yearly otter survey on its 
main catchment areas. The aim was to estimate the presence/absence of the species. The last 
conclusions were the effective recolonisation of otters along more and more rivers in the Park

2   MATERIALS AND METHODS 
 
Since 1991, the Cevennes Park agents annually surveyed from 93 (at the beginning) to about 
150 sites throughout the whole Park. Usually, the survey consisted of a search for spraints or 
otter tracks along riverbanks, to a distance of about 150 metres up and downstream from a 
selected topographical point, such as bridges, dams, rocks, waterfalls, confluences or other 
accessible strategic sprainting places. If signs were found, the site was recorded as positive, if 
not, up to three visits were made, with a minimum 15 days interval, before concluding the site 
was negative. The survey sites were selected according to their accessibility, so that the most 
streams could be prospected throughout the Park to ascertain if and how the river networks 
were being recolonised.  Sites were usually about five kilometres apart.  Originally, the survey 
was carried out during winter to maximise the chances of finding signs.  However, since 
1993, some searches were also undertaken in the spring and, since 2001 some were carried 
out during the summer.  Fieldtrips were delayed in the case of floods. 
 
Two particular weeks were spent in January 2003 to search for tracks in the snow at 
places where no spraints had ever been found. The search effort was concentrated at 
hypothetical recolonisation fronts.

About 3,000 field observations were made by more than 45 people at 279 survey sites 
in 13 years. Each site was manually geo-referenced on 1:25000 scale topographic maps (IGN 
France) as a point. Each record includes the observer, date, presence/absence and number of 
spraints, tracks or other signs observed. An additional set of 40 observations made by 
naturalists from the ALEPE (Association Lozérienne pour l’Étude et la Protection de 
l’Environnement) inside the National Park boundaries since 1991 were added to this database. 
Geographical Information System (ESRI, ArcGis 8.2) was run to locate and analyse 
the spatial distribution of otter signs across the whole of the National Cevennes Park. 
Elevation and hydrographical network from BD Carto, IGN France, were used as additional 
information layers.  
 
 
3   RESULTS 
 
Otter signs from 1991 to 2003 are shown in Figure 2. This overview map clearly shows that 
for 13 years otter spraints were found along almost all the Atlantic rivers and the Altier and 
Luech Mediterranean rivers. Spraints or tracks were discovered only occasionally in the 
southern Mediterranean catchment areas in spite of frequent field visits. The ratio of the 
number of positive observations to the total number of observations was 30% and 6% for the 
Atlantic and Mediterranean basins respectively. This success was only 1% along 
Mediterranean rivers other than the Rivers Altier and Luech.

was constructed with the aim of simultaneously displaying the yearly spraints observations

To make interpretations easier, sites were aggregated by sub-catchment areas (Figure 
4). The Cevennes hydrographical network was divided into 254 sub-basins (average area: 23 
km²) containing from 0-17 survey-sites. Presence/absence of patrol effort and 
presence/absence of spraints were computed annually for each sub-catchment and displayed 
as shown in Figure 3. Figure 4 shows the generalisation and analysis of the whole data on one 
singe map. 
This space-time representation of the data allows classification by sub-catchments 
according to eight types, bringing out some nuances compared with DE KERMABON (2000). 
First, there are systematically surveyed basins where a lot of spraints are observed: each year 
(1), more and more each year (2), occasionally (3), recently (4), or never (5). Next, there are 
occasionally or recently surveyed basins where spraints were observed proportionally to the 
patrol effort (6). Then, there are recently surveyed basins where no spraints were observed 
(7). Finally, there are the un-surveyed basins (8). 
The Rivers Nize, Lot and Tarn could be classified in type 1; Mimente and Tarnon in 
type 2; Luech and Dourbie in type 3; Altier and some parts of the Jonte in type 4; Gardon Ste 
Croix river and the Hérault in type 5; Chassezac, parts of the Jonte rivers and tributaries of the 
Tarn and Homol rivers in type 6; the Vis, some tributaries of the Hérault, and the Gardon St 
Jean and Gardon d’Alès in type 7; and the Cèze basin in type 8. 
 
In order to document the recolonisation process, the sub-catchments are then classified 
by year of first spraints observation (Figure 5).  This suggests a space-time structured 
recolonisation of sprainting otters in some basins. A very large area, including the upstream 
Lot and Tarn catchments, could make a northern origin of otter dispersal. From there, new 
spraints appeared progressively south eastwards along the Rivers Altier, Tarnon and Mimente.

The corridors and barriers for dispersal cannot be given at these regional scale 
analyses. Local scale analyses, i.e. on the stream and the landscape features scale, were 
performed for each main river and each watershed. Regressions were tested on the data for 
each river with the aim to find some correlations between the number (or the success) of 
spraints observations and the years, the months, or the location along each river. No 
significant results were found due to the lack of systematic data and the abundance of biases. 
Nevertheless, data showed a progressive recolonisation along some rivers. Some watersheds 
seemed barriers for this process, others did not. Otters seem to cross plateau watersheds as 
well as the asymmetric watershed between Atlantic and Mediterranean rivers. They marked   
both sides with their spraints. 
Date of first spraint observations at sites surveyed since 1991 were finally compared 
with the “swimming distances” from the hypothetical catchments of origin where spraints 
were found in 1991. Only 56 out of 279 sites were used in this model because they have been 
surveyed annually since 1991 and contained spraints on at least one occasion before 2003. 
Buffer areas of zero, 100 or 500 metres from water were used in the model. The buffers were 
used because otters move not only along the streams and water pools, but if necessary over 
land (HARRIS, 1968; VAISFELD, 1996). The distances were computed using GIS tools 
following the hydrographical network or its buffer area. Best results were obtained with a 500 
metre buffer (Figure 6), showing a highly significant (R² = 0.49; n = 56; p < 0.0001; slope 
parameter estimate = 0.0053 ± 0.0015) linear regression between the time and the distance of 
dispersal. R-square were 0.09 and 0.29 for 100 metres buffer and without buffer respectively. 
 
4   DISCUSSION 
 
The Cevennes watersheds lines can be classified into three categories (DEFONTAINES, 1999). 
The Atlantic basins dividing lines consist usually of gently sloping marsh or peat bogs areas 
on both sides - plateau watersheds (1). The Mediterranean slopes are sharply sloping on both 
sides - steep watersheds (2). The line separating the Atlantic and Mediterranean basins is an 
asymmetric watershed (3), shaped by a gently sloping area on one side and a steep incline on 
the other side. 
Otters are progressively recolonising all the Atlantic catchment areas, as mentioned by 
DEFONTAINES (1999). They follow the rivers network or readily cross the abundant plateau 
watersheds. The Mediterranean catchment areas present much more difficulty and a barrier to 
recolonisation. Watersheds are usually asymmetric or both sides are sharply sloping, and the 
rivers offer more and more unfavourable conditions to otters progressing to the south: high 
human density, intense tourist disturbances, summer droughts, dams and high levels of 
pollutants (DEFONTAINES, 1999; JIMENEZ AND LACOMBA, 1991). A few passing areas through 
asymmetric watershed lines seem evident and were already crossed by otters between Atlantic 
and Mediterranean catchments, e.g. the Aubaret plateau separating the Tarn and Luech rivers 
(FONDERFLICK ET AL., 1995; DEFONTAINES, 1999).  
Estimates and characterisation of the recolonisation movements are quite difficult 
from these data. Indeed, it was not the aim of the survey. The patrol effort was not systematic 
and data are affected by biases. Firstly, each site and each river were not surveyed every year, 
some survey sites were not suitable for sprainting and some observers differed from others in 
their experience, ability and motivation. Moreover, sprainting can disappear if the otters’ 
density and competition is too low, if individuals lose contact with others (LOCKIE, 1966, in 
BROYER, 1982; LIBOIS AND HALLET, 1995), and if the otters are disturbed or stressed (PRENDA 
ET AL., 1996; ROSOUX, 1998). The discovery of otter tracks in the snow at places where no 
spraints were found could give new information about the status and the movements of a

population. Finally, transient otters, e.g. sub-adults, can deposit isolated spraints in areas 
where there is no resident otter population (ROSOUX, 1998).   
The otter dispersal in most valleys is not totally quantifiable and unexplained on the 
maps. Nevertheless, an average recolonisation rate of about two kilometres a year could be 
visually estimated in Tarnon, Mimente and Altier basins (see arrows in Figure 5). This rate is 
equivalent to the slope factor estimate of the linear regression between distances and days of 
first spraint observation (0.0053 ± 0.0015 km/day = 2 ± 0.5 km/year (Figure 6).  This model 
took as an hypothesis that otters move from catchments of origin following the hydrological 
network within a buffer of 500 metres from the water. But the regression and its slope have to 
be interpreted carefully. The dispersion of the plotted points is relatively high around the 
linear model and data have the same biases as introduced above: some sites were not surveyed 
each year.  
 
To conclude, this detailed space-time analysis at regional scale highlights the 
complexity of interpretation of an otter survey data (1), even if the aggregation of data by sub-
catchments was advantageous to pattern otter dispersal along some rivers (2).  An analytical 
framework should be considered, with the aim of structuring the survey database and to bring 
out the different stages of the movements and establishment of an otter population. These 
further analyses, combined with complimentary surveys using genetics and data about 
environmental resources and landscape features, could allow a better understanding of the 
dynamics of otter recolonisation.
