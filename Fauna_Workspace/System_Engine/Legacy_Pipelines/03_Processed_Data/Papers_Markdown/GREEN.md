AN ESTIMATE OF THE NUMBER OF OTTERS (LUTRA LUTRA) KILLED ON SCOTTISH 
ROADS 
 
Rosemary GREEN 
  
Barjarg, Barrhill, Girvan, Ayrshire,KA26 0RB, Scotland U.K. 
email: rosemary.green1@talk21.com  
 
Abstract:  Although a database of over 1,000 Scottish otter road deaths had been compiled by 1996, the quality 
of data was too uneven for statistical analysis.  The only factors correlating with the geographical and temporal 
distribution of the deaths were the distribution and density of otters and recorders.  A systematic study of 
mortality on the A75 Gretna to Stranraer road over 12 months was undertaken in 1996-97.  Fifty deaths over 
156 kilometres in 12 months gave a result of one death/3.12 kilometres/year.  The reliability of this figure was 
considered.  The A75 was compared with three other trunk roads and the results from the study period compared 
with data for other years on the same road to assess their typicality.  Extrapolating from the A75, a figure for 
deaths on Scottish trunk roads can be calculated.  Otters are not killed at the same rate on all road classes.  
Using the Scottish database, now over1,600 records, the percentage of deaths on trunk roads can be used to 
calculate an estimated total death rate on Scottish roads. . 
 
 
Keywords. Eurasian otter, Lutra lutra, road traffic accidents, mortality, numbers, Scotland   
 
1   INTRODUCTION 
 
By mid 1996 over 1,000 records of Scottish otter road mortality, dating from 1969, had been 
amassed, with over 85% (n=929) dating from 1985. The size of this database should have 
given scope for statistical analysis of the effects of habitat features and human impacts on this 
significant non-natural mortality.  However results were disappointing for two related 
reasons.  One was the uneven quality of the data; some were excellent, based on detailed 
examination of corpses, others were vague, with no record of age or sex or weight and 
dubious grid references and dates. Secondly, in Scotland a dead otter is not particularly 
noteworthy so, apart from a few dedicated recorders, the recording of accurate (or any) data is 
a matter of luck.  
Following on from this is the influence of the location of those recorders on the 
distribution of known otter mortality.  The only factors correlated with the geographical and 
temporal distribution of otter road mortality were the distribution and density of otter 
populations (GREEN, 1991) and the location of recorders.  
The A714 is a typical example of the influence of the location of recorders on the 
known distribution of otter deaths.  This is a quiet road running 77 kilometres from Port 
William on the Solway Coast to Girvan on the Firth of Clyde.  Until 1987 all records were on 
the southern part of the road, between Newton Stewart and Port William, but since then 16 
out of 20 records were between Newton Stewart and Girvan.  The reasons for this change 
were the removal of an assiduous recorder from the southern section and the arrival of the 
author near the northern section in 1987.  Otters were killed in the north before 1987 and have 
been killed on the southern part since, but accurate data are lacking. 
Variation in observer effort made a reliable estimate of death rate or isolation of 
factors predisposing to mortality impossible.  In view of these deficiencies, a twelve-month 
systematic survey of a single road was undertaken, with the primary aim of obtaining a 
minimum estimate of the number of otters killed over a known time and distance and of 
extrapolating from a situation of constant observer effort to one variable effort to obtain an 
estimate of total kill.

2   STUDY AREA 
 
The A75 is a trunk road of 156 kilometres running from the M74 interchange at Gretna to the 
ferry ports on Loch Ryan in south west Scotland.  It formerly traversed, but now bypasses, 
Dumfries (population 37,600) and the smaller towns of Annan (9,000), Castle Douglas 
(3,700) and Newton Stewart (3,700), ending at Stranraer (11,500).  It is in the administrative 
region of Dumfries and Galloway with a human population density of 23.2/square kilometres 
and a ubiquitous and growing otter population.  There is some local traffic, but traffic flows 
are dominated by the Northern Ireland ferries plying out of Loch Ryan.  Three companies 
together ran nearly 300 services a week with capacity for 8,500 cars and 8,000 freight 
vehicles per day.  Most of the ferry traffic passes along the A75, with convoys of heavy 
freight traffic at night.  However, despite the impact of ferry traffic, overall traffic levels are 
low.  The average volume of traffic on rural British trunk roads is 15,600 per day, whereas the 
busiest sections of the A75 has only around 8,000 vehicles, with 4,300 in the quieter parts. 
Although it runs through open countryside and has less than two kilometres subject to 
a 30mph speed limit, the A75 is noted for traffic accidents and speed violations.  Widened and 
straightened stretches alternate with narrow winding sections with poor sight lines.  For most 
of its length it lies below 50 metres above sea level, with a maximum height of 120 metres.  
The A75 runs east to west along the north Solway coast, sometimes alongside the shore and 
never more than 20 kilometres from the sea.  Because the drainage pattern is entirely north to 
south the road crosses every major and most minor watercourses.  It crosses several mosses 
and wetlands, an extensive salt marsh and runs along the shore of a freshwater loch.  More 
than 20 lochs and ponds lie within one kilometre of the road. 
  
   
3   METHODS 
 
The A75 was chosen as it was conveniently located and of a suitable length to be surveyed in 
a single day.  A substantial number of detailed records, dating from 1985, were available, 
amounting to 11% of all Scottish records from that period, although the A75 represents only 
0.3% of the Scottish road network.  It was not known whether this route was intrinsically 
dangerous or whether the situation resulted only from observer effort. 
Two trials were undertaken to test the accuracy of identification of bodies at different 
speeds and to refine data recording.  At 72 kilometres per hour (45mph) it was possible to 
identify undamaged bird and mammal bodies and to spot remains needing closer inspection.  
Trials were used to familiarise the driver with landmarks, place names, lay-bys and parking 
places.  The road was driven in both directions twice monthly from July 1996 to June 1997, at 
45mph, unless speed limits or traffic conditions dictated a slower speed.  Dry, clear days in 
the second and fourth week of each month were chosen to maximise visibility and the 
condition of corpses. 
Data were recorded on a voice-operated tape recorder, the driver keeping up a running 
commentary on road junctions and farm names, noting and identifying as far as possible each 
corpse on one side of the road, and locating it by the nearest landmark.  Otters and badgers, or 
their possible remains, were checked on foot, accurately located, aged, sexed and freshness 
and state of the corpse noted.  Presence or absence and condition of corpses were recorded on 
subsequent drives.  Corpses were not moved, to permit assessment of the survival rates of 
corpses of different sizes and in different parts of the carriageway.  Colleagues using the road 
regularly were apprised of the study and asked to pass on data to assess the scale of multiple 
reporting.

When the drives were completed all mortality records from the A75 were accurately 
mapped, every mortality site was visited and the vicinity checked measured and described. 
 
 
4   RESULTS 
 
4.1. Number of records. 
Before the study started, 103 otter deaths had been recorded in the period from February 1985 
to end of June 1996.  Thirty-eight dead individuals were recorded on 24 drives along the A75, 
a further 12 were recorded during the same time span and ten were recorded up to the end of 
1997.  During the study, nine additional deaths were notified by others, while four were 
recorded by the author.  Of these, 12 disappeared by the time of the next regular survey.  
Despite publicity, only one record from the driving surveys was independently duplicated.  
The total recorded death rate over the survey was 50 otters over 156 kilometres and 12 
months.  The A75 has produced an average recorded death rate of 19.6 per year since 1990, 
with a maximum of 41 in 1996, of which 24 were recorded during the study.  The highest 
yearly total, apart from the study, was 33 in 2001.  Total deaths recorded on the A75 to the 
end of March 2004 is 308.

5   DISCUSSION 
 
5.1. Minimum estimate of otter mortality over known time and distance 
Over 12 months of systematic surveillance 50 otter deaths were recorded on 156 kilometres, 
or a minimum of one death/3.12 kilometres/year.  The systematic recording at two week 
intervals missed 24% of known mortality thus was too infrequent to record all deaths.  
Although A75 traffic is not heavy, it is sufficient to rapidly render otter corpses 
unrecognisable and cause most of them to disappear in less than a fortnight. 
The position of the corpse on the road influenced the length of time it lasted; 22 (47%) 
bodies were found at the side of the road, only nine (41%) of those lasted for 15 days or more, 
but of 25 (53%) found on the carriageway, only four (16%) survived more than one day, with 
a maximum duration of six days.  Thus the majority of otters were killed in a situation where 
their corpses were unlikely to remain visible for more than a few days.  This was borne out by 
a recorder driving 78 kilometres of the A75 daily and recording six dead otters over 14 days 
in 1998, which were whole and freshly dead on his morning run, of these, four were so 
crushed as to be almost unrecognisable by the return evening run, even by someone knowing 
where to look (J.MCCLEARY, personal communication).  
Theoretically, if 38 otters were found in 24 days (1.58/day) over 12 months, the true 
death rate on the A75 could be as high as 577/year.  However if deaths were occurring at this 
rate around 111 bodies would be expected to last 15 days or more and be visible at the side of 
the road (47% of 577=271: 41% of 271= 111.1) and two bodies on the carriageway should 
last long enough to be seen on each drive.  However only three bodies were decomposing

when first found at the side of the road.  If these three represent the 41% of bodies found there 
which last 15 days or more then the number of unrecorded deaths on the A75 is around 
14/year (If 3=41%; n=7: if 7=47%; n=14). 
As the systematic result of 38 deaths is supplemented by 12 other recorded deaths, it is 
concluded that the figure of 50/year is reasonably close to the actual death rate, but such a 
result could only be achievable on roads with as good a network of recorders as has developed 
for the A75. 
 
5.2. Comparison with results from other years on the A75 
The number of deaths recorded per year has increased steadily with yearly averages rising 
from 4.6/year between 1985-1989, to 22.3/year from 2000-2003.  Once extra records 
collected during the survey and the associated surveys of mortality sites are removed, figures 
for 1996 and 1997, follow the overall trend of results and do not suggest that the time span 
chosen had an abnormally high number of deaths. 
 
5.3. Comparability of A75 with other trunk roads 
Before results from the A75 can be used to estimate total mortality on Scottish roads, it must 
be established that the recorded death rate is due to a higher reporting rate, rather than unusual 
features of the road.  Just how atypical the A75 database of 284 records is can be seen from 
the distribution of 1,356 records over 131 A class roads, 90 B class roads, three motorways 
and many minor roads.  Apart from the A75, only 20 roads (8 Trunk roads, 11 A class and 1 
B class) have more than ten records, totalling 533, the maximum being 65.  Of these 20 roads, 
five are on Shetland, one on Skye and one on Mull, all islands where observer effort is high 
because of the presence of people working on otters. The rest are more typical, being trunk or 
A class roads on the mainland, accounting for 292 deaths. 
Three mainland trunk roads with the high numbers of mortalities are the A9, A82 and 
A87, with 65, 38 and 23 respectively.  All three have many topographical features in common 
with the A75 and similar traffic flows.  Until the Skye Bridge opened, the A87 also 
experienced traffic bunching at its western end caused by ferry traffic.  
There is not only a difference in the quantity of records but also in the quality.  Most 
A75 records have come from individuals, usually reporting as the deaths occur and checking 
or sending in corpses, but the majority of records from other roads have been collected by the 
author trawling the collections and databases of museums and other institutions.  Records so 
derived are biased, as only whole, relatively undamaged specimens are involved, representing 
a small proportion of the total kill. Records derived from the files of institutions are 
influenced by the postings of interested staff and management changes.  For example, the 
largest group of A82 records came from Scottish Natural Heritage at Fort William, but 
dwindled when a key officer moved to Inverness.        
The other major difference is the number of records from the author, on the A75 they 
amount to 99 (40%), but on roads rarely travelled such records are sparse.  It is notable that in 
only three journeys on the A9 the deaths of a courting pair and two juveniles within 500 
metres of each other a month apart were recorded (Table 4).  Further evidence that the level of 
reporting is the major factor determining the distribution of known otter mortality comes from 
the A714, mentioned in the introduction, which has the highest number of deaths recorded for 
any mainland A class road 
Such records as are available from the A82, A87 and A9 show similar seasonal trends 
and sex and age ratios to those shown on the A75 and similar geographical clustering of 
reported deaths.

5.3. Estimate of total otter mortality on Scottish roads 
The A75 had one death/3.12 kilometres/year over the period of intensive survey, which was 
estimated to be close to the total kill.  If otter deaths occurred at the same rate on all of 
Scotland’s 53,000 kilometre road network the total death rate would be around 17,000.  
However otters are not killed at the same rate on all road classes.  Extrapolating from the rate 
on the A75, the other 2793 kilometres of trunk roads would be expected to have a yearly 
death rate of 895 otters.  Otter deaths on trunk roads, other than the A75, amount to 336 or 
24.7% of the total database, so if 895 equals 24.7% of the total kill, 100% would be 3,623, 
plus the 50 from the A75 giving an estimate of 3,673 otters killed each year on Scotland’s 
roads.    
There are few systematic data from other mainland roads to check the validity of this 
estimate, except for five deaths in 13.5 kilometres over four months on the A87.  The months 
in question, November to February, account for 37.3% of deaths on the national database and 
13.5 kilometres is 16.9% of the length of the road, so using a similar extrapolation, the death 
rate on the A87 could be as high as 79.3/year or one death/1.01 kilometres/year, which is 
considerably higher than the A75.  It is not known whether these records are typical of the 
A87.  It is possible by arbitrary selection to find only two four-month periods when five 
deaths were recorded in 13.5 kilometres on the A75, and these overlap in time.  This suggests 
that the estimate is in the right order of magnitude. 
HARRIS ET AL. (1995) estimated the Scottish otter population in the mid 1980s to be 
6,600+/-3,300 adults.  The adult death rate (31) on the A75 gives a ratio of one adult 
death/5.03 kilometres/year which would give a figure of 555 adults killed on trunk roads and 
an annual rate of 2,279 adult otters killed in Scotland.  Alternatively the percentage of adults 
on the national database (75.4%) can be used with the estimate of total death rate, to give an 
estimate of 2,771 adults killed per year.  
Taking the higher end (9,900) of the estimated population size as more likely, figures 
of 23% or 28% can be calculated as the level of adult otter death annually.  The impact of a 
cull of this size depends on the levels of other external pressures and also on how the 
mortality is distributed geographically and among sex and age groups within the otter 
population.  Analysis of deaths on the A75 suggests that road mortality bears more heavily on 
young animals and adult males than has been reported before and therefore the estimate of a 
23% adult death rate is considered more probable.
