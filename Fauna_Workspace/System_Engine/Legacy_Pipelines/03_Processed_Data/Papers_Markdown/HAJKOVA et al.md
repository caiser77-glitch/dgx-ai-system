TESTING THE METHODOLOGY OF MICROSATELLITE DNA TYPING OF SPRAINTS 
FOR STUDYING OTTERS IN THE CZECH AND SLOVAK REPUBLICS 
 
Petra HÁJKOVÁ1, 2, Josef BRYJA1, 2, Barbora ZEMANOVÁ1, 2 & Jan ZIMA2 
 
1.  Department of Zoology & Ecology, Faculty of Science, Masaryk University, Kotlářská 2, 611 37 Brno, Czech 
Republic.  email: hajkova@ivb.cz 
  
2.  Institute of Vertebrate Biology, Academy of Sciences of the Czech Republic, Květná 8, 603 65 Brno, Czech 
Republic 
 
 
Abstract.  A project has recently started in the Czech and Slovak Republics that aims to assess otter population 
size and structure in three different habitats through non-invasive microsatellite DNA typing of spraints.  In the 
first phase of this project, tissue samples from otter carcasses were analysed in order to verify if the level of 
microsatellite polymorphism was high enough for individual identification by a non-invasive method. The mean 
observed heterozygosity on five polymorphic microsatellite loci was 0.596 and average number of alleles per 
locus 4.0 for otters from the Czech Republic (n=23), and 0.657 and 3.2 respectively for otters from Slovakia 
(n=7).  The methodology was subsequently tested on very fresh spraints from captive otters. The success rate of 
analysis varied considerably, therefore, comparisons of different types of samples (spraint, spraint with anal 
jelly, anal jelly), two sample preservation and two DNA extraction methods, and three different time points of 
DNA extraction, are now being performed using fresh spraints from wild populations. To date, eight different 
otter genotypes have been identified from a 35 kilometre watercourse in the mountainous area of the Slovenský 
Raj National Park. Preliminary results show that success rate is influenced by type of sample and season (i.e. 
temperature) rather than by time of DNA extraction, sample preservation method or DNA extraction method.  
 
Keywords: Eurasian otter, Lutra lutra, DNA typing, spraints, non-invasive, microsatellites, SRY 
 
 
1   INTRODUCTION 
 
The otter (Lutra lutra) is probably one of the most studied mustelid species in recent years, 
however, many basic population parameters, such as effective population size and sex ratio, 
are still not fully understood.  Over most of its distribution, otters are nocturnal and very 
elusive in their behaviour, and spraints (faeces) usually represent the only available biological 
material.  Spraints contain cells shed from the gut lining, thus DNA from its owner can 
potentially be extracted and analysed.  Genetic analysis of faeces using microsatellite (highly 
variable simple sequence repeats) and SRY (a male specific gene located on the Y 
chromosome) markers can provide identification of individuals, sex, relatedness, estimates of 
population size and the level of genetic polymorphism within or between populations, all 
without direct contact with animals (KOHN AND WAYNE, 1997).  The method, however, has 
limitations resulting either from low DNA quantity, low DNA quality (i.e. degraded DNA) or 
poor extract quality (i.e. PCR inhibitors) (TABERLET, WAITS AND LUIKART, 1999).  These 
factors are responsible for many 'technical' problems and the low success rate of analysis, as 
well as its high time and financial costs.  
 
In the Czech and Slovak Republics, a project has just begun which aims to assess otter 
population size and structure in three different habitats through non-invasive microsatellite 
DNA typing of spraints.  In the first phase of this project, tissue samples from otter carcasses 
were analysed with the aim of assessing the level of microsatellite polymorphism.  
Subsequently, very fresh spraints from captive otters, as well as the first samples from wild 
populations, were analysed with the aim of testing several sample preservation and DNA 
extraction methods, and potentially to improve the methodology and to increase success rate.  
This paper presents the preliminary results of this test.

2   MATERIAL AND METHODS 
 
For testing of the microsatellite polymorphism level, tissue samples from otter carcasses (23 
from the Czech Republic and 7 from Slovakia) were analysed.  The phenol-chloroform 
method was used to extract the DNA (SAMBROOK, FRITSCH AND MANIATIS, 1989).  Extracted 
DNA was used as a template in PCR reactions at five microsatellite loci (Lut701, Lut715, 
Lut717, Lut832, Lut833; DALLAS AND PIERTNEY 1998) and in duplex PCR for Lut-SRY/914 
(DALLAS ET AL., 2000).  Microsatellite PCR products were analysed through fragment analysis 
on an ABI Prism 310 Genetic Analyser, using GeneScan 3.7 software (Applied Biosystems: 
www.appliedbiosystems.com), and by electrophoresis on 2% agarose gels stained with 
ethidium bromide for Lut-SRY/914.  Results are presented as number of alleles per locus and 
observed heterozygosity per locus (Ho). 
 
Subsequently, the methodology was tested on six very fresh spraints from captive 
otters, collected within 15 minutes of deposition and stored in kit buffer.  The DNeasy Tissue 
Kit (Qiagen: www.qiagen.com) was used to extract the DNA, having been used previously for 
DNA extraction from spraints by the Alterra group (JANSMAN, CHANIN AND DALLAS, 2001, 
VAN RIJSWIJK, 2001).  Another ten very fresh spraints from captive otters were analysed, with 
the aim of comparing two commercial kits, the above-mentioned Qiagen tissue kit and the 
Invisorb Spin Stool DNA Kit (Invitek: www.invitek.de).  The success rate of analysis varied 
considerably, therefore, comparisons of different types of samples (spraint, spraint with anal 
jelly, anal jelly), two sample preservation methods (buffers from kits and 96% ethanol), two 
DNA extraction methods (Invisorb Spin Stool DNA Kit (Invitek) and QIAamp Stool DNA 
Mini Kit (Qiagen)), and three different time points of DNA extraction (2 weeks, 2 months, 6 
months), are now being performed using fresh spraints (≤18 hrs) from a wild population 
living in the mountainous area of the Slovenský Raj National Park, Slovakia.  Each fresh 
sample found in the field was gently mixed and collected into four tubes: buffer from the 
Invitek kit, buffer from the Qiagen kit, and twice to 96% ethanol.  The first half of the 
samples were collected in cold weather (February–April; nights -6°C maximum; days 5°C 
maximum) and the second half in warm weather (May; nights 14°C maximum; days 30°C 
maximum).  To date, 32 spraint samples have been analysed: 23 within two weeks of 
collection and nine at two months.  Stringent precautions were taken to avoid contamination. 
DNA extraction from spraints was performed in a flow-box after ultraviolet sterilisation in a 
separate pre-PCR room and negative controls were used at each step to detect possible 
contamination. For PCR, HotMaster Tag DNA polymerase (Eppendorf: www.eppendorf.com) 
was used and analysis was performed using the multiple-tubes approach, in order to reduce 
the possibility of incorrect genotyping due to allelic dropout and false alleles (TABERLET ET 
AL., 1996).  PCR products were visualised using the ABI Prism 310 Genetic Analyser and 
GeneScan 3.7 software, both for microsatellites and SRY/914. PCR success rate was 
calculated as number of positive PCRs (PCR with at least one 'correct' allele). 
 
 
3   RESULTS 
  
Analysis of tissue samples from otter carcasses verified that genotyping on five microsatellite 
loci was sufficient for reliable identification of all tested individuals.  All analysed loci were 
polymorphic, with the number of alleles ranging between two and five (Table 1).  The mean 
observed heterozygosity was 0.596 and average number of alleles per locus 4.0 for otters from 
the Czech Republic, and 0.657 and 3.2 respectively for otters from Slovakia.  The highest

In the first test with fresh spraint samples from captive otters (DNA extracted by the 
DNeasy Tissue Kit (Qiagen)), five of six samples (i.e. 83%) yielded amplifiable otter DNA.  
In the second test, where DNA extraction by the above-mentioned Qiagen tissue kit and the 
Invisorb Spin Stool DNA Kit (Invitek) were compared, the Qiagen tissue kit provided six 
positive samples (i.e. 60%), whilst the Invisorb kit gave positive results for all ten samples 
(i.e. 100%).  However, the number of positive PCRs and frequency of allelic dropout varied 
considerably between samples.  It was therefore decided to perform several other tests using 
fresh spraints collected from a wild otter population.  To date, eight different otter genotypes 
have been identified from a 35 kilometre watercourse in the mountainous area of the 
Slovenský Raj National Park.  When comparing different sample types, storage, DNA 
extraction methods, etc., preliminary results showed that success rate is influenced by type of 
sample (percentage of positive PCRs ca. 80% for anal jelly vs. 10-45% for spraint and spraint 
with jelly), and by season (samples collected in winter ca. 60% vs. ca. 25% in late spring with 
hot weather), rather than by time of DNA extraction (positive PCRs ca. 50% when extracting 
within two weeks vs. ca. 40 % at two months), sample preservation method (range 40-50%), 
or DNA extraction method (Qiagen kit 46%, Invitek kit 42%).  
            
                                                                                                                                                                              
4   DISCUSSION  
 
Analysis of tissue samples from carcasses verified that genotyping at five polymorphic 
microsatellite loci is sufficient for individual identification.  Despite the low number of tissue 
samples available for analysis (especially from Slovakia), the level of heterozygosity appears 
to be high enough, and thus the probability of identity (i.e. two individuals with identical 
genotypes, and therefore non-distinguishable) is expected to be sufficiently low in otter 
populations from the Czech and Slovak Republics.  However, the carcasses analysed were 
collected from a wide area, not only from the areas were spraint DNA typing was to take 
place.  Thus, it is not possible to assess reliably if the observed level of polymorphism could 
be sufficient for intensive individual-level analyses (e.g. determination of relatedness); it is 
probable that typing of more than five loci will be necessary.  For studying population 
structure, as well as for comparing microsatellite variability within other otter populations 
(e.g. DALLAS ET AL., 1999 for Scotland, PERTOLDI ET AL., 2001 for Denmark, DALLAS ET AL., 
2002 for all Britain, RANDI ET AL., 2003 for different European countries), analysis of 
additional samples is necessary.  Therefore, all newly found otter carcasses, as well as all 
available otter tissue samples, should be analysed. Moreover, a further benefit of tissue DNA

analysis is reliable identification of alleles for each locus in the population, very important for 
detection of genotyping errors resulting from false alleles (PCR artefacts) when analysing 
DNA extracted from spraints (TABERLET, WAITS AND LUIKART, 1999). 
 
The results of the test comparing the Qiagen tissue kit and the Invitek stool kit suggest 
that extraction using the faeces kit may give superior results to the tissue kit.  However, the 
cost of such a specialised kit is higher and DNA extraction is much more time consuming. 
The finding that anal jelly samples have a much higher success rate as compared to normal 
spraints is consistent with other authors (COXON ET AL., 1999; VAN RIJSWIJK, 2001).  
However, it is not possible to collect and analyse only anal jelly samples as they are relatively 
rarely found, are probably produced only by adult animals and, generally, very little 
information is know about them (but see GORMAN AND JENKINS, 1978).  The strong effect of 
season can be explained by fast degradation of DNA (higher activity of hydrolytic enzymes) 
in spraint under higher temperatures and could be reduced by collecting samples in cold 
months only, very early in the morning, and by appropriate storage.  It appears that there are 
no significant differences between kit buffers and 96% ethanol as storage material, but it has 
not been statistically tested so far due to the small number of samples analysed. 
 
 
5   CONCLUSIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
 
Based on the preliminary results from tissue samples, it seems that microsatellite 
polymorphism in otter populations in the Czech and Slovak Republics is high enough for 
individual identification using a non-invasive method.  The most variable locus was Lut833 in 
both populations, but all five tested loci are suitable for genotyping. 
 
The proportion of negative PCRs and frequency of allelic dropout is considerably 
higher when comparing spraint samples from wild populations with samples from captive 
otters, suggesting that degradation of otter DNA in spraint is very fast under natural 
conditions.  It is very important; therefore, to collect samples as fresh as possible, and to 
apply the multiple-tubes approach rigorously.  Further, the collecting of more samples (2-3) 
from each spraint and preferable analysis of loci with the highest variability can be useful 
when the amount of otter DNA is limited.  Preliminary results suggest that the success rate is 
influenced considerably by type of sample and season.  We recommend, therefore, that anal 
jelly samples are collected whenever possible, and that as much of the jelly part as possible is 
taken from spraint samples.  The samples should preferably be collected in cold months, 
placed in cooling boxes in the field, and transferred to a freezer at -20°C as soon as possible.
