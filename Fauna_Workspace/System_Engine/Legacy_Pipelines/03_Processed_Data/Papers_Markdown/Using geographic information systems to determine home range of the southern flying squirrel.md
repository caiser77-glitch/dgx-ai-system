 Using Geographic Information Systems To Determine Home
 Range Of The Southern Flying Squirrel (Glaucomys volans)
 KAREN D. STONE1, GARY A. HEIDT AND PAUL T. CASTER
 Department of Biology, University of Arkansas at Little Rock,
 Little Rock 72204
 MICHAEL L. KENNEDY
 Division of Ecology and Organismal Biology, Department of Biology,
 University of Memphis, Memphis, Tennessee 38152
 ABSTRACT.-Home ranges of 12 southern flying squirrels (Glaucomys volans) were studied
 in the Ouachita Mountains of west-central Arkansas during the winters of 1990-1991 and
 1991-1992. Because traditional planimetric home ranges do not account for differences in
 topography, a Geographic Information System (GIS) which incorporated topography was also
 used to determine home range. Mean female and male planimetric and (topographical)
 home-range estimates were 3.8 ha (3.9 ha) and 7.8 ha (9.0 ha), respectively. Differences in
 home-range size were not statistically significant between sexes, however, home-range esti-
 mates of males were more variable. Topographic estimates were significantly larger than
 planimetric estimates. The GIS more clearly represents home range with respect to habitat
 characteristics in the context of topographic features and it should be used in future studies.
 INTRODUCTION
 Although investigators often calculate home ranges, the significance of these values and
 configurations may not be biologically relevant without an understanding of the various
 features of the home range. Geographic Information Systems (GIS) can be used to explore
 the relationship among home range and differences in attributes such as topography or
 habitat characteristics. Johnson (1990, 1993) noted that, due to a number of factors, ecol-
 ogists have been rather slow to use GIS including the perception that GIS: (1) is simply a
 map utility (2) requires expensive and user-unfriendly software and (3) lacks appropriate
 interfaces with quantitative tools such as statistical testing. However, with software updates
 and increased usage by other disciplines, acceptance of GIS technology among animal ecol-
 ogists is growing.
 GIS can be used to elucidate complex spatial problems that are difficult to resolve with
 traditional means. For example, GIS can incorporate changes in the slope of topography
 into estimates of home-range size. Most investigations have focused only on the horizontal
 component of landscape (planimetric area) where the slope of the terrain is assumed to
 be zero. Topography adds an important element to landscape because the slope of terrain
 often fluctuates throughout an individual's home range. Because changes in topography
 can increase surface area, techniques such as GIS that account for topographic changes
 should increase the accuracy of estimates of home-range size and provide information re-
 lated to possible use of available terrain.
 Investigations of home range in the southern flying squirrel (Glaucomys volans) have only
 used planimetric area (e.g., Madden, 1974; Gilmore and Gates, 1985; Bendel and Gates,
 1987; Fridell and Litvaitis, 1991); therefore, the effect of topography on measures of home-
 range size in this species is unknown. In addition, studies of flying squirrels in the south-

 We investigated aspects of natural history related to home range (winter home-range size
 and den sites) of southern flying squirrels in Arkansas. We also used GIS to examine the
 effect of terrain on measurements of home-range size in this population. Although the ca-
 pabilities of GIS are vast, we addressed only the ability of GIS to incorporate slope fluctuations
 to test the hypothesis that topography had no effect on estimates of home-range size.
 STUDY AREA
 The study was conducted in the U.S. Forest Service Camp Clearfork within the Ouachita
 Mountains, 3 km W of Crystal Springs, Garland Co., Arkansas. These mountains comprise
 a series of east-west ridges separated by narrow valleys; ridges rise to an elevation 280 m
 above mean sea level, and valleys dip to 207 m above mean sea level. The composition of
 this 70-yr-old forest is approximately 70% shortleaf pine (Pinus echinata) and 30% hard-
 wood dominated by oaks (Quercus spp.) with sweetgum (Liquidambar styraciflua) , blackgum
 (Nyssa sylvatica) and hickory (Carya spp.) interspersed. The understory is open and is
 primarily flowering dogwood (Cornus florida), American holly (Ilex opaca) and poison ivy
 (Rhus radicans). See Stone et al. (1996) for general habitat characteristics.
 METHODS
 As part of a long-term ecological study of the southern flying squirrel (Stone, 1993; Caster
 et al., 1994; Stone et al., 1996), two grids (Areas A and B) each consisting of 30 nest boxes
 were established during March 1990. Both grids were located in similar habitat. Boxes,
 placed 35 m apart in three rows of 10, were on hardwoods (n = 40) and pines (n = 20)
 at heights of 3-4.5 m. Box design was modified from Heidt (1977), with inside dimensions
 measuring approximately 25 X 23 X 23 cm and entrance holes measuring either 3.8 or 6.4
 cm in diameter. Because flying squirrels consistently used these boxes for winter nesting
 sites, they provided an easy and reliable means to capture squirrels.
 In November 1990, eleven female flying squirrels were radio-collared with transmitters
 (SMI model with 3-4 month lithium battery life, AVM Instrument Co., Livermore, CA
 94550). Six radio-collared squirrels were located in Area A and five in Area B. Because of
 mortality and battery failure, data used in subsequent analyses were collected from only
 three squirrels in Area A (All, 23, 37) and four in Area B (B4, 6, 9, 11). Squirrels were
 tracked from mid-November 1990 through mid-March 1991 (or until battery failure). This
 period was chosen because it spanned the winter breeding season as well as the period of
 maximum squirrel aggregations (generally 6-12 squirrels per aggregation). Activity, time
 and location of the squirrel were recorded for each telemetry fix. Squirrels were tracked
 to identify exact locations of den trees, or if they were foraging, to find the area of activity
 (within 20 m).
 In December 1991, seven male and three female flying squirrels from Area A were radio-
 collared in the same manner as in 1990. Data used in subsequent analyses were collected
 from five males (A6, 20, 32, 44, 52). Activity of squirrels was monitored from early December
 1991 through late March 1992.
 Tracking was conducted during both inactive and active periods, usually beginning at
 sunset and ending at midnight or beginning at midnight and ending at sunrise. Because
 battery life was limited, multiple locations were collected in short periods of time while the
 squirrels were active (resulting in variable numbers of locations per animal per night). Thus,
 locations were not considered independent, and no statistical methods of determining
 home-range size were used.

 Locations for each squirrel were plotted on United States Geological Survey (U.S.G.S.)
 topographic maps and the minimum-convex polygon method (Mohr, 1947) was used to
 delineate home-range polygons. Polygons were digitized into ARC/INFO (Environmental
 Systems Research Institute, Redlands, CA) mapping-database management system to deter-
 mine planimetric area. Contour intervals from a U.S.G.S. map (scale: 1:24000) were also
 digitized into ARC/INFO, and topographical images of the study area were produced using
 TIN, an ARC/INFO three-dimensional mapping program. Home-range polygons were over-
 laid on the topographical images and topographical areas were computed.
 All statistical analyses were conducted using Statistical Analysis Systems (SAS; SAS Insti-
 tute, 1989) with significance level set at P = 0.05. Both planimetric and topographical home-
 range estimates were calculated for 12 squirrels (seven females: All, 23, 37, B4, 6, 9, 11
 and five males: A6, 20, 32, 44, 52) and the percent difference between the two methods
 was compared using a paired-comparisons t-test (SAS Institute, 1989). Male and female
 home-range sizes were compared using a modified Student's t-test that corrects for unequal
 variances (SAS Institute, 1989); variances were compared using an F-test. Although data
 were collected for 2 years, all squirrels monitored were present in the population through-
 out the study. Measurements are reported as mean ?SE.

 Squirrels alternated between active and inactive periods from within 1 h of sunset and
 sunrise; squirrels first became active outside dens approximately 1 h after sunset. Although
 squirrels were occasionally observed foraging on the ground, they generally glided quickly
 (often 20-40 m at a time) from nesting sites to feeding stations (characterized by middens),
 where they spent most of their non-nest time. Squirrels were never found outside their nests
 during the day.
 Den trees were often on the periphery of the home range, with escape trees and feeding
 stations located throughout. An escape tree was characterized as a place that provided
 squirrels with a refuge but on which no nest or middens were found. Squirrels used on
 average of 5.3 ? 0.3 (n = 12) den sites during the study. Den sites, including nest boxes
 and natural cavities, were sites in which flying squirrels were found during the day. An
 average of 54.2 ? 6.5% (n = 12) of the den sites were established in nest boxes, with the
 remainder located in snags or living trees with natural cavities. On two occasions, nests were
 established at ground level in the base of trees. One outside nest of twigs, leaves and pine
 needles was located in the branches of a pine tree.
 DISCUSSION
 Previous estimates of home ranges of southern flying squirrels vary from 0.4 ha (Madden,
 1974) to 3.4 ha (Fridell and Litvaitis, 1991) for females and 0.5 ha (Madden, 1974) to 9.9
 ha (Fridell and Litvaitis, 1991) for males. Estimates of home ranges determined from our
 study were within this range.
 Although we did not find a significant difference between the size of male and female
 home ranges, other studies have reported that males have larger home ranges (Bendel and
 Gates, 1987; Fridell and Litvaitis, 1991). The size of male home ranges is also more variable
 than female home ranges (Fridell and Litvaitis, 1991; present study). Population demog-
 raphy and social strategies may be important in intersexual variance in home-range size;
 therefore, in addition to differences in mean male and female home-range sizes, future
 studies of home-range dynamics should address intersexual variance in home-range size.
 Due to discrepancies in results among studies investigating territoriality (e.g., Madden,
 1974; Bendel and Gates, 1987), intrasexual home-range overlap should be examined fur-
 ther. Because only a small proportion of the total squirrel population is included in most
 studies (the present study included), calculations of core-activity area and home-range over-
 lap can be deceiving. Depending upon which and how many squirrels are examined, various
 conclusions may be drawn. It should be noted that the presence of territoriality cannot be
 demonstrated unless a high percentage of animals of a population are included.
 Flying squirrels are strictly nocturnal (Schwartz and Schwartz, 1981; Bendel and Gates,
 1987) and we did not locate any squirrels outside nests during the day. While denning,
 squirrels used nest boxes extensively, perhaps because boxes were large enough for aggre-
 gations. The effect of these boxes on home-range size is unknown. In agreement with

 Topography significantly increased estimates of home-range size; the supposition (made
 by planimetric methods) that the slope of the terrain equals zero is not valid. This study
 demonstrates the potential utility of using GIS.to determine the effect of topography on
 home range. In addition to more accurately determining home-range size, GIS provides a
 more spatially-explicit description of home range in context of topographic features (Figs.
 lA-IB); and this technique should be used in future studies. The GIS approach can be
 easily applied to most species; and as improvements in software continue, many diverse
 ecological applications using the same data base are possible. For example, Nicholson et al.
 (in press) used GIS to analyze habitat and diversity of terrain in a study of mule deer.
