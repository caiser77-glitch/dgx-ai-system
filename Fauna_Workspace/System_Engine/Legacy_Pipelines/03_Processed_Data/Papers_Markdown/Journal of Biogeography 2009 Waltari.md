ABSTRACT
Aim The goal of this study was to determine the extent of suitable habitats across
the basins and ranges of the Great Basin for 13 montane mammals in the present
and during the Last Glacial Maximum (LGM). For all these mammal species, we
test whether: (1) more suitable habitat was available in basin areas during the
LGM; (2) suitable habitat shifted upwards in elevation between the LGM and the
present; (3) more ranges have suitable habitat than are currently occupied; and
(4) these species are currently restricted to suitable habitats at higher-elevation
range areas. We also examine whether and how much distributional response
varies among these montane mammal species.
Location The Great Basin of western North America.
Methods We re-examine the past and present distributions of 13 Great Basin
montane mammals using ecological niche modelling techniques that utilize now
widely available species occurrence data and new, ﬁne-scale past climatological
GIS layers in the present and at the LGM. These methods provide an objective,
repeatable
means
for
visual
comparison
of
past
and
present
modelled
distributions for species examined in previous biogeographical studies.
Results Our results indicate greater areal and lower elevational suitable habitat
in the LGM than at present for nearly all montane mammals, and that there is
more suitable habitat at present than is currently occupied. Our results also
show that lowland areas provide suitable dispersal routes between ranges for
most of the montane mammals both at the LGM and at present. However,
three of the 13 species have little to no predicted suitable habitat in the
LGM near currently occupied ranges, in contrast to the pattern for the other
10. For these species, the model results support more recent long-distance
colonization.
Main conclusions Our ﬁnding of suitable lowland dispersal routes in the
present for most species supports and greatly extends similar ﬁndings from
single-species studies. Our results also provide a visually striking conﬁrmation
that changes in species distribution and colonization histories of Great Basin
montane mammals vary in a fashion related to the tolerances and requirements of
each of these species; this has previously been hypothesized but not rigorously
tested for multiple montane mammals in the region.
Keywords
Climate change, ecological niche modelling, Great Basin, insular biogeography,
Last Glacial Maximum, montane mammals, North America, species distribution.

INTRODUCTION
Brown’s (1971, 1978) seminal papers on the insular biogeog-
raphy of montane mammals in the Great Basin of western
North America showed a depauperate fauna as compared with
predicted values based on species–area relationships. In order
to explain the results, Brown postulated a simple but at the
time untested model, in which formerly widely distributed
species were vicariantly isolated onto mountain peaks as
climates warmed from the Late Pleistocene to the present. He
further postulated that, after these mammals had been isolated
on mountaintops, there were extinctions but no colonizations.
Figure 1 shows this pattern of range fragmentation resulting
from warming.
The aim in this paper is not to re-examine Brown’s
empirical ﬁndings. Other workers have more completely
sampled the Great Basin (Lawlor, 1998; Grayson, 2006) and
shown that the montane mammal fauna is not as depauperate
as Brown initially suggested. What remain untested for most
mammal species in the Great Basin are a number of questions
regarding species distribution shifts from the Last Glacial
Maximum (LGM) to the present. The ﬁrst question is whether,
at the LGM, at 21 ka (18 ka in carbon-dating years), there was
suitable habitat that spanned basins and ranges such that
mammals were able to colonize all or nearly all of the Great
Basin lowlands and ranges. A related second question is
whether this habitat was generally lower in elevation than
currently occupied habitat. Another question is whether
extreme warming events such as the mid-Holocene Climatic
Optimum at 6 ka led to local extinctions from some ranges,
resulting in more ranges with suitable habitat than are
currently occupied. A ﬁnal question is whether populations
are isolated on mountain tops at present, preventing gene ﬂow
between populations. These questions relate not just to the
changing environment of the Great Basin but also to the
physiological
tolerances
of
the
species
themselves.
This
suggests the importance of an approach that examines multiple
montane taxa simultaneously.
The existing evidence addressing the ﬁrst two questions
above comes directly from the fossil record, which shows that
some Great Basin montane mammal species were more
widespread and at lower elevations during the LGM than they
are at present. For example, the American Pika, Ochotona
princeps, was found at elevations 800 m below current known
elevations as recently as 12 ka (Grayson, 2005). Guralnick
(2007) used the Faunmap data base of fossil occurrences
(Graham et al., 1994) to show that such elevational shifts were
common for cold-adapted mammals at southern range edges,
such as those found in montane areas in the Great Basin. Since
mammals were probably found at lower elevations during the
LGM, it is presumed that they were in more continuous
habitat, with multiple corridors across the Great Basin, as
opposed to being isolated onto mountaintops with intervening
poor habitat (Grayson, 1993, 2006).
Despite the relatively high quality of the mammal fossil
record in the Great Basin, it has still been difﬁcult to obtain a
complete picture of past distributions using fossil data alone.
There are two reasons for this. First, the dating of fossils is
often imprecise, making it difﬁcult to say with certainty if a
fossil dates from the height of the last glaciation or from the
rapid warming that commenced afterwards. Perhaps more
importantly, there is also variation in sampling intensity
among species. Whereas a few taxa, such as Ochotona princeps
(Grayson, 2005) and the yellow-bellied marmot (Marmota
ﬂaviventris), have been relatively well sampled in the Great
Basin fossil record, most other groups are still only poorly
sampled at or around the LGM (Grayson, 1993). It is difﬁcult
to determine if the differential sampling results from a bias in
collections and in our ability to discriminate species in the
fossil record, or if it reﬂects actual absolute patterns of
occurrence and abundance. Ultimately, the fossil record is
complete enough to verify that a few montane and alpine Great
Basin mammal species were found in lowland areas in the
Great Basin during the Late Pleistocene (Grayson, 1993), but
not sufﬁcient to enable the LGM palaeodistributions of those
species to be fully reconstructed, except in exceptional cases.
For the majority of the montane mammal species in the Great
Basin, the presumption of a continuous lowland distribution is
untested owing to a lack of fossil occurrence data.
Until recently, our inability to reconstruct palaeodistribu-
tions for multiple species has made it difﬁcult to determine
how similar or different responses to climate and landscape
change were for those species. Grayson (2006) has argued
that these responses depend on the unique tolerances and
requirements of each species. Mountain-dwelling species in
the Great Basin vary in their elevational ranges and habitat
requirements in the present, and these present-day differences
suggest strongly that each species may have had different

Another approach to determining palaeo- and modern
distributions of extant species is the use of ecological niche
modelling (ENM). ENM relates known occurrences of species
to data describing landscape-level variation in environmental
parameters of importance to species’ distributional ecology,
creating models that can be used to predict potential
distributional patterns for the species in the present (e.g.
Pearson et al., 2007), future (e.g. Hijmans & Graham, 2006),
and past (e.g. Peterson et al., 2004; Waltari et al., 2007). These
predictions are more robust if two assumptions are met. The
ﬁrst assumption is that a species’ distribution is mainly
determined by the environment, and not by other factors such
as competition or predation. The second assumption is that a
species has conserved its niche (Peterson, 2003) over time (for
example from the LGM to the present) and space, which
appears to hold true over a variety of case studies (e.g. Peterson
et al., 1999; Peterson & Holt, 2003; Wiens & Graham, 2005;
Martı´nez-Meyer & Peterson, 2006), and especially in moun-
tain-dwelling taxa (Guralnick & Pearman, in press). If these
assumptions are met, ENMs can be projected onto palaeocli-
mate reconstructions to identify past potential distributions
(Hugall et al., 2002; Martı´nez-Meyer et al., 2004). Waltari
et al. (2007) have shown that ENM approaches provide
consistent and spatially explicit predictions of Pleistocene
refugia, compared with predictions from gene distributions
across landscapes.
An important pitfall with ENM approaches has been that the
spatial resolution of modelled LGM climates has until recently
been coarse (Waltari et al., 2007). Such coarse-resolution
climate data obscure sharp environmental gradients such as
those present in the topographically diverse Great Basin.
However, workers have now developed ﬁner-resolution, GIS-
based global interpolations of LGM climate parameters,
permitting a more detailed picture of LGM environments
(R. J. Hijmans, pers. comm.). In addition, GIS-based palaeo-
reconstructions of major hydrological features that were
present in the Great Basin during the Late Pleistocene but
are now absent (Fig. 2) are now available. Finally, marked
improvements have been made in sharing and distributing
modern species occurrence data that are used in constructing
ENMs (Edwards et al., 2000; Guralnick et al., 2007).
The use of ENM approaches that combine high-quality
environmental, climatic and species occurrence data allows us
to create a predictive model of present and past distributions
on the current and LGM landscape of the Great Basin,
respectively. Models of past distributions provide a means to
test whether montane mammals colonized all or nearly all of
the Great Basin ranges via the lowlands, and thus whether
suitable habitat surrounded more ranges than are currently
occupied. In addition, the present-day distributional models
used to generate predicted palaeodistributions are themselves
useful for testing whether, currently, lowland areas in the Great
Basin really are unsuitable habitats that prevent colonization
between ranges and that limit gene ﬂow. Although Lawlor
(1998), Grayson & Madsen (2000) and Floyd et al. (2005)
concluded that current dispersal can occur between mountain
ranges, Lawlor (1998) did not explicitly test this, and Grayson
& Madsen (2000) and Floyd et al. (2005) focused on single
species. The ENM approach we utilize here allows us explicitly
to examine this question in many more species than has been
possible before.

Basin mammals, making the following predictions: (1) more

MATERIALS AND METHODS
Environmental data
For ENM development, we used current and LGM monthly
climate data at 2.5’ (4 · 4 km) spatial resolution. Waltari et al.
(2007) and Peterson & Nya´ri (2008) discuss the process of
layer development more fully. In brief, LGM climate data were
based on two general circulation model (GCM) simulations:
the Community Climate System Model (CCSM) (Collins et al.,
2004); and the Model for Interdisciplinary Research on
Climate (MIROC, version 3.2) (Hasumi & Emori, 2004;
http://www.pmip2.cnrs-gif.fr). Each model was initially gen-
erated at a spatial resolution of 2.8, or roughly 300 · 300 km.
Outputs from these models were further processed by inter-
polating differences between LGM and recent (pre-industrial)
conditions based on the WorldClim (http://www.worldclim.
org/) data set to create monthly climate surfaces at 2.5’
(4 · 4 km) spatial resolution.
Ecological niche modellings for both the present and the
LGM were based on the 19 bioclimatic variables in the
WorldClim data set (Hijmans et al., 2005). These variables
represent summaries of means and variation in temperature
and precipitation, and characterize the dimensions of climate
considered particularly relevant in determining species distri-
butions. Present-day ENMs were developed within one of two
masks: ‘North’, 30–180W, 30–85N and ‘West’, 100–130W,
20–60N.

Modern occurrence data
We chose 13 montane or alpine Great Basin mammals that
have been extensively used by other workers (Brown, 1971;
Grayson, 1993, 2006; Lawlor, 1998) to examine biogeographi-
cal patterns (Table 1). We chose to exclude the short-tailed
weasel, Mustela erminea, because its extent includes areas
outside North America, and this large extent makes palaeo-
distributional modelling less tractable. The vagrant shrew,
Sorex vagrans, as used in older studies included what is now
recognized as the separate species S. monticolus. Although the
latter species is more alpine than S. vagrans sensu stricto, it is
not found in the Great Basin ranges examined in the studies
above (Smith & Belk, 1996), and therefore we use only
S. vagrans. We used networked biodiversity information
systems of natural history collection data (e.g. MaNIS and
other DiGIR providers; Stein & Wieczorek, 2004) to collate
georeferenced species occurrence information (3721 total
records used) from multiple repositories of natural history
collections for the focal taxa, thus drawing on data from
numerous institutions. Table 1 provides a list of the taxa and
number of georeferenced occurrences from these repositories;
a list of institutions from which material was gathered is
available in Appendix S1 in Supporting Information).
When processing records downloaded from natural history
collections, we ﬁrst removed duplicate records for the same
species collected at the same site. To avoid basing ENMs on
imprecise occurrence data, only records with a radius of
geographical uncertainty of less than 5 km (Wieczorek et al.,
2004) were retained for analysis. We utilized the Biogeomancer
workbench
(http://bg.berkeley.edu/latest)

records that did not contain geographical uncertainty mea-
surements, retaining only records with uncertainty less than
5 km extent (see Guralnick et al., 2006 for more on Biogeo-
mancer extent calculations).

ENM approaches
Recent studies have advised a consensus approach in ENM
development, in which multiple algorithms are used to infer
niche dimensions (Arau´jo et al., 2005; Pearson et al., 2006,
2007). As a result, we applied both the maxent (Phillips et al.,
2006) and the garp (Stockwell & Peters, 1999) algorithms to
construct ENMs. Both programs generate ENMs using only
presence records, contrasting them with pseudo-absence or
background data resampled from the remainder of the study
area. In each case, we developed present-day ENMs, and then
projected the ENM to LGM conditions.
For maxent (version 3.0b; Phillips et al., 2006), we used the
default convergence threshold (10)5) and maximum number
of iterations (1000) values, using 25% of localities for model
testing. We let the program select both suitable regularization
values and functions of environmental variables automatically,
which it achieves based on considerations of sample size.
maxent outputs a continuous probability value, ranging from
0 to 100, an indicator of relative suitability for the species,
based on the principle of maximum entropy, as constrained by
the input occurrence data.
We also used Desktop garp (version 1.1.6; Stockwell &
Peters, 1999) to construct ENMs. For each species, we created
100 random replicate garp models, using the default param-
eters for the convergence limit (0.01) and maximum iterations
(1000), and setting the training percentage to 75% in order to
be consistent with maxent settings. To select the best ENMs
from among the replicate model runs, we followed Anderson
et al. (2003) in prioritizing low-omission models for further
consideration (the 20% of replicate models showing the lowest
extrinsic omission error), and then retaining the central 50%
of the distribution of areas predicted present to avoid models
showing high commission error rates.
Thresholds and model combination
Given two LGM climate reconstructions and two ENM
algorithms, we have potentially two present-day and four
LGM reconstructions of suitable conditions for each species.
For the purposes of the present analysis, we opted to seek
consensus among different LGM projections for each species.
Hence, to reconcile among the model outputs, we ﬁrst chose
two thresholds for garp (raw output ranged as integers from
0 to 10) and maxent (raw output ranged as a real numbers
from 0 to 100) results, representing different levels of model
agreement. For the less stringent model agreement, we used a
modiﬁed lowest-presence threshold (LPT; Pearson et al., 2007),
in which we used the maxent or garp value that resulted in
95% of all occurrences in the training data sets falling into
suitable habitat, or a 5% omission rate, abbreviated as LPT95%.
These values are still more conservative than those used in
recent studies (Pearson et al., 2007; Waltari et al., 2007). For an
even more stringent threshold, we used a threshold of nine or
greater for garp (G9) and a threshold of 50 for maxent (M50).
These algorithm thresholds identiﬁed smaller areas than a
lowest-presence threshold that yielded zero omission error, thus
resulting in more restricted pictures of potential LGM distri-
butions. We chose this higher threshold in part because work by
Waltari et al. (2007) suggests that less stringent thresholding
may still overpredict LGM refugial areas compared with results
from phylogeographical analyses. An LPT95% threshold may
also be warranted, given that some sampled populations may
represent population sinks, and therefore are not located in
long-term suitable areas. Application of these thresholds creates
a map that has three categories: high consensus presence (M50/
G9), lower consensus presence (LPT95%), or predicted
absence.
In order to generate a ﬁnal summary LGM model, we ﬁrst
averaged together the CCSM and MIROC climate model
outputs from the individual garp and maxent runs, creating a
single consensus climate model generated from each algo-
rithm. Then we applied the two thresholds above to each of
these outputs. In order to combine garp and maxent outputs
into summary LGM and present-day models, we kept only
those areas that both algorithms agreed were suitable (equiv-
alent to an ‘and’ operation in joining the layers) for the higher
threshold. In keeping with our aim to examine a broad
spectrum of ENM predictions, for the lower threshold we kept
areas predicted by either of the algorithms, equivalent to an
‘or’ operation. In addition, we determined the quality of model
outputs by examining area under receiver operating curve
(AUC) scores, and determined overall omission rates when
LGM model outputs were combined.
Final maps were constructed for each species by overlaying the
predicted present-day and LGM palaeodistribution maps onto
the topographical and, in the case of the LGM models,
palaeohydrology maps. We assumed that the mammals used
here could not have occurred in lakes, and masked any suitable
areas found there. For the maps of each species’ distributions, we
coloured Great Basin mountain range areas above 2100 m black
(Fig. 3) if the species did not occur in that range in the present
(as per Lawlor, 1998). Ranges occupied at present are coloured
red instead. Finally, locations of LGM fossil occurrences are
shown as closed black circles on the maps for comparison with
predictions from the palaeodistributional modelling.

and climatic layers in the past and present, these represent
independent data sets for comparison (Table 2).
We compared present and LGM niche-modelling results
under two thresholds with the current distribution of montane
mammals in 16 Great Basin ranges as studied by Lawlor
(1998). Table 3 provides an overall summary of the results. For
the present day, the table shows the number of predicted
suitable Great Basin ranges for each species based on the
ENMs, along with current distribution numbers from Lawlor
(1998). These data are also used to examine the amount of
suitable habitat between ranges. To do this, we visually
examined each species’ present-day modelled suitable habitats
within the 15 basins that connect the 16 Great Basin ranges,
counting the number of basin transects that have a continuous
path of suitable habitat. We binned the results into three
categories: 10–15 connected basins indicates support for a
prediction of dispersal at present across lowland areas; 1–9
connected basins indicates marginal support; and zero con-
nected basins indicates lack of support for this prediction.
To examine if suitable habitat at the LGM was widespread
across lower-elevation areas, we determined if there were
suitable habitats (deﬁned as one or more pixels considered
suitable under the thresholding applied) in basin areas
located within 5 km of each of the 16 ranges for each
species. We also examined whether there are more ranges
predicted to be suitable habitats than are currently occupied
at present. To do this, we determined if there were any
predicted suitable habitats (as deﬁned above) higher than
2100 m a. s. l. based on the current-day models in each
range for species and compared that to the actual occur-
rences from Lawlor (1998).
RESULTS
Overall, the two ENM thresholds predicted somewhat different
areas where species could be found for both the present and
the LGM. Retrodicting these models to the LGM resulted in
suitable habitat shifting to lower elevations (Table 1), as
predicted for all species except the American water shrew,
Sorex palustris. The average elevation in the present across
the 13 species was 1841 m, and it was 1652 m in the LGM at
the lower (LPT95%) threshold (Table 1). The areal extent of
the predictions varied greatly by threshold, however, with the
extent of high-threshold suitable habitat much less (e.g. Fig. 3)
in both the present and LGM modelled distributions.
Correspondence with fossils was available for nine of the 13
species, given our relatively stringent requirements concerning
the timeframe in which the fossil was deposited (14–25 ka). Of
the total of 40 fossil records, 28 were located in suitable habitat
under the lower threshold. Only ﬁve of the 40 fossil
occurrences were found within the predicted suitable habitat
at the higher threshold (Table 2), probably in part owing to
the high stringency of this threshold and to the reduced LGM
area that was considered suitable. In further tests of the model
quality, the ENM present models were strong in their
predictive power: maxent AUC values ranged from 0.966 to
0.998 (mean 0.984). Combining garp and maxent data sets
utilizing an ‘or’ function for the less conservative threshold led
to a reduction in the total omission rate. Total omission scores
when combining data layers ranged from 0 to 1.6% with a
mean of 0.9%.
Maps of ENM predictions at both thresholds, for both
present and LGM time periods, are shown for four of the 13
species in Fig. 4. All species distribution predictions are
available in Appendix S2. ENM prediction data as they pertain
to suitability on (present) or surrounding (LGM) Great Basin
ranges and their support for our predictions are summarized
in Table 3.
For present ENM predictions, both thresholds predict more
ranges having suitable habitat than are currently occupied,
with the average number of ranges occupied presently and
predicted under lower and higher thresholds being 11.4, 16.0
and 13.3, respectively (Table 3). At the LGM, we predicted
suitable habitat in basins surrounding ranges, and that these
habitats would have shifted towards higher elevations and onto
the ranges during the latest Pleistocene and Holocene warm-
ing. The number of suitable basin habitats surrounding ranges
at the LGM depends on the threshold applied: 13.5 at the lower
threshold but only 5.4 at the higher threshold (Table 3). LGM
habitat predictions under different thresholds vary more in
areal extent than those in the present. This difference can be
seen visually in the examples in Fig. 4.

DISCUSSION
Ecological niche modelling provides a quantitative, repeatable,
inexpensive method to determine and visualize current and
past areas of suitable habitats for multiple species that may
have different habitat requirements and thus different magni-
tudes of response to climatic changes (see Kozak et al., 2008;
Swenson, 2008, for reviews). The power of this method is, in
part, that the models are geographically explicit; that is, the
model outputs provide a visual model of potential geograph-
ical distributions at various points in time, which is often the
core information needed to test biogeographical hypotheses.
Here we are able to use these modelled distributions, as
opposed to just species occurrences, to determine how
widespread Great Basin mammal species were at the LGM
and are at present across the area. Based on previous work by
Lawlor (1998) and Grayson (1993, 2006), we made the
following set of predictions: (1) all species were widespread
across lowland areas in the Great Basin at the LGM; (2) species
probably shifted their ranges to higher elevations as warming
commenced from the LGM to the present; (3) in the present,
more ranges have suitable habitat than are currently occupied
as a result of extinctions during extreme past events; and
(4) despite warming, most montane species can still utilize
suitable habitat in lowland areas to disperse between mountain
ranges in the present. As discussed in more detail below, our
results are broadly concordant with these predictions. How-
ever, one of the main and novel ﬁndings from this work is that
no prediction was uniformly supported across all 13 species.
Through our species distributional modelling approach, we are
easily able to document these exceptions, which in most cases
have not been discussed previously in the literature. This
documentation ultimately provides a strong framework for
further hypothesis testing.

Concordance between fossil occurrences and LGM
predictions
A ﬁrst test of our ENM predictions of LGM species distribu-
tions is to compare them with fossil occurrences from the same
period. At the lower threshold used (LPT95%), 28 of 40 fossils
were found in predicted suitable habitat, and two of 12 fossil
occurrences not found in suitable habitats were within 15 km
of predicted suitable habitat. For those species with more than
two occurrences, only Sorex palustris had poor matches
between fossil occurrences and suitable habitat at the less
restrictive threshold (Table 2). However, as Guralnick &
Pearman (in press) note, species that range into less moun-
tainous lands in eastern North America show less niche
conservatism. Of all the species included here, only S. palustris
currently occurs in eastern North America, and only the ﬁve
fossil occurrences in the eastern part of its range do not match
with predicted suitable areas. Our conclusion, therefore, is that
in western North America niche conservatism is strongly
supported at the 95% LPT threshold. At the higher threshold
(M50/G9), only ﬁve of 40 fossils were in predicted suitable
habitat, suggesting that this threshold is underpredicting
habitat suitability. Given these results, we place more weight
on the less conservative thresholds, where the match to fossil
occurrences is much better.
Tests of current and past connections between Great
Basin ranges
In Figs 3 and 4 we show ﬁve of the 13 montane Great Basin
mammal species that typify our results. Seven of the 13 species
are known to be in 13 or more of the 16 Great Basin ranges as
shown in Lawlor (1998), and so we expect these species to be
predicted in nearly all the ranges now, and surrounding all of the
ranges at the LGM. Typical results are seen in our summary
maps of the bushy-tailed woodrat, Neotoma cinerea (Fig. 3). For
present ENM predictions we see all ranges as having suitable
habitat at both thresholds. However, both less and more
conservative thresholds show extensive portions of the lowlands
between ranges also having suitable habitat for these wide-
ranging species. These results are consistent with the study of
Floyd et al. (2005), who found that gene ﬂow (and hence
dispersal) was likely in Marmota ﬂaviventris, and with the
ﬁndings of Grayson & Madsen (2000), who showed that
Neotoma cinerea occurred in low-elevation sites in the more
recent past and that the species may still be using lowland routes.
Ecological niche modelling results at both thresholds simi-
larly suggest that many lowland routes are also available at
present for the golden-mantled ground squirrel (Spermophilus
lateralis), the mountain cottontail (Sylvilagus nuttallii), the cliff
chipmunk (Tamias dorsalis) and the Uinta chipmunk (Tamias
umbrinus). Using the higher threshold, all four of these species
have fewer basin connections (ranging from 4 to 10) than for
the lower threshold. The present ENM result for the long-tailed
vole (Microtus longicaudus) differs at the higher threshold,
although at the lower threshold it is typical in having
continuous suitable habitat throughout the Great Basin.
Although it is predicted to be in all 16 ranges under the high
threshold, there are no lowland connections. Despite this result,
because both our thresholds are relatively conservative and our
higher threshold is very likely to be underpredicting at both the
LGM and the present (Fig. 3) time periods, our results suggest
that extensive colonization between ranges is almost certainly
possible at present for these seven widespread species.
These results are not inconsistent with known present
distributions. All of the taxa we used have some species
occurrences below 2000 m elevation in the Great Basin, and
many of them have occurrences below 1500 m. Given that the
basin elevation is almost always above 1000 m elevation, there
is ample evidence that these taxa are occasionally found at
lower elevations. Therefore we consider that our models are
adequately representing lowland suitable areas and are not
overpredicting the wide swath of presently suitable habitat in
lowlands shown in the outputs for most species.
Niche modelling results of LGM palaeodistributions for the
currently widespread species are exempliﬁed by Neotoma
cinerea (Fig. 3). In this species, suitable habitat is predicted
around most or all of the ranges at the lower threshold, but at
the higher threshold very little area in the Great Basin is
predicted to be suitable (Fig. 3). Given that the lower threshold
result is much more consistent with LGM fossil occurrences
and is still consistent with recent evidence of present-day
lowland dispersal routes, we believe that the lower-threshold
model more accurately represents suitable habitats in the past.
A species with a different modelled LGM distribution from
the other six widespread species is Tamias dorsalis (Fig. 4a). At
both high and low thresholds, the LGM potential distribution
for T. dorsalis is restricted to the southern and western part of
the Great Basin into the Mojave Desert, unlike the case for other
widespread species such as Neotoma cinerea (Fig. 3). In
contrast, the ENM prediction of T. dorsalis at present is

widespread.

Two of the six montane mammals with more limited Great
Basin distributions have ENM predictions that suggest that
they have different biogeographical histories from the others,
namely Belding’s ground squirrel (Spermophilus beldingi;
Fig. 4c) and the vagrant shrew (Sorex vagrans; Fig. 4d). For
the modelled present distribution, Sp. beldingi is similar to
other more limited species with suitable habitat at both
thresholds (Fig. 4c). The present ENM result for So. vagrans is
more restricted: at the higher threshold, only two ranges are
predicted to be suitable habitat (Fig. 4d). The LGM results for
both of these species are similar, suggesting that the western
and northern portions of the Great Basin contained the
majority of suitable habitat at the LGM and that there was very
little suitable habitat near the ranges where these species are
currently found. These results, along with those for Tamias
dorsalis, showcase the utility of the ENM approach. Without it,
we could not visualize the dramatic differences in LGM
distributions for these taxa compared with Lepus townsendii,
for example, which is also presently located in a limited set of
ranges in the Great Basin and does not at ﬁrst glance appear to
have a dramatically different current distribution.
If the results for T. dorsalis, So. vagrans and Sp. beldingi are
correct, it suggests that these taxa were not initially widespread
during the LGM, and that the likely pattern in the recent past
or present is colonization via relatively long-distance dispersal
from western and southern source populations. This propo-
sition is falsiﬁable by examining genetic variation within and
between populations of these three species, similar to the
approach used by Floyd et al. (2005) for Marmota ﬂaviventris.
The ENM predictions for these three species strongly support
Grayson’s (2006) assertion that the distributional response of
species to climate and landscape change may vary extensively
owing to their different requirements and tolerances.
The prediction that species have shifted their ranges towards
higher elevations from the LGM to the present is supported in
nearly all species (Table 1). Somewhat unexpectedly, however,
Sorex palustris shows a minor decrease in elevation from the
LGM to the present (Table 1). Microtus longicaudus also shows
nearly no difference in elevation across time periods. These
two species are found at the lowest elevations in the present.
Whereas subalpine species today are likely to have had
expanded ranges in favourable habitat at the LGM, lowland
species are likely to have had more fragmented ranges, with
latitudinal as opposed to elevational shifts (DeChaine &
Martin, 2004; Guralnick, 2007). It appears that S. palustris
and M. longicaudus might be intermediate between colder- and
warmer-adapted forms that experienced range expansion
and fragmentation, respectively, during the LGM, and thus
remained at basically the same elevation through climate
shifts. However, it is important to note that the amount of
elevation shift does not directly correlate with current elevation
ranges. For example, S. vagrans shows a very strong upward
shift in elevation from the LGM to the present but is not a
high-elevation form (Table 1). This suggests that the habitat
requirements that are likely to deﬁne the ecological niche for
individual species vary, leading to unexpected outcomes.
Caveats of ecological niche modelling
We do acknowledge some important caveats concerning ENM
methods as they relate to Great Basin mammal biogeography.
Our predictions of palaeodistributions assume that niches of
mammals have remained relatively conserved over the last
21 ka. This assumption has been supported in a variety of test
cases (Peterson et al., 1999; Peterson & Holt, 2003; Wiens &
Graham, 2005; Martı´nez-Meyer & Peterson, 2006), and is
generally likely to hold true in the heterogeneous habitats of
western North America.
In cases where fossil occurrences known to be deposited
during the LGM do not match predicted suitable habitat at the
LGM, there are two potential solutions that could be
considered in future synthetic approaches. One is to adjust
the presence threshold at the LGM so that all fossils must be
included and then to use that same presence threshold in the
present. Another solution, if enough fossil material is available,
is to generate predicted suitable habitat at the LGM using fossil
occurrences and LGM climate conditions (Martı´nez-Meyer
et al., 2004). These models could be used separately, or
averaged with the retrodicted predictions based on the modern
occurrences, for ﬁnal LGM niche model construction.
A second caveat regarding ENM methods is that they create
potential species distributions, not actual distributions. Limi-
tations in dispersal or other biotic factors such as competition
or predation pressures could result in a species not occupying
all suitable habitats. Therefore, the potential distribution, at
the threshold chosen during the modelling exercise, represents
the widest possible extent of a species. Regarding the biogeo-
graphical predictions here, the ﬁnal potential distributions, at
whatever threshold, might be considered the least conservative
predictions about where populations were located; the distri-
butional extent could be smaller since not all suitable habitats
need be occupied. Therefore, if model results show that a
lowland area near a mountain range was suitable habitat in the
LGM, and the species is not found in that range in the present,
we cannot falsify that the species was excluded from that
lowland region for other reasons despite the suitable condi-
tions. It is for this reason that we chose two thresholds that are
both conservative, one extremely so.
CONCLUSIONS
Our ENM-based results show that there are probably low-
elevation basin connections for most species, even when using
very conservative thresholding that limits distributions to only
the most suitable possible areas, thus supporting and comple-
menting results from Lawlor (1998), who used a completely
different method to generate his inferences. Our results are also
generally consistent with the predictions of Brown (1971,
1978) and Grayson (1993, 2006) of widespread LGM distri-
butions for most, but not all, species utilizing the preferred less
conservative threshold. Our results also support the prediction
that at present there are more ranges with suitable habitat than
are currently occupied, potentially as a result of extinction

Robert Guralnick’s broad interests include the mapping and analysis of biodiversity data, utilizing in part past and present species
