BITE WOUNDS, DENTAL LESIONS AND SEPSIS –  
THE SILENT KILLERS 
 
Vic SIMPSON 
 
Wildlife Veterinary Investigation Centre, Chacewater, Truro, Cornwall, TR4 8PB. England, UK.  
email:wildlife.vic@rmplc.co.uk    
 
Abstract: In the period 1988-2002 post mortem examinations were performed on 304 otters found dead in south 
west England.  The majority were animals found dead on roads.  Bite wounds were seen in 60/181 males (33%) and 
36/123 females (29%).  The majority of wounds were attributed to attacks by other otters and by American mink but 
injuries consistent with attack by domestic dogs were seen in nine cases.  The prevalence of bite wounds increased 
from a mean of 15.6% in the first eight years to 42% during 2001-02.  Dental lesions, particularly fractures, became 
more frequent and in some cases secondary bacterial infections led to death.  Lesions involving incisor teeth were 
seen more frequently in otters with bites (16%) than in those without (6.7%).  Fractures of the fourth upper premolar 
tooth were a significant cause of mortality but were not thought to be related to fighting.  Between 1988 and 1998 
mortality associated with bite wounds was around 4-6.5% but during 2000-2002 it rose to 10.7%.  It is concluded 
that mortality from bite wounds and dental lesions may be at least as significant as that caused by road traffic but 
the factors responsible are unclear and merit further study. 
 
 
Keywords: Eurasian otter, Lutra lutra, mink, bite wounds, fighting, fractured teeth, sepsis, bacterial infection 
 
 
1   INTRODUCTION 
 
During the 1970s and 1980s there was widespread concern about the status of the otter (Lutra 
lutra) population in England and Wales.  It was suspected that the massive decline, which started 
in the late 1950s, had been caused by organochlorine insecticides or polychlorinated biphenyls, 
but the evidence against these pollutants was largely circumstantial (CHANIN AND JEFFERIES, 
1978; MASON, 1989).  However, although chemical analyses had been performed on otters, no 
one had made an in-depth study of the health status of the species.   In order to address this, the 
author carried out detailed post mortem examinations from 1988 onwards on otters found dead in 
southern and south west England.  There was a marked increase in the numbers submitted each 
year and, by the end of 2002, a total of 339 specimens had been received (Figure 1).  Of these, 
304 (181 males and 123 females) were suitable for inclusion in this study.  
Approximately 80% of the specimens had been killed in road accidents and the majority 
of these were apparently healthy animals.  Of the remaining 20%, a small number had been killed 
by trains, caught in snares or drowned in fishing nets but the commonest causes of death in this 
group were severe bite wounds and bacterial infections. In most cases the bacterial infections had 
arisen from septic bite wounds or fractured teeth.   
However, irrespective of the cause of death, many of the otters had bite wounds and some 
had dental lesions (SIMPSON, 1997; 2002).  The bites ranged from minor to severe but they were 
only recorded as bite wounds if there were multiple, typical, punctures or tears and they were 
recent.  Healed wounds were not included.  Teeth were not recorded as fractured if the damage 
appeared to have arisen as a result of trauma at the time of death, for example, in a road traffic 
accident.

2   BITE WOUNDS 
 
Sixty (33%) out of the 181 male otters and 36 (29.2%) out of the 123 females  had bite wounds.  
However, these are overall figures and the prevalence of bite wounds in the early years of the 
study was much less (Figure 2).  For example, during the first seven years (1988-1994) the 
combined figure for males and females was 19.2% but for the last three years (2000-2002), it was 
42.6%; the difference in these proportions was highly significant (Fisher’s Exact test, P = 0.003).

Based on the size and nature of the wounds they were classified as those known or believed to 
have been inflicted by domestic dogs and those thought to be the result of conflict with other 
species. 
 
2.1 Dog bites 
Bite wounds in otters have been attributed to attacks by domestic dogs (KRUUK AND CONROY, 
1991; MASON AND O’SULLIVAN, 1992).  In this study one otter was known to have been killed in 
this way and a further eight had post mortem lesions that were consistent with them having been 
bitten by dogs.  Six were young animals with estimated ages ranging from two to six months and 
most of these had been bitten across the thorax.  Post mortem examinations in these cases showed 
extensive subcutaneous haemorrhage and bruising, multiple fractured ribs and severe trauma to 
internal organs.  In two animals it was noted that, despite the characteristic crushing injuries, 
there were no puncture wounds to the skin caused by canine teeth.   
 
In several of the otters that were thought to have been killed by dogs the wounds were 
more randomly placed but with most on the ventral body surface.  One cub with such wounds 
also had a set of much smaller punctures, circa 12-13 millimetres apart, around the anus and had 
clearly been bitten by a second, smaller, aggressor.   
Although it was not always possible to identify dog bites with certainty, the majority of 
puncture wounds seen in otters were too small and too closely spaced to have been caused by 
dogs. 
 
2.2 Bites due to intra- and interspecific aggression 
In the great majority of bitten otters the wounds were present at three main sites: the face, the feet 
and the perineum.  The characteristic pattern of the bites indicates a stylised form of fighting and 
this is typically seen in intraspecific aggression.  In some cases the spacing of the puncture 
wounds matched that of an otter’s canines (about 18-20 mm.) but frequently it was not possible 
to be certain that the wounds had been inflicted by another otter. Interpretation was made more 
difficult due to the elasticity of otters’ skin and, in many cases, the presence of multiple punctures 
or lacerations. 
 
During 2002 several otters were examined that had a single, discrete, set of bite wounds 
to the upper lip and in each case the spacing was approximately ten millimetres.  These punctures 
were too small and too close together to have been caused by an otter and, after measuring the 
jaws of a number of carnivores, it was concluded that the bites had been inflicted by American 
mink (Mustela vison).  
Although 33% of the male otters examined had bite wounds, 29.2% of females also had 
bites and the rise in prevalence of bites in females was even more marked than in males.  This 
was particularly striking in 2000/01 when 63% of the 27 females submitted had recent bite 
wounds (Figure 2).   
 
Apart from those bitten by dogs, it is presumed that most of the bite wounds in males 
were caused during territorial disputes with other males (ERLINGE, 1968).  Females are believed 
to be less territorial, but breeding females, especially when they have cubs, may become 
dominant over other females and will even attack males (KRUUK 1995).  Although defence of 
breeding territory or offspring may explain some of the wounds seen in both sexes, it does not 
explain the attacks on sexually immature otters. 
 
Wherever possible the body weight and total length was recorded and when these values 
are plotted on a graph (Figure 3) it is apparent that around 25 to 30% of the bitten otters are 
underweight, some by as much as 50%.   Approximately 60% of all male otters with bite wounds 


fell in the 5-5.9 kilogramme body weight range and initially it was suspected that these were sub 
adults.  However, further examination of the data showed that about half of these bitten males 
were full grown adults that had lost around 3 – 5 kilogrammes.  Although it is possible that 
animals in poor condition are more likely to be bitten than healthy ones, the loss of condition was 
usually consistent with the severity of the wounds. In most cases the loss of condition appeared to 
be a direct result of being bitten.

3   TEETH 
 
3.1 Incisors and canines 
In the early years of this study it was noticeable that the majority of the otters had healthy teeth 
and there was very little evidence of calculus, wear or damage.  In recent years, however, worn, 
lost and fractured teeth have become common findings. 
 
Missing and/or fractured incisors, particularly in the lower jaw, were observed in 9.5% of 
otters.  By boiling and cleaning the skulls it was shown that in many cases the apparently missing 
incisors had, in fact, been sheared off close to the root and that the gum had healed over.  It was 
observed that damage to incisors was more prevalent in otters that had bite wounds.  Fifteen of 79 
bitten otters (16%) had damaged or missing incisors compared with 14 of 210 of otters (6.7%) 
without bite wounds (Fisher’s Exact test: P = 0.018).  Fractured or missing canines were seen in 
approximately 10% of otters overall. The frequency with which canine lesions were seen in bitten 
and non-bitten males was similar (10-12% of animals) but damaged canines were somewhat more 


prevalent in bitten females (14.3%) than in non-bitten ones (6.8%), although this difference was 
not statistically significant (Fisher’s Exact test, P = 0.29).  
 
It is tentatively suggested that otters that are involved in conflicts are more likely to 
sustain damage to incisors, and possibly canines. 
 
3.2 Premolars and molars 
Otters have three premolars either side in the lower jaw and four in the upper jaw.  The fourth 
upper premolar, or carnassial tooth, is a large broad tooth with three roots.  Whereas the incisors 
and canines are used for prehension and premolars 1 – 3 for shearing, the carnassial is used for 
crushing.  Fractures to the cusps, or across the whole carnassial tooth, were seen in eight otters. 
This type of damage is likely to have occurred due to otters masticating food contaminated by 
hard, unyielding objects such as gravel or small stones.  In several cases the damage led to a root 
abscess followed by bacteria entering the blood stream and causing pneumonia, pleurisy, 
pericarditis and, in two cases, endocarditis.   
 
 
4   BACTERIAL INFECTIONS 
 
Bacterial infections, whether from infected bite wounds or fractured teeth were a common cause 
of mortality.  A range of organisms was isolated but Streptococcus species were the most 
common.  However, not all infections proved fatal.  Many otters had small, white, focal lesions 
scattered throughout the lungs and histological examination suggested that most of these were old 
microabscesses.  They almost certainly resulted from earlier episodes where bacteria had entered 
the blood stream, mostly from infected bite wounds. 
 
 
5   CONCLUSIONS 
 
As the number of otters found dead each year in southern and south west England has increased, 
so has the proportion with bite wounds.  The reasons for this are not clear but the author suspects 
that as the otter population of England has expanded it may have led to more competition for 
territory. This may not simply be competition between otters, as the marked rise in the proportion 
of bitten animals has coincided with evidence of conflict with mink.  Unfortunately, the bite 
wounds are often not sufficiently distinct to enable species identification.   
 
As the proportion of otters suffering from bite wounds has increased, so has the 
proportion of deaths attributable to bites or fighting.  During the first ten years of this study 
around 4–6.5% of submissions were attributable to bite wounds.  In the three years 2000 to 2002, 
and excluding otters killed by domestic dogs, the figure has risen to 10.7%.   
 
The true level of mortality in the otter population due to bite wounds, fractured teeth and 
secondary bacterial infections will be far greater than the figures quoted in this study.  The figure 
of 80% mortality from road casualties must reflect the bias in favour of finding and collecting 
otters that have died in this way.  Large sums of money are being invested in trying to reduce the 
risk of otters being killed on roads.  However, it is the author’s opinion that mortality resulting 
from aggressive interactions between otters, and between otters and mink, may be at least as 
important as deaths caused by traffic.  Further studies are indicated to understand the reasons for 
these interactions.
