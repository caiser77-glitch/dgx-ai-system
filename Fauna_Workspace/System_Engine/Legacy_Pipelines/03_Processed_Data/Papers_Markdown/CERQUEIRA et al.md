DIET AND DISTRIBUTION OF THE EURASIAN OTTER (LUTRA LUTRA, L, 1758) 
IN SOUTHERN PORTUGAL – RIA FORMOSA NATURAL PARK. 
 
Laura CERQUEIRA1, Nuno GRADE3 and José VINGADA2 
1.  Portuguese Wildlife Society. Dep. Biologia Universidade do Minho, Campus de Gualtar 4710-057 
Braga Portugal.  email:   lauracerqueira66@iol.pt 
2.  Dep. Biologia Universidade do Minho, Campus de Gualtar 4710-057 Braga, Portugal 
3.  Instituto da Conservação da Natureza: Parque Natural da Ria Formosa, Portugal. 
 
ABSTRACT: Otter densities have declined in several countries in the recent past while in Portugal 
otter populations are considered stable.  Studies have emphasised that otters respond to prey 
availability and severe habitat quality and therefore in terms of nature conservation and management 
it is very important to evaluate the status of otter.  As the Ria Formosa Natural Park includes areas 
with severe human pressure, otters may be affected by the degradation of water courses and associated 
riparian vegetation, with a subsequent decrease of food resources.  The main objectives of this study 
were to evaluate otter distribution and to assess diet.  Results indicate two source areas, corresponding 
to the best-preserved habitats where the otter occurs permanently.  However, these nuclei are 
gradually becoming isolated due to increasing human pressure, which is making dispersal difficult.  
The high number of UTM squares where otters were absent supports this idea. In relation to otter diet 
differences were detected according to seasons and habitats.  These results confirm, that otters adapt 
their diet to food availability. 
 
Keywords: Eurasian otter, Lutra lutra, diet, distribution, Portugal 
 
 
1   INTRODUCTION 
 
Although otter populations have suffered huge declines in several countries in the past 
few decades (KLOSKOWSKI, 2000; RUIZ-OLMO, SAAVEDRA AND JIMÉNEZ, 2001), in 
Portugal, they are considered stable (CHAMBEL, 1997; GOMES, 1998; TRINDADE, 
FARINHA AND FLORÊNCIO, 1998).  However, few studies have been made in Portugal 
(TRINDADE, FARINHA AND FLORÊNCIO, 1998) and therefore there is an enormous lack 
of fundamental ecological data which could be used in the decision processes related 
to the management of protected areas (GOMES, 1998; SILVA, 1999).  TRINDADE, 
FARINHA AND FLORÊNCIO (1998) mentioned that the present otter situation, resulting 
from diverse factors makes the application of conservation strategies and the 
execution of action plans difficult.  This fact gives us the responsibility in 
conservation terms to increase our knowledge of otter ecology.  In Ria Formosa 
Natural Park human pressures (industries, agriculture and human settlements) have 
been increasing and presently only a few sites preserve their original wildness.  It 
became important to assess the effects of these changes on the local otter population 
and, therefore the objectives of this work were to study their diet and distribution in 
relation to these pressure factors. 
 
1.1 Study area 
Ria Formosa Natural Park is the most important Portuguese lagoon system in the 
south of the country.  It is protected from the sea by a system of sand dunes and 
includes extensive salt marshes (16,000 ha).  It is also possible to find a well-
developed system of drainage streams and several rivers, most with riparian 
vegetation.  These structures are the main sources of freshwater.  Salt water lagoons, 
aquaculture tanks and saline areas are other habitats that occur in the Park (PAULO, 
1993).  Due to the environmental conditions and geomorphologic structure, this area

has a high productivity and high diversity of species especially bivalves, crustaceans 
and fish, which are very important otter prey species (ABREU AND MACHADO, 2000).  
However, in this area a strong increase in human pressure is being recorded, with 
watercourses the most disturbed habitat. 
 
 
2   METHODS 
 
2.1 Otter distribution 
The study area was divided into 220 UTM, one kilometre squares, and 117 were used 
to characterise otter distribution and habitat use according to LÓPEZ-MARTÍN, JIMENÉZ 
AND RUIZ-OLMO, 1998; RUIZ-OLMO, SAAVEDRA AND JIMENÉZ, 2001.  In each selected 
square, a 600 metre long transect was surveyed seasonally to search for different signs 
of otter presence, such as footprints and spraints (BUENO AND BRAVO, 1998; 
MORALES, ET AL., 1998; JIMENÉZ, RUIZ-OLMO AND PASCUAL, 1998; KLOSKOWSKI, 
2000).  A distribution map with different occurrence categories was produced and 
related to habitat characteristics and pressure factors.  Low occurrence corresponds to 
0-10 spraints found in a transect, medium occurrence corresponds to 10-20 spraints, 
and high occurrence refers to the detection of more than 20 spraints or reproduction if 
detected within the square unit. 
 
2.2 Otter diet 
Otter diet was determined by spraint analysis (n=380).  Spraints were collected 
seasonally and frozen for later identification.  In the laboratory, they were dissolved in 
a detergent solution for 24 hours, then washed and sieved and the prey remains 
analysed.  Prey was identified by comparison with reference collections and 
bibliography (MONTEIRO, 1986; TEROFAL, 1991; PRENDA AND LORENCIO-GRANADO, 
1992; CAMPBELL, 1994; WISNIOWSKA, 1996;).  Identification of crustaceans, 
particularly the Louisiana crayfish (Procambarus clarkii), was easier and simple, 
because of their colour (RUIZ-OLMO, LÓPEZ-MARTÍN AND PALAZÓN, 2001).  The 
number of occurrences of a particular prey item was expressed as percentage of 
frequency of occurrence (WATT, 1995). 
A chi-square test was used to detect significant differences between diet 
values with Yate’s Coefficient (FOWLER AND COHEN, 1989).

3.2 Otter diet

(Anguilla anguilla), golden mullet (Liza aurata), black goby (Gobius niger), thick-
lipped grey mullet (Chelon labrosus) and P. clarkii. Other prey was present in lower 
occurrences (Table 1).  In spring, the most consumed prey were L. aurata

frequently recorded.  In autumn the more important prey types were L. aurata 
(F=20%) and A. anguilla (F=19.18%).  Finally in winter, G. niger (F=27.27%) and L. 
aurata (F=22.97%) were the most important prey.

4   DISCUSSION 
 
4.1 Otter distribution 
Of the whole prospected area, more than 60% revealed absence (32.48%) or low 
frequency of otter occurrence (30.77%).  These values may indicate the lack of habitat 
quality.  The destruction of the aquatic systems and the vegetation cover along with 
poor water quality and the subsequently low food availability and resting sites are the 
major problems that we identified in the area and may justify these low values of otter 
presence.  KRUUK (1995) (cited in RUIZ-OLMO AND PALAZÓN, 1997) demonstrated 
that food availability is very important to otter presence and conservation.  Habitat 
destruction and pollution are some reasons that lead to a decrease in otter dietary 
resources. 
In the map of otter distribution it is possible to identify two areas where their 
presence is higher, acting as source areas to other regions.  These two areas are 
associated with the best-preserved habitats within the Natural Park (Figure 2).  But in 
general this map emphasises that otter distribution is rather scattered with large areas 
showing no signs of the species.  Despite the absence of physical barriers it seems that 
the loss of habitat quality and human pressure is fragmenting the otter population and 
dispersion corridors between the main nuclei are difficult to identify.

4.2 Otter diet 
Although we noted a huge diversity in otter diet, analyses by frequency of occurrence, 
showed that the most important prey were A. anguilla, L. aurata, G. niger, C. 
labrosus and P. clarkii.  Other prey in the diet was estimated in low frequencies, 
meaning they were less important prey.  It is evident that fish species are the main 
prey, although P. clarkii was also important.  In other studies in Portugal the results 
were similar.  In southern Portugal CHAMBEL (1997) showed the importance of fish in 
otter diet, while GOMES (1998), in Ria de Aveiro (central Portugal) showed the 
diversity of otter diet with fish consumption at a huge scale, (86% of total diet) 
although P. clarkii was also of great importance.  
The statistical analysis of the major food items revealed that otter diet differs 
significantly between seasons.  Major variations were detected between summer and 
winter, with seven prey types contributing to these dietary variations.  This is induced 
by the huge habitat quality differences that occur between these two seasons.  In fact, 
in summer most watercourses are dry (ephemeral habitats) and consequently the 
dietary diversity decreases.  These results are similar to a study in south west Portugal 
(SILVA, 1999), where the major differences also occurred in summer.  By comparison, 
spring and autumn were the two seasons more similar with only one prey type 
contributing to the differences found as these two seasons are similar in terms of 
weather conditions.  This value emphasises that differences occurring between 
seasons are due to otter prey availability.  
Comparing the diet between habitat types it was possible to detect differences 
for all main prey items, except for L. aurata.  Probably, the high density and large 
distribution of this species in the study area, are the main reasons for its constant 
occurrence in the otter diet.  The results also emphasise that there are no dietary 
differences between ditches and streams;  they present similar characteristics and the 
prey types occur in similar densities in both. 
The major dietary differences were found between stream and aquacultures 
and lakes, with five prey types consumed differently.  In these habitats the otter found 
different prey availability, eating other kinds of prey or in different quantities.  In 
contrast, only one prey species contributed to the differences between river vs saline 
areas and river vs saltmarsh. These results emphasise that the otter adapts its diet, both 
in relation to seasons and to different habitats.
