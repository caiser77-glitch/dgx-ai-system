In biodiversity conservation and management
of natural resources, from policy decisions under
the Convention for Biological Diversity to the
implementation of national and local legislation,
there is hardly a project where information on
species occurrence is not needed. But animal
and plant species are mobile, ranges shift over
time, and individual animals move within their
range and on a variety of scales. Representing
complex ecological patterns such as species oc-
currences on a map inevitably presents a gross
simpliﬁcation of the real world, and models have
always been the only tool available to research-
ers. The black blotches on the maps of many
atlases of species distribution are merely the ear-
liest and simplest models, worked out by experts
on the basis of available data and their personal
experience. In spite of their often-rough approx-
imation, much conservation and management
continues to be based on such simple models,
which remain the only available means for rep-
resenting the distribution of many species at a
global scale.
Species–habitat relationships were a great im-
provement for exploring the internal composi-
tion of species distributions, and they launched
the new ﬁeld of predicting species occurrences.
The widespread application of computers of-
fered a vast array of new tools, such as GIS and
statistical packages and their ever-increasing
ability to crunch large quantities of data, and
remote sensing, which facilitated the collection
of large amounts of environmental data. Ecolo-
gists did not miss these opportunities. However,
we are still far from having solved all problems
of predicting species occurrences. Spatial and
temporal variations in the processes determining
species occurrence still pose enormous challeng-
es to modelers, and it is possible that accurate
modeling will never be satisfactorily accom-
plished.
In spite of the well-known axiom that ecological
patterns should be studied at a variety of scales
(Levin 1992), modelers often struggle for the mag-
ic model that should synthesize and predict com-
plex ecosystem interactions. Scientists (often)
know that this is largely a chimera, but they are
under pressure from managers who ask for simple
answers and manageable problems. In fact, there
is no ideal model, best scale, or optimal statistical
method to build a predictive model. Quite the op-
posite is true: the best solution often involves an
array of several models built with different tech-
niques and at multiple scales.
All this and much more is clearly presented in
this beautiful book, which is by far the best syn-
opsis on the state of our current knowledge of
models of species occurrence, and a substantial
contribution to spatial ecology. It is an extraordi-
nary book that touches on virtually every aspect
of this difﬁcult ﬁeld of conservation biology, from
the scientiﬁc foundations to the statistical tech-
niques and the management impacts of producing
models of species occurrence. Although only a
few chapters speciﬁcally address mammal species
(most case studies are on birds and plants), the
book is of great interest to all mammalogists be-
cause it emphasizes the theories, methods, and ca-
veats of modeling species occurrences rather than
the presentation of limited distribution models. The
book is the result of a symposium held in Snow-
bird, Utah, in 1999 and is explicitly presented as
a continuation of the previous major compendium
on modeling species–habitat relationships (Verner
et al. 1986). Editing a large (28.5 by 22 cm) vol-
ume of almost 900 pages and 65 chapters by more
than 100 authors is no easy task, particularly or-
ganizing the vast amount of information and case
studies so that the reader is not intimidated. Yet,
the editors deserve praise for their largely suc-
cessful attempt to guide the reader with a thought-
ful layout of review chapters and introductions to
the sections that deal with the main themes.
The volume is opened and closed by 2 ma-
gistral essays. In the ﬁrst, M. A. Huston dis-
cusses in his usual rigorous style the shortcom-
ings of several difﬁcult assumptions that con-
strain many current modeling efforts, among
them the critical role of limiting resources, their
spatial and temporal variability, and their inter-
action with other limiting factors in determining
species’ presence or absence and abundance. In
the concluding essay, J. A. Wiens writes with a
wonderfully colloquial style and presents a fo-
cused summary of where the whole ﬁeld of pre-
dicting species occurrence currently stands, the

(e.g., misuse of tools, variable selection, model

To make the book’s contents easier to digest,
I suggest that the reader start with these 2 essays
and the chapters introducing the 5 parts into
which the book is divided. The introductory
chapters provide not only very concise summa-
ries of the contents of the chapters of each part
but also original perspectives and suggestions on
future directions. All other chapters can then be
read in almost any order, with the exception of
those in Part 1, ‘‘Conceptual Framework.’’ The
introduction by R. J. Connor and the following
8 chapters set the theoretical stage for the whole
book, and together they nearly constitute a com-
plete textbook for any student wishing to under-
stand this ﬁeld of ecological modeling. They of-
fer a good survey of the conceptual foundations
of modeling species distributions, including the
many caveats and limitations of these models for
both scientiﬁc and practical utilization. Echoing
Huston, Connor argues for a fundamental para-
digm shift from analysis of environmental cor-
relates to analysis of constraints. M. L. Morrison
and L. S. Hall make a good case for the need to
standardize terminology and they offer deﬁni-
tions for most common terms, such as habitat,
ecosystem, landscape, extent, and grain. Al-
though I doubt that their deﬁnitions will go
much beyond this book, their message is clear:
whatever meaning one gives to a term should be
stated before presenting any data. D. F. Stauffer
writes a short history of the modeling of species
occurrence, but he also includes excellent pre-
cautionary comments on the potential of mod-
eling species occurrence. Chapters 4, 5, 7, and
8 complete the conceptual framework by deﬁn-
ing model types, discussing different techniques
in relation to goals, warning of the need for gra-
dients in sampling design, commenting on the
different logic of category deﬁnitions, offering
methods to test the accuracy of habitat models,
and exploring the implications of errors of omis-
sion and commission.
Part 2, ‘‘Temporal and Spatial Scales’’ (Chap-
ters 9–20), deals with 1 of the central themes of
the book—the inﬂuence of temporal and spatial
scales in determining model output and the
question of what is the correct scale for habitat
evaluation. The best answer is that there is no
‘‘correct’’ scale because the appropriate scale is
dependent on the speciﬁc goal of a model, and
often the best result can be achieved only using
a multiscale model to account for the diversity
of interactions across scales and levels. B. A.
Maurer provides an extremely clear introduction
to much of these issues, which are further de-
veloped in the following chapters. A particularly
interesting chapter by M. J. Conroy and C. T.
Moore highlights the difﬁculties of model vali-
dation and advocates the need for an adaptive
approach testing alternative models in an itera-
tive process.
Part 3, ‘‘Modeling Tools and Accuracy As-
sessment’’ (Chapters 21–39), includes papers
that report on almost every possible method of
predicting species occurrence. Generalized lin-
ear models, generalized additive models, logistic
regression, Poisson regression, Mahalanobis dis-
tance, classiﬁcation and regression trees, genetic
algorithm for rule-set prediction (GARP), and
gap analysis are just some of the techniques ex-
plained and discussed here and in Part 4, often
comparing different methods with a common
data set. As for scaling issues, there is no ‘‘cor-
rect’’ modeling technique, and the most appro-
priate scale again depends on the goal of the
model: a multimodel approach and the quanti-
tative comparison of their performance using
Akaike’s Information Criterion often is advocat-
ed as the most solid strategy. I know of no other
book in which all these techniques are explained
and demonstrated with case studies. Together
these chapters provide an excellent review of ex-
periences and failures that should be considered
by anyone about to begin a new modeling pro-
ject. Finally, whereas many different determin-
istic and probabilistic methods are available for
developing models, the number and quality of
methods to assess model accuracy is limited. In
an especially important review, A. H. Fielding
offers a rigorous discussion of the characteristics
of an ideal accuracy measure and emphasizes
the need for a test of model accuracy to be strict-
ly related to the model’s intended use.
Part 4, ‘‘Predicting Species: Presence and
Abundance’’ (Chapters 40–58), deals with pre-
dicting probable species absence or presence and
abundance over large areas. Models using sev-
eral different methods and covering a great va-
riety of taxa (including fungi and ﬁshes!) are
presented. Broad-scale species–habitat associa-

Part 5, ‘‘Predicting Species: Populations and
Productivity’’ (Chapters 59–64), is opened by E.
O. Garton, who appears to portend a difﬁcult
future for predictive models of population char-
acteristics or productivity based on habitat fac-
tors. Models that have been at least partially suc-
cessful have generally employed multiple scales
or have signiﬁcantly increased model complex-
ity and data quality than are available in most
habitat-based models (or both).
The book is well edited, and I found only a
few typos. The general index is sufﬁciently de-
tailed to make it possible to ﬁnd all important
topics. A uniﬁed literature cited section avoids
the inevitable repetition of frequently cited ref-
erences while providing a single updated list that
is very helpful for students and beginners. A
large section of color plates is bound into the
center of the volume. Although it is not easy to
refer to the plates while reading a distant chap-
ter, the alternative solution of spreading color
plates throughout the book would have made the
cost of the book excessive. Considering its size,
the current cost is relatively low and certainly
justiﬁed by what it provides.
In short, this is the most comprehensive ref-
erence book available on the science of devel-
oping predictive models of species occurrence.
As such, it is obligatory reading for any scientist
(and natural resource manager) working in this
ﬁeld. Style and lack of jargon also make it ac-
cessible to students who will have to be guided
by their teachers on the most signiﬁcant papers.
I strongly recommend it to anyone interested not
only in species distribution but also in the broad
challenges of conservation biology.—LUIGI BOI-

This volume is a festschrift to honor 2 ex-
traordinary contributors to mammalogy in gen-
eral and the discipline in Poland in particular.
Zdzislaw Pucek and Roman Andrzejewski have
each been publishing in the ﬁeld of mammalogy
for more than 45 years. These gentlemen, and
numerous colleagues in Poland and neighboring
countries, were part of a magniﬁcent group of
scientists who accomplished both excellent em-
pirical studies and signiﬁcant modeling efforts
for a number of decades under Soviet domina-
tion. These efforts, largely on the ecology of
small mammals, are clearly a major feature of
the biological sciences in Eastern Europe during
the Soviet era.

leading in that the topics are rather selected and
do not cover the range of topics found in mam-

Admittedly I have a bias, but I liked the 1st

items stand out in these articles. First, the vari-
ous population phenomena are multifaceted;
thus, complex explanations are to be expected.
Current works, built on the earlier, more sim-
plistic views of population dynamics in rodents,
are now accounting for the integration of several
or many factors inﬂuencing populations of small
mammals. Second, there has always been a need
for long-term databases to explore phenomena

density-independent factors. Funding and inves-
tigator persistence are 2 factors that certainly fa-
cilitate or impede progress in this regard. Third,
spatial scales are also important to a compre-

development. The sets of empirical data and
analyses in these 3 articles alone make the vol-

Also included in this initial section is an ar-
ticle by Sheftel and Hanski. They used meta-
analyses to explore species richness of Sorex
species in Eurasian boreal forests. Another use-
ful offering is a wonderful recounting of litera-
ture on the idea and measurement of home rang-
es by Andrzejewski, including some fresh ideas
for how we should view and use this concept. I
felt that Goszczyn´ski’s article on spatial relations
in red foxes (Vulpes fulva) expressed some con-

sive use). The 2 concepts are not synonymous;
an animal with a large home range may not de-
fend all of it, in which case the territory is a
smaller area within the home range.
The next section of the volume contains 5 ar-
ticles loosely falling under the heading of evo-
lution and life histories. The article by Klevezal
concerning the use of fossils and particular teeth
is fascinating. The variety of techniques and as-
sociated detective work, all of which could be
applied to other, related problems, make this a
‘‘must read.’’ I also was impressed by Church-
ﬁeld’s contribution on variation in sizes of
shrews. Smaller shrews may be at a competitive
advantage with regard to a winter-feeding niche,
where they can consume sufﬁcient numbers of
small arthropods to maintain their high meta-
bolic rate at a time when somewhat larger
shrews need, but cannot always ﬁnd, larger prey
items.
The ﬁnal section on ecophysiology contains
only 2 articles. Kostelecka-Myrcha has done a
nice compilation of data across a wide spectrum
of mammals to test hypotheses about the amount
of hemoglobin and erythrocyte surface area rel-
ative to body mass in a group of 54 mammals.
Small mammals have a greater surface area of
erythrocytes per blood volume than large mam-
mals. The last offering, a joint effort by Merritt
and Zegers, addresses the metabolic costs and
mechanisms of survival of overwintering small
mammals including representatives of Blarina,
Cletherionomys, Peromyscus, and Glaucomys.
Resting metabolic rate, nonshivering thermogen-
esis, body mass, and communal nesting all con-
tribute to winter survival via varying combina-
tions of mechanisms.
One general issue that is well documented by
the articles in this volume is a pronounced ten-
dency for authors from areas within Europe or
from North America to cite primarily literature
from their region, ignoring the potential for set-
ting their work in a larger context. Making these
connections to the wider literature is something
that mammalogists in general need to pursue
with greater attention. Ecologists, physiologists,
animal behaviorists, and others have succeeded
quite well in the past 25–30 years with journals
that span the world in terms of the sources of
articles published therein. For mammalogy, and
to a lesser degree ornithology, there is still some
work to be done to surmount whatever barriers
exist so that we can provide our students with a
world-based perspective rather than mostly a re-
gional knowledge. One possibility would be to

An area of complex geological history in-
volving plate tectonics and volcanism, the West
Indies has long been of interest to geologists,
biogeographers, and evolutionary biologists.
Like other island archipelagos, its faunas and
ﬂoras exhibit high levels of endemicity and re-
duced and taxonomically skewed diversity. The
mammal fauna, for example, currently includes
4 endemic families, 68 genera (of which 92%
are endemic, excluding bats), and 148 species
(all are endemic, except for bats, with 50% en-
demics). The relative importance of dispersal
versus vicariance as mechanisms behind these
patterns has been debated for decades. Of cur-
rent interest is the validity of the MacPhee and
Ituralde-Vinet hypothesis that the Aves Ridge
was an important dispersal route from South
America to the Greater Antilles for mammals
and other organisms. Finally, extinction rates in
birds and mammals have been high, particularly
in the past few thousand years, owing to climatic
changes and the hand of man. Three-quarters of
West Indian parrots and macaws, 10 of 12 in-
sectivorans, and 27 bats are extinct; remaining
populations of some land mammals (e.g., Solen-
odon and Geocapromys) are extremely restrict-
ed.
This volume is a successor to Wood’s (1989)
edited book on the same subject. The present
volume focuses on taxonomic reviews and bio-
geographic patterns and contains 27 chapters, of
which at least half will be of interest to mam-
malogists. Blair Hedges sets the biogeographic
stage by reviewing the history of West Indian
vertebrates. He argues strongly that this fauna is
the product of overwater dispersal and not of
vicariance or dispersal from South America via
the Aves Ridge, which emerged above sea level
for about 2 3 106 years, beginning about 35 3
106 years ago. He concludes that molecular
clock evidence and the taxonomically skewed
nature of the West Indian mammal fauna do not
support the MacPhee and Ituralde-Vinet hypoth-
esis.
Chapters focusing speciﬁcally on mammals
include a brief review of the functional adapta-
tions of vertebrates to island life (McNab); an
intriguing discussion of the biogeographic im-
plications of the presence of rhinocerotoid and
possibly plesiadapiform or primate fossils from
the mid-Eocene on Jamaica (Portell, Donovan,
and Domning); a cladistic analysis of West In-
dian ground sloths, which apparently reached
the islands from South America in the early Ol-
igocene (White and MacPhee); 2 chapters on the
insectivorans
Solenodon
and

whose dispersal route to the Caribbean is still

(Woods, Pae´z, and Kilpatrick); 2 chapters on

The market is ﬂooded with textbooks on an-
imal behavior, and this cornucopia makes it a
‘‘buyer’s market’’ for faculty members attempt-
ing to select texts for their classes or graduate
students attempting to refresh their memory in
preparation for oral comprehensive exams. A
long-time leader in my estimation has been the
very readable and enthusiastic writing of John
Alcock. This 7th edition received a considerable
makeover, with an estimated 20% reduction in
length and the addition of colored graphics
(most of the 444 ﬁgures are now in color). The
strength of this edition is reﬂected in the author’s
approach, designed ‘‘to help my readers see how
researchers have been able to reach satisfactory
conclusions about how and why animals do the
things they do’’ (p. xii). The style of writing is
pleasing and truly comes from the perspective
of a scientist engaged in and enthralled with his
research questions and model systems. My im-
pression has always been that this enthusiasm
carries over to the student perusing the text.
The book consists of 15 chapters that include
an evolutionary approach to animal behavior,
proximate and ultimate causes of behavior, de-
velopment of behavior—heredity, development
of behavior—environment, control of behav-
ior—neural mechanisms, organization of behav-
ior—neurons and hormones, antipredator behav-
ior, feeding behavior, choosing where to live,
communication, reproductive behavior, mating
systems, parental care, social behavior, and hu-
man behavior. A glossary, a signiﬁcant bibliog-
raphy (1,290 references), illustration credits, and
an index complete the text. Each chapter has a
summary, discussion questions, and several sug-
gested readings.
The most compelling and distinctive chapters
are the ﬁrst 2, for these deﬁne the approach used
by the text and by practicing animal behavior-
ists. In the remainder of the text, readers are pro-
vided a solid foundation in the mechanistic, on-
togenetic, and functional aspects of behavior, but
placed within an evolutionary context. Alcock
does a ﬁne job of reviewing current thinking in
the ﬁeld as well as including most of the classic
examples that one ‘expects’ to see in an intro-
ductory text. Unlike some other textbooks, he
uses the most recent interpretation of polygyny
in yellow-bellied marmots (Marmota ﬂaviven-
tris) by Ken Armitage; this is one of the exam-
ples that I always use to measure the quality of
the revisions.
Not surprisingly, there are minor errors, such
as a missing appendage in the line art of a pseu-
doscorpion (p. 372) and the use of nonstandard
common names (see Wilson and Cole 2000). An
example of this is the discussion of the classic
studies of mating systems and reproductive suc-
cess in elk (Cervus elaphus), for which the use
of red deer is so well established by tradition in
the behavior literature. That the quantity of in-
formation has been reduced, and perhaps dilut-
ed, is sure to be the most common complaint
about the new edition. I can imagine that some
readers familiar with previous editions might
initially have this response. For a target audience
of undergraduates in an introductory course,
however, these changes in content and graphics
are primarily beneﬁcial. The quality of graphics
is clearly an aesthetic upgrade and is likely suc-
cessful in conveying the color and life of the
discipline to undergraduates. I found that the ad-
dition of color to the graphics aided in their in-
terpretation and the changes to the text facilitat-
ed reading. This text is less encyclopedic than
most and is probably of less use to graduate stu-
dents reviewing in preparation for comprehen-
sive exams. A virtual absence of theoretical and
mathematical examples in the main text, at least
for topics such as game theory (although such
an approach for game theory is used in discus-
sion questions at the end of the chapter), is not
likely to appeal to faculty that prefer a more

(Rattus) and mice (Mus) and feral pigs (Sus

Schmidly next identiﬁes the challenge that
lies ahead. Predicting the future of the mam-
malian fauna is difﬁcult because of the great di-
versity of species in Texas and because so few
inventory and monitoring efforts are under way.
Ongoing use of standardized protocols in any
state or region is important so that trends, es-
pecially declines, can be noticed in time to take
action. Such efforts in Texas may be particularly
important because an uncommonly large pro-
portion of the mammalian diversity of the Unit-
ed States occurs in Texas. For example, the 33
species of bats in Texas include all except 10 of
the species and more than half of the subspecies
of bats occurring in the United States.
The chapter concludes with a brief history of
mammalogy in Texas, beginning with Bailey’s sur-
vey. Notable ﬁgures who carried the torch in the
1st half of the 20th century were John K. Strecker
(Baylor University) and Walter P. Taylor and Wil-
liam B. Davis (Texas A&M University). Growth
of museums, collections, and academic programs
during the past half century has led to a population
of professional mammalogists that is, perhaps, un-
matched in any other state of the United States.
Accordingly, the Texas Society of Mammalogists
was founded in 1981 to promote the study of liv-
ing and fossil mammals in Texas. A photograph
of the participants of this organization’s 1st annual
meeting is included.
The book concludes with an appendix giving
old and current scientiﬁc names and common
names of mammals, birds, reptiles, and plants
noted in the book, a ‘‘Literature Cited and Ref-

entries, and an index.
For those concerned with conservation in Texas
or who farm, ranch, or otherwise utilize the land
in Texas, this book is essential reading. It provides
a great deal of speciﬁc information on our natural
heritage and on how our practices have affected
the land and biota and offers insight for projecting
the future impact of human activities in Texas. For
those not having particular interest in the biota of
Texas, this book still is important as a case study.
It documents the magnitude of change, and its
myriad causes, that can occur in merely 100 years.
It addresses the implications of such change. It
demonstrates how one can assemble the evidence
needed to document such change. Studies pat-
terned after Schmidly’s Texas project are crucial if
the biotas of other places are to be conserved.—

Mammoths, sabertooths, and hominids is in-
tended for a wide range of readers who are in-
terested in the last 65 million years of mam-
malian evolution in Europe, with Europe deﬁned
broadly to include the area from Iberia to the
Urals and Georgia. The book is divided into 7
chapters, 1 each on the Paleocene, Eocene, Ol-
igocene, Pliocene, and Pleistocene, and 2 on the
Miocene, 1 covering the early to middle Mio-
cene and the other the late Miocene. To cover
this broad sweep of time, the authors limit their
coverage of systematics and phylogeny (focus-
ing the discussion primarily at the generic level
and limiting their coverage of generic origins to
those instances that shed light on matters of bio-
geography or paleoenvironmental conditions)
and take a relatively broad chronological focus.
Rather than describing the numerous climatic
trends, glaciations, immigrations, emigrations,
and extinctions that are known to have occurred
in each period, the authors cover broader trends,
emphasizing the climatic and ecological state of
affairs at the beginning, middle, and end of each
period. The study gives balanced attention to
herbivores, carnivores, insectivores, and omni-
vores as well as to large and small species. Pri-
mates, including humans, receive coverage, but
not at the expense of other groups. On those few
occasions when it is relevant in the ecological
context, some attention is given also to reptiles.
The authors’ goal is to update earlier book-
length studies of the Age of Mammals in light
of the last 30 years of research by providing an
annotated outline of regional mammalian evo-
lution and biogeography, excluding marine
mammals. Although the authors certainly bring
readers up to date on their subject, they face an
obvious difﬁculty: an outline will tend to read
like an outline. Often, that is the case here. The
bare details of the shifting fortunes and distri-
butions of different families and genera some-
times arrive with too much speed and too little
context, with the result that they sometimes risk
becoming reduced to trivia. Although the au-
thors tell us what is believed to have happened,
based on the best available evidence, during var-
ious periods during the last 65 million years,
they do not always succeed in making that in-
formation relevant to the broad range of readers
they are addressing.
Those portions of the study that go beyond
the terseness of the outline format are the most
engaging and memorable—and likely will be
most useful for the book’s broad target audience.
Among these standout sections are a character-
ization of Paleocene fauna as an odd extension
of an impoverished Cretaceous world rather than
the beginning of anything resembling the pre-
sent, more familiar mammalian fauna (pp. 1–8);
an analysis of the middle Eocene fauna pre-
served in the Messel shales and its probable con-
nection, through Africa, to South America (pp.
43–50); and an overview of the middle Miocene
event and the ecological consequences of this
major oceanic reorganization and climatic shift
(pp. 123–129). In sections such as these, the text
illuminates mammalian evolution far more ef-
fectively than does a series of brief, periodic up-
dates on the distribution of one lineage or an-
other, allowing readers to develop a sense not
merely of the bare facts of the subject but also
of the underlying themes that give rise to the
details on which the discussion so often focuses.
In fairness to the authors, however, the details
they provide about the changing fortunes of var-
ious lineages make their study a useful general
reference. Brief bursts of analysis interspersed
throughout these sections also provide useful in-
sights into the process by which paleoecologists
draw conclusions about past environments based
on the presence or absence of particular genera,
or the morphological adaptations typical of these
taxa during particular periods.
