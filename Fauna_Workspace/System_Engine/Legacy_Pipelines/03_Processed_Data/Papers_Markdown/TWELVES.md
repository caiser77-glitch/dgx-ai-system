OTTERS AND FISH FARMING – A GOOD NEWS STORY 
 
Jane TWELVES 
 
2 South Locheynort, South Uist, Outer Hebrides, HS8 5SL, Scotland, UK  email:  jane@salar.co.uk 
   
 
Fish farming seems to attract often-unsubstantiated bad publicity from some sections of the 
press, but otters and fish farming is a good news story.  My involvement with otters and fish 
farming began over 29 years ago and most of my work has taken place on South Uist, an 
island in the Outer Hebrides off the north west coast of Scotland. 
 
I came to live in South Uist in April 1974 when my husband, Eric, was given the job 
by Booker McConnell, a multi-national company, to set up a salmon farm in Loch Sheilavaig, 
one of five sea lochs on the east coast of the island.  The late sixties and seventies was the 
pioneering time in salmon farming and this was one of the first farms to be set up in Scotland 
and the first to be set up in the Outer Hebrides.  
 
Although I did not work at the salmon farm, I was very interested in what was 
happening and sometimes went down to the loch.  It was not long before I saw otters there – 
and this put me in a real dilemma ………  I was concerned that, on the one hand, here was the 
embryonic industry of salmon farming which, if it proved successful, would give a very much 
needed boost to the local economy, but on the other hand here were these rare animals, otters.   
 
Otters had been widespread and reasonably common throughout the British Isles until 
the late 1950s and early 1960s but the sudden and widespread use of persistent pesticides in 
agriculture decimated their numbers.  Although numbers are at last beginning to recover, in 
the early 1970s the animals were still in a dire situation.  However, small pockets had 
survived in areas where pesticides had not been used and where the countryside remained 
much as it had been for centuries.  The islands of the Outer Hebrides were one such pocket 
and in 1974 it seemed that the Hebridean otters may be important to the survival of the 
species as a whole.    
 
I was concerned that there should not be a conflict between otters and salmon farming, 
that these animals should not be adversely affected by salmon farming, but also that the 
industry should develop successfully.  I therefore decided to monitor the situation. 
 
One of the early decisions that was taken at the salmon farm was that the fish cages 
should be predator proof. 
 
Two men who worked with Eric used to trap otters (in 1974 it was still legal to do 
this).  The £5 earned from an otter skin was much needed money at one time.  These men 
really knew about otters, and they taught me how to recognise the signs of the animals.  They 
showed me holts, the runs that otters invariably take when they travel overland and also the 
shallow pools beside the runs where they set large gin traps to catch the animals.  They also 
told me to look for ‘green stones’ which marked the otters ‘toilets’ as they called them.  Otters 
are territorial animals and they leave their droppings, or spraints, on these prominent 
sprainting sites. 
 
I decided to do transects from west to east across the island, mapping the signs of 
otters to see if there was a pattern to their distribution and to see if they used the different 
habitats in any particular way. 
 
The geomorphology of South Uist is most interesting.  The island is orientated north – 
south and is approximately 33 kilometres long and 14 kilometres wide.  The west coast is an 
almost unbroken stretch of sandy beach, interrupted only by the occasional rocky headland.  
The sand is composed of ground-up remains of seashells and is highly calcareous.  Westerly 
gales have, over centuries, blown this sand inland to form sand dunes and the machair, a strip 


of land about one kilometre wide.  To the east is the ‘black land’, a rib of land about one 
kilometre wide where the calcareous sand has overblown the underlying acid peat to form a 
soil of neutral pH.  Still further east are the acid peat moorlands and hills of South Uist, 
bisected by five fjordic sea lochs.  The east coast is rocky and the shore clothed in luxuriant 
growths of brown seaweeds.  Inland there are hundreds of freshwater lochs with pHs ranging 
from high in the machair to low in the peaty moorland areas. There  are also brackish lochs at  
the heads of the sea lochs. 
 
South Uist was completely covered by ice during the last Ice Age.  When the ice sheet 
retreated the only fish which could populate the inland freshwater lochs were those which 
could swim into them from the sea.  The fish fauna of these lochs is therefore restricted to five 
euryhaline species, which can tolerate both fresh and salt water.  All the lochs contain eels 
(Anguilla anguilla), most also have brown trout (Salmo trutta) and some additionally have sea 
trout (Salmo trutta), salmon (Salmo salar) and three-spined and, less commonly, ten-spined 
sticklebacks (Gasterosteus aculeatus and Pungitius pungitius respectively).  Many species of 
marine fish inhabit the coastal waters of South Uist.  Some are fast moving pelagic species, 
some inhabit the forests of seaweed which grow in sub-tidal areas and, to a lesser extent, the 
intertidal zone, while others live on the seabed.  
During the transects I did across the island, virtually no signs of otters were found on 
the west coast beaches, although occasionally there was a set of tracks in the sand.  Inland I 
found an amazing and complex network of otter runs, marked by sprainting sites, linking all 
the low-lying freshwater lochs and leading to the rocky seashores.  I discovered that there was 
a predictable pattern to this network – otters almost invariably taking the shortest route 
between adjacent lochs.  It was on the rocky east coast of the island, and particularly in its 
sheltered sea lochs, where most of the holts and other signs of otters were found - but it was 
also the same sheltered east coast sea lochs which the salmon farmers were most interested in.  
Otters living in the marine environment require freshwater for drinking and also for rinsing 
their fur.  Every small pool of freshwater close to the sea was marked by sprainting sites and 
showed signs of use.  I soon realised that there was a direct relationship between the standing 
crop of brown seaweeds, both intertidal and sub-tidal, and the number of otters.  The reason 
for this, of course, is food supply.  Otters principally forage in the seaweed for their prey. 
Small fish can be eaten at the surface whereas bigger fish are taken to the nearest reef and 
shore to eat. 
 
Most female otters, with dependent cubs, live throughout the year on the rocky 
seashore fishing in the sea, because it is here that there is the best year-round food supply.  
During the summer months many single adult males and sub-adult animals roam the extensive 
freshwater habitat feeding exclusively on eels and when these hibernate in the loch beds 
during winter and are difficult to catch, these otters return to the rocky coast to feed. 
 
In 1978 I mapped the locations of otter holts, water holes, runs and sprainting sites in 
the north east of South Uist, an area which included both Loch Sheilavaig, where Eric had set 
up the salmon farm, although he no longer worked there, and Loch Carnan, where Eric and I 
subsequently set up our own salmon farm.  Later in 1978 a group of fish cages was moored in 
North Channel, Loch Sheilavaig - within a few metres of an otter holt.  I thought that if there 
was going to be a conflict between otters and fish farming it would be here, and there was a 
chance that this holt might be abandoned.  I monitored the holt frequently for a year.  The 
otters continued using it - and still use it to this day. 
 
From December 1980 to January 1982 I did some radio telemetry work with Don 
Jefferies and Tony Mitchell-Jones of the then Nature Conservancy Council.  We caught and 
radio-tagged six otters, two of which lived in Loch Sheilavaig.  It was immediately obvious 
that both these animals were accustomed to the fish farm and all the associated activity. 


Basically they ignored them.  Both animals retained their radio harnesses for approximately 
three weeks. 
 
An adult male otter used a holt on an island near the mouth of the loch, some 500 
metres from fish cages.  On most days it left the holt to fish and feed around an extensive reef 
about 150 metres from the fish cages.  Of particular interest was a sub-adult female which 
inhabited a holt on an island half way between the shore base and the group of cages in North 
Channel.  During three weeks of radio tracking, the only reaction I saw it make to the fish 
farm was one day when it was on a reef near to its holt having just eaten a fish, and a fish 
farm boat came very close.  The otter slipped into the water and lay under the seaweed until 
the boat left, then it came out of the water and carried on as though nothing had happened.  
Neither otter predated the fish farm, and I did not see any evidence from spraints or hear from 
the men working at the fish farm that any of the otters in the loch ate any salmon.  The otters’ 
diet consisted mainly of butterfish (Pholis gunnellus) (55% of the diet throughout the year), 
supplemented with species of wrasse, pipefish, scorpion fish and small flatfish, gadoids and 
sea sticklebacks (Spinachia spinachia).   
 
In 1983, nine years after the fish farm in Loch Sheilavaig began, I was satisfied that 
the industry was not having an adverse effect on the otters and when Eric and I had the 
opportunity of setting up our own salmon farm in Loch Carnan, we took it.  Then I started 
monitoring the holts in Loch Carnan as well as continuing to monitor those in Loch 
Sheilavaig, and I am delighted to tell you that all the holts continued to be used as before. 
 
Two years ago Marine Harvest, who now own the salmon farm in Loch Sheilavaig, 
asked me to do another otter survey of the loch as part of an Environmental Impact 
Assessment.  I carried out the work at the same time of year as I did in 1978, a week of 
glorious weather in September 2001. It was a most exciting and interesting week.  There were 
signs of otters everywhere!  All the holts were being used, there was fresh spraint on 
sprainting sites, crushed vegetation, footprints in the wet peat - there were just as many signs 
of otters in Loch Sheilavaig in September 2001 as there had been in September 1978.  But 
although the situation regarding the otters was the same, the situation regarding the fish farm 
certainly wasn’t;  it had expanded considerably over the intervening 23 years.  There were 
now three large groups of cages in the loch, North Channel, Hole Bay and North Bay, and 
each of these has a large, active holt within a few metres of it.  Otters can, therefore, tolerate 
having cages very close to their holts.  There is a subsidiary holt close to the shore base and 
boat moorings, demonstrating that they can also tolerate these.  I then wondered ‘How much 
salmon has been harvested from Loch Sheilavaig since the farm was first set up?’ In excess of 
10,000 (ten thousand) tonnes had been harvested between 1974 and 2001.  If we assume an 
average food conversion ratio of 1.3:1 during this time, we can quickly calculate that 13,000 
(thirteen thousand) tonnes of fish food had been put into the loch and 10,000 tonnes of salmon 
taken out.   
 
All this fish farming activity over 27 years has not affected the top predator in the 
marine ecosystem, the otter.  Their presence in aquatic habitats is taken as an indication that 
the habitat is in a healthy state and in Scottish lochs where there are also fish farms this means 
that the latter are not having the dire effect on the environment that anti-fish farm activists 
would have us believe.  So, I am happy to be able to tell you that otters and fish farming is 
indeed a good news story.  And it is important for us to know this because fish farming is now 
one of the main stays, if not the main stay, of the Scottish rural economy.
