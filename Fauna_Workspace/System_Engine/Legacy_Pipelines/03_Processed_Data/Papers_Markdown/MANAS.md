ALEUTIAN DISEASE IN SEMI-AQUATIC CARNIVORES – A POSSIBLE ROLE OF LUTRA LUTRA  
IN THE PROCESS 
 
Sisco MAÑAS 
 
Servei de Proteccio de Fauna, Flora i Animals de Companyia,  
Direccio General de Boscos i Biodiversitat, Departament de Medi Ambient, 
Generalitat de Catalunya, Dr Roux 80, 08017 Barcelona, Spain.. 
email: francesc.manas@gencat.net/sisco.emink@terra.es 
 
 
The European mink (Mustela lutreola) is a riparian carnivore in high danger of extinction. The 
complete reason for this status remains unclear. In order to investigate the influence of diseases in 
the process, sanitary samples from this species, as well as from other riparian carnivores (American 
mink (Mustela vison), Polecats (Mustela putorius) and Eurasian otters (Lutra lutra)), were 
processed. 
Aleutian mink disease parvovirus (ADV), is a common, persistent viral infection of 
American mink and the ferret population worldwide. ADV is highly persistent in the environment, 
may be spread by asymptomatic carriers, and transmission can be both horizontally and vertically.  
The knowledge of the prevalence and effects of this disease in a threatened species like the 
European mink, and in other riparian carnivores may be of importance for their conservation. 
 
ADV in otters, was suspected in one animal from England in 1989, through postmortem 
findings and histopathological examinations, and a viral strain was detected by PCR in a carcass 
from Galicia (NW Spain), in 2001. 
 
Our study, actually in progress, involves clinical examination, serologies and protein 
electrophoresis in live animals and necropsies, histopathology and PCR and viral sequence studies 
in carcass samples. 
 
Preliminary results show a high prevalence of the disease in all species studied. The viral 
sequences obtained, until now, show no obvious segregation of sequence type for a particular 
species. 
 
In areas with several viral strains and high prevalence of ADV, otters are expanding their 
distribution range and suggest that viral strains present in these areas, could not affect the entire 
otter population. However, due to their large movements, otters could play a role in the 
dissemination of the disease over the riparian population.   
 
In order to avoid dissemination of diseases, strict sanitary protocols will have to be 
developed and monitored during reintroduction programs and translocation.
