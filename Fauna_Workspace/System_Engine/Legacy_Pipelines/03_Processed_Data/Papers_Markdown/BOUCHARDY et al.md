THE OTTER (LUTRA LUTRA) IN FRANCE. 
AN UPDATE OF THE RECOLONISATION OF OTTERS FROM THE MASSIF CENTRAL AND 
ATLANTIC COAST POPULATIONS 
 
Christian BOUCHARDY1, Rene ROSOUX2 & Rosemary GREEN3

Abstract.  In common with the rest of Europe, the French otter population started declining in the 1930s and in 
half a century had vanished from two thirds of the country, as a result of trapping, hunting and habitat 
degradation.  Stable populations were left only in the Massif Central and on the Atlantic coast. Studies in 1984 
showed the first signs of a recolonisation, which has continued in parts of France.  In other areas the decline has 
not been halted.   As part of the national Species Action Plan, the French Otter Group (French Mammal Society) 
has updated the distribution map to show changes since the 1993 survey.  The otter is now present in 50 out of 
93 Departements, the decline in the southern part of France has stopped for the first time in 50 years and the 
two main centres of population have almost merged. 
 
Keywords:  Eurasian otter, Lutra lutra, France, population increase 
 
 
1   INTRODUCTION   
 
As in many other parts of Europe, the decline in otter numbers throughout France began in the 
1930s (MATHIAS, 1933; KEMPF, 1980; BOUCHARDY, 1984, 1986), became significant in the 
1950s and reached its most critical phase in the 1970s, (BOUCHARDY, ROSOUX, AND 
BOULADE, 2001; BOUCHARDY AND ROSOUX, 2002).  Within 50 years the otter had 
disappeared from 60 of the 93 French Departements and the only stable populations 
remaining were in not more than ten Departements in the Massif Central and the marshes and 
rivers along the Atlantic coast.  In another 22 Departements, it had become very rare.  Both 
these regions had been recognised in 1933 as the most important habitat for the species 
(MATHIAS, 1933).  Elsewhere, otters had apparently disappeared or were present only in 
small, sporadic populations.  The main causes for the decline were hunting, trapping, water 
pollution and habitat degradation.  This article deals with changes in the otter distribution in 
France during the last 15 years. 
 
 
2   FIRST SIGNS OF RECOLONISATION   
In 1984, distribution studies in the Massif Central (BOUCHARDY, 1985) showed unexpected 
signs of recolonisation.  The otter had started to re-occupy some rivers and wetlands that had 
been deserted for many years. 
       In 1993, a series of surveys were carried out to produce a national otter distribution map 
(BOUCHARDY ET AL., 1993).  Over the whole of France two opposing trends were found to co-
exist: in regions where the otter was not abundant the inexorable decline continued, but in the 
Massif Central and along the Atlantic coast, from Brittany to the Spanish border, 
recolonisation was in progress.  This recolonisation is still underway today (COLL, 1999; 
BOUCHARDY, ROSOUX AND BOULADE, 2001; ROSOUX ET AL., 1995; ROSOUX, 1998). 
 
 


3   CURRENT SITUATION  
In 2001, the Otter Group of the French Mammal Society decided to update the otter 
distribution map as part of the national Species Action Plan, showing the changes in the status 
of the otter since the 1993 survey.  The new map clearly shows that distribution has changed 
considerably since the last survey (BOUCHARDY ET AL., 1993; ROSOUX ET AL., 1995).   For the 
first time since 1950, the status of the otter has improved in the south of France.  The species 
is now present in 51 Departements, and of these, 12 are fully or almost fully occupied (Figure 
1).  In Central France, recolonisation started from the core areas of Creuse (23), Corrèze (19) 
and Cantal (15).  In Departements where the otter had been absent or very scarce, such as 
Haute-Vienne (87), Dordogne (24), Aveyron (12), Lot (46) and Ardèche (07) the number of 
occupied waterways is increasing considerably.  
          The Atlantic coast has seen similar increases in many places, such as Brittany 
(LAFONTAINE in COLL, 1999), and in the region of Pays de Loire, (LODÉ in ROSOUX 1998; 
MONTFORT, in litt.; PAILLAT, in litt.).  For the first time for more than 50 years the two 
populations are about to merge. 
           On the other hand, the situation in the northern half of France remains critical and the 
otter is still threatened.  Very small, remote, threatened populations survive in a few 
Departements.  Signs of the species were found in Aube (10), on the River Orne (14-61), 
Drôme (26) and on parts of the upper Loire in the 2001 survey (BOUCHARDY AND ROSOUX, 
2002). 
           In Alsace (67-68), six captive-bred otters from the Centre de Reproduction des Loutres 
de Hunawihr were re-introduced since 1998 (RENAUD, 2002; MERCIER, 2004). The re-
introduced population currently occupies 130 kilometres of rivers, streams and marshes.  
However, now that natural otter recolonisation is underway, the French Otter Group 
recommends habitat protection, for food resources as well as for resting places, and 
environmental improvements to facilitate this natural spread, rather than the attractive, but 
hazardous re-introduction route as the way ahead for the otter in France (ROSOUX AND 
BOUCHARDY, 1990).   
 
Otter recolonisation in France is mainly due to legal protection which first came into 
force en 1972, when hunting was prohibited, and then in 1981 when the species became 
strictly protected.  Furthermore, the use of gin-traps to control semi-aquatic rodents was 
forbidden in 1994 and this seems to have had a really positive impact on the species’ 
populations.  Finally, quality and productivity of watercourses have improved due to human 
desertification.  On the other hand, in the large Atlantic marshes, habitat loss and pollution 
have increased, in particular in the Marais Poitevin (90,000 ha) and in the Aunis marshes 
(Charente-Maritime); although the species is still present, the number of otter signs have 
severely decreased since 1990.
