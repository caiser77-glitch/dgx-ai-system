Abstract: Saber toothed cats are one of the most popular extinct mammals even to paleontologists because 
of their extraordinarily long canines. The function of the long canines of the saber toothed cats has 
been the major interest of vertebrate paleontologists. And a great deal of theories has been proposed 
to explain the function of the long saber teeth, including their use as a can-opener on glyptodonts, 
a carrion slicer, a tree-climbing aid, a tool to grub for mollusks, and as a way of stabbing with a 
closed mouth. More recently, ‘shear-bite hypothesis’ and ‘throat bite for severing main vessels’ got 
more attention among paleontologists. However, the function of the elongated canines and the hunting 
strategy of the saber toothed cats are still controversial. This paper is purposed to review recent studies 
and understand the function of the saber teeth from a different point of view, and ultimately propose 
the most possible hunting behavior of the saber toothed cats. 
Key words: saber toothed cats, nimravids, felids, canines, gape 
INTRODUCTION
Extinct cat-like mammals having extremely elongated canine teeth are called saber toothed tigers 
or saber toothed cats in usual. However, all of these animals were not actually tigers and cats. Although 
there are still some debates, most paleontologists generally agree to categorize the saber toothed cats 
or cat-like carnivores into four groups: creodonts, nimravids, felids, and marsupial carnivores (Emerson 
and Radinsky, 1980; Valkenburgh, 1991).  
Creodonts (Order Creodonta) were the predominant predators in North America, Europe, Asia, and 
Africa during the Late Paleocene and Eocene epochs. They were once thought to be the ancestors 
of modern carnivores, however, nowadays creodonts are usually distinguished from modern carnivores 
and creodonts and modern carnivores are allocated in two separate orders: Creodonta and Carnivora 
(Emerson and Radinsky, 1980; Valkenburgh, 1999) (Table 1). Creodonts radiated into a number of 
different forms which resembled modern civets, dogs, bears, and cats. Oxyaenids (Family Oxyaenidae) 
were one of the earlier creodonts. They had rather cat like appearance. Hyaenodontids (family 
Hyaenodontidae) were dog-like creodonts with some cursorial adaptations (Valkenburgh, 1999).  
Most of the saber toothed carnivores that are mentioned in this paper are belong to the Order Carnivora. 
Nimravids (Family Nimravidae) are actually a separate family that evolved parallel to the true cats. 
It is commonly accepted that nimravids are not ancestors of true cats and actually on a different 
lineage in phylogeny. Nimravids appeared earlier in the late Eocene of Eurasia and North America. 
They shared some characteristics with true cats: short faces, carnassials, reduction of posterior molars, 
and retractile claws (Martin, 1980). The main differences are the more plantigrade (walks on the entire 
sole of the feet) and the lack of bony septum in auditory bulla (Martin, 1980). Most Nimravids had 
a poorly ossified bulla and a cartilaginous septum in it. In true cats, auditory bulla for sound perception 
is well developed (Savage, 1977; Martin, 1980; Joeckel et al., 2002) (Fig. 1). Some of nimravid species 
had long canine teeth as in the true saber toothed cats. That is why nimravids are called false saber 
toothed cats. Martin (1980) grouped the types of their canines into three: short, round canines (conical-tooth-
ed); highly elongated, flattened canines with fine serrations (dirk-toothed); and somewhat elongated, 
more coarsely serrated flattened canines (scimitar-toothed) (Fig. 2). The dirk-toothed forms include

CANINE LENGTH AND MOUTH OPENING
It seems likely that long saber teeth were more effective and powerful killing tools than relatively 
short upper canine teeth of modern cats. However, as it is going further, the long saber teeth pose 
us difficult questions. As Martin (1980) pointed out, the canine teeth of nimravids and felids show 
diversity in their shape and length. In spite of the diversity in canine shape and length, it is certain 
that the size of object is in inverse proportion to the canine length. In other words, cats with longer 
canines should open the mouth wider to bite the same sized objects (Fig. 3). 
Regarding mouth opening, it is important to distinguish potential rotational arc of the mandible 
from the effective gape. The arc of rotation is the angle between the occulusal planes of mandible 
in closed and opened position. But it is not equal to the size of the objects that can enter the mouth. 
The effective gape is the arc measured tip to tip between the upper and lower canines as the mouth 
is opened. So the effective gape is the main determinant for the size of biting objects. The arc of 
rotation of the mandible in most modern cats is within the range of 60o-65o. The potential rotational 
arc of Barbourofelis is estimated over 100o, but the effective gape is just about 60o (Martin, 1984). 
It implies that even though Barbourofelis could open the mouth much wider, the object size that 
they could bite was almost same with modern cats do.
It has been advocated that some osteological characteristics such as shortened coronoid process, 
lower glenoid fossa, and anterior rotation of occipital region are suitable for wider gape (Martin, 1980).

gape in saber-toothed cats.  First they measured the degree of temporalis stretch to predict the maximum

THE MUSCLE STRETCH AND FORCE
Jaw movement and mouth opening are closely related with jaw muscles. Muscle anatomy and physiology 
is essential to understand jaw movement of saber toothed cats. The sarcomere is the basic functional 
unit of skeletal muscle. Each muscle cell is composed of myofibrils, and each myofibril is a chain 
of sarcomeres. The sarcomere is composed of two types of protein filament (Fig. 6). The thick myosin 
filaments are composed of myosin proteins and located in the A band. The thin actin filaments are 
located in the I bands at the resting state, but extend into the A bands for muscle contraction. Muscle 
contracts when the myosin is stimulated by a nerve impulse. Then the myosin proteins of thick myosin 
filament bind with the actin to form actomyosin. Consequently actin filaments of both sides are pulled 
toward the center of the sarcomere. The overlap of myosin and actin filaments induces the contraction 
of muscle fibers. As a nerve impulse disappears, the myosin filaments are released from actin filaments 
and a skeletal muscle itself is relaxed.
A muscle contracts to produce power. The muscle length is inevitably related with power (tension). 
This relationship has been explained by the tension-length curves of a muscle fiber (Fig. 7). Muscle 
contraction is developed by the interaction of actin-myosin complex. So the relationship of muscle 
length and tension should be explained as the interaction of these myofilaments. A muscle fiber develops 
its greatest tension when there is optimal overlap between actin and myosin filaments because of 
the maximum number of cross bridges between them. This occurs at a sarcomere length of approximately 
2.25 µm. If the sarcomere is stretched to a longer length, fewer actin-myosin complexes are produced 
and that results in a decrease in tension. If the sarcomere is stretched to 3.65 µm or longer, no myosin 
cross bridges can bind to the actin filaments, as a result, no contraction occurs. When muscle length 
is short, the number of cross bridges and resultant muscle tension are decreased. In ammals, the resting

TEMPOROMANDIBULAR JOINT
Only the bony structures of saber-toothed cats are preserved as fossils and it is hard to get information 
of soft tissue, such as musculatures and coat patterns, through the fossil remains. Just minimal information 
about soft tissue of saber-toothed cats can be acquired through a careful inspection of bony surface 
and anatomical comparison of their skeletons with the modern relatives. Furthermore, there have been 
little published papers explaining ligament structures around the temporomandibular joint in saber-toothed 
cats. The movement of temporomandibular joint in saber-toothed cats would supposedly be similar 
with that of human or other mammals in some respects. Here, a possible model of temporomandibular 
joint movement in saber-toothed cats can be proposed on the basis of functional morphology and 
human anatomy. The temporomandibular joint is a freely movable articulation between the condyle 
of mandible and squamous portion of the temporal bone. The articular surface of the temporal bone 
is composed of the central, concave fossa (usually called glenoid fossa) and the convex bony protrusion 
(usually called articular eminence). The meniscus or articular disc is a fibrous, saddle shaped structure, 
which is interposed between the condyle and the temporal bone, separating the joint spaces into upper 
and lower divisions. The meniscus varies in thickness: the thinner, central intermediate zone separates 
thicker portions, the anterior and the posterior band. The posterior band in the posterior aspect the 
thickened disc is confluent with highly innervated and vascularized tissue, which is termed the bilaminar 
zone. The bilaminar zone provides considerable anterior freedom of movement for the meniscus. On 
the lateral aspect the temporomandibular joint is protected by a capsule or capsular ligament. And 
the portion of the capsular ligament that has been thickens laterally and anteriorly is called the tempor-
omandibular ligament. The temporomandibular ligament is divided into the inner horizontal portion 
(sphenomandibular ligament, internal lateral ligament) and the outer oblique portion (external lateral 
ligament) (Gray, 1984; McCarthy, 1990) (Fig. 9). Soft tissue is usually composed of two types of 
collagen, Type I and III. And collagen types are closely related with mechanical tissue function. For

STABBING
Most previous studies have been focused on the movement of upper canines as a killing tool. In 
the early 20th century, Matthew (1910) proposed ‘the stabbing theory’ to explain the main killing 
mechanism of saber-toothed cats. He explained that the long upper canines would move downward, 
like a human hand wielding a knife, and the mandible would play no role in killing the prey. To 
support this hypothesis, he emphasized the importance of brachiocephalic muscles and scalenic muscles 
as head depressors. However, some authors pointed out some problems in the stabbing theory (Turner, 
1997). First, the flat and long canines are too fragile, and vulnerable to tooth fracture. If the canines 
stroke a bone during the attack, they would be broken easily. Second, saber teeth were much blunter 
than a steel knife and it would be so hard to penetrate thick hide of preys with the blunt canines. 
Third, The greater force would be required to inset the upper canines deep into the flesh if there 
were no counter action of the mandible. Akersten (1985) suggested a different interpretation, ‘canine 
shear-bite theory’, to overcome these problems. The upper canines would play the main role in biting, 
and the mandible would play a supporting role as the counter part. He also emphasized the importance 
of the altanto-mastoid musculatures as head flexors. More recently Anton et al. (2004) clarified the 
importance of atlanto-mastoid musculature through a dissection of extant felid cadaver specimens. 
In extant felids, well-developed obliquus capitis cranialis and obliquus capitalis caudalis were identified. 
They applied this finding to the fossil specimens of Homotherium latidens and concluded that most 
muscle fibers of the obliquus capitis cranialis would extend well below the center of rotation of the 
atlanto-occipital articulation, and therefore obliquus capitis cranialis would act as a strong head flexor 
(Fig. 15).

or shear-biting. However, it does not mean the head depressor muscles produce the main force for 
insertion of canines deep into the flesh. The upper jaw including upper canines was a relatively fixed 
part and more movable lower jaw should rotate upward for a successful stabbing or bite. The tip 
of the upper canines could play as an anchoring point and the mandible could rotate upward pushing 
flesh toward the upper canines. It might be more natural and powerful. However, it is actually unnecessary 
to distinguish which jaw moves first or dominantly because both jaws should work at the same time 
as scissors, nippers and hands in handclap.

THE CURVATURE OF CANINE
To understand the function of saber teeth, not only the size but also the curvature of the canines 
should be taken into consideration. The relationship between the curvature and potential penetration 
of dinosaur claw has been studied already. The hind limb of Deinonychus, a dromaeosaurid dinosaur, 
was peculiar. Its second digit was strongly built and curved greatly as the name Deinonychus (terrible 
claw) implies. And the mechanical structure of the curved claw can be analyzed on the basis of functional 
morphology (Ostrom, 1969) (Fig. 19). It is actually a circular movement. The angle between the acting 
line of the claw and the tangent line being perpendicular to the radius of the acting circle at any 
given point of the circle indicates the force that can be produced by the claw; that is, the smaller 
the angle, the stronger the force. The greatest force able to be produced by the claw should act along 
the tangent line of the acting circle.
This geometric analysis of the claw movement can be applied to the saber toothed cats because 
their canines would show similar circular movement, even though the most propulsive power was 
produced by the mandible and the upper canines would show only a passive movement (Fig. 20).

TOOTH CONTOUR AND SERRATIONS
The diversity of saber teeth has been well described by Martin (1980). However, the functional 
differences in three types of canines are not clearly defined yet. It would probably be closely related 
with other characteristics of skeletons and hunting patterns of the cat. The relatively short legs of 
dirk-toothed cats make us to postulate they were ambush hunters. On the contrary, the long legged 
scimitar-toothed forms probably were cursorial predators (Martin, 1980, 2000). He insisted that the 
upper canines of saber-toothed cats do not resemble sabers because real sabers have the cutting edge 
on the outside of the curve, whereas the cutting edge on the canine teeth is on the inside. He proposed 
the Arabian Jambiya as a model of saber teeth. The Arabian Jambiya is a curved dagger with a vertical 
crest (or a groove) on the flat surface. Most Arabians use this knife for throat cutting, but not for 
stabbing (Fig. 21).
The teeth of predatory animals usually reflect their habit of feeding and hunting patterns. Carnivorous 
dinosaurs also showed diversity of teeth in shape and size. The flat, finely serrated teeth of 
Carcharodontosaurus and the conical, vertically grooved teeth of Spinosaurus can be examples. 
Carcharodontosaurus, a huge carnivorous dinosaur found in the Cretaceous deposit of northern Africa, 
had flat teeth with fine serrations around the edge, which resembles scimitar- or dirk-toothed forms 
of saber toothed cats. Spinosaurus, another carnivorous African dinosaur, had conical teeth with vertical 
grooves running from the root to the tip on the surface, which resembles conical teeth of saber toothed 
cats (Fig. 22).

Regarding diversity of tooth shape, some issues come into question: What are the differences of 
the serration and vertical groove? And how do the flat and conical teeth work differently? Basically 
serrations and vertical grooves are designed to facilitate cutting and stabbing action respectively. They 
can reduce the contact area between the tooth and the object, and as a result the frictional force 
of the tooth can be decreased. Furthermore, serrations can trap and cut a lump of meat to a depth 
determined by the height of the serrations. In addition to this, serrations help to grip and rip when 
teeth are withdrawn (Abler, 1992; Anyonge, 1996). Dirk-toothed felids had canines with shallower 
serrations than scimitar-toothed forms, suggesting that the canines of dirk-toothed forms were less 
effective in ripping prey than that of scimitar-toothed forms. For example, Smilodon, a dirk-toothed 
felid, had fine serrations around the edges of the saber teeth. The serrations were more apparent in 
juveniles and wear smooth with age. Aged individuals of Smilodon may have relied more on penetrating 
power of their smoothly worn saber teeth than on the ripping ability of serrations (Anyonge, 1996). 
The hunting strategy of saber toothed cats may have differed according to their canine contour. 
The scimitar- or dirk-toothed cats would possibly adopt shearing or severing vital structures of the 
prey as their hunting strategy. And the serrations around the edge of saber teeth would support this 
action. On the contrary, conical-toothed cats would prefer stabbing or suffocation to kill the prey. 
Their somewhat robust and shorter canines with vertical grooves along the vertical axis would be 
suitable for this.
 
TOOTH WEAR AND BREAKAGE
 
Microscopic findings of the canine surface also provide us an important clue to understand the 
canine function of the saber toothed cats. The severity of tooth wearing was roughly correlated with 
the food-consuming patterns and the number of bite for killing and feeding. Smilodon fatalis had 
fewer features than any of the extant carnivores, and showed no consistent similarity in features of 
microwear on canine teeth to any of the modern carnivores (Anyonge, 1996). Based on this microscopic 
finding, he suggested that perhaps Smilodon used its upper canines exclusively for killing, but not 
in feeding, which resulted in fewer features. Smilodon may have avoided the contact of upper canines 
with bone during killing and feeding. Anyonge’s interpretation is reasonable and consistent with previous 
stabbing theory or shear-bite hypothesis. But it seems to be somewhat different from Valkenburgh’s 
investigation of tooth breakage in Rancho La Brean carnivores (Valkenburgh and Hertel, 1993). Based 
on their study, the upper canines of Smilodon were not fractured at a significantly higher frequency

OTHER POSSIBILITIES
Many possible theories have been proposed to explain the function of the long saber teeth, including 
their use as a can-opener on glyptodonts (Brandes, 1900), a carrion slicer (Bohlin, 1940), a tree-climbing 
aid, a tool to grub for mollusks, and as a way of stabbing with a closed mouth (Matthew, 1901). 
Some of these interpretations sound bizarre and hard to accept. Nowadays most paleontologists consider 
the saber toothed cats as active hunters using their long canines as killing tools whether it is stabbing 
or shear-biting. Saber toothed cats are not the only creatures that have that long canines. Walrus 
and musk deer also have long canines. (Fig. 25). The tusks of walrus are much bigger than the canines 
of saber-toothed cats. Walrus usually eats mollusks and shells and musk deer eats plants. Then why 
do they have these long canines? Walrus uses the tusk for fighting with rival males and supposedly 
grubbing for mollusks at times. Musk deer, an herbivore, also uses the long canines for fighting with 
rival males at times. However, the main purpose of their long canines is probably just to display 
their magnificent appearance to females and rival males in herd. Most researchers have tried to interpret 
the saber teeth as killing tools only. However, our endeavors to interpret their long canines from 
a different point of view will be necessary to understand them better.
Many paleontologists have mentioned the fragility of long, slender saber teeth (Martin 1980; Turner, 
1997). The long saber teeth may have been vulnerable to bone-tooth contact and torsion force. If 
the canines struck bony structures during inserting into the flesh, they would be broken easily. So 
the canines should be used with an extreme caution. The predator could use the long canines just 
after complete subduing the prey. So some authors have proposed scenarios of saber toothed cats 
hunting in groups (Gonyea, 1976; Akersten, 1985). And there might be another problem in using 
the long canines. The withdrawal of canines would be more difficult than the inserting of them. And 
the withdrawal of the longer canines would be more difficult than that of shorter one. If the hunter 
leaped on the prey and inserted the canines deep into the flesh but failed to pull down the prey, 
it would be critical to the hunter. If the hunter could not withdraw the teeth swiftly, the cat would 
be dragged away by the prey animal. Therefore the upper canines of saber-toothed cats should be 
used in the case of complete subduing the prey only and hunting in groups would probably be safer 
and more effective.

CONCLUSIONS
Even though there have been many hypotheses, the function and killing mechanism of their long 
canines in saber toothed cats is not clarified yet. Based on author’s own interpretation and other previous 
studies, the function of the long saber teeth and the possible hunting strategy of saber toothed cats 
can be summarized as follows. 
1. It is beyond dispute that the longer canines would entail the wider gape.
2. The head flexor muscles that inserted on the mastoid tip would not play the main role for inserting 
canines. The masticating muscles such as temporalis and masster, rather than head flexors, would 
play more important role in saber teeth action. In other words, the mandible would play more important 
role than some paleontologists thought before.
3. The longer, more recurved canines could inflict only superficial wounds to the prey. And attacking 
on the flat surface of the abdomen may have been less effective to kill the prey. So the saber 
toothed cats having highly elongated and recurved canines should attack the ventral or lateral neck 
of the prey and sever the carotid arteries to kill their preys.  
4. The possibility of their social behavior and the usage of long canines for the purpose of display 
to the rival males or females should not be excluded.
