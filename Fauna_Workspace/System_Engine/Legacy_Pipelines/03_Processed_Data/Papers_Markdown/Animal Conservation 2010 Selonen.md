by long-term diet analyses of avian predators

Abstract
Conservation of species requires knowledge on population changes in time, but
achieving such data in proper spatio-temporal scales can often be difﬁcult for rare
and vulnerable species. We used long-term (1950–2005) diet data of three avian
predators (the nocturnal Ural and eagle owls and the diurnal goshawk) from four
landscapes to study population changes of two forest-dwelling species, the
Siberian ﬂying squirrel Pteromys volans and the red squirrel Sciurus vulgaris in
Finland. We also determined the role of vulnerable ﬂying squirrel in the diet of
these predators. Both squirrel species were relatively rare in the diets of the three
avian predators, with the exception of red squirrel in the diet of goshawks. The
Siberian ﬂying squirrel also appears to be a less important prey for avian predators
than ﬂying squirrel species (Glaucomys spp.) are in North American forest
communities. Numbers of consumed ﬂying squirrels, but not of red squirrels,
increased as the abundances of voles increased in the diets of eagle and Ural owls,
that is more ﬂying squirrels were consumed during the good vole years than the
poor ones. The declining trends in the diets of three avian predators appeared to be
quite similar for both studied squirrel species, possibly indicating that the process
behind the decline has been the same.
Introduction
Critical steps in the conservation of endangered species are
achieving knowledge on population change in proper spa-
tio-temporal scales and identifying factors that cause popu-
lations to decline or prevent their recovery (Primack, 2006).
One important factor that can affect population density of
vertebrates is predation (Valkama et al., 2005; Salo et al.,
2010). Predators may be generalists feeding on several prey
species or specialized on one or a few main prey species. In
the latter case, the predator may use alternative prey species
that are preyed on more when numbers of the main prey
decrease (e.g. Korpim ¨aki, Huhtala & Sulkava, 1990).
Possible alternative prey species used by vole-eating and
other middle-sized vertebrate predators in boreal forests of
Eurasia are the Siberian ﬂying squirrel Pteromys volans and
the red squirrel Sciurus vulgaris (Wid ´en, 1987; Korpim ¨aki
et al., 1990). The Siberian ﬂying squirrel is a nocturnal,
arboreal rodent, which nests in spruce-dominated boreal
forests from Finland to eastern Siberia and Japan (Ognev,
1966). In North America, ﬂying squirrels (Glaucomys sabri-
nus and Glaucomys volans) are important prey for several
predator species, being, for example, the main prey for the
endangered spotted owl Strix occidentalis (Carey, Horton &
Biswell, 1992; Forsman et al., 2001). Consequently, Glauc-
omys species have been the focus of considerable research
during the past two decades in North America (e.g. Carey
et al., 1992; Forsman et al., 2001; Smith, 2007). Instead, the
relationship with predators is not studied for the Eurasian
equivalent of Glaucomys species, the Siberian ﬂying squirrel,
although the ecology of Siberian ﬂying squirrel is otherwise
quite well known (e.g. Reunanen et al., 2002; Hurme et al.,
2008; Selonen & Hanski, 2010a,b). The relationship of
Pteromys (from now on ﬂying squirrel) and its predators is
particularly interesting because it is protected by an EU
habitat directive (Anonymous, 1992) and classiﬁed vulner-
able in Finland (Rassi et al., 2001). According to the data of
Hokkanen, T ¨orm ¨al ¨a & Vuorinen (1982), the ﬂying squirrel
population has declined in Finland from 1949 to 1979.
Based on short-term data sets on mark-recaptured popula-
tions, the decline appears to have continued since 1979
(Hanski, 2006; Lampila et al., 2009).
An interesting co-existing species to be compared with the
ﬂying squirrel in terms of population change is the red
squirrel. The red squirrel is a diurnal, arboreal rodent, which
is wide spread in forested areas throughout the Palaearctic
Region (Corbet, 1978). The red squirrel (weight 200–390 g)
is larger than the ﬂying squirrel (100–170 g). The red squirrel
is still common in North European boreal forests, thus
being good reference species when the possible population

One reason for lower numbers of ﬂying squirrels compared
with red squirrels is that ﬂying squirrels are found in a much
narrower range of habitats than the red squirrel. The ﬂying
squirrel is dependent on deciduous trees, in particular aspen
Populus tremula, and alder Alnus incana, as a food and
cavity source within the spruce forest. Because of forest
management actions, suitable habitat for ﬂying squirrels has
become scarce in the landscape (e.g. Reunanen et al., 2002;
Selonen & Hanski, 2004). Instead, the red squirrel has been
dramatically declining in British Isles and in Italy (Gurnell
& Lurz, 1997) due to interaction with the introduced grey
squirrel Sciurus carolinensis (Gurnell et al., 2006). Surpris-
ingly, proper data for the population trends of red squirrel
are lacking in North Europe.
We used long-term diet data of three avian predators, the
Ural owl Strix uralensis, the eagle owl Bubo bubo and the
goshawk Accipiter gentilis collected from prey remains in
nest sites of four different landscapes to analyse the occur-
rence of ﬂying and red squirrels as a prey of these predators.
The among-year variation in the diet composition of these
three predator species is well studied (Valkama et al., 2005),
and in this study, we concentrate on the occurrence of
squirrels in the diet. Ural owls mainly prey on voles of the
genera Microtus, Clethrionomys and Arvicola, and the vole
abundance strongly determines diet composition and repro-
ductive output of Ural owls (Korpim ¨aki & Sulkava, 1987;
Pieti ¨ainen, 1989). Although eagle owls are more diverse
predators, their main prey in Finland are voles, but they
also include larger prey items than Ural owls in their diet
(Korpim ¨aki et al., 1990). A major proportion of the diet of
the goshawk in North Europe is not only woodland grouse
(Tetraonidae) but also corvids and small- and medium-sized
mammals; in particular, red squirrels are much used prey
items (Tornberg, Korpim ¨aki & Byholm, 2006). All the three
predator species are site-tenacious and mainly monogamous
(Cramp & Simmons, 1980; Saurola, 1987; Penteriani, 1996).
Ural and eagle owls are mainly hunting at night and at dawn
and dusk (Korpim ¨aki & Huhtala, 1986; Sergio et al., 2008),
whereas diurnal goshawks are mainly hunting in the morn-
ing and in the evening (Reif & Tornberg, 2006).
Here, our ﬁrst aim is to quantify the importance of ﬂying
and red squirrels as prey species for Ural owls, eagle owls
and goshawks. Second, we attempt to detect the possible
decline of ﬂying squirrel numbers in the predator diet data
covering several decades and to compare ﬂying squirrel
change in prey numbers to that of the more common red
squirrel. Third, we study whether the consumption of
squirrels by vole-eating Ural and eagle owls are dependent
on the number of main prey (i.e. voles) in their diets.
Because red squirrels are day active but ﬂying squirrels are
mainly nocturnal, we expected that the latter is more
common in the diet of nocturnal owls than in the diet of
day-active goshawks (and vice versa for the red squirrel).
However, red squirrels can be active very early in the
morning (Tonkin, 1983) when Ural and eagle owls are still
hunting. In addition, ﬂying squirrels can also be active
during daytime. This is particularly the case during the
mating season in spring and also during early summer when
juveniles and lactating females often forage during daytime
(V. Selonen, unpubl. data).
Methods
Study areas and diet data
We conducted our study in four landscapes in south-western
and central Finland: (1) Kanta-H ¨ame (KH) c. 611N, 241E in
the vicinity of H ¨ameenlinna; (2) Central Finland (CF) c.
621300N, 241E in the vicinity of Virrat-Keuruu; (3) South
Ostrobothnia (SO) c. 631N, 231E in the vicinity of Ilmajoki-
Kauhava; and (4) Central Ostrobothnia (CO) c. 641N,
241300E in the vicinity of Ylivieska-Kalajoki. The study
areas covered 4000 km2 in KH, 2500 km2 in CF, 6000 km2
in SO and 3000 km2 in CO. Landscapes in SO and CO are
mainly covered by pine- and spruce-dominated forests
(50%), agricultural land (30%) and peatland bogs (20%),
whereas in KH and CF the landscape mainly consists of
spruce- and pine-dominated forests (70%) and water bodies
(10–20%); but, the proportions of agricultural land and
peatland bogs are much lower in the latter two areas.
We had diet data from 335 avian predator territories. The
nest site in some of the territories was sampled during
several years (mean  SD: 1.7  1.3 years, maximum 7 years)
and altogether we had diet data from 576 predator nests
(from now on ‘diet sample’) between the years 1950–2005
(Table 1). A diet sample consisted of a collection of all
pellets and prey remains from one nest and from the ground
near the nest during the breeding season. Searching effort
was kept constant over the years of the study period. Prey

Statistical analyses
All the statistical analyses were performed with the GLIM-
MIX procedure in SAS 9.2. The occurrence of squirrels in the
diet-sample data was modelled with events/trial option
(binomial data). The number of squirrel individuals in the
diet sample was a dependent variable and the total number
of prey individuals in the diet sample was a binomial
denominator. Individual raptors may specialize on certain
prey and we avoided this problem by selecting territory as a
repeated measure in the models. Based on the repeated
measure, the degrees of freedom were adjusted with the
Kenward–Roger method of determination of error degrees
of freedom (procedure GLIMMIX; Kenward & Roger, 1997).
Year (1950–2005) was selected as a covariant in the model.
Variables predator species (Ural owl, eagle owl or goshawk)
and study area (KH, CF or SO; CO was omitted because
data existed only for Ural owls and the number of diet
samples was low) were included as class variables in the
model. The effects of interactions were tested during the
model selection, but these were not included in the ﬁnal
models to allow convergence in the parameter estimation
procedure. To test whether yearly variation in the number of
diet samples affected our results, we added the total number
of diet samples in each year in the models. Because this
variable did not have any effects, it was omitted from the
ﬁnal models. The differences between study areas and
predator species were further tested with pairwise compar-
isons with the Tukey–Kramer adjustment for multiple
comparisons (least squares means comparisons in the
above described model, procedure GLIMMIX). We also calcu-
lated mean numbers of squirrels in diet samples per each
territory for presenting the data for different study areas and
predators.
To test whether the ﬂying and red squirrel numbers
changed similarly in time, we pooled the ﬂying and red
squirrel data as a single dependent variable (the number of
squirrels within a diet sample) and added a class variable for
squirrel species (ﬂying or red) in the model (model described
above; territory was a repeated measure, but note that when
the ﬂying and red squirrel numbers were compared with
each other within the same diet samples, the Kenward—
Roger-adjusted degrees of freedoms were similar to degrees
of freedoms without the repeated measure, procedure GLIM-
MIX). We evaluated the effect of interaction term between
year and squirrel species in this model, which could indicate
whether the red and ﬂying squirrel numbers changed differ-
ently in time.
We analysed whether the total number of voles (Microtus
spp. and Clethrionomys spp.) in the diet sample was asso-
ciated with the ﬂying and red squirrel numbers in predator
diet. This analysis was modelled as a Poisson distribution
(procedure GLIMMIX) with the number of squirrel indivi-
duals being the dependent variable. Year and the total
number of voles were selected as a covariant. Study area
was class variable and a territory-repeated measure. Analy-
sis was performed separately for Ural and eagle owls, which
not only use voles as their main prey but also use much
alternative prey when the vole numbers are low (Korpim ¨aki
et al., 1990; Sulkava et al., 2008). In addition, for the eagle
owl we analysed whether the number of water voles Arvicola
terrestris affected squirrel numbers (numbers of water voles
and other voles do not necessarily ﬂuctuate in close syn-
chrony; Korpim ¨aki et al., 2005). We also calculated the
percentage weight of squirrels from the total mass of prey in
the diet sample. Weight of prey was calculated by multi-
plying the number of identiﬁed individuals in a diet sample
with an average adult weight of the species. This was not
performed for goshawks, because for some diet samples of
goshawk we had only data for total number of prey
individuals in the diet sample and lacked the information
for which species these belonged to.
Results
Both squirrel species were relatively rare in the diets of three
avian predators (the ﬂying squirrel mostly o1% from the
total number of prey individuals), with the exception of red
squirrel in the diet of goshawks (Tables 2 and 3). The ﬂying
squirrel numbers were lower in the goshawk than in the
eagle owl diet samples, but between other inter-speciﬁc
comparisons there were no differences in the number of
used ﬂying squirrels (Table 3). Flying squirrels composed a
similar proportion of the total prey mass consumed by Ural
owls (0.3  0.8%) and eagle owls (0.3  0.7%; for goshawk
we lacked data for the total prey mass). The red squirrel
numbers were lower in the owl than in the goshawk diet
samples, but between Ural and eagle owls there was no
difference in the number of used red squirrels (Table 3).

Discussion
We found that the numbers of ﬂying and red squirrels have
declined during 1950–2005 in the diets of three avian
predator species. Both squirrel species were relatively infre-
quent in the diets of avian predators, except the red squirrel
in the diet of goshawks. The number of consumed ﬂying
squirrels, but not of red squirrels, increased as the number of
voles increased in the diets of eagle and Ural owls.
Our work depends on the assumption that the decline of
squirrel numbers in the diet samples of predators reﬂects the
abundance of squirrels in the landscape and not the changes in
prey preferences of avian predators. In the absence of evidence
to the contrary, this is a reasonable assumption, as main prey
species of studied predators are known to either decline or do
not show any long-term trends in Finland (forest grouse:
Tornberg, M ¨onkk ¨onen & Pahkala, 1999, voles: e.g. Kor-
pim ¨aki et al., 2005; Lehikoinen et al., 2009). The red squirrel
was also intensively hunted in Finland in the beginning of our
study period (up to 1960s), but the hunting pressure has
declined since then, nowadays being few thousand individuals
per year (Finnish Game and Fisheries Research Institute,
2008). Thus, if anything, the decreasing hunting pressure
should be reﬂected as an increase in the red squirrel numbers
in our data. The ﬂying squirrel has not been hunted during our
study period (1950–2005).
The data for long-term population trends of ﬂying and
red squirrels have been limited in boreal forests of North
Europe. For the red squirrel, there appears to be no proper
data for evaluating population trends (Harto Lind ´en, pers.
comm., but see Helle & Wikman, 2007). As our results
suggest that the red squirrel numbers have been declining, it
might be worthwhile to increase the effort in monitoring the
population trends of this species. For the ﬂying squirrel, the
available data for population changes were based on public
and hunter inquiries and these data ended to year 1979
(Hokkanen et al., 1982). Inquiry data may also have
considerable weaknesses when trying to ﬁnd out population
trends (Lind ´en et al., 1996). Since Hokkanen et al.’s (1982)
study, a declining trend in ﬂying squirrel numbers has been
recorded in a few mark-recaptured populations (Hanski,
2006; Lampila et al., 2009). In our study, the declining trend
appeared to be quite similar for both ﬂying and red squir-
rels. This might suggest that the process behind the decline
has been the same, forest management being one plausible
explanation for the observed decline, as both ﬂying and red
squirrels are known to potentially suffer from the forest
habitat loss (Delin & Andr ´en, 1999; Lampila et al., 2009).
More ﬂying squirrels were consumed by Ural and eagle
owls when the number of voles in the diet samples was high.
Vole numbers in the diet of these owls closely positively
correlate with their abundance in the landscape (Korpim ¨aki
et al., 1990). Therefore, our results indicate that more ﬂying
squirrels are consumed by these owls in the good rather than
poor vole years. Indeed, the actual number of alternative
prey killed by predators may be higher in years with high
rather than low densities of their main prey (Norrdahl &
Korpim ¨aki, 2000). The possible explanation for the high
number of ﬂying squirrels killed by Ural and eagle owls in
the good vole years could be the increased hunting effort of
owl parents due to larger owl broods when voles are
abundant. That the red squirrel numbers did not correlate
with the vole numbers may be the result of among-year
ﬂuctuations of red squirrel populations, which likely are not
in synchrony with the vole cycles (Gurnell, 1983).
The vulnerable ﬂying squirrel was relatively infrequent in
the diet of all the studied predators. In addition to the
predators studied here, the only predators that may have
an inﬂuence on ﬂying squirrels in our study areas are the
pine marten Martes martes, the tawny owl Strix aluco and
the feral cat Felis catus. The ﬂying squirrel does not appear
to be any major prey item in the diet of pine martens
(Hanski et al., 2001) and numbers of feral cats have likely
been decreasing in our study areas since 1950. Of our four
study landscapes, tawny owls frequently breed only in the
KH, where only ﬁve ﬂying squirrels out of 3718 prey
individuals of tawny owls have been recorded (S. Sulkava,
unpubl. data). It appears that the Siberian ﬂying squirrel is a
less important prey for avian predators than ﬂying squirrel
species (Glaucomys sp.) are in North American forest com-
munities, where they are an essential link in the food chain
(Carey, 2000; Smith, 2007). For example, northern ﬂying
squirrels G. sabrinus can consist up to 60% of the biomass
consumed by spotted owls (Carey et al., 1992). Compared
with the abundance of Glaucomys species in the North
American forests (Carey, 2000; Smith, 2007), the Siberian
ﬂying squirrel is a relatively rare species. The rarity of ﬂying
squirrel probably explains the low numbers observed in the
diet of three avian predators. The numbers of the ﬂying
squirrel in the diet samples were also clearly lower than
those of the red squirrel. In our data, prey preferences of
predators have obviously inﬂuenced the observed squirrel
numbers, but in general, the red squirrel is clearly common
in Finnish forests, the ﬂying squirrel being much less so.
There also appears to be much variation in the ﬂying
squirrel densities between different areas in Finland (Hans-
ki, 2006). Indeed, we found signiﬁcant differences in the
ﬂying squirrel, but not in the red squirrel, numbers between
study areas.
The nocturnal ﬂying squirrel was more frequent in the
diet of eagle owls than in the diet of goshawks, but
unexpectedly Ural owls did not use more ﬂying squirrels
than goshawks. The numbers of diurnal red squirrels were
expectedly higher in the diet of diurnal goshawks than in the
diet of nocturnal owls. The observed proportion of red
squirrels in the goshawk diet (around 10% of the total
number of prey individuals) appears to be close to average
value observed in the earlier studies of goshawk diet (re-
viewed by Petty, Lurz & Rushton, 2003; Tornberg et al.,

2006). During the breeding season, proportions of red
squirrels in the diet of goshawks are observed to increase
up to 30% of total prey number (Sulkava, 1964; Tornberg
et al., 2006) and during winter even higher values have been
recorded (Wid ´en, 1987).
Here, we used unique long-term data from the diet
samples of three avian predators to infer the decline of two
arboreal squirrel species. The declining trends appeared to
be quite similar for both squirrels, possibly indicating that
the process behind the decline has been the same. The
vulnerable ﬂying squirrel was relatively rare in the diets of
all avian predators, probably indicating that these predators
do not have large effects on the population dynamics of this
species, at least during the breeding season.
