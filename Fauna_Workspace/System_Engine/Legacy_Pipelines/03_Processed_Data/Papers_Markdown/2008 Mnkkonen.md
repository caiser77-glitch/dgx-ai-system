flying squirrel Pteromys volans in old-growth forests of 
northern Finland

Landscape characteristics associated with the occurrence of the flying squirrel 
Pteromys volans in old-growth forests in northern Finland. - Ecography 20: 634- 
642. 
We investigated whether the occurrence of the flying squirrel, a declined virgin forest 
species, was accounted for by the structure of the landscape in northeastern Finland. 
We sampled 20 forest areas (1-77 km2 in size) in 1995 for the occurrence of the 
species using a sampling plot method. Landscape structure around occupied and 
unoccupied areas were compared using seven different radii ranging from 100 m to 
10 km. Classified satellite images were analyzed with Geographic Information System 
(GIS). Ten areas could be determined to be occupied by the flying squirrel. Results 
showed that occupied areas did not deviate from unoccupied ones in total area. In 
unoccupied areas there were more mature pine-spruce forests, and less matrix habitat, 
at the home range scale (100 m and 200 m radii) than in occupied areas. Further- 
more, in unoccupied forest areas open habitats (clear-cuts, open fens) were more 
common than in occupied ones at distances between 200 m and 2 km around 
sampling plots. These differences resulted from both larger mean patch size of and 
smaller mean nearest neighbor distance between open habitat patches in unoccupied 
than in occupied forest areas. According to a logistic regression analysis, information 
on the amount of open habitat within 1 km radius alone correctly classified 75% of 
the areas into occupied and unoccupied ones. The probability of the presence of 
flying squirrel decreases with the increasing amount of open habitat within 1 km. The 
results suggest that increasing the amount of open habitats (e.g. clear-cutting) at the 
local scale has a negative impact on the flying squirrel. It is obvious that in the 
present situation where the population has severely declined and its optimal habitat 
is still diminishing, every patch suitable for the species - whether occupied or not - 
may be important for the population persistence.

The landscape where organisms live in can be dis- 
tinguished by spatial relationships between component 
parts, called the landscape structure (Turner 1989). 
The variables describing landscape structure can be 
grouped into two general categories (Dunning et al. 
1992). First, landscape composition refers to the 
relative amount of different patch types in the land-

Andr~n (1994) suggested that the amount of habitat 
remaining (landscape composition) is a more important 
factor than landscape physiognomy influencing the 
abundance and distribution of species in landscapes 
with relatively low level of habitat fragmentation. With 
increasing levels of habitat loss, landscape physiognomy 
gains more weight. Results of a literature review (An- 
dr~n 1994) suggested that a critical threshold for birds 
and mammals lies between 10 and 30% of original 
habitat remaining. Above this threshold the random 
sample hypothesis is a sufficient predictor of animal 
abundance and distribution. 
Critical spatial scales and critical thresholds in land- 
scape patterning for populations certainly vary among 
species according to home range size, dispersal abilities 
and other life-history characteristics. Comparing distri- 
bution and abundance data of organisms in landscapes 
with different patterns and different proportions of 
habitats would help us to find such thresholds (see e.g. 
Helle et al. 1994, Helle and Nikula 1995, 1996). 
The flying squirrel Pteromys volans L. belongs to the 
Siberian fauna type. Its range extends from the Pacific 
coast of Eurasia and Hokkaido across Siberia west to 
eastern Fennoscandia and the Baltics (Siivonen and 
Sulkava 1994). The westernmost populations in Finland 
(Hokkanen et al. 1982, Anon. 1996), Estonia (Timm 
1992), and Latvia (Pilats pers. comm.) have declined 
severely during the 20th century. The species went 
extinct in Lithuania in the 19th century (Balciauskas 
pers. comm.). It has been included on the red-data lists 
in Finland (declined, needs care), Estonia (endangered) 
and Latvia (endangered) (Ingel6f et al. 1993). Current 
population estimates are 40 000-50 000 individuals in 
Finland (Anon. 1996), probably a few hundred in Esto- 
nia (Timm 1993) and a few tens in Latvia (Pilats pers. 
comm.) 
Flying squirrels prefer old, mixed forests as their 
habitat. Main components of occupied forests in south- 
ern Finland according to Eronen's (1996) description 
are: cavity trees (often aspen Populus tremula) for nest- 
ing and roosting, deciduous trees (birch Betula spp., 
alder Alnus spp., and aspen) for foraging, and mature 
conifers, preferably Norway spruce Picea abies. Flying 
squirrels can also be found around human settlement, 
particularly in southern Finland, in areas with suitable 
forest structure and nest-boxes (Sulkava et al. 1994). In 
northern Finland (north from 64?N), only a few known 
flying squirrel occurrences have so far been found out- 
side natural old-forest areas (Reunanen unpubl.), and it 
has been considered an old-growth forest specialist in 
the northwestern part of its range. Hokkanen et al. 
(1982) considered the population in northern Finland 
very close to extinction. 
Forest fragmentation and the loss of suitable mixed 
coniferous-deciduous forests has been considered the 
main reasons for the population decline (Hokkanen et 
al. 1982, Anon. 1986, Timm 1993). Proliferation of 
clear-cuts, economically preferred monocultures and 
even-aged stands have resulted in landscapes where 
suitable habitats for the flying squirrel occur as more or 
less isolated patches. Particularly, the diminishing of 
deciduous trees and large cavity trees within forests 
have presumably had an adverse effect on flying squir- 
rel populations. Opening up the forest structure due to 
forestry has possibly increased vulnerability to preda- 
tion (Hokkanen et al. 1982), although no data to verify 
this exist. It has also been assumed that flying squirrels 
have difficulties in dispersing in present forest land- 
scapes where continuity of old-growth forests has been 
exterminated (Anon. 1986). 
Recently, the flying squirrel has become a central 
issue in the conservation debate in Finland. It is one of 
the species whose presence is considered as a sign of 
particularly valuable natural forest stand in the forest 
inventory system of the Finnish Forest and Park Ser- 
vice. Consequently, special care is recommended when 
logging in areas where the species occurs (Anon. 1993). 
Despite of this role, the species has received rather little 
scientific attention, and practically none in northern 
Finland, close to the northwestern border of its distri- 
bution. 
Flying squirrel individuals, particularly old females, 
are usually site tenacious, living in the same home 
range year after year (Makeli 1996). From the squir- 
rel's point of view three different scales in landscape 
can be distinguished: 1) Home range level, which covers 
the area used by one individual. According to anecdotal 
evidence (Rahilin 1968), the home range of an individ- 
ual covers ca 1-3 ha of suitable habitat, but total 
activity areas can be much larger (Hanski pers. comm.). 
Our preliminary results from radio-tracking studies in 
northern Finland support Rahilin's (1968) estimates 
(Reunanen and M6nkkdnen unpubl.). Home range 
sizes of North American flying squirrel species have 
been reported to be up to a few ha (Bendel and Gates 
1987, Fridell and Litvaitis 1991, Witt 1992). 2) Local 
level, a continuous area covering several suitable home 
ranges, subpopulation level. In practice, the largest 
continuous natural forest areas are a few tens km2. 3) 
Landscape level, including several local level forest 
tracts with potential (sub)populations, and intervening 
'non-habitat'. 
We studied the landscape characteristics of the forest 
areas where flying squirrels occurred by comparing 
them with areas lacking the species. Classified satellite 
images were analyzed with Geographic Information 
System (GIS) to investigate local and landscape level 
patterns. We studied which landscape structural charac- 
teristics of the forest areas that were associated with the 
presence of flying squirrels. The study was conducted in 
NE Finland, close to the northwestern border of distri- 
bution of the species. Combined with the knowledge on 
habitat requirements (i.e. home range scale) of the 
species in northern Finland (Reunanen unpubl.) knowl-

Materials and methods 
Study area and sampling design 
This study was conducted in northern Kainuu, NE 
Finland (Fig. 1). The whole landscape under consider- 
ation covers ca 9000 km2. Phytogeographically, the 
study area is situated at the border of central and north 
boreal forest zones (Ahti et al. 1968). Forest land 
covers ca 75% of the area, peat land close to 15% and 
lakes ca 10%. Dominating forest type is dryish Vac- 
cinium-Myrtillus-type (VMT) with Scots pine Pinus 
silvestris as a dominating tree species. Spruce domi- 
nated stands are more common in western than in 
eastern parts of the study area. Most peat lands are 
pine bogs, open fens being more common in the eastern 
part of the study area (Iisakka 1972). 
Less than 5% of the forest land has been protected, 
and most forests are therefore under commercial 
forestry (Anon. 1995). Approximately 7.5% of the total 
forest area in Kainuu can be classified as natural forests 
(with little or no visible human influence), although ca 
25% of forests are older than 120 yr. Therefore, old- 
growth forests occur in northern Kainuu in more or 
less isolated patches in the sea of commercially man- 
aged forests. 
For this study we selected forest areas systematically 
using the following criteria: 1) maximal dispersion of 
forest areas on the study area, 2) there are suitable 
habitats for the squirrel (see below), 3) forests are 
natural forests and are situated in either protected or 
unprotected areas (so-called 'old-growth forest inven- 
tory areas'), 4) the sample should contain both large and 
small forest areas. Totally, 20 forest areas were selected 
(Fig. 1) which were situated at 160-380 m a.s.l. These 
areas varied between 0.9 and 77 km2 in size (Table 1).

Suitability of a forest tract for flying squirrel was 
judged according to independent data. We visited be- 
forehand several known home ranges which were lo- 
cated in forest areas in northern Kainuu. These areas 
were not included in this study. We consulted forest 
statistics for these stands from the Finnish Forest and 
Park Service forest files to get an impression of suitable 
habitat structure. Based on this knowledge the follow- 
ing criteria were used to search potential sites of occur- 
rence (suitable habitats) in selected forest areas: 1) age 
of dominating trees > 120 yr, 2) spruce >50% of 
standing crop (tree volume), 3) deciduous trees > 10% 
of standing crop (tree volume), 4) area > 1 ha. Using 
these criteria suitable forest compartments were picked 
from forest files pertaining to selected forest areas, and 
these compartments were then located on field maps. 
Each forest area was surveyed in April-August 1995 
to find signs of the presence of the flying squirrel in 
suitable habitats. Presence was determined by the oc- 
currence of fecal pellets under large trees, both decidu- 
ous trees and spruces. This method is shown to be 
practicable in studying this mainly nocturnal species 
(Skartn 1978, Paakkonen 1991). 
Forest areas were sampled using sampling plots. We 
selected the location of the sampling plots in the field 
by the presence of large deciduous trees amongst large 
spruces in predetermined suitable habitat. Sampling 
plots had a radius of 40 m yielding an area of 0.5 ha. 
On these plots, we checked all large trees for the 
presence of pellets. Distance between adjacent sampling 
plots was at least 300 m, and there were maximally 5 
sampling plots per I km2 of suitable habitat. This 
sampling design resulted in 1-43 sampling plots per 
forest area (Table 1). Each sampling plot was located 
on the map of scale 1:20 000. In addition to sampling 
plots, all signs of the presence of flying squirrels were 
tallied. All areas, however, could be determined as 
occupied or unoccupied based on sampling plot data 
only, i.e. additional signs were only found on areas with 
occupied sampling plots. 
GIS analysis 
For information on land use and forest data we used 
classified satellite images produced by national forest 
inventory (Tomppo 1991, 1993). National forest inven- 
tory of Finland employs satellite images together with 
digital map data and field sample plots to produce 
estimates of forest resources. The method produces 
inventory variables such as tree volume estimates for 
main tree species for each pixel (25 x 25 m). The result 
is a multi-channel image which can e.g. be imported to 
GIS (Tomppo 1991, 1993). Each channel (classified 
image) thus presents e.g. volume estimate for some of 
the tree species (Scots pine Pinus sylvestris, Norway 
spruce Picea abies) separately and deciduous trees (Be- 
tula pendula, B. pubescens, Populus tremula, Salix spp.) 
as one image. Classified images describing volume esti- 
mates for pine, spruce and deciduous trees were im- 
ported to GIS layers as well as digital maps of fields, 
roads, inhabited areas and waters. These data were 
combined to single layer land use and forest data where 
each pixel belongs to one of the following classes: 1) 
Water (lakes, ponds, rivers), 2) Open area (open peat- 
lands, clear cuts, fields), 3) Pine forest, > 150 m3 ha-', 
4) Mixed pine-spruce forest, > 150 m3 ha-', 5) Spruce 
forest, > 150 m3 ha-1, 6) Mixed coniferous-deciduous 
forests, > 150 m3 ha-' (including the very few pixels of 
pure deciduous forests), 7) Matrix, all other patch 
types. 
A pixel was classified as one tree species dominated 
forest if the proportion of this species of the total 
volume was > 80%. Otherwise pixels pertain to mixed 
forest patch types according to two most dominating 
tree species. Therefore, for example, mixed pine-spruce 
forest may have a substantial deciduous component (up 
to 30%) even though classified as mixed conifer forest. 
The limit of 150 m3 ha-1 is based on the average 
standing crop of mature forests in northern Finland 
which is 121 m3 ha-'. This rather stringent criterion 
results in a classification where all strictly not old- 
growth forests belong to the matrix. 
Sampling plots were superimposed on the satellite 
image, and their surroundings were studied on a circu- 
lar area using seven different radii: 100 m, 200 m, 500 
m, 1 km, 2 km, 5 km and 10 km. The area, conse- 
quently, varies between 3.25 ha (52 pixels) and 314 km2 
(502 400 pixels). The shortest radius encompasses the 
home range of individual animals, while areas within 
200 m-2 km can be considered to pertain to local scale. 
The two largest scales refer to landscape level. Using 
Fragstat program (McGarigal and Marks 1993) we 
calculated the following metrics for each patch type: 
%Land = class (patch type) area as a percentage of 
total landscape area. At any scale, %Land illustrates 
the area of patch types. Using relative rather than 
absolute patch type area makes different spatial scales 
more directly comparable. NP = number of patches of 
a particular patch type. Number of patches is related to 
the patch type subdivision in a landscape. MPS = mean 
patch size. MPS is also related to habitat subdivision, 
and can serve as a habitat fragmentation index. 
MNN = mean nearest neighbor distance for patches 
comprising the class. %Land refers to landscape com- 
position, whereas NP, MPS and MNN describe land- 
scape physiognomy. We made these calculations 
separately for each sampling plot and scale except for 
the 10 km radius. Within this radius, the circular areas 
around sampling plots within a forest area are almost 
totally overlapping yielding redundant data. Therefore, 
at this scale, calculations were only done by using the 
mid-point of the forest area as a center co-ordinate.

Statistical analysis 
To describe the landscape characteristics associated 
with the occurrence of the flying squirrel in northern 
Kainuu forest area specific averages were compared 
between occupied and unoccupied areas. Averaging was 
employed, first because we were primarily interested in 
overall landscape characteristics rather than site specific 
properties of occupied/unoccupied areas, and second, 
because sampling plots are not necessarily independent 
from each other, i.e. may represent the very same home 
ranges. 
We used t-test for independent samples when statisti- 
cally comparing the occupied and unoccupied forest 
areas at each scale separately to yield a univariate 
description. Because an extensive number of variables 
were extracted (7 scales x 7 patch types x 4 variables/ 
scale/patch type; 196 variables), we used a hierarchical 
logic in the analysis. Otherwise we would have found ca 
10 statistically significant results (at the level of 0.05) 
just by chance. At each scale, we first tested if %Land 
differed between occupied and unoccupied forest areas 
for each patch type (49 tests, 2-3 significant at 0.05 
level by chance alone). If %Land proved to be signifi- 
cant, other variables (NP, MPS, MNN) were analyzed 
for deeper insight. Biological relevance of the results, 
naturally, must be considered given the large number of 
pair-wise tests. It is also important to notice that some 
indices measured are partially or completely redundant 
and may be highly correlated (McGarigal and Marks 
1993, see also Andr~n 1994). 
We also combined different scales and performed a 
multivariate analysis to find out the most important 
factors affecting flying squirrel occurrence across spatial 
scales. We used forward step-wise logistic regression to 
model presence/absence of the species. The following 
patch types were allowed to enter at all scales: Open 
areas (patch type 2), Mixed pine-spruce forest > 150 
m3 (4), Mixed conifer-deciduous forest > 150 m3 (6), 
and Matrix (7). The selection of patch types to enter 
was based on the result of univariate analysis.

studied forest areas (Table 1). Occupied areas did not 
deviate from unoccupied ones by total size (averages 
15.1 km2 and 15.4 km2, respectively; Mann-Whitney 
U = 28, p > 0.10).

pling plots within the 10 inhabited forest areas. No 
differences emerged at any of the scales. This indicates 
that presence/absence of the flying squirrel was not a 
consequence of the inherent differences between sam- 
pling plots (e.g. biased sampling). This also suggests

ables were associated with the presence/absence of the 
species in forest areas. At the landscape scale, matrix

landscape scale hierarchies, such as our classification of 
scales into home range, local and landscape scales, are 
artificial constructions imposed on nature (Wiens 1995). 
We have little more than just a hunch of the opera- 
tional scales and domains of the flying squirrel, because 
knowledge on the movements of individuals and their 
dispersal is practically nil (but see Mikelii 1996). Due 
to sampling design, our home range scale (100 m 
radius) encompasses only characteristics of the studied 
natural forest areas, and only at larger spatial scales 
human-induced landscape changes appear on the scene. 
A critical question for the conclusions from this 
study is how reliable are the negative observations, i.e. 
what proportion of areas determined as unoccupied is, 
in fact, occupied by the flying squirrel. We admit that 
some of the areas determined unoccupied may, in real- 
ity, be occupied, but we think this is not a serious bias 
in our study. First, sampling effort per unit area was 
kept constant, and therefore, even though not strictly 
empty, unoccupied areas evidently have relatively low 
densities of flying squirrels. Second, because flying 
squirrels use fecal pellets in their marking of home 
ranges, our sampling emphasizes territorial individuals, 
i.e. reproductive part of population. Whether or not 
unoccupied areas are used by immature individuals 
and/or during dispersal is not critical for the conclu- 
sions drawn. 
In the univariate analyses, some of the results might 
be artifacts, resulting from analyzing relative propor- 
tions of patch types. If there is variation in the amount 
of one patch between occupied and unoccupied forest 
areas at a given scale, there would, by necessity, be 
variation in the amount of some other patch type but in 
the opposite direction. We think, however, that this 
bias is-not detrimental in our study because different 
patch types appeared to be important at different scales 
(pine-spruce forest and matrix at the home range scale, 
open areas at the local scale). According to multivariate 
analysis, only the amount of open habitat affects the 
probability of a forest area to be occupied. 
The amount of pine-spruce forest (patch type 4) 
seemed to be negatively associated with the presence of 
the flying squirrel at the home range scale. This 
matches well with the results from southern Finland 
where none of the home ranges described by Eronen 
(1991) located in dry forests with considerable pine 
dominance. The easternmost study areas close to the 
Russian border, where edaphic conditions are on aver- 
age poorer than further west, were unoccupied (Fig. 1).

The high proportion of the matrix at the home range 
scale, inside natural forest areas, in occupied forest 
areas may at first sound counterintuitive. This indicates 
that home ranges are variable and diverse in structure 
containing forests of different ages and composition. 
Similarly, Eronen (1991) observed that in southern 
Finland home ranges were diverse in terms of tree 
species composition and tree age. 
The amount of open habitat (open fens, clear-cuts 
and fields) had clearly a negative effect on the flying 
squirrel at the local scale (500 m-2 km). Also in the 
multivariate analysis the amount of open habitat within 
a 1 km radius was the most effective variable explaining 
75% of the presence/absence of the flying squirrel. At 
the local scale, the more there was of this non-habitat, 
the larger the patches were and the closer they were 
situated to each other, the less probable it was to 
encounter flying squirrels. 
Multivariate analysis also suggested that open areas 
within the home range (< 100 m) affected positively the 
probability of encountering an occupied home range 
after controlling for the amount of open habitat at 
larger scale. Flying squirrels, therefore, do not seem to 
avoid forest openings. In southern Finland, field edges 
with deciduous trees have been suggested to provide 
profitable foraging areas for the species (Eronen 1991, 
Sulkava et al. 1994), but, once again, data on habitat 
use of individual squirrels to support this are missing. 
The large amount of open peatlands together with 
clear-cuts in the eastern parts of the study area may be 
linked with the absence of the flying squirrel. Also pine 
bogs (classified as matrix), a non-habitat for the flying 
squirrel, are more common in eastern than in western 
Kainuu. By the same token, no flying squirrels were 
found during extensive old-growth forest inventories in 
Russian Karelia, in areas situated just east from our 
study area (Kerfinen pers. comm.). 
The present study cannot settle the question whether 
flying squirrel is an old-growth forest specialist or not 
because all the sampling plots were situated inside 
old-growth forests. The results indicate, however, that 
not necessarily very large areas of old forest are needed 
by the species. The total size of the forest areas did not 
differ between occupied and unoccupied areas. Note, 
however, that the smallest forest areas in this study 
were ca 1 km2 in size. Only a long-term study could 
show to what extent populations in the smallest occu- 
pied forest area (1.24 km2) found in this study would be 
more extinction prone than larger ones. This depends 
on the dispersal abilities of the species in the present 
landscape which determines whether the population in 
northern Kainuu is structured as a metapopulation, as 
source-sink populations or just as a patchy continuous 
population (Harrison 1991). 
It is probable that flying squirrel populations in 
northern Kainuu are divided into subpopulations in the 
remnant natural forest patches because the species 
clearly prefers old spruce dominated stands with a 
considerable mixture of deciduous trees (Reunanen un- 
publ.). Clear-cuts, sapling stands and young forests 
cover presently ca 65% of the total forest land in 
Kainuu. It is, therefore, equally probable that in the 
present landscape movements through the landscape 
are unlikely for a species which is specialized on natural 
forests with a marked deciduous component (the land- 
scape does not percolate, see Turner 1989, Gardner et 
al. 1992). Large open areas very likely pose a dispersal 
barrier, because flying squirrel moves with ease only by 
gliding from a tree to another. 
At present, detailed operational recommendations 
based on scientific research for forestry practices that 
allow viable populations of the flying squirrel in north- 
ern Finland to persist cannot be made. The results 
suggest that increasing the amount of open habitats 
(e.g. clear-cutting) at the local scale has a negative 
impact on the flying squirrel. Pine has been preferred in 
forest cultivation after cuttings in Finland. Therefore, 
future mature forests in northern Kainuu are going to

dominance in northern Finnish landscape, i.e. making 
landscapes more like in eastern part of our study area,
