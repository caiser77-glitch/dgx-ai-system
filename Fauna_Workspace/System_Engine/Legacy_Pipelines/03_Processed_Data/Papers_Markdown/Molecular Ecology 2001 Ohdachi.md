Intraspeciﬁc phylogeny and geographical variation of six 
species of northeastern Asiatic Sorex shrews based on the 
mitochondrial cytochrome b sequences

Abstract
Intraspeciﬁc phylogeny and genetic variation were investigated based on nucleotide
sequences of the mitochondrial cytochrome b gene in six soricine shrew species, Sorex
unguiculatus, S. caecutiens, S. shinto, S. gracillimus, S. minutissimus and S. hosonoi, col-
lected primarily from northeastern Asia. Maximum likelihood trees and a phylogenetic
network were generated to estimate intraspeciﬁc phylogenies. S. minutissimus showed
high congruence between phylogenetic position and geographical origin and S. gracillimus
showed low congruence. In contrast, there was no congruence between phylogeny and
geography in S. unguiculatus and the S. caecutiens from Sakhalin-Eurasia. Positive cor-
relation between genetic and geographical distances was found in S. minutissimus and
S. gracillimus, but not in the other species (or regional populations). The results of the
phylogenetic and genetic analyses suggest that S. minutissimus and S. gracillimus have occu-
pied their present ranges for a longer time than the other species if we assume a stepping-
stone model of population structure. In addition, there was no contradiction between the
present investigations and the hypotheses of multiple immigration by S. gracillimus and
a single immigration by S. unguiculatus into Hokkaido Island. It is proposed that these
six northeastern Asian species experienced different historical processes of range expan-
sion and dispersal despite the fact that some of them currently show similar patterns of
distribution.
Keywords: biogeography, Eurasia, geographical distances, genetic distances, molecular phylogeny,
nucleotide diversity, Sorex

Introduction
To estimate the process of formation of a biological
community (or species assemblage), it is desirable to
compare historical backgrounds among the members of
the community. Intra- and interspeciﬁc phylogenies are
essential information for understanding the biogeo-
graphical history of a community (e.g. Wiley 1981; Brooks
& McLennan 1993; Morrone & Crisci 1995; Walker & Avise
1998; Patterson 1999), in addition to providing insight
into palaeontological data and present/past distributions.
Furthermore, to infer the process of range expansion or
dispersal of a species, the effects of gene ﬂow and genetic
drift on population structure should be clariﬁed by
comparing genetic distance with geographical distance
throughout the species’ range (Hutchison & Templeton
1999). For example, if there is a positive correlation
between genetic and geographical distances, a population
(species) is expected to have occupied its present range
long enough to reach an equilibrium between gene ﬂow
and genetic drift if we assume a stepping-stone model
of population structure (Hutchison & Templeton 1999).
Comparison of range expansion among community
members should thus provide meaningful insights into
the process of community organization.

In northeastern Asia (the Japanese Islands, the Korean
Peninsula, Manchuria and the Far East of Russia), up to
nine species of soricine shrews (Mammalia; Insectivora)
coexist (Dolgov 1985; Nesterenko 1999). This high species
diversity suggests that the region was important to the
evolution and diversiﬁcation of shrews. In addition, there
are a number of large and small islands in northeastern
Asia each carrying different species associations of shrews.
This situation favours comparison of the history among
regional communities because demarcations between local
populations are explicit.
Ohdachi et al. (1997a) inferred the interspeciﬁc phylo-
geny of northeastern Asiatic shrews from partial nucleotide
sequences (402 bases) of the mitochondrial cytochrome b
gene. Based on the phylogeny and present/past distribu-
tions of shrews, Ohdachi et al. (1997b) hypothesized the
biogeographic history of northeastern Asiatic shrews.
However, their discussion (Ohdachi et al. 1997b) has to be
reconsidered because phylogenetic relationships among
local populations were not investigated for most species
(Ohdachi et al. 1997a). Dobson & Kawamura (1998) also
investigated the origin of the Japanese mammalian fauna
and suggested a hypothetical history of community organ-
ization of the shrews around the Japanese Islands, based
mainly on fossil records and a hypothetical phylogeny.
Because they relied on incomplete information about
shrew phylogeny (Dobson & Kawamura 1998), some parts
of their discussion were obviously erroneous. For instance,
their biogeographical hypothesis on shrews depends on
the assumption that Sorex caecutiens Laxmann and S.
unguiculatus Dobson are direct sister species, but this has
been widely rejected (e.g. George 1988; Ohdachi et al.
1997a; Fumagalli et al. 1999). Accordingly, it is now time to
reconsider the biogeographical history of shrews in north-
eastern Asia by investigating intraspeciﬁc phylogeny and
genetic structure throughout their ranges.
In northeastern Asia, S. unguiculatus, S. caecutiens, S.
gracillimus Thomas and S. minutissimus Zimmerman are
found on more islands and in more regions than the other
Sorex species (Dolgov 1985; Nesterenko 1999). Among
these four species, S. caecutiens and S. minutissimus are
widely distributed throughout northern Eurasia with
sibling species S. shinto Thomas and S. hosonoi Imaizumi in
the Honshu archipelago (southern Japanese Islands)
(Ohdachi et al. 1997a) (Fig. 1). In contrast, S. unguiculatus and
S. gracillimus occur only in northeastern Asia (Fig. 2).
Hence, S. caecutiens–S. shinto and S. minutissimus–S. hosonoi
are representatives of widely distributed groups among
Eurasian shrews, while S. unguiculatus and S. gracillimus
are the endemic species of northeastern Asia.
Herein, we infer intraspeciﬁc phylogeny from partial
(630 or 501 bases) or full (1140 bases) nucleotide sequences
of the mitochondrial cytochrome b gene in S. unguiculatus,
S. caecutiens, S. shinto, S. gracillimus, S. minutissimus and
S. hosonoi from various regions, mainly in northeastern
Asia. Furthermore, we compare the relationships between
genetic and geographical distances throughout their distri-
butional ranges in order to investigate the genetic structure
of species or populations. we discuss the biogeographical
history of the six Sorex species in northeastern Asia, parti-
cularly around the Japanese Islands.
Materials and methods
Specimens
A total of 99 individuals of six Sorex species (Mammalia;
Insectivora), 31 Sorex unguiculatus, 18 S. caecutiens, 15 S.
shinto, 19 S. gracillimus, 10 S. minutissimus and six S. hosonoi,
from 59 locations in northern Eurasia (Table 1, Figs 1 and 2)
and one S. isodon Turov were used for analysis. Among
them, two nucleotide sequences of S. minutissimus from
Paljakka (L. Fumagalli, personal communication), Finland,
were cited from Fumagalli et al. (1999) to complement our
own data (GenBank/EMBL/DDBJ accession numbers,
AJ000442 and AJ000443). S. isodon was used as an outgroup
in phylogenetic analysis for S. unguiculatus, S. caecutiens
and S. shinto, since it is phylogenetically moderately
differentiated from these three target species (Ohdachi
et al. 1997a; Fumagalli et al. 1999). Biological information
(sex, age, collection date, etc.) on the 98 shrews sequenced
in the present study is stored in GenBank/EMBL/DDBJ
together with nucleotide sequences: accession numbers,
AB028500–AB028597.
Previous molecular studies (Ohdachi et al. 1997a;
Fumagalli et al. 1999) indicated that S. shinto and S. hosonoi
are sibling species of S. caecutiens and S. minutissimus,
respectively. Hereafter, we call these groups S. caecutiens/
shinto and S. minutissimus/hosonoi groups. In this paper,
we followed Hutterer (1993) and Wolsan & Hutterer (1998)
for the scientiﬁc names of most species, but followed
Ohdachi et al. (1997a), Koyasu (1998), and Dokuchaev et al.
(1999) for the names of the S. caecutiens/shinto group.
DNA analysis
Full sequences (1140 bases) of the mitochondrial cytochrome
b gene were revealed for S. unguiculatus, S. caecutiens,
S. shinto and S. isodon. The 630-base partial region was
sequenced for S. gracillimus, S. minutissimus and S. hosonoi.
Total DNAs were extracted by the phenol/proteinase K/
sodium dodecyl sulphate method (Sambrook et al. 1989),
from c. 27 mm3 tissues (muscle, liver, or hind-foot) pre-
served in 70–95% ethanol. For tissues of this size, digestion
by proteinase K did not require initial homogenization.
A gene region that included the whole region of the
mitochondrial cytochrome b gene (1140 bases) was ampli-
ﬁed by polymerase chain reaction (PCR) using rTaq DNA

After the double-stranded PCR products were puriﬁed
by QIA quick (Qiagene), the puriﬁed products were
directly sequenced in a CATALYST 800 Molecular Biology
LabStation (Perkin Elmer) using a PRISM Ready Reaction
Dye Terminator Cycle Sequencing Kit (Perkin Elmer). The
sequencing reaction products were analysed by electro-
phoresis and sequence data was collected by an auto-
sequencing system (ABI PRISM 377 DNA Sequencing
System; Perkin Elmer). All experimental procedures
followed the manufacturers’ instructions.

data sets of 630 bases were used for S. gracillimus. In the
S. minutissimus/hosonoi group, analyses were based on 501
bases shared by the published sequences from Finland
(Fumagalli et al. 1999) and our own 630-base region.
To infer intraspeciﬁc phylogenies, maximum likelihood
(ML) trees were estimated by the quartet-puzzling method
implemented in the tree-puzzle ver. 4.0.2 program (Strimmer
& von Haeseler 1996) with an HKY85 substitution
model (Hasegawa et al. 1985). We applied the discrete
gamma-distribution with eight categories (Yang 1996) for
the site heterogeneity. Conﬁdence of a node was assigned
by a support value (Strimmer & von Haeseler 1996). The
tree-puzzle is an approximate method of ﬁnding an ML
tree to overcome the difﬁculty of the topology search prob-
lem due to the explosive increase in the number of possible
tree topologies, when the ML method is applied to a large
number of sequences such as our data sets. We also used
the NucML analysis in molphy ver. 2.3 (Adachi & Hasegawa
1996) with the local rearrangement option starting from
neighbour-joining (NJ) trees (Saitou & Nei 1987). The
results were congruent between the two methods, and we
show only the result of the tree-puzzle.
A parsimonious phylogenetic network (Bandelt 1994)
was also constructed for S. gracillimus. However, the net-
work was not constructed for other species or groups,
because the network contained no phylogeographic
information other than that of the ML tree. In addition, we
generated trees by the NJ method using the computer
program clustal w ver. 1.5 (Thompson et al. 1994) and
the maximum parsimony method using paup ver. 3.1.1
(Swofford 1993). These trees, however, are not shown in
the present paper, because the topologies were essentially
the same as for the ML trees.
Nucleotide diversity, π (e.g. Chapter 10; Nei 1987) and
other information on nucleotide polymorphism were calcu-
lated for shrews using the program dnasp ver. 3.50 (Rozas
& Rozas 1999). We conducted a Z-score test to evaluate the
statistical difference in π between populations or species
(Chapter 12; Nei & Kumar 2000). Note that the values
obtained are a rough estimation of nucleotide diversity,
because sampling locations and individual shrews could
not be systematically chosen. This procedure, however,
was sufﬁcient for the purpose of the present analysis.
To estimate the historical process of dispersal, pairwise
comparison between genetic and geographical distance
were conducted within a species or population (Hutchison
& Templeton 1999). Genetic distance (substitutions/site)
between individuals was estimated by the program tree-
puzzle ver. 4.0.2 using the HKY85 model. Geographic dis-
tance was taken as the minimum distance between two
locations, and calculated from latitudes and longitudes of
sampling locations by the Bessel method (http://vldb.gsi-
mc.go.jp/sokuchi/surveycalc/bl2stf.html). To avoid under-
estimation of distance, a distance between a location in
Hokkaido Island (including nearby minor islands) or
Sakhalin Island and any other location was measured via
Nikolayevsk-na-Amure, a continental city adjoining north-
ern Sakhalin (location #51; Fig. 2). For instance, the dis-
tance between Sapporo (#20) and Sidima (#53) was the sum
of the distance between Sapporo and Nikolayevsk-na-Amure
and that between Nikolayevsk-na-Amure and Sidima.
Similarly, a distance between either of the two locations in
the Kamchatka Peninsula (#45 and #46) or Paramushiru
Island (#44) and any other location was measured via the
point 62°00ʹ′00.0ʹ′ʹ′ N, 166°00ʹ′00.0ʹ′ʹ′ E, which is located at the
base of the peninsula. The signiﬁcance of correlation
between genetic and geographical distances was assigned
by Mantel test (10 000 permutations; Mantel 1967).

Phylogeny of S. unguiculatus
Among the 31 individuals, 29 haplotypes were obtained
and three individuals shared an identical sequence
(Table 2). In the ML tree (Fig. 3), there were two main
clusters of more than one operational taxonomic unit
(OTU) at the base node, but the support values were low
(57% and 51%).
There was no congruence between the phylogenetic
positions and geographical origins of the shrews (Fig. 3).
Even when two individuals were analysed from a single
location, they were never clustered together. Furthermore,
some individuals clustered together with high support
values were not from adjacent localities, e.g. Shikotan (location
#32) and Samani (#19) with an 82% support value (Fig. 3).
Phylogeny of the S. caecutiens/shinto group
All the 33 shrews examined possessed unique haplotypes.
S. shinto and S. caecutiens were completely separated in the
ML tree (Fig. 4).
Within S. shinto, two main monophyletic groups were
recognized (Fig. 4); one consisted of S. shinto from Sado
Island (Sado cluster; the Sado shrew, S. s. sadonis Yoshiyuki
and Imaizumi) and the other of shrews from Honshu
Island and Shikoku Island (Honshu-Shikoku cluster). In
the latter group, the subcluster of ﬁve OTUs with 87% sup-
port values consisted of shrews from central to northern
Honshu (Fig. 4), while the other OTUs were mainly from
central Honshu to Shikoku, except for that from Chokaisan
(location #13). Two individuals from Shikoku Island did
not cluster together (location #1 and #2; Fig. 4), although
they belong to a single local subspecies (the Shikoku shrew,
S. s. shikokensis Abe).

Nucleotide diversity
There were two groups with regard to nucleotide diversity
π; one had low π and included two regional populations
of S. caecutiens, S. gracillimus and S. hosonoi, and the other
had high π and included S. shinto, S. minutissimus and
S. unguiculatus (Table 2). Nucleotide diversity differed
signiﬁcantly (P < 0.01) between the two species (or
regional populations) from the two groups, respectively,
but there was no signiﬁcant difference within each group.
In particular, the nucleotide diversity of S. caecutiens
from Sakhalin–Eurasia (π = 7.59 × 10–3; Table 2) was signi-
ﬁcantly lower than that of S. shinto (π = 20.48 × 10–3 or
15.34 × 10–3 without Sado shrews), while the distributional
range of the former cluster was much greater than that of
the latter (Fig. 1). The nucleotide diversity of S. caecutiens
from Hokkaido Island was not signiﬁcantly lower than
that from Sakhalin–Eurasia despite the former having a
much smaller range than the latter (Table 2).

S. minutissimus and S. hosonoi throughout the entire
sampling area and for S. caecutiens from Sakhalin–
Eurasia and S. shinto from Honshu–Shikoku (Fig. 4).

Discussion
Intraspeciﬁc phylogeny
Sorex unguiculatus is recorded from Hokkaido Island and
some of its adjacent minor islands, the southern Kurile
Islands, Sakhalin Island, Manchuria, Primorye and the
Khabarovsk region (Fig. 2, Table 3; based on Voronov 1974;
Yudin 1975; Krivosheev 1984; Dolgov 1985; Ohdachi &
Maekawa 1990; Kholin 1993; Han 1994; Ohdachi 1995;
Zhang 1997; Nesterenko 1999; with modiﬁcation). The
sampling locations of the present study covered the main
regions of the range of this species. Absolutely no con-
cordance between phylogenetic positions and geographical
origins of the specimens was observed in S. unguiculatus
(Fig. 3). This ﬁnding implies that the cytochrome b gene
has not diverged sufﬁciently to be distinct among regions,
and thus ancestral polymorphisms are being maintained.
Nesterenko (1999) considered that S. unguiculatus immig-
rated into Hokkaido Island some 24 000–25 000 years ago
at the time of the development of the broad-leaved tree
forest (Korotkii et al. 1996). Furthermore, the single immig-
ration event into Hokkaido Island is inferred from fossil
records (Nesterenko et al., in press). However, if S. unguiculatus
had immigrated into Hokkaido Island from the contin-
ent several times with sufﬁcient time intervals, we would
expect there to be several local clusters of minor islands,
such as Rebun (location #26), Daikoku (#35) and Kunashir
(#31), which are thought to have been separated from the
mainland Hokkaido at different periods (e.g. Ohshima
1990; Igarashi 2000). In reality, even shrews from the same
site were located at different positions in the ML tree
(Fig. 3), and thus there was no strong evidence of multi-
immigration of S. unguiculatus into Hokkaido Island. There-
fore, there is no contradiction between the hypothesis of
single immigration and the intraspeciﬁc phylogeny
observed here (Fig. 3).
The taxonomic status of the S. caecutiens/shinto group,
which is based primarily on morphology, has caused much
confusion. Although some taxonomists (e.g. Stroganov 1957;
Abe 1967, 1994; Corbet 1978) have not treated S. shinto as a
species distinct from S. caecutiens, their investigations did
not include specimens from throughout the distribution
range of S. shinto and S. caecutiens (see Dokuchaev et al.
1999 for a history of the debate). In contrast, karyological
(Obara 1988; Tada & Obara 1988), molecular phylogenetic
(George 1988; Ohdachi et al. 1997a; Fumagalli et al. 1999),
and cranio-morphological (Dokuchaev et al. 1999) studies
have demonstrated that these two taxa of shrews should be
treated as distinct species. Furthermore, Ohdachi et al.
(1997a) suggested that shrews from the islands Honshu,
Shikoku and Sado, of the Honshu archipelago, should be
considered S. shinto and those from Hokkaido Island,
Sakhalin Island and the Eurasian Continent should be
thought of as S. caecutiens, on the basis of the molecular
phylogeny of partial sequences of the cytochrome b. In the
present study, we reconﬁrmed the results of Ohdachi et al.
(1997a) using full sequences with more samples (Fig. 3).
Thus, we conclude that the distribution of S. caecutiens
extends throughout the northern parts of the Eurasian
Continent, Sakhalin Island, Hokkaido Island and some of
their adjacent small islands, whereas S. shinto is restricted
to the Honshu archipelago islands speciﬁed (Fig. 1, Table 3;

S. caecutiens spread throughout the continent, includ-

disperse.

Among the species endemic to northeastern Asia (Fig. 2),

and geographical distances but S. unguiculatus did not

present range longer than S. unguiculatus, assuming a
stepping-stone model with insigniﬁcant interspeciﬁc
difference in dispersal ability. This prediction is concordant
with that from the relationship of phylogeography discussed
above; there is absolutely no congruence between phylo-
genetic and geographical positions in S. unguiculatus
(Fig. 3) and thus this species may not yet have diverged
sufﬁciently among regions.
Nevertheless, S. unguiculatus has a greater nucleotide
diversity than S. gracillimus (Table 2). This result initially
contradicts the prediction of more recent dispersal in
S. unguiculatus from correlation between genetic and
geographical distances and intraspeciﬁc phylogeny. In
Hokkaido, S. unguiculatus occurs in various habitat types
(Ohdachi & Maekawa 1990) and the habitats are obviously
continuous. On the Asian Continent, however, this species
occurs only in broad-leaved forests or mixed forests with
coniferous trees, and the habitats are fragmented (Nesterenko
1999). The habitats in the continent seem to have been as
fragmented as they are now before the immigration from
the continent to Hokkaido, but they seem to have become
continuous once during the period of the immigration as
vegetation type changed (V. Nesterenko, personal commun-
ication). Thus, the high nucleotide diversity in S. unguiculatus
might have developed on the continent and Hokkaido
Island consequently been colonized by a genetically hetero-
geneous population.
Among the widely distributed species in Eurasia (Fig. 1),
S. minutissimus showed a positive correlation between
genetic and geographical distances but S. caecutiens from
Sakhalin–Eurasia did not (Fig. 7). Therefore, S. minutissimus
may have spread through the Eurasian Continent earlier
than S. caecutiens. This prediction is congruent with those
based on intraspeciﬁc phylogeny and nucleotide diversity
discussed previously.
Phylogeography around the Japanese Islands
The Tsugaru Strait between Hokkaido Island and Honshu
Island (Fig. 2) is known as Blakiston’s line, a well known,
signiﬁcant biogeographic line in Eurasia (e.g. Tokuda 1941;
Dobson & Kawamura 1998; Masuda 1999a) that clearly
divides faunas of terrestrial vertebrates and arthropods
and ﬂoras into two. In the present study, the distributions
of sibling species of the S. caecutiens/shinto and S.
minutissimus/hosonoi groups (Figs 4 and 6) were also
clearly separated by this strait (Fig. 1, Table 3). For
S. unguiculatus and S. gracillimus, the Tsugaru Strait is
the southern boundary of their ranges in the Japanese
and Sakhalin Islands (Fig. 2). In mammals, similar phylo-
geographic relationships over the Tsugaru Strait have been
reported for sciurids (Oshida 1999), mustelids and canids
(Masuda 1999b).
It is estimated that the Tsugaru and Korean Straits were
opened about 100 000–140 000 years ago (Japan Association
for Quaternary Research 1987; with modiﬁcation following
Ohshima 1990, 1991). However, there is debate as to whether
the Tsugaru Strait was closed again at the maximum
stage of the last glacial age, c. 18 000 years ago (e.g. Ohshima
1990), when Hokkaido Island was connected with the
continent via Sakhalin Island. From our phylogenetic
investigations with distributional data, it is implausible
that the shrews crossed the present boundary, Blakiston’s
line, 18 000 years ago. If Hokkaido Island and Honshu
Island had been interconnected at that time, the clear phylo-
genetic and distributional separation over the Tsugaru
Strait would not be observed. Thus, we support the
hypothesis of Ohshima (1990, 1991, 1992) that the last
opening of the Tsugaru Strait happened 100 000–140 000
years ago and it has not been closed since that period.
The present investigations support the hypothesis of
multiimmigration of S. gracillimus into Hokkaido Island
(Nesterenko 1999). Among other mammalian groups in
Hokkaido Island, the brown bear (Ursus arctos) is thought
to have immigrated into Hokkaido Island several times
from the Eurasian Continent, on the basis of molecular
phylogenetic studies (Masuda 1999b, 2000; Matsuhashi
et al. 1999). In contrast, there was no evidence of multi-
immigration of S. unguiculatus into Hokkaido Island in
the present study.
Conclusion
The distribution patterns are similar between the S.
caecutiens/shinto group and the S. minutissimus/hosonoi
group, and between S. unguiculatus and S. gracillimus
(Figs 1 and 2, Table 3). Therefore, members of these two
‘twin’ combinations (S. caecutiens/shinto–S. minutissimus/
hosonoi and S. unguiculatus–S. gracillimus) have been
thought to move with their partners (e.g. Ohdachi et al.
1997b; Nesterenko 1999). However, the results of the
present phylogenetic and genetic investigations suggest
that S. minutissimus and S. gracillimus have occupied
their present ranges longer than S. caecutiens and S.
unguiculatus, respectively. Thus, comparisons of the
analyses of intraspeciﬁc phylogenetics and genetics
among several members of a community (species
assemblage), in addition to distributional and palaeont-
ological information, are necessary to understand bio-
geographical history.
