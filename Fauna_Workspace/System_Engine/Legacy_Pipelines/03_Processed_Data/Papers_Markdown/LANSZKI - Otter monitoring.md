EXPERIENCES OF OTTER (LUTRA LUTRA) MONITORING ALONGSIDE  
THE DRAVA RIVER, IN SOMOGY COUNTY, HUNGARY 
 
József LANSZKI 
 
University of Kaposvár, Ecological Research Group, P.O. Box 16, 7401 Kaposvár, Hungary, 
email: lanszki@mail.atk.u-kaposvar.hu 
 
Abstract. The monitoring of the strictly protected Eurasian otter started during the winter of 2000, in the 
Danube-Drava National Park, along the Drava River in Hungary. The study areas were the Drava River 
(İrtilos, 
Bélavár, 
Vízvár), 
backwaters 
(Bélavár, 
Babócsa, 
Kis-Bók), 
gravel-pit 
pools 
(İrtilos, 
Somogyudvarhely), watercourses and streams (Dombó Canal - two points, Babócsa Rinya, Barcs-Komlósd 
Rinya, Korcsina Canal), in ox-bow lakes and alder forests or abandoned ponds (in Lankóci Forest, Középrigóci 
Ponds). In this comparative study, also further otter habitats in Somogy County were examined. The relative 
density of otters was surveyed by spraint counts along a standard route (line transect survey). The highest otter 
spraint density was measured on natural backwaters (26.0  ±  17.2; mean ± s.e.) and relatively high values were 
also found on ponds (13.7 ± 2.8) where the human disturbance was low. On the Dráva River, the mean relative 
density was moderate (12.8 ± 6.6), which may be associated with the high fluctuations of water levels and the 
steep riverside. Otter density was lower on gravel-pit lakes and backwaters with intensive angling (6.3 ± 2.6), as 
well as on watercourses and streams (4.9 ± 0.6) in connection with the Dráva River. The latter wetlands had 
high fluctuations in water supply throughout the year, and their food availability was also poor. From 2002 the 
monitoring was completed by genetic analysis (using DNA markers) from freshly collected spraints. The results 
of the diet composition examined by spraint analysis are also summarised. It can be concluded on the basis of 
the above findings that the otters’ population sensitively reflects the changes of the habitats, preservation of the 
riverside vegetation and that of the landscape.  
 
Keywords: Eurasian otter, Lutra lutra, biomonitoring, relative density, diet, Drava, Hungary 
 
 
1   INTRODUCTION 
 
The Drava is one of the least artificially modified rivers in Europe, and is rich in natural 
habitats possessing a wide variety of fauna and flora. It rises on the border between Italy and 
Austria, flows through Slovenia and Croatia before reaching Hungary and forms a state 
border for some 130 kilometres, crossing the national border numerous times, before flowing 
into the River Danube in Croatia. There are now plans for the construction of a high-capacity 
power station in the region of Novo Virje.  In the winter of 2000 the Danube-Drava National 
Park began monitoring a natural asset, the Eurasian otter (Lutra lutra), on the banks of  the 
River Drava running through the county of Somogy, with the aim of examining the possible 
effects of this planned development.  
 
The distribution of the species in Hungary has previously been monitored by means of 
questionnaire surveys in the past 15 years (GERA, 2001; HELTAI, 2002). These revealed that 
the species was largely stable with respect to its population size and distribution, as it is in 
other countries in Central Europe (KRANZ, 2000; GERA, 2001; HELTAI, 2002).  This is 
probably due chiefly to the existence of systems of artificial fishpond and conservation 
measures.  According to these surveys, the otter shows a continuous presence in all the 
habitats along the Drava River.  The occurrence and population density of the species, 
together with the extent of its territory and its predatory behaviour, are dependent on the 
available food supply (e.g. ERLINGE, 1967b, 1968; KRUUK AND MOORHOUSE 1990; KRUUK, 
CONROY AND MOORHOUSE, 1991).  In addition to the abundance of available food, other 
factors affecting occurrence are, the quality of bankside vegetation, steepness of waterside 
banks and the degree of human influence to which the area is exposed (CARSS, 1995; 
KEMENES AND DEMETER, 1995; LANSZKI, KÖRMENDI AND HANCZ, 1998).  


 
2
 
The otter in freshwater habitats is largely solitary, secretive, rare and mainly nocturnal; 
therefore spraints and tracks indicating the presence of the species (REUTHER, KÖLSCH AND 
JANΒEN, 2000) are the principal means of confirming its presence and for monitoring changes 
in occurrence by means of systematic questionnaire-based surveys.  Determining the size of 
an otter population poses practical problems.  Genetic analysis (e.g. DALLAS ET AL., 1999) is 
now the most suitable method for this.  The relative density of otters can also be measured on 
the basis of the number of spraints and tracks indicating the presence of the species in relation 
to the unit length of the route used; this allows comparison of areas studied by identical 
methods. The line transect method (e.g. ROBSON AND HUMPHREY, 1985; MASON AND 
MACDONALD, 1986; REID ET AL., 1987; PRIGIONI ET AL., 1995) is suitable for this, but can also 
entail errors. Primary considerations are the frequency of sampling and the determining and 
fixing of the route, together with the importance of extending the study for as long as 
possible.  Territory marking by the otter (i.e., frequency of defecation) is influenced by social 
behaviour related to breeding, and also by season (e.g. CONROY AND FRENCH, 1985; KRUUK 
AND CONROY, 1987).  
The objective of this study, initiated in 2000, was to monitor the otter population 
living beside the Drava, to assess the basic situation in sampling areas and to examine 
possible factors influencing distribution. 
 
 
2   MATERIAL AND METHODS 
 
The main data recorded for the habitats alongside the Drava, which served as sampling areas, 
are shown in Table 1.  This study included not only the above areas, but also several aquatic 
habitats in the Balaton-Drava ecology network, i.e. the ponds in the Boronka Nature 
Conservation Area, Fonó Fishpond and the adjoining stream, the fishpond system in 
Petesmalom and Tetves Stream.  Sampling, along a standard route, commenced on the Drava 
in January 2000 and was repeated every six weeks at first, then every four weeks from May 
2002 to December 2002 to ensure that sufficient fresh samples were collected for the genetic 
study (Table 1, at end of paper).  Fresh samples were collected early in the morning, between 
0500 and 1000 hours.  
 
The feeding habits of the otter were examined by spraint analysis.  The data obtained 
related to samples for winter and spring and summer and autumn collectively.  Dietary 
composition was determined by microscope examination, on the basis of characteristics of 
feathers, bones, scales, pharyngeal teeth, chitin shell, teeth and hair (LANSZKI ET AL., 2001; 
LANSZKI, KÖRMENDI AND HANCZ, 2002).  Dietary composition and food niche breadth were 
calculated on the basis of the relative frequency of occurrence of items in the spraints, this in 
turn being based on minimum number present per sample.

where pi = the relative frequency of a given taxon (KREBS, 1989).  The taxa used were: (1) 
mammals, (2) birds, (3) reptiles and amphibians, (4) fish, (5) invertebrates and (6) plant 
matter.  
To calculate relative otter density the number of spraints (both the total and fresh) 
collected was taken relative to the unit length of the route section. Calculation of density 
ranking involved correction on the basis of duration and frequency of sampling. The data 
were recorded in an Excel spreadsheet, and the SPSS 7.5 programme (GREEN, SALKIND AND 
AKEY, 1997) was used for data processing. The hierarchical cluster analysis (UPGMA) was 
based on Pearson correlation.

3   RESULTS 
 
3.1 Dietary composition 
Various fish species composed the main proportion of the otter diet in the Drava (İrtilos and 
Bélavár), in some of its backwaters (Babócsa, Bélavár and Barcs), in streams with constant 
flow rate (Babócsa), and in the fishponds (Table 1).  In addition to these fish species which 
formed the principal food source, a significant role was played by secondary food sources, i.e. 
amphibians and birds, along certain stretches of the Drava (at Vízvár), and in the gravel-pit 
pool close to the Drava (Somogyudvarhely).  Consumption of mammals and invertebrates, not 
usually characteristic of the otter diet, occurred frequently in the habitats alongside the Drava 
which periodically dried up, e.g. the Lankóc alder forest, the canals and some streams.  
Classification based on similarity in the dietary composition of the otters living in the 
different areas and determined by means of cluster analysis is illustrated by a dendrogram 
(Figure 1).  The habitats where there was a supply of fish beneficial to the otter, i.e. the 
Drava, its backwaters and the ponds, were categorised in the first group.  The majority of 
these habitats were used for angling or commercial fishing.  The Drava-bound streams with 
constant flow rate were also grouped in this category.  Streams which periodically dried up or 
had a low flow rate were classified in the second group. These had a supply of fish or 
amphibians which served as important sources of food in some seasons.  The third group 
comprised canals and streams which periodically ran dry and whose surroundings provided 
only a sparse food supply.

3.3   Relative population density 
The indirect line transect method based on the number of spraints collected over the three 
years of the study was used to monitor otter density ranking for each habitat (Figure 3) and to 
follow changes in this ranking.  It would not be possible to draw distinct boundaries between 
the areas of high, medium and low otter density shown in Figure 3.  The highest otter density 
data were recorded in the habitats in which human influence was minimal (in areas protected 
by enhanced conservation management: in the backwater and on the Drava at Bélavár, in the 
Középrigóc Ponds, and in places where the primary aim was fish production, such as Fonó 
Fishpond).  Medium otter density was recorded in the rapidly flowing, open stretches of the 
Drava (i.e., in İrtilos and Vízvár), the backwaters alongside the Drava (Barcs and Babócsa), 
the ponds under conservation management (Boronka and Petesmalom) and the streams with 
constant flow rate.  Otter density proved low in the habitats which periodically ran dry (the 
Lankóci alder forest, the Dombó Canal and the Korcsina Canal), the streams with low flow 
rate (Tetves Stream) and the ponds where there was intensive fishing (Somogyudvarhely).  
 
According to the density ranking on the basis of fresh spraints per unit length (Figure 
4), most of the areas of high otter density were also classified into the upper quartile range.  In 
contrast, areas of moderate otter density along the Drava River were classified in the low 
quartile range on the basis of total samples (Figures 3 and 4).

4   DISCUSSION 
 
In this study, fish of various species constituted the main food source for the otter in the 
Drava, its backwaters and in fishponds, where there was an abundant supply of fish, and also 
in the fishponds.  Similar observations have been made in other freshwater studies (e.g. 
ERLINGE, 1967a, 1969; WISE, LINN AND KENNEDY, 1981; MASON AND MACDONALD, 1986; 
KEMENES AND NECHAY, 1990; CARSS, 1995; KRUUK, 1995).  Low frequency of occurrence 
(below 50-60%) of the principal fish prey indicated that they were periods when the fish 
species were low, or that the quantities available fluctuated greatly from season to season.  At 
such times, secondary food sources became more significant.  In the habitats prevalent in 
Hungary, these sources were most frequently amphibians and birds (Table 1).  Nevertheless, 
where fish were in abundant supply the proportion accounted for by typical secondary sources 
did not increase above ca. 20%.  Frequent consumption of other taxa not usually characteristic 
of the diet of otters (e.g. mammals and invertebrates) highlighted a lack of commonly 
available (i.e., primary and secondary) food sources.  Such disadvantages arose in habitats 
which periodically ran dry and where the food supply was sparse.  
 
When fish supply is plentiful, the food niche of the otter is generally narrow, as the 
largest proportion of its diet consists of various species of fish.  The otter is a non-specialist 
with respect to the fish it consumes, i.e., if a sufficient quantity of any species is available in a 
given habitat these will feature in significant proportions in the diet.  For example, the rasbora 
(Pseudorasbora parva) occurred at a frequency of 90% in the winter-spring diet of the otters 
living alongside Barcs-Komlósd Stream, which has a slow flow rate.  Thus, in the course of 
hunting, the otter is capable of adapting to less than optimal conditions, and, when necessary, 
can prey on small fish, as well as on frogs, birds, aquatic insects and small mammals.  There 
proved to be a correlation between increasing breadth of food niche and periodically arising 
inadequacies in fish supply (e.g. the aquatic habitat running dry). In some stretches and 
backwaters of the Drava and in fishponds this narrow food niche (Figure 2) was accompanied 
by higher otter density (Figure 3).  
 
The generally moderate density of otters recorded on the Drava (Figures 3 and 4) may 
have been related primarily to strong river current and substantial fluctuation in water levels 
(100-900 m3/sec, difference between flood and low-water marks is approximately seven 
metres), and, when the level was low, may also have been due to the fact that the riverbank 
was too steep for the otters.  The species is discouraged by steep banks or those with 
unfavourable degrees of plant cover (i.e. where vegetation is too dense or bare) which 
prevents them getting in and out of the water (KEMENES AND DEMETER, 1995; KRUUK, 1995).  
Water entry places play an important role in social behaviour, e.g. in play, grooming and the 
marking of territory.  Deterioration of these areas (e.g. removal of vegetation on the banks) 
has a negative effect, as does substantial change in water level.  Otters only occasionally mark 
their territory on the stones surrounding the banks of the Drava, which protrude when the 
water level is low.  Substantial fluctuations in water levels, which can occur every few days, 
result in spraints which serve as important chemical and visual markers being washed away 
prematurely by the water. 
 
It can thus be established that the hydroelectric power stations currently in operation 
exerts an adverse effect on the rhythm of life of the otters studied on the banks of the River 
Drava.  If a new, high-capacity power station is built on this tail-water-like river section, 
habitats alongside the Drava would further suffer from the effects of running dry, 
accompanied by deepening of the riverbed, moderate formation of river shoals and further 
lowering of the underground water table.  This could pose a threat to the stability of 
populations of species associated with water, including otters living in the surrounding area.
