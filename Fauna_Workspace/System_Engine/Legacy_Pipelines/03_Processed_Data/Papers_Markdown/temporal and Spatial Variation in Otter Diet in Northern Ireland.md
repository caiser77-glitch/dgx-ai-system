TEMPORAL AND SPATIAL VARIATION IN

IRELAND
S.J. Preston, A.A. Portig, W.I. Montgomery, R.A. McDonald,
J.T.A. Dick and J.S. Fairley
ABSTRACT
The European otter (Lutra lutra L.) has a highly specialised diet that is composed predominantly of
fish. The current study investigates the percentage composition of food items in otter spraints
collected in six river catchments in Northern Ireland in 1980 and again from the same locations in
2003. Spraints contained significantly more salmonids than any other prey item. The composition of
spraints differed among catchments. More salmonids and three-spined stickleback (Gasterosteus
aculeatus L.) were found in spraints from the Glens of Antrim, while spraints from the Lagan
catchment had significantly more eel (Anguilla anguilla L.). There were significantly more spraints
containing non-fish food items in 2003 compared with 1980. These non-fish items were insects,
amphipods, birds, rats (Rattus norvegicus Berkenhout) and lagomorphs. Increased consumption of
non-fish items was apparent in all but one of the river catchments. The mean diversity of spraint
composition was significantly greater in 2003 than in 1980. Therefore, our findings indicate that
otters have diversified their diet since 1980 and now eat more non-fish prey.
INTRODUCTION
The European otter (Lutra lutra L.) has suffered a
decline in both range and numbers over much of the
UK
and
western
Europe
since
the
1950s
(Macdonald and Mason 1994). Prior to the first
National Otter Survey (Chapman and Chapman
1982), little was known about the status of the otter
in Ireland. Otters were widespread in 1980, with
91.7% of sites surveyed having otter signs. However,
a recent survey in Northern Ireland found a small
but significant decline in the occurrence of otters
since the original survey (Preston et al. 2006). This
decline is not restricted to particular habitats or
catchments, indicating that it may be due to some
widespread factor such as water quality or food
availability (Preston et al. 2006).
The conservation of native species requires an
understanding of factors affecting their distribution
and abundance. Many carnivore species are limited
by food (Estes 1996). Prey availability is crucial
because it can regulate factors such as population
density; length and success of the breeding period;
carrying capacity; time spent feeding in different
patches; and mortality (Ruiz-Olmo et al. 2001).
Most mammalian predators are capable of eating or
subsisting on many kinds of food, though they are
rarely indiscriminate in the types of food they eat
(Dunn 1977) and often show preferences for a
particular range of dietary items. However, where
animals are restricted to particular habitats, dietary
choice can be limited and prey availability becomes
more crucial to survival. Thus, the detection of
dietary shifts over time may indicate the likelihood
of changes in the status of a population. Examples of
dietary shifts in response to changes in density of
predators or prey exist for a diversity of animals,
including reindeer (Rangifer tarandus L.) (Klein
1968) and voles (Microtus sp.) (Cockburn and
Lidicker 1983). Similar case studies are lacking for
mammalian carnivores (Watt et al. 2000), but the
review by Berger (1998) highlights the importance
and malleability of consumerprey relationships
across this group.
Here, we investigate the diet of the European
otter,
which
feeds
in
coastal
and
freshwater
environments and has a highly specialised diet
compared to other carnivores (Kruuk 1995). The
otter has been described as a ‘fish specialist’ (Mason
and Macdonald 1986). However, several studies
suggest that it may be better described as an
opportunistic specialist, with its feeding behaviour
largely dependent on the availability of prey (Ottino
and Giller 2004). Diet may comprise fish, mammals,
amphibians (at certain times of the year) and birds
(Conroy et al. 1993). However, fish are the most
important food item, constituting over 80% of otter
diet (Mason and Macdonald 1986). There have been
several detailed studies of otter diet throughout
Europe (see Clavero et al. (2003) for review),
including
several
studies
in
southern
Ireland
(O’Sullivan 1994; Fairley 2001; Clavero et al.

METHODS
The Vincent Wildlife Trust supplied 89 otter
spraints collected from freshwater sites between
July and September 1980. The spraints had been
stored in individual plastic vials at room temperature.
The sites from which these spraints were collected in
1980 were revisited at the same time of year (92
weeks) in 2003 and a fresh spraint obtained if
present. These sites and spraints, which were
independent of the sites surveyed by Preston et al.
(2006), represented the Glens of Antrim, Lough
Neagh and the Foyle, Lagan, Erne and Mourne
catchments (Fig. 1). Fresh otter spraints were found
at 70 of the original 89 sites surveyed in 1980.

DISCUSSION
The current investigation aimed to assess any
difference in the diet of otters in Northern Ireland
between 1980 and 2003 by the analysis of spraint
composition. We have confirmed the findings of
earlier authors that otters are mainly predators of
fish, particularly salmonids. Other fish species such
as stickleback, cyprinid, eel and stone loach occur at
significantly lower frequencies. Non-fish prey items
including
frogs,
birds,
mammals,
insects
and
crustaceans are present but usually only in small
amounts.
Spraints collected from the Glens of Antrim
had a significantly greater percentage composition
of salmonid remains compared to spraints from
other catchments. These rivers are short and
precipitous, with good water quality. Typically
such
oligotrophic
rivers
contain
a
limited
freshwater fish species assemblage dominated by
Atlantic salmon and brown trout (Kruuk et al.
1993). Therefore, the abundance of salmonid
remains in spraints may simply reflect the limited
choice of prey items in these rivers, since spraints
from more eutrophic rivers have been found to

have a wider range of fish species present (Jenkins
and Harper 1980; Ruiz-Olmo 1998). If otters are
limited by food availability (Kruuk et al. 1993;
Kruuk 1995; Ruiz-Olmo et al. 2001), we might
predict otter numbers in this catchment to be low.
This is borne out in the results of a recent study of
otter distribution in Northern Ireland that found
the lowest percentage occurrence of signs of otters
in the Glens of Antrim (Preston et al. 2006).
Although fish specialists, otters have been
identified as opportunists and their diet tends to
change with variation in the relative abundance of
the different prey types (Ottino and Giller 2004).
Ottino and Giller (2004) found that frogs and birds
increased in importance in otter diet when fish prey
were known to be less abundant. High levels of
predation on frogs has been noted during the frog
breeding season (Fairley 2001). Water birds may
also be taken on a regular basis (Hewsen 1973).
Mammals are usually a minor component of diet of
otters, and invertebrates are generally ingested with
other prey unless the otter happens to come across
large numbers of them (Ottino and Giller 2004).
Here we found a significantly greater incidence of
non-fish prey items in spraints collected in 2003,
with otters consuming a wider range of non-fish
prey items, including frog, bird, rat, lagomorph,
insects and crustaceans, suggesting a change in otter
diet since 1980. In addition, the mean diversity of
spraint composition was greater in 2003 compared
to 1980.
Competition
from
other
mammalian
fish
predators such as mink seems unlikely due to fact
that several studies have shown otters to be the
dominant competitor (Chanin 1985; Bueno 1996;
Bonesi et al. 2000). Therefore, it seems more likely
that the changes observed in otter diet have been
triggered by other factors such as an increase in the
availability or profitability of non-fish prey items, a
decline in fish prey or an increased difficulty in
finding preferred fish prey items. There is a close
relationship between otter diet and the relative
frequency of occurrence within the fish stock
available (Lanszki et al. 2001). A shift in the diet
of otters in Loch Lomond has recently been
demonstrated
following
the
introduction
and
proliferation of non-native ruffe (Gymnocephalus
cernuus
L.) (McCafferty 2005). Since it seems
unlikely that fish have become more elusive to
foraging otters, it is more reasonable to assume that
the change in otter diet is a result of a decline in the
number of favoured fish prey. Similar case studies
have been carried out for the sea otter (Enhydra
lutris L.) in Alaska, where changes in both sea otter
diet and prey availability provided the first clear
signal of the otter population decline (Watt et al.
2000).
The current investigation may contribute to an
understanding of the findings of the most recent
survey of otter distribution in Northern Ireland,
which showed that there has been a decline in the
occurrence of signs of otters since the early 1980s
(Preston et al. 2006). This decline may be due to
limitations in food supply, and consequently otters
may have become less discriminatory and may
now consume a more varied diet consisting of
more
non-fish
prey
items.
Therefore,
future
investigations of the conservation status of otters
should consider more analysis of otter diet in
relation to both water quality and fish stocks in
rivers.
