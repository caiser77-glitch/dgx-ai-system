Welcome to the fourth edition of ‘Making Tracks’, the bi-annual newsletter of the
‘Celebrating Cornwall’s Mammals - from dormice to dolphins!’ Project. 
Over the last few months the Project has centred around carnivores with the release of the
Carnivore pack. In this issue we focus on the carnivore tracks and signs which you may come
across whilst out and about. We find out why Vic Simpson (Wildlife Investigation Centre) is
appealing for Stoat and Weasel corpses. We report back on the General Mammal Walk held
at Trehudreth Small-Holding in February and the all the wonderful things we came across. We
discover the most recorded and under recorded mammal species in Cornwall in our brief
review of the last (or indeed first) two years of the Project, and find out which are the latest
mammal identification packs available. 
I would like to say a very big thank you to Kim Jelbert, Fay Robinson, Chris Bowen and Alison
Forward for their invaluable help with the project.
I would also like to thank all of you who have supported the Project either by attending events,
adopting 10x10km squares or sending in your records, your help and input is invaluable!
I would like to express my sincere thanks to The Marjorie Coote Animal Charities Trust for
their very generous donation to the Mammals’ Project.
Alex Howie

As part of the ‘Celebrating Cornwall’s Mammals – from
dormice to dolphins!’ Project you can adopt a
10x10km square (hectad) and record the mammals,
tracks and signs you see in this area.  To help you with
recording, we are producing free mammal packs
containing identification guides, species information,
and other fascinating facts.  So far there has been an
overwhelming response and we would like to take this
opportunity to thank all those who have participated.
Some squares however remain under recorded and for
this reason we still need your help. On the 17th
February, Alex Howie (Mammals’ Project Co-
ordinator), Jenny Roberts (a volunteer from Germany)
and myself (Cornwall Wildlife Trust Volunteer) decided
to survey one of these under recorded squares in the
Newquay area.  It was absolutely great and I was really
surprised at what you can see when you look for it!  
Within a few minutes of stepping onto the footpath, we had
established that the area was home to Grey Squirrels, Rabbits
and Moles.  Chisel-marked Hazelnuts alerted us to the presence
of Wood Mice, whilst piles of chewed grass stems, found
amongst runs in the long grass, suggested that Field Voles were
also present.  It was, however, the carnivore signs that caught
my attention.  Red Foxes are primarily nocturnal (active at
night) so we were unlikely to actually see any, they are solitary
animals that occupy a territory which they mark using faeces
and urine.  Both fox scats (faeces) and urine have a strong acrid
smell and the scats often contain bones and fur of small
mammals and bird feathers.  We found numerous scats and
frequently noticed a strong acrid smell indicating the presence
of Foxes in the area.
The habitat within the area ranged from lowland unimproved
grassland, semi-improved grassland, and cultivated land to
woodland and areas of scrub and heath.  Evidence of
Cornwall’s mining heritage was present not just from the
deserted mines but also from the red colour of the stream bed.
Pollutants such as Zinc, Lead, Manganese, Copper and Iron
leak into rivers following rising groundwater and erosion of
waste rock from mines. Otters are rarely found in these areas
due to the toxicity that these pollutants present to freshwater
life.  Our stream was also particularly shallow and unlikely to
support Otters, however, we still looked for Otter spraints
(faeces) as they often use smaller streams to travel between
larger rivers.  Otters are carnivores and like the Red Fox mark
their territory using faeces.  Spraints are dark in colour, may
contain undigested bones of aquatic life, and have a distinctive
fishy-scented odour (akin to Jasmine tea!).  Favoured spraint
sites include rocks and tussocks along the riverbank, under
bridges, and on the ‘saddles’ of overhanging trees.
Unfortunately, no Otters seemed to be living on our stretch of
the river!  
All was not lost as we did find a Stoat den in a tree overhanging
the river.  Stoat dens can be found in hollow trees and burrows
or in rock crevices such as those in dry stone walling.  Alex first
noticed the hole and when we looked inside, we were surprised
to find a dead Rabbit.  Stoats feed on a variety of small
mammals, birds, and Rabbits.  To catch a Rabbit the Stoat
employs a most impressive tactic; it does back flips and leaps
around like a crazed thing, then suddenly runs straight at the
captivated Rabbit and kills it with a single bite to the neck.
Following a successful kill the Stoat drags the Rabbit back to its
den to consume it at its leisure, hence the Rabbit in the tree hole.
Further along the footpath, not far from the river bank we
noticed recently excavated pits and when we looked closer
realised that these were Badger latrines.  Badgers are
particularly clean animals that dig pits to defecate in (latrines).
Following the trail of latrines along the hedgerow, we found the
Badger sett.  The sett characterised by its ‘D’ shaped entrances
was extremely large; we counted 19 entrances, however, on
returning to the path realised that many more were concealed
by the undergrowth.  Badgers live in a system of interconnected
tunnels and chambers that are passed from parent to offspring.
Each generation adds tunnels and entrances indicating that our
sett was decades old perhaps even over a century.  
So after only a couple of hours wondering around the
countryside we found evidence of numerous species including
small mammals, Squirrels, Moles and of course my favourite the
carnivores!  It was a great day and I recommend it to anybody
who is interested in wildlife, as like me I think you will be
surprised just how much you can see once you know what to
look for.  
Kim Jelbert

Veterinary pathologist Vic Simpson established the
Wildlife Veterinary Investigation Centre in 2001. The
workload of the centre has steadily increased and Vic
has now taken on another veterinary surgeon, Alex
Tomlinson, to work at the Centre in the afternoons.
Post-mortem examination of wildlife casualties not
only tells us about cause of death (which may be quite
clear from the case history), but also, possibly more
significantly, adds to our knowledge of anatomical and
physiological parameters in healthy individuals and
the presence of diseases in the population.  
There is concern that numbers of Stoats and Weasels are in
decline in Britain. Since August 2001, 17 Weasels and 15 Stoats
have been submitted to the Centre for post-mortem examination. 
Causes of death
The majority of Weasel deaths appear to be due to predation,
predominantly by Cats, whilst the majority of Stoat deaths
appear to be due to collisions with road traffic. 
Diseases and conditions found in Stoats and Weasels
Parasitic worms have been found in the nasal passages of 18
% of Weasels and 27 % of Stoats and a fungal infection of the
lungs of 29% of the Weasels and 20% of Stoats. The fungus
has also been found in Otters and Moles. Further studies are
required to determine the
significance of these and
other infections.
A number of British wildlife
species, including Stoats and
Weasels, are at risk of
rodenticide 
poisoning
through eating poisoned
prey. 
Specimens 
are
examined at the Wildlife VIC
for evidence of poisoning
and 
liver

are
submitted for rodenticide
analysis.

dolphins!’ Project was launched in
June 2003. Now that we are
nearing the half way point of the
Project we thought we would give
you a brief summary of the progress
so far! 
At the time of writing 45 out of the 64 10
x 10km squares that cover Cornwall are
adopted, with 87 people signed up to
record in their relative adopted squares. 
Generally there has been an increase in
records received over the last two years
resulting in much healthier looking
distribution maps and many more gaps
filled! There are still a couple of areas with
very few records; in particular SX28 and
SX29 in the Bodmin Moor and Week St
Mary areas, and SW83, the Roseland
peninsula. There are also very few
records for certain species.
Some facts and figures!

Lesser White-toothed Shrew (only found

A Wildlife haven!
The 
‘Celebrating 
Cornwall’s
Mammals’ 
Project 
has 
been
extremely fortunate in that we have
been granted the use of a beautiful
wildlife haven to run a number of
mammal events. Over the last few
months, we have held a general
mammal walk and a small mammal-
trapping event at Trehudreth Small-
Holding, Blisland, Bodmin. The small-
holding includes a variety of habitats
including 
spring 
and 
summer
flowering meadows, humid rush
pasture, coppiced Hazel woodland,
Cornish hedges and a river. 
In February, a group of us decided to
brave the elements for a general
mammal walk, despite the freezing cold
and a few numb fingers and toes we
managed to find numerous mammal
tracks and signs. These included Otter
spraint, Fox scat, an active Badger sett,
Hazel nuts gnawed by Grey Squirrels,
Bank Vole and Wood Mice, a Field Vole
nest and numerous runs, droppings and
feeding remains, Red and Roe Deer
slots, Roe Deer droppings and even a
Roe Deer antler!
We returned in April (this time graced
with much better weather!) to carry out
some small mammal trapping. We set
40 Longworth traps around the small-
holding and caught 3 Field Vole, 7 Bank
Vole, 10 Wood Mouse, 5 Common
Shrew, 1 Pygmy Shrew and even a
Water Shrew. This is pretty good going! 
We expected to find a wide range of
species in this area, as there are so many
places where the small mammals can
make their nests and burrows. So much
food is also available for them in the way
of berries, seeds, fruits, nuts, insects etc.
throughout the year. 
Although we didn’t find any evidence of
Dormice this time, we will be returning
to Trehudreth in July to put up some
Dormouse nesting tubes - a method to
establish the presence or absence of
Dormice in an area - as part of the two-
day ‘Cornwall’s Mammal Medley’ event.
We will also have a walk around the site
to see what mammal tracks and signs we
come across at this time of year. 
If you would like to join us for the
‘Cornwall’s Mammal Medley’ event on
Friday 1st and Saturday 2nd July and
haven’t already returned you booking
form then please contact me.
