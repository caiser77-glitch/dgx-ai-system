PRELIMINARY RESULTS OF THE POST MORTEM ANALYSIS OF OTTERS IN HUNGARY 
 
József LANSZKI1, Dénes NAGY2, Enikı OROSZ3, László SUGÁR1, Erzsébet GAÁLNÉ-DARIN3, 
András HIDAS4, Gábor NECHAY5

email: lanszki@mail.atk.u-kaposvar.hu 
2Eötvös Lóránd University, Department of Systematic Zoology and Ecology, 1117, Budapest, Pázmány Péter

Abstract:  Since 2002, post mortem analysis has been performed on 25 otters (2 juvenile; 4 subadult;  19 adult). 
In all but one instance the cause of death was a road trafficaccident, and the other death was as a result of bite 
wounds caused by another otter. The body weight (mean ±s.e.) in males was 8.84 ± 0.87 kilogrammes (range 
2.78-11.98 kg; n = 10) and in females 5.14 ± 0.43 kilogrammes (1.21-7.52 kg; n = 15). In otters older than two 
years the body weight in males was 9.52 kilogrammes (n = 9) and in females 5.81 kilogrammes (n = 10). The 
condition index in males was 1.35 ± 0.09 (n = 10) and in females 0.89 ± 0.05 (n = 11). One female was pregnant 
(three embryos), while placental scars (mean 2.6) were found in another five. The weight of the stomach content 
was 109 ± 26 grammes (0.1 - 349 g) but the stomach was empty in nine occasions. Bioaccumulation of 
pollutants was examined from livers (24 cases) and once from muscle (strongly damaged body). The 
concentration of metals (mean ± s.e.; dry weight; mg/kg) in tissues were: lead 0.07 ± 0.03 (range 0-0.56), 
cadmium 0.10 ± 0.03 (0-0.54), mercury 3.33 ± 0.57 (0-11.24), copper 25.3 ± 2.40 (8.5-58.9) and zinc 109.1 ± 7.4 
(67.7-207.2). Mean data recorded  were similar to moderate European values, but concentrations were 
generally higher in rivers from  the eastern part of Hungary. Concentrations of PCBs were low (0.20 ± 0.05 
mg/kg; wet weight). From the kidneys DNA analysis was performed for four microsatellite loci (Lut-457, 615, 
604 and 832). 
 
Keywords: Lutra lutra, post mortem analysis, accumulation of pollutants, DNA, Hungary 
 
 
1   INTRODUCTION 
 
In many parts of Europe, and eslwehere in the wider world, researchers have performed 
detailed post mortems or examined the accumulation of different pollutants (summarised e.g. 
by SMIT ET AL., 1998; SHORE, WALKER AND WIENBURG, 2002). Initially these studies were 
motivated by concerns for industrial and agricultural contamination or catastrophes, which 
caused serious problems in the environment.  Recently the population monitoring aspect on 
the basis of post mortem protocols for carnivores, e.g. otters, has also been identified as being 
important. However, neither the extent of pollution, nor the consequent damage it caused to 
carnivores has been adequately clarified.  
 
These examinations were carried out on Eurasian otters (Lutra lutra) from Hungary, 
where few data are available. Only GUTLEB (1995) and GUTLEB ET AL. (1998) have published 
comparative results from the Middle European region. 
 
On the basis of studies (GERA, 2001; HELTAI, 2002) it is clear that the otter is a 
widespread predator throughout Hungary (Figure 1), where the population is largely stable or 
increasing slightly.  Despite their wide distribution, the collection and analysis of dead otters 
began only in recent years.  The known numbers of otters killed in road trafficaccidents were: 
1995 - 4 animals; 1996 - 7; 1997 - 11; 1998 -19; 1999 -18; 2000 - 27; 2001 - 30; 2002 - 31 
(GERA, 2003). Most dead otters were male, but not all bodies were suitable for post mortem 
examination and not all dead otters were collected. The otter is strictly protected in Hungary,

but persecution does occur.  Analysis of road traffic kills give special results because it is not 
known whether the individuals were resident or migrant.

The start of the examination was motivated by the cyanide and other heavy metal 
spills that occurred on the eastern rivers in Romania.  Furthermore we tried to identify more 
data about the otters living along freshwater in order to establish a conservation strategy and 
possible population management for the species in Hungary. 
 
The aim of the study was to undertake detailed post mortem analysis of otters to 
quantify the accumulation of some important heavy metals and polychlorinated biphenyls 
(PCBs), in livers.  From the samples, DNA analysis was undertaken to study the genetic 
diversity of otters in Hungary. 
 
 
2   MATERIAL AND METHODS  
 
The more detailed post mortem analysis started in 2002.  The dead otters were collected by 
the staff of the National Parks.  Two-thirds of the bodies originated from the eastern area of 
the country, mainly close to the Tisza, Kıröses and Maros Rivers, and partially from fish 
ponds (Figure 2). One-third of the bodies came from the western part of the country near  
Lake Balaton or Small Balaton, the Kapos River or fish ponds.  From the 25 reported 
individuals, ten were male and 15 female. Two were young (2 months old), four sub-adult 
(under 2 years) and 19 adult, above two years.  Exact age determination on the basis of teeth 
has not yet been made. 
 
 
 
Figure 2:  Geographic location of otter carcasses collected in Hungary in this study 
 
The bodies were frozen (-18oC) until analysis and the post mortem analysis was 
carried out following the prescription of SIMPSON (2002).  In the examination we recorded 


 
3
general data (location, date, cause of death, date of analysis, body weight, body length, tail 
length, to calculate the condition index of both sexes as described in KRUUK (1995).  In 
addition, we registered the different types of wounds (eyes, ears, feet, foot pads, claws, skin, 
genitals), scored the fat deposition over flank (from 1 to 3), observed the condition of the 
mammary glands (milk secretion or not), measured the size and weight of the internal organs 
(adrenal gland, thymus, lungs, heart, liver, kidney, testis, ovaries, uterus, stomach), and a 
veterinarian examined the health status of organs.  We also counted the placenta scars.  Some 
bodies were so damaged from the road accident that it was not possible to make all the 
measurements, for example, if the vertebral column was broken, the body length data were 
not real, so in these cases no condition index was calculated.  Therefore in some traits 
different sample sizes can be found.  The tissues (liver, heart, muscle, rib bone) were stored 
frozen, and the samples of reproductive organs (testis, ovary) preserved in formaline solution.  
 
The measurement of pollutants was made from the liver, except in one case when  
muscle was used because the internal organs were greatly damaged.  Analyses of metals and 
PCBs were carried out in the Central Veterinary Institute, Budapest. Homogenised tissue 
samples (0.8 grams) were digested with three milllitres of nitric acid (67%, purified in sub-
boiling distillation system) and two millilitres of hydrogen peroxide in a microwave digestion 
unit (Milestone Ethos Plus). The liver samples were analysed in parallel, the test solution 
volume was 20 millilitres. The copper and zinc concentrations were determined by AAS 
(atomic absorption spectrophotometer, PU 9200X), using flame atomisation.  The limit of 
quantitation was 2.0 mg/kg; wet tissue.  The lead and cadmium concentrations were 
determined by AAS equipped with graphite furnace (PU 9390X) and autosampler (FS 90).  
The limit of quantitation were 0.10 and 0.02 mg/kg; wet weight respectively.  The mercury 
concentrations were determined by AAS equipped with accessories used for cold-vapor 
technique (UNICAM VP 90).  The limit of quantitation was 0.10 mg/kg; wet weight.  Levels 
of (PCBs) in tissues were determined according to the Hungarian standard method (1987).  
Analysis was performed by gas chromatograph (Packard 437A), using Ni63 electron capture 
detector.  Five different PCB congeneers (2,4,4'; 2,2'5,5'; 2,2'4,5,5'; 2,2'4,4'5,5'; 2,2',3,4,4'5,5') 
were used for qualification and quantification. 
From kidney samples (n = 25) genetic analysis was performed for four microsatellite 
loci (DALLAS AND PIERTNEY, 1998; DALLAS ET AL., 1999).  The suitable PCR products 
originating from Cy5-marked microsatellite markers, producing primers of proper quality 
were run on ALFexpress DNA analyser. 
Consideration of the low sample size, statistical analysis was made occasionally (two-
sample t-test, Mann-Whitney U-test).  
 
 
3   RESULTS AND DISCUSSION 
 
3.1 Post mortem analysis 
The principal cause of death was road trafic accidents.  Only one animal, a female found at 
the Tisza River, was not killed by a vehicle, and she had bite wounds around the genitals 
caused by another otter . 
 
In five cases damaged claws (old, scarred) were found, and on five animals small cuts 
and abrasions were observed on the pad.  Except for one case from the Tisza River, no 
damage was recorded on  the skin or ears.  
Mean body weight for all males was 8.84 ± 0.867 kilogrammes (mean ± s.e.) range 
2.78 -11.98 kg; n = 10)  and 5.14 ± 0.43 kilogrammes (range  1.21 - 7.52 kg;  n = 15) for all  
females.  For adult males, the mean weight was 9.52 kilogrammes (n = 9), for adult females it 
was 5.81 kilogrammes (n = 10).  Smallest weights were found in winter, but the number of

The stomach was empty in two-thirds of the cases or contained less than 50 grams of 
food. In the other (not empty) stomachs the mean weight of food was only 109 ± 26 grams (n 
= 16).  The otters died on roads mainly in autumn, the time of harvesting at the fish ponds and 
therefore coincides with a period of food deficiency which can develop very quickly. 


 
6
The main food eaten by otters was fish (65.8%); the secondary most important type 
was amphibians (19.3%) as well as birds (14.0%), and in low quantities small mammals, 
reptiles and invertebrates.  
 
The reproduction characteristics of otters were investigated in the females. Ten of the 
15 treated females were adults, more than two years old.  We found early  reproduction in  six  
(60%).  Forty per cent of the adult females were either nulliparous or we could not detect 
early pregnancy. One female was pregnant, with two embryos in one uterine horn and one in 
the other; this horn was, however, damaged and may have contained a second  one. 
 
Occurrence of early pregnancy and the possible number of embryos were counted by 
the number of placental scars in the uterine horns.  Suckling was detected in two females, 
(they were lactating) and the number of active teats equalled the number of placental scars (3 
and 4).  Mean number of placental scars recorded was 2.6 ± 0.4, which shows a  litter size of  
wild-living otters of between two and three. 
 
3.2 Accumulation of pollutants in the livers of otters 
The mean concentration of lead in the liver was 0.07 ± 0.03 mg/kg (range n.d.-0.56) and 
cadmium 0.10 ± 0.03 mg/kg (n.d.- 0.54) was low.  The concentration of these two metals in 
livers did not differ significantly (P>0.05, t-test) in the eastern rivers (the Tisza, Kıröses and 
Maros Rivers) which are more open to contamination, compared to the western areas (Lake 
Balaton, Small Balaton) or fish ponds.  In the present study, the concentration of the lead and 
cadmium in the liver of otters was low compared to other studies (e.g. MASON AND  
MACDONALD, 1986; GUTLEB ET AL., 1998; RUIZ-OLMO ET AL., 2002) and 10–20% that of  
earlier  Hungarian results (GUTLEB ET AL., 1998). 
 
The concentration of mercury in the liver of otters differed significantly (P<0.05, t-
test) between the eastern rivers and other areas.  The mean value was 3.33 ± 0.57 mg/kg (n.d.-
11.24) and was in the middle of the ranges recorded from other areas (MASON AND  
MACDONALD. 1986; KRUUK, 1995; DULFER AND  ROCHE, 1998; GUTLEB ET AL., 1998; MIERLE 
ET AL., 2000; RUIZ-OLMO ET AL., 2002; GUTLEB, 2002), but five times higher than from earlier 
measurements in Hungary (GUTLEB ET AL., 1998), when otters originated mainly from fish 
ponds. 
 
After the cyanide spill in Romania in February 2000, heavy metal (iron, lead, copper, 
zinc) spills also occurred in March of 2000 on the Szamos and Tisza Rivers.  The copper 
concentration (mean 25.3 ± 2.4 mg/kg; range 8.5 - 58.9), was significantly (P<0.01, t-test) 
higher on the eastern rivers compared to other areas.  The zinc concentration (mean 109.1 ± 
7.4 mg/kg; range 67.7 - 207.2) did not differ significantly between areas. In the present study 
we measured medium or medium-high concentration levels compared with other studies 
(GUTLEB ET AL., 1998; RUIZ-OLMO ET AL., 2002; SIDOROVICH, 2002; MASON AND STEPHENSON, 
2001) and a little higher than in the earlier analysis from Hungary (GUTLEB ET AL., 1998). 
 
In numerous countries the accumulation of PCBs caused serious problems especially 
to top predators, including the otter, and have been linked to the declining populations 
(GUTLEB, 2002).  In our study the accumulation of PCBs was low (0.20 ± 0.05 mg/kg, wet 
weight); there was no significant difference between areas, although the highest values were 
measured on standing waters.  The concentration of total PCBs in the otters’ livers in Hungary 
was at a very low level in international comparisons (MASON AND MACDONALD, 1986; 
GUTLEB, 1995; GUTLEB ET AL., 1998; SMIT ET AL., 1998; RUIZ-OLMO ET AL., 2002; SIDOROVICH, 
2002), however further, harmonised measurements are needed. 
 
3.3 DNA analysis 
The analysed microsatellite loci, and the number and size of alleles per loci were Lut-457 (n = 
5, 180-192 bp), L-615 (n = 7, 254-266 bp), L-604 (n = 5, 132-140 bp), L-832 (n = 6, 206-232 


 
7
bp).  On the same locus, greatly different range of size of alleles were amplified, when 
compared to the study of DALLAS AND PIERTNEY (1998), except L-615, where the size ranges 
overlapped.  The number of alleles on each loci was similar.  
In further work we plan to reveal the alleles of other microsatellite loci from otter 
tissues.  We are starting the examination of the more informative loci (with most alleles) from 
DNA isolates obtained from fresh spraints. 
 
 
4   CONCLUSIONS 
 
The post mortem analysis of otters has started in Hungary and it is intended to continue and  
to widen the  analysis.  The National Parks are partners in this work. 
 
Through the post mortem analysis, supplementary data are available, for example, 
about the condition or reproduction of both females or males.  These data are not easily 
available in any other way.  Larger sample size would allow deeper statistical analysis, which 
could explain the causal relations better. 
 
On deep frozen samples, further examinations are possible, for example, age 
determination, parasitology, DNA analysis etc.  From the frozen stored samples (liver, 
kidney, muscle, etc.) further contaminants can be measured. 
 
Occasionally, differences were found between habitats inside the country, in the 
accumulation of metals and PCBs.  However, the mean values of pollutants measured, were 
generally low or medium compared with other European studies.  Further examination would 
be important not only from liver, but also from kidney and muscle or spraint, in the 
biomonitoring of wetlands.
