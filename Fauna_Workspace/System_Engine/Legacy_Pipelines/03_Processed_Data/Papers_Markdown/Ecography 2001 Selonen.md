We studied the space use of the Siberian flying squirrel in fragmented forest
landscapes in southern Finland in 1996- 1999. Twenty-four adult tnales and 23 adult
females were radio trucked in three different study areas. The use of different
landscape elemetits by flying squirrels was compared to their availability using
eompositional analysis. Effects of fragmentation on flying squirrels were studied by
cotnparing ihe use of different habitat types, number of used patches, and size of
home range to the principal components formed IVom the landscape variables patch
size, patch isolation, area of spruee-dominated forests, and area of other habitats
with trees.
Flying squirrels preferred spruce- and deciduous-dominated forests, but other habi-
tats with trees were also used for moving and foraging. These less preferred habitats
connected spruce and deciduous forest patches in a matrix of open areas. Males used
several separate patches of preferred habitat, but females usually stayed in one patch.
The spacing behavionr of males was affected by the structure of the landscape in a
way that home ranges were larger and males used more spruce patches in the n:iore
highly fragmented landscape. Home-range size of females was not alTected by the
landscape structure. Basically, the space use of males seemed lo be reflected by the
location and lutmber of fetnale home ranges. In the study area where female density
was high, the home ranges of males were small. Our results give empirical evidence
of the effects of habitat loss and landscape connectivity for the Siberian liying
squirrel and the implications of these for the conservation of the species.

In addition to differences between species, the degree
of fragmentation and the level of connectivity may be
the reasons for different patterns in space use among
populations of the same species. When an animal is not
capable of compensating for habitat loss by using sev-
eral patehes (expansion response), for example due to a
lack of connectivity, they arc forced to live in areas
smaller than originally used (fusion or fission response).
It is often assumed that patches of preferred habitat arc
isolated from one another by a hostile matrix (Addicott
et al. 1987), but in fact the matrix surrounding patches
of preferred habitat is an important factor affecting the
movements of animals (Verboom and Apcldoorn 1990,
Celada et al. 1994, Wauters et al. 1994a, b, Aberg et al.
1995, Delin and Andren 1999, Bowne et al, 1999).
Therefore, movement patterns are dependent on land-
scape structure and the level of connectivity between
patches (Merriam 1995),
At the landscape scale, connectivity affects the recol-
onization of local patches after extinction (Henderson
et al, 1985, Hanski and Simberloff 1997). At the indi-
vidual scale, connectivity affects the ability of individu-
als to move between patches, especially if they require
resources from several patches (Taylor et al. 1993).
Our current understanding of mammalian responses
to different landscape patterns is affected by the fact
that empirical work concerning matnmalian landscape
ecology is highly imperfect and mainly restricted to
small rodents, such as voles (Bowers 1997, Krohne
1997). In particular, data are needed on those speeies
that are possibly suffering from fragmentation.
The Siberian flying squirrel Pteroniys volans is a
nocturnal, arboreal rodent, which inhabits spruce-dom-
inated boreal forests from Finland to eastern Siberia
and Japan (Ognev 1966). It requires deciduous trees as
a food resource and tree cavities for roosting and
nesting. In Finland it has declined probably due to
modern forestry (Hokkanen et al. 1982), In the Finnish
red data book, it has been elassified as a declining
species with a need for monitoring its population abun-
dance (Rassi et al. 1992), In the habitat direetive of the
European Union, the Siberian Hying squirrel has been
classified as a priority speeies, whose conservation re-
quires special areas for conservation (Anon. 1992).
In this paper, we report results on the space use of
the Siberian flying squirrel in southern Finland. 1) We
investigate the use of different landseape elements by
the flying squirrel in relation to their availability at the
scale of study area, and at the scale of home range. We
define the preferred and less preferred elements of the
landscape for the flying squirrel. 2) We analyse the
effect of forest fragmentation on the landscape use and
home-range size of flying squirrels.

Comparing used habitats with those available is
problematic (Johnson 1980, Aebischer et al. 1993) and
different methods (e.g. j ^ goodness-of-fit method (Neu
et al. 1974) and habitat preference method (Johnson
1980)) can give different results (McClean et al, 1998),
We used compositional analysis to avoid problems with
correlation between individuals, and to reduce the effect
of an arbitrary definition of study areas (Aebischer et
al. 1993). En the compositional analysis, habitat used by
an individual is compared with that available using
log-ratio transformation of habitat proportions (Aebis-
cher et al. 1993). The log-ratio is formed by comparing
the use of two landscape elements and their availability
as follows: In(x^,i/Xu,) — In(x.,i/X3j), where x is the propor-
tion of element (i or j) that is utilized (u) and available
(a). Log-ratios are calculated in a matrix for all habitats
and for each individual. Mean and standard errors are
calculated by pooling all studied aniinals and are com-
pared for significance with two-tailed one-sample t-
tests. Differences in means of log-ratios are used to
rank habitals according to their relative use.
Utilized landscape elements were compared with
available ones at two scales. First, we compared utilized
landscape elements within the 100% MCP home range
to that available in the study area. Second, we com-
pared the proportion of radio locations within each
landscape element used to the proportion of each land-
scape element available in the 1OO'X< MCP home range.

Effect of fragmentation
The intensity of use of different landscape elements (the
proportion of locations in each landscape eletnent).
home-range size, and number of used patches were
compared to landscape structure. The following mea-
sures of landscape structure were used to describe
fragmentation:
Patch size: the size of the occupied patch (spruce
forest and deciduous forests, see results). For males
that used several patches, we calculated the mean size
of frequently used patches (patches with more than live
locations from one individual).
Inter-patch distance: the distance belween the occu-
pied patch and nearest-neighbour patch. For males that
used several patches, we calculated the mean of nearest-
neighbour distances.
Area of spruce and deciduous forest: the area of
spruce and deciduous forests were calculated (preferred
habitat, see results) within a buffer zone of 200 m
radius for females and 500 m radius for tnales from the
centre of the home range. The centre of the hotne range
was calculated from the arithmetic mean of locations.
These buffer areas represented a similar-sized area to
the average home-range size of male and female fiying
squirrels in this study (see also Hanski et al. 2000),
Area of connecting landscape elements: this was mea-
sured as the area of habitats with trees other than
spruce and deciduous forest IVotn the same buffer areas
around home ranges as above. Connecting habitats
were young forest, sapling, clear cut with trees, clear
cut. pine forest, and pine bog. These habitats connected
spruce patches in the matrix of fields and other open
areas.
The variables that were not normally distributed
were log-transformed, and proportions of locations
were arcsine transformed for statistical analyses. The
landscape variables were analysed by principal compo-
nent analysis to form new variables. These were used in
linear multiple regression models. Statistical tests were
performed with Statistix for Windows 2.0.

Discussion
Based on lhe compositional analysis, spruce- and decid-
uous-dominated forests were the preferred landscape
elements for male and female Siberian flying squirrels.
These are the elements where reproduction of female
flying squirrels occurs. In Finland the fragmentation of
forest landscape caused by humans is due to cutting of
mature sprtice forests, a preferred landscape type also for
flying squirrels. Because the flying squirrel numbers have
declined in Finland apparently due to forest management
(Hokkanen et al. 1982), we expect that the seale of forest
fragmentation influenees the spacing behaviour of the
flying squirrel. However, flying squirrels also used other
forest types, like young forests and sapling stands, for
moving and foraging. These mostly younger forest stands
act as connecting habitat between preferred spruce- and
deciduous-dominated forest patches.
Use of less preferred habitats was concentrated in both
males and females to the elements with larger or more
trees (young forest > sapling and clear cut with trees >
clear cut). Flying squirrels were seldom found in clear
cuts (only when some trees present), pinebogs and pine
forest, but some males and juveniles are known to use
them for patch change.
Space use and fragmentation
Male flying squirrels inhabited a greater number of
preferred habitat patches in more fragmented than in
more continuous landscapes. After excluding An-
jalankoski, the home-range size was larger for individuals
that lived in several small patehes than for individuals
that lived in a few large patehes. For males, this indicates
the expansion response to fragmentation (Ims et al.
1993), In the expansion response an animal expands its
home range to include several habitat patches, when the
patch size becomes smaller than the minimum spaee
requirements of an animal. From our data, it was not
possible to calculate home-range overlaps, but based on
the home-range size, our results do not support the fusion
response (Ims et al. 1993). Both the expansion response
(Wegge and Rolstad 1986, Carey et al, 1992, Geffen et
al. 1992. Tjernbcrg et al, 1993. Wauters et al. 1994a,
Redpalh 1995. Andreassen et al, 1998) and the fusion
response (Andreassen etal, 1996, 1998, Wolff et al. 1997)
have been found among vertebrates.
Female flying squirrels had smaller home ranges than
males and usually lived within one patch and in non-
overlapping home ranges with other females (see also
Hanski et al. 2000), Therefore, for females the hypotheses
by Ims et al, (1993) were not relevant. Home-range size
of females was not influenced by any of the measured
landscape variables. The result indicates that females had
selected patches large enough for their home ranges and
were not sensitive to size variation of those patches. That
females were living in suitable sized patches is also
supported by the result that their use of preferred
habitats was not correlated with the size of the occupied
patch.
When females live within one pateh, they cannot
compensate for the loss of that patch without changing
the location of their home range (Morris 1987b). In a
landscape

patches are few and scattered, females may face
diffieulties in finding a new suitable, unoccupied patch
where to settle. In contrast, males are able to compensate
the loss of one patch by using other patches within his
home range.
However, also females used less preferred habitats
when they were available, A probable explanation for the
use of less preferred habitats is that when lhe area or
quality of preferred habitat deelines, individuals may not
find all neeessary resources from ihat habitat and they
have to move to other habitats (Dunning et al. 1992), or
they select food patches outside the spruce patch if they
are easily available. In the case of the female flying
squirrels, the limiting resource within a habitat patch is
probably the amount of deciduous trees, which are used
for foraging, not the patch area per se. Nevertheless,
there may be a minimum size of the suitable breeding
patch. The flying squirrel is sedentary, and especially in
winter females may face problems in finding food, if the
food resources are scarce in the occupied pateh. The
result that deciduous forest was preferred over spruce in
females but not in males may indicate the importance of
deciduous trees for females. Females probably spend
more time foraging than males especially while raising
young. In this study, in most cases when females were
found in less preferred habitats, they were foraging in
deciduous trees.
For males, the more frequent use of connecting habitat
patches in fragmented areas was probably because males
were moving between separate patehes more often when
the area of spruce forest and patch size decreased, but
also they were often found foraging in deciduous trees
while located in less preferred habitat.

At high female density males had smaller home ranges

Connectivity of the landscape
For male flying squirrels the lirsl principal component
explained the number of patches used and the home-
range size. This means that males used less spruce patches
and had smaller home ranges when the occupied spruce
patch was large and area of less preferred habitat around
Ihe center of home range was small. Less preferred
habitats were connecting spruce patches, and thus our
result could indicate that some male home ranges were
small, because individuals were not able to move between
patches. However, we think this seems unlikely, and the
correlation with patches used and home-range size with
PCI was mainly due to patch size and not due to less
preferred habitat enhancing movement between patches.
The effect of connectivity in the PCI was mainly due to
four males living in one fairly isolated and large (62 ha)
patch. Home ranges of those four males were only half
the size of that patch and individuals were allowed to
move in a larger area within the patch than they actually
did. Also two narrow sapling corridors left from ihat
patch, allowing individuals to leave the patch (one male
that is not included in this data regularly moved between
that patch and a neighbouring patch). All other patches
used by flying squirrels in our study were connected by
varying amount of connecting habitat (Table 2). If the
spruce patches are not totally isolated by open areas, the
male flying squirrels are very elTicient at discovering
movement routes to separate patches (Selonen et al.
unpubi,).
Thus the landscape was probably functionally contin-
uous for flying squirrels in our study areas. Males were
capable in moving between spruce patches through less
preferred habitats, and the distance between patches did
not affect space use. However, a lack of connectivity
certainly has some importance for the spacing behaviour
of flying squirrels. For example the Anjaiankoski study
area was divided into two parts by a 100-200 m wide and
> 1-km long fleld. Males were never observed to cross
the fleld. although a few juveniles have crossed that field
during dispersal (unpubl.).
Delin and Andren (1999) concluded that landscape for
the red squirrels in south-central Sweden was ftinction-
ally continuous, when they found that red squirrels were
able to reach all forest stands in their study area. In many
other studies, in more fragmented areas, isolation and
patch size have affected the occurrence of red squirrels
(e.g. Verboom and Apeldoorn 1990, Celada et al, 1994,
Wauters et al. 1994a, b), Delin and Andren (1999)
concluded that the difference between these studies was
due to different inter-patch distances and the hostility of
the matrix. For example, in the study of Verboom and
Apeldoorn (1990) maximum inter-patch distance was
3500 m and the matrix was mainly fields, whereas in
Delin and Andren (1999) the ma,\itnum inter-patch
distance was 600 m and the matrix consisted of managed
forest. In our study the greatest inter-patch distance was
400 m, and the landscape between patches consisted of
a mixture of fields and young forest.
Andren (1994). in his review on the effects of fragmen-
tation on mammals and birds, found that the negative
effects of decreased patch size and increased patch
isolation occurred when the landscape consisted of
10 30'%! of the original habitat. In our study areas, the
landscape consisted of 21-23% of preferred habitat in
Anjalankoski and litti (Table I). In practice, the propor-
tion of preferred habitat was < 20%, because many
spruce forest patches were either too small or lacked
deciduous trees and nest cavities (Selonen unpubl.) that
are essential for ihe flying squirrel {Hanski 1998. Hanski
ct al, 2000). Therefore, all patches are not suitable for
reproduction or permanent occupancy. The proportion
of totally unsuitable open habitats for the flying squirrel
ranged from 12-36%. in our study areas. Consequently,
in mosl study sites flying squirrels were able to move
between patches, but in extreme cases, when connecting
landscape elements are missing, movements may be
restricted. Monkkonen et al, (1997) found that forest
areas unoccupied by flying squirrels in northern Finland
had more open areas than in forest areas occupied by the
flying squirrel.

cantly smaller patches than in "natural"' conditions in
managed forests. The smallest occupied patch of flying

Our results indicate that the loss of area of preferred
forests is a more important reason for the decline of the
flying squirrel population m southern Finland, than the
landscape connectivity. Flying squirrels are able to
move in other habitats with trees than the preferred one
(also during dispersal, Selonen et al. unpubl). Thus.
movement between patches is usually possible, because
most of the forests in southern Finland are not isolated
from each other by totally open areas. The female
flying squirrel requires a suitable large spruce patch,
deciduous trees tor food to survive throughout the year.
and cavities for nesting and roosting. Therefore, it is
plausible, that loss of area of suitable habitat, rather
than the detrimental effects of patch isolation, lowers
the density of females and reduces their reproductive
output. The flying squirrel seems to correspond the
species, presented by Andren et al. (1997), which is not
too sensitive to fragmentation (at least not at the scale
of fragmentation in our study areas), but suffers from
area loss and structure of the forest within the patch
(e.g. tree-species composition, cavities; see, Hanski
1998). However, one should be cautious when drawing
conclusions. At present, the effect of fragmentation on
the survival, for example due to predation. of both
adults and juveniles is unknown. If the fragmentation
continues, we do not know the threshold where isola-
tion together with area loss, begins to influence the
dynamics of flying squirrel populations.
