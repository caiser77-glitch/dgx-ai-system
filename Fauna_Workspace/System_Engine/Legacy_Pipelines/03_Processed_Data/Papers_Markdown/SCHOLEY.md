NATIONAL AND INTERNATIONAL LEGISLATION AND  
OTTER CONSERVATION IN THE UK 
 
Graham SCHOLEY 
 
Environment Agency, Isis House, Wallingford, Oxfordshire, OX10 8BD, England 
email: scholgd@environment-agency.gov.uk  
 
Abstract: The Eurasian otter (Lutra lutra) is an emblem for nature conservation in the UK.  It has been the focus 
for nature conservation activity for over 25 years.  The legislative framework conferring protection for the otter 
includes European Directives and Conventions and domestic UK legislation which together provide a high level 
of safeguard.  There have been a significant number of active players in otter conservation in the UK including 
statutory authorities, voluntary bodies, academic institutions and committed individuals.  The UK Biodiversity 
Action Plan (BAP) and associated Species Action Plan for the Otter provide the contemporary framework for 
otter conservation activities in the UK, overseen by a national BAP otter steering group.  Current priorities 
include the continuation of effective population monitoring, post-mortem studies and tissue analyses, addressing 
road deaths, continuing to improve water quality and habitat, and resolving conflicts with fishery interests. 
 
Keywords:  Eurasian otter, Lutra lutra, legislation, conservation activity, species action plan, conservation 
priorities 
 
 
1   INTRODUCTION 
The Eurasian otter (Lutra lutra) is an emblem for nature conservation in the UK.  As a top 
predator in our river and wetland habitats and along our coastlines, its status is considered a 
barometer of the health of the water environment.  The species has been the focus of 
conservation activity in the UK for over 25 years, following concerns about its decline from 
the late 1950s through to the late 1970s, which were mirrored by declines throughout much of 
Europe.  Current conservation activity in the UK is underpinned by international and national 
legislation which gives the otter one of the strictest levels of protection of any European 
mammal.  This paper reviews current European and UK legislation as it affects the otter in the 
UK, and assesses the priorities for conservation action in the UK for the recovering otter 
population. 
 
 
2   EUROPEAN LEGISLATION AND INTERNATIONAL CONVENTIONS 
 
2.1 Habitats Directive 
The primary piece of European conservation legislation relevant to the otter is Council 
Directive EC/92/43, the ‘Habitats Directive’.  The Directive requires EU member states to 
adopt necessary measures to conserve the habitats of wild fauna and flora deemed to be of 
particular ‘community interest’, and to adapt specific conservation measures for a range of 
named species. 
The otter appears on both Annex II and Annex IV of the Habitats Directive. Annex II 
species require the designation of protected areas by range states (Special Areas of 
Conservation – SACs) as set out in articles 3, 4 and 6 of the Directive.  Annex IV species 
require strict protection measures by Member States in accordance with Article 12 of the 
Directive.  These measures not only include the prohibition of capture, killing, deliberate 
disturbance at e.g. breeding sites and destruction of breeding and resting sites, but also 
include a requirement to monitor the incidental capture and killing of Annex IV listed species. 
The otter’s inclusion in Annexes II and IV of the Habitats Directive clearly provides a 
very strong basis for protection throughout EU member states.  However, the designation of 


 
2
protected areas and strict protection from intentional harm for a species does not guarantee of 
course either the integrity of habitat throughout the species range or the effective recovery of 
significantly reduced populations.  In this respect two articles of the Directive are of note. 
Article 10 requires member states to encourage the management of landscape features of 
major importance, and gives as an example linear features essential for migration and 
dispersal such as rivers and their banks.  This provides legislative support at EU level for a 
positive approach for the protection of river corridors, and although the wording of Article 10 
could be seen as ‘weaker’ than those articles supporting Annexes II and IV, the spirit of 
Article 10 is clear and the author has quoted this article many times when influencing the 
development planning process in the UK. 
Article 11 of the Directive requires member states to undertake surveillance of the 
conservation status of habitats and species of community interest – in essence, all member 
states should be ensuring that they have comprehensive monitoring tools in place for the otter. 
 
2.2 International conventions 
Two conventions are of particular note with reference to otter conservation in a UK (and 
European) context.  The UK is a signatory to the Convention on the Conservation of 
European Wildlife and Natural Habitats (the Bern Convention) (1979).  The Bern Convention 
made provisions for the protection of species, and the otter is listed on Appendix II, species 
for which Article 6 requires special protection.  In a European context the Habitats Directive 
effectively enshrines the provisions of the Bern Convention into European law, although for 
instance the UK had substantially implemented the convention in domestic legislation as early 
as 1981 (see below).  At the time of writing, 45 European and African states as well as the 
European Community are party to the Convention. 
The Convention on International Trade in Endangered Species and of wild flora and 
fauna (CITES) (1973) has been implemented throughout EC member states through 
Regulations 3626/82 and 338/97.  The otter is listed on Appendix I of CITES, for which trade 
is only allowed in ‘exceptional’ circumstances.  No EC member states have entered a 
reservation with respect to the otter, and therefore all should be taking strict measures to 
ensure adequate policing of compliance with CITES and the detection of any illegal wildlife 
trade in Lutra lutra. 
 
 
3   UK LEGISLATION 
 
3.1 Wildlife and Countryside Act 
The Wildlife and Countryside (W&C) Act 1981 is the primary wildlife conservation 
legislation in the UK (although Northern Ireland has its own legislation, see below).  The Act 
superseded and considerably improved upon the provisions of the Conservation of Wild 
Creatures and Wild Plants Act 1975, an amendment of which first gave the otter statutory 
protection in England and Wales in 1978.  The W&C Act not only extended this protection to 
Scotland but also strengthened the protection for the species.  The otter is listed on Schedule 5 
of the W&C Act, which in accordance with the provisions of Part 1 Section 9 makes it an 
offence to kill, injure or attempt to kill or injure an otter, to damage or obstruct access to an 
otter’s place of shelter or intentionally disturb it while in occupation, or to be in possession of 
a live or dead otter or part thereof unless it can be demonstrated that it has been legally 
obtained.  Schedule 6 restricts methods by which otters can be killed or taken, and the 
licensing for the taking or removal of otters (and other protected species) is allowed for in 
Section 16 (3) where it can be shown that they are causing serious damage.  With respects to 
this last, it should be noted that licenses are very rarely issued, and the Habitats Directive 


 
3
effectively provides tighter provisions on the issuing of licenses with respect to the need to 
ensure that such licenses do not represent a threat to the integrity of the population. 
The Wildlife (Northern Ireland) Order 1985 implements the provisions of the W&C 
Act 1981 in Northern Ireland.  The otter is listed again on Schedule 5, and Part II Article 10 is 
very similar to Part I Section 9 of the W&C Act in the protection it offers to listed species. 
 
3.2 Conservation regulations 
The Conservation (Natural Habitats &c.) Regulations 1994 transpose the obligations of the 
EC Habitats Directive into domestic UK legislation.  The otter was already strictly protected 
in the UK by the Wildlife and Countryside Act, and Section 39 (1) of the Conservation 
Regulations serves to reinforce Schedule 5 of the W&C Act.  However, the Conservation 
Regulations also require the designation of Special Areas of Conservation for which the otter 
is either the primary or a significant feature.  The system of site protection set up by the W&C 
Act – Sites of Special Scientific Interest or SSSIs - rarely were of sufficient scale to be 
designated with otters as a prime species of interest, nor has the SSSI system been particularly 
suited or intended for wide ranging, low-density terrestrial species such as the otter, until 
perhaps the more recent designation of a significant number of river SSSIs from the mid-
1990s.  The system of candidate SACs now proposed for the UK include a number of river 
systems for which the otter is a primary reason for designation, as well as a number of other 
freshwater and coastal sites for which the otter is a secondary ‘qualifying feature’.  Currently 
(last updated October 2003) the otter is listed as a feature for a total of 72 candidate SACs in 
the UK (out of a total of 601).  All SACs must first be designated as SSSIs under the W&C 
Act before they can be formally proposed as SACs (with the exception of marine SACs for 
which there is no facility for SSSI designation). 
 
3.3 Countryside and Rights of Way Act 
The Countryside and Rights of Way (CROW) Act 2000 covers a number of activities 
affecting the environment and countryside in England and Wales.  With relevance to the otter, 
it strengthens the powers of statutory nature conservation agencies in relation to SSSIs, 
overcoming some of the weaknesses in the W&C Act 1981, parts of which are replaced by 
updated provisions in the CROW Act. 
Another significant aspect of the CROW Act was the quasi-statutory status it 
conferred on priority habitats and species listed in the UK Biodiversity Action Plan, of which 
the otter is one.  The Act requires the Secretary of State for the Environment and the National 
Assembly of Wales, and therefore all relevant statutory bodies coming under their 
jurisdiction, to promote action to further the conservation of these habitats and species.  This 
requires that positive action is undertaken to conserve the otter, a priority BAP species, by all 
those statutory bodies with a remit for nature conservation. 
 
 
4   OTTER CONSERVATION IN THE UK – THE  BACKGROUND 
 
4.1 A brief history of status 
The timing of the rise in otter conservation activity in the UK reflects not only the generally 
increased awareness in environmental and conservation issues from the 1970s onwards, but 
also the period of significant decline of the species in the UK (and indeed Europe).  The 
baseline for our understanding of the status of the otter prior to the widespread declines from 
the late 1950s is the report produced by Marie Stephens on behalf of the Universities 
Federation for Animal Welfare (STEPHENS, 1957).  This report concluded that the otter was 
still generally common throughout the UK in the early 1950s. 


 
4
There was a dramatic decline in the distribution and population of the otter from the 
late 1950s which probably reached its nadir in the late 1970s.  The decline was first detected 
by the otter hunts which were still legally active in the UK at the time.  It was CHANIN and 
JEFFERIES (1978) who first correlated the decline in the success rates of the otter hunts with 
the introduction of cyclodiene pesticides in the mid-1950s, which had had a concomitant 
impact on other predatory species such as peregrine falcons.  Much has been written since 
then on other possible causes or contributory factors to the otter’s decline, but the link with 
cyclodienes, particularly dieldrin, remains compelling (e.g. JEFFERIES AND HANSON, 2002).  
The role of other toxic pollutants in this decline, such as PCBs, appears less clear and there is 
no consensus on this for the UK. 
The otter was lost from most of England, substantial parts of Wales and much of 
central and southern Scotland by the late 1970s, with the strongest populations remaining in 
northern and north western Scotland and the Scottish islands, as well as much of Ireland.  
There has been a gradual recovery which is strongly linked to the phasing out of dieldrin and 
related pesticides, a recovery tracked by a series of national surveys as well as much local 
conservation effort inspiring more localised survey and monitoring.  The most recently 
published results for the fourth national survey of England (CRAWFORD, 2003) confirm this 
continuing recovery. 
 
4.2 A review of the main organisations contributing to otter conservation in the UK 
There have been, and still are, many players in otter conservation in the UK, which reflects 
the universal appeal of the animal as well as its very real status as a pivotal indicator of the 
health of the water environment.  As with all species which are subject to a sustained 
conservation effort, it is not possible to identify all the committed individuals, wildlife 
journalists and broadcasters, hardworking campaigners and fieldworkers who have 
contributed to the whole, but what follows is a brief summary of the main institutions which 
have played a significant role.  There will be many other organisations and voluntary groups 
not mentioned below which have played their part, locally at least, and omitting them by no 
means diminishes their contribution. 
The growing concern over the decline of the otter in the 1970s led the UK 
Government’s Nature Conservancy Council to set up the Joint Otter Group (JOG) in 1976, 
which included members from the Mammal Society and the Royal Society for Nature 
Conservation.  The JOG was concerned with establishing the true status of the otter and 
identifying the measures needed to conserve it.  At the same time the Friends of the Earth 
otter campaign was calling for statutory protection for the otter; the JOG concurred that some 
legal protection was required and after parliamentary debate the otter was given protection in 
England and Wales in January 1978 (later extended to Scotland, see above).  The JOG was 
also instrumental in initiating the national survey programme, which has since been 
undertaken roughly every seven years in England, Wales and Scotland (in whole or part), less 
frequently in Ireland, with the first surveys undertaken in 1977-79. 
The Vincent Wildlife Trust, founded by Vincent Weir in 1975, is an independent 
charity which has focused much of its effort on the conservation of British mammals.  From 
1977, with the establishment of its Otter Haven Project, it was closely involved in otter 
conservation for about 20 years.  The Otter Haven Project saw the establishment of a small 
number of full time advisors who undertook local population monitoring and gave advice on 
management and conservation activities.  The VWT funded a series of national otter surveys 
up to the mid-1990s.  They also funded research into the design and effectiveness of otter 
guards on eel fyke nets, undertook radio tracking of otters as part of restocking programmes 
(notably in Yorkshire) with rehabilitated orphaned and injured otters, and produced numerous 
reports in support of the above (BIRKS AND MACMILLAN, 2001).  Although the VWT has now 


 
5
directed its energies to other species its significant role in otter conservation to date is to be 
acknowledged. 
The Otter Trust, founded by Philip Wayre in 1971 to promote the conservation of 
otters throughout the world and to educate the public and research the captive breeding of 
otters, has also been a significant player on the otter conservation scene.  The Otter Trust is 
best known for its three centres open to the public and for its captive breeding and release 
programme, which resulted in 117 otters being released during the period 1983-1999.  
Initially undertaken in close co-operation with the NCC, in its later years the release 
programme was undoubtedly controversial and did not receive support from many operating 
in the otter conservation field.  However, the release programme is almost certainly 
substantially responsible for the current otter populations in the Anglian and Thames regions 
of England.  The release programme was drawn to a close in 1999 following releases of 17 
otters in the upper Thames catchment at three locations (JEFFERIES, WAYRE and  SHUTER, 
2000). 
The International Otter Survival Fund (IOSF), based on the Isle of Skye, Scotland, 
was founded in 1993 by Grace and Paul Yoxon.  The purpose of the IOSF is to help conserve 
all 13 species of otter through the support of research and practical measures.  This includes 
much work for Lutra lutra particularly in Scotland including otter surveys, rehabilitation, 
post-mortem and toxicology, and road kill mitigation. 
The Wildlife Trusts partnership (a network of 47 local Wildlife Trusts managed at a 
UK level by the Royal Society for Nature Conservation) considerably stepped up their role in 
otter conservation through the initiation of Otter Projects in several areas in 1989, leading to 
the more formal establishment of their Otter and Rivers Projects (OARPs) in 1996.  
Throughout the 1990s a growing number of full-time project officers located in the County 
Wildlife Trusts have promoted otter conservation through a wide range of practical and 
advisory measures and through monitoring supported by trained volunteers (RSNC, 2001).  
The OARP has now been replaced by the ‘Water for Wildlife Project’ (from 2002), reflecting 
the broader emphasis on a more holistic approach to wetland wildlife conservation, but with 
the otter still very much a focus of concern and effort for a number of the local projects. 
Before privatisation, many of the Water Authorities in England and Wales were 
adopting more sensitive practices with respect to river management, with some directly 
supporting otter conservation work.  This greatly increased post-privatisation in 1989, with 
the water companies often supporting local otter conservation projects and through their 
umbrella body, Water UK, providing substantial three-year support for the Otters and Rivers 
Projects from 1998-2001. 
From 1989, regulation of the water environment and flood defence responsibilities fell 
to the National Rivers Authority and since 1996 to their successor body with wider powers, 
the Environment Agency.  [In Scotland these responsibilities rest with the Scottish 
Environmental Protection Agency (SEPA) - editor].  Like their predecessor the NRA, the EA 
has over-arching conservation duties and has been very active in the field of otter 
conservation, supporting and often initiating local otter projects, funding specific conservation 
activities, and supporting R&D such as otter post-mortem work.  In addition, the NRA and 
EA have overseen and enforced the adoption of much stronger legislative protection for the 
water environment, as well as implemented conservation policy and practice to inform its 
regulatory, operational and advisory activities.  All of this serves to contribute towards a more 
holistic approach to catchment management.  The EA will also be the principal competent 
authority responsible for the implementation of the Water Framework Directive.  It is worth 
noting, as an example of the collaboration between some of the organisations discussed 
above, that between 1998 and 2001 the Water UK and the Wildlife Trusts Otters and Rivers 
Project, supported by the Environment Agency and ‘Biffaward’ (an environmental trust set up 


 
6
by a waste company to award landfill tax credits), as well as other local partners, together 
invested in excess of £2.3 million in support of 22 projects based with the Wildlife Trusts 
across England and Wales. 
A brief summary of main players would not be complete without mention of the 
academic and research institutions which have played an important role in underpinning our 
knowledge of the ecology of the otter in the UK.  A particularly notable example is the wealth 
of government-funded research undertaken by the Institute of Terrestrial Ecology - ITE (now 
the Centre for Ecology and Hydrology - CEH) at Banchory, Scotland under the aegis of Dr. 
Hans Kruuk and colleagues.  A number of researchers at key universities have also made 
substantial contributions to our understanding of aspects of otter biology/ecology. 
 
 
5   THE UK BIODIVERSITY ACTION PLAN AND THE OTTER 
 
5.1 Background 
The UK produced its Biodiversity Action Plan (BAP) in 1994 in response to its commitments 
as a signatory to the 1992 Rio Declaration on Biological Diversity.  The otter is a priority 
species within the UK BAP, and is one of ca.400 such species for which there is an individual 
Species Action Plan (SAP) produced in the years following the production of the BAP.  
Along with a number of priority habitats, these priority species comprise the focus for much 
of the current UK conservation effort, representing as they do those habitats and species 
which are deemed to be most deserving of conservation action due to their declining status, 
vulnerability and/or importance in the UK at an international level.  The UK BAP may not 
have captured all the ‘deserving cases’, and could be seen as monopolising much (but not all) 
the conservation effort, but it has succeeded in bringing together the numerous government 
and non-government organisations involved in nature conservation to work towards a 
common programme. 
The otter Species Action Plan, produced in 1995, is overseen by a national steering 
group, which is currently chaired by the author, representing the Environment Agency as the 
‘contact point’ for the otter under the UK BAP.  The Agency’s lead partner in delivering the 
SAP for the otter is ‘The Wildlife Trusts’, also members of the steering group.  Also 
represented on the steering group is the Joint Nature Conservation Committee (JNCC), which 
has an umbrella role in government for nature conservation in the UK and produced A 
framework for otter conservation in the UK: 1995-2000 (JNCC, 1996), which complements 
the SAP.  Other members of the steering group are English Nature (EN), Countryside Council 
for Wales (CCW), Scottish Natural Heritage (SNH), Scottish Environment Protection Agency 
(SEPA), The Environment and Heritage Service (Northern Ireland), and the corporate 
‘champions’ of the otter SAP, Water UK. 
 
5.2 UK Species Action Plan targets 
There are three main targets outlined in the Species Action Plan for the otter (revised in 
2002): 
i) 
To maintain existing otter populations 
ii) 
To expand existing otter populations 
iii) 
By 2010, restore breeding otters to all catchments and coastal areas where they 
have been recorded since 1960 (which effectively is the whole country) 
 
The targets in the Species Action Plan are supported by 18 actions which, at the time of 
production (1995), were deemed to be priority actions required to facilitate the achievement 
of the targets.  Although some of these actions have now been completed and others deemed 


 
7
now a lower priority, the SAP actions still provide the foundations for the current focus of 
otter conservation work. 
The 18 actions in the SAP for the otter are further broken down and underpinned by 
55 actions in the ‘otter workplan’, produced by the UK BAP otter steering group and last 
updated in February 2003.  The purpose of the work plan is to better identify those specific 
actions to achieve the SAP objectives.  Although the SAP actions and those of the work plan 
identify particular organisations which are best placed to take these actions forward, needless 
to say there are a great many organisations and individuals involved and much of what is 
achieved depends on the combined dedication and hard work of individuals in public, private 
and voluntary sectors. 
The primary role of the Steering Group is to endeavour to ensure that the BAP targets 
are met and the necessary actions undertaken, through the influence and support they can 
bring to bear and by tackling difficult issues and barriers to progress.  It is ultimately the 
responsibility of the Environment Agency, as contact point, to report to Government on 
progress, and jointly with the Wildlife Trusts to take the lead in striving to ensure delivery. 
Although the results of our most recent national surveys for Northern Ireland and 
Wales are yet to be published and that for Scotland is currently underway, the recent results of 
the fourth otter survey of England 2000-2002 (CRAWFORD, 2003) give strong support to the 
belief that the first two targets in the UK BAP are reasonably secure.  However, the third SAP 
target of restoring breeding otters to all catchments by 2010 (notwithstanding the problems of 
proving ‘breeding’) is by no means assured.  It is important therefore to identify the 
immediate future priorities for ensuring that the current recovery trend for the otter is 
maintained and that possible barriers to this are removed. 
 
 
6   CURRENT PRIORITIES FOR OTTER CONSERVATION IN THE UK 
 
6.1 Monitoring 
It is of course essential that the monitoring of otter populations continues.  The current round 
of recently completed and ongoing national surveys in the four countries of the UK, funded 
primarily but not wholly by statutory agencies, are providing an invaluable snapshot of 
current status which can be tested against the SAP targets and the recovery ‘milestones’ 
established by the Steering Group.  Repeat national surveys in another seven years will 
hopefully be timed to conveniently inform the third BAP target of restoring breeding otters to 
all catchments by 2010, but the resourcing of these national surveys is by no means yet 
assured.  However, significant local survey effort by a large number of otter project officers 
and other individuals around the country has made an invaluable contribution to our 
understanding of trends between these national survey periods, and also helps to fill in the 
blanks where national surveys do not comprehensively cover the whole country (e.g. 
England).  Local survey effort will be particularly important for closely monitoring the 
situation where recovery of populations seems to be poorer than expected, and is the only 
means by which progress on expansion and consolidation can be assessed between national 
surveys. 
Local monitoring, however, requires resources and adequately experienced field 
surveyors.  The current framework of Wildlife Trust (Water for Wildlife/Otters and Rivers) 
project staff provides such a solid base of locally-run conservation effort supporting survey 
and monitoring but is by no means safeguarded for the future.  Other organisations, 
particularly the Environment Agency but including many others, have keen local staff who 
undertake ad-hoc surveys, support local projects etc, but are restricted in the amount of time 
they can give to monitoring given a plethora of other commitments.  The recently launched 


 
8
Tracking Mammals Partnership, a collaborative initiative involving ca.21 organisations 
working to improve the quality and dissemination of information on the status of mammals in 
the UK, is co-ordinated by the JNCC.  It may help by providing an additional focus for the 
need to maintain surveillance of otter populations (which is in fact a statutory requirement of 
EU member states).  However, the otter is deemed as a species for which there is good 
information compared to other species.  This has been achieved both through the national 
surveys and through local project and volunteer effort, and to maintain this ‘good 
information’ into the future the security of both these approaches needs to be assured. 
 
6.2 Post-mortems and tissue analyses 
For over ten years now the Environment Agency has been funding the post-mortem of dead 
otters in England and Wales and subsequent tissue analysis for toxic pollutants.  By far the 
majority of these are found on roads as a result of collisions with traffic.  Similar analyses 
have been undertaken in the past by ITE (now CEH) in Scotland who reviewed the data for 
SNIFFER (Scotland and Northern Ireland Forum for Environmental Research) in order to 
inform future policy on this issue (CARSS and SHORE 2003).   
The current funding commitment for otter post-mortem and tissue analyses in England 
and Wales ceases at the end of 2003 with reporting in the following year.  Previous interim 
reports (e.g. BRADSHAW AND SLATER, 2003; SIMPSON, 1998) on this work has already 
demonstrated the invaluable nature of this research in monitoring toxic pollutant levels in 
otters and relating these findings to the condition and physiological status of the animals.  The 
UK Steering Group firmly believe that continued monitoring of our otter populations by 
comprehensive post-mortem and tissue analysis is imperative to ensure adequate surveillance 
of the health of these populations.  It is important not to forget that toxic pollutants were the 
cause of the major crash in otter populations in the first place.  Currently there are over 100 
chemicals that are being considered for closer environmental scrutiny in the water 
environment, in a review being undertaken by the Environment Agency (STEVE DUNGEY, 
personal communication).  This is due to their having potentially bio-accumulating toxic 
properties, some of which (such as brominated flame retardants) may be candidates for future 
analyses in otter tissues. 
The scale of current otter post-mortem and tissue analysis in England and Wales may 
not be possible to maintain, and we may need to be more tightly focused on priorities in the 
future.  This may influence targeting both spatially, in terms of the areas from which 
carcasses are analysed, and also specifically on those pollutants which represent the greatest 
risk in order to ensure that new potential ‘threats’ are addressed.  Priorities for Northern 
Ireland and Scotland require similar consideration.  However, as a tool for responsible 
environmental surveillance and as a barometer of the ‘condition’ of the otter population, a 
continued programme of targeted post-mortem and tissue analyses for this top predator is 
deemed a high priority.  
 
6.3 Road mortalities 
As otter numbers continue to increase and expand in the UK, more and more otters are being 
killed on our roads.  In fact, it is these otters which provide the core source material for the 
work discussed in section 6.2.  However, growing concern over the number of roadkills has 
led to greater co-ordination between statutory and non-statutory bodies and academic research 
institutions in addressing the problem, and has also led to the implementation of more and 
more ‘solutions’ on the ground. 
Statistics on otter road casualties have been collected for many years by the Vincent 
Wildlife Trust, who have amassed a considerable database of casualties from around the UK.  
More recently, the Countryside Council for Wales, Environment Agency, County Wildlife 


 
9
Trusts and others have come together to address priority black spots in Wales.  For England, 
the Highways Agency (HA), who are responsible for managing our major (‘trunk’) roads 
which constitute ca.5% of the road network, are also targeting resources to this problem.  
They have published a specific section on otters in their Design Manual for Roads and 
Bridges, and funded an advisory manual and report in collaboration with the Wildlife 
Conservation Research Unit at Oxford University (GROGAN, PHILCOX and MACDONALD, 
2001). 
The HA are currently co-ordinating a strategic survey to assess the level of risk at all 
trunk road watercourse crossings with a view to informing prioritised mitigation, advised by a 
steering group of experienced representatives from statutory and voluntary bodies and expert 
consultants. 
The question that should be asked is: ‘how much is road mortality a serious threat to 
otter recovery and therefore a conservation priority?’  The truth of the matter is that we do not 
really know, but what we do know is that otter road mortalities are on the rise and they are 
occurring in areas where other monitoring shows there to be very few otters.  It is entirely 
feasible to imagine that where otters are just recolonising river catchments and where traffic 
densities are high (and much higher than otters would have experienced prior to their decline), 
such as south east England, road kill could be a significant barrier to successful recovery. 
If we do nothing to address this problem, then time will tell whether road kill is a 
limiting factor for this low-density predator.  However, given the unacceptability of allowing 
one of our largest predators to be accidentally killed in significant numbers without taking at 
least some preventative action, and the precautionary approach of taking action now to deflect 
a possible constraint to further recovery, then a strong argument can be articulated for 
continuing with a programme of minimising the risk to otters being killed on our roads, 
however daunting the task may appear.  For new roads, this should be less difficult and less 
expensive than retro-fitting mitigation on old roads. 
 
6.4 Water quality and habitat quality 
Once the organochlorines largely responsible for the otter’s decline in the UK were gradually 
removed from use, the otter began its slow but continual recovery.  It has been tempting to 
assume therefore that habitat quality has not been a constraining factor in its recovery nor a 
significant factor in its decline.  However, this belies the fact that the otter’s decline coincided 
with a period of almost unprecedented drainage of wetlands and associated river engineering, 
the legacy of which we are still dealing with in ecological terms.  Nevertheless, the last 15 
years or so have seen a sea-change in how ecological principles and priorities have influenced 
river catchment management and regulation of the water environment, and although there is 
indeed always room for improvement we have seen significant improvements in water 
quality, improved fisheries and an enlightened policy approach to river maintenance. 
All these recent changes are important for the ability of our rivers and wetlands to 
support the variety of wildlife we aspire to and certainly their ability to support top predators, 
of which the otter is perhaps at the pinnacle of biological indicators.  It would seem self-
evident, therefore, that if we are to have viable, self sustaining otter populations on all our 
catchments, then we need to continue to restore the effective functioning of our river and 
wetland ecosystems in order to have the healthy food supplies (primarily fish populations) 
which can support them.  A healthy freshwater ecosystem will at the same time support the 
sustainable exploitation of these fish populations by fishermen. 
This requires continued and sustained effort to address both the various current 
pressures and the legacy of problems affecting our rivers and wetlands.  These measures 
include wholesale restoration of river channels and floodplains, addressing diffuse pollution 
and eutrophication, influencing and applying safeguards for river corridors as part of the land-


 
10
use planning process, and the tough application of statutory controls on activities that affect 
the water environment (including crucially the implementation of the Water Framework 
Directive). 
In order to ensure that all these activities take specific account, where needed, of the 
requirements of otters, experienced workers are essential both in the statutory and voluntary 
sectors to ensure that the momentum for restoring habitat quality is maintained. 
 
6.5 Conflict resolution 
The return of a large predator to parts of the country from where it has been lost, and to 
former numbers within areas where its population shrank, is inevitably going to arouse 
concern from those with vested interests in what it eats.  The otter is no different in this 
respect. Although there is perhaps a much greater general understanding of the ‘importance’ 
of wildlife conservation and perhaps even the role of predators in an ecosystem, this does not 
prevent real conflicts occurring and the need to address them sensitively. 
There has been growing concern in recent years in the UK about the localised impact 
of otters on fish farms and stillwater recreational fisheries, particularly specialist carp waters.  
The Environment Agency, Wildlife Trusts and a few independent consultants have for some 
years been providing advice on possible mitigation measures such as fencing and decoy 
ponds.  This work has also necessitated diplomacy in addressing the occasional demands for 
‘direct action’ from the angling press and fishery managers, and ‘defending’ the otter by 
explaining its behaviour and ecology and attempting to dispel some of the popular myths 
about predators (with variable success).  Discussions have also been held with specialist 
fishery interests regarding possible financial support for otter-proof fencing for e.g. specialist 
carp fisheries and, although to date the latter has been unsuccessful, dialogue continues on 
best practice mitigation and advice. 
Given the highly protected status of the otter and its general continuing recovery it 
would be easy to ignore the conflicts and voices of concern (and even poorly veiled threats 
from some quarters) as of little consequence.  However, the need both to educate broadly on 
the role of predators, and to address legitimate areas of concern over conflicts that have 
economic and/or recreational impacts on individuals and specific sectors, is as important now 
as it ever has been, given the impact and demands placed on the environment by an ever-
growing human population.  Sensitivity to these concerns and the continued provision of 
sound advice to reduce conflicts (whether real or perceived) will be essential tools in ensuring 
the otter is fully welcomed in its rightful place as our top aquatic predator. 
The five areas of otter conservation work discussed above are not of course the only 
areas of work identified in the UK SAP for the otter, and nor are they the only areas where 
conservation effort is currently directed.  However they do represent, in the author’s view, the 
main priorities for action if we are continue to see the recovery and consolidation of otter 
populations in the UK, and to continue to effectively monitor and safeguard these populations 
for the future.
