LIVERS OF THE OTTER (LUTRA LUTRA) FROM CONCENTRATIONS
IN SCATS AND FISH

A-1210 Vienna, Austria

Abstract. The decline of the European otter (Lutra lutra) seems to be related to environmental conta-
mination especially with polychlorinated biphenyls (PCBs). Only a limited number of tissue samples
from otters killed accidently as trafﬁc victims or drowned in ﬁshnets are available for residue analysis.
Calculating the levels of PCBs in otter tissues from concentrations in ﬁsh and scats would be a very
valuable tool to estimate potential risks for this endangered species. PCB levels measured in otter
livers, scats and ﬁsh from a Middle European otter population were used to examine the suitability of
mathematical models for calculating PCB concentrations in otter liver from ﬁsh concentrations and
from scat levels. Our results suggest that the models and input parameters currently used to calculate
PCB concentrations in otters to estimate risks do not predict mean concentrations in otter livers in
our Middle European study area, giving lower concentrations than actually analysed in the tissues.
Possible causes for the observed discrepancy between measured and calculated values are discussed.
Keywords: Lutra lutra, otter, PCB, polychlorinated biphenyls
1. Introduction
The European otter (Lutra lutra), once a widespread species on the Eurasian conti-
nent has declined in numbers in most countries of Western Europe and has become
extinct in some of them (Foster-Turley et al., 1990). Environmental contaminants,
especially mercury and polychlorinated biphenyls (PCBs) are the major factors
thought to be responsible for the steep decline, which occurred also in countries
where hunting was banned and in areas without obvious habitat destruction (Ma-
son, 1989).
Smit (1990) and Leonards et al. (1994) established a model to calculate PCB
levels in otters and minks (Mustela vison) from food levels. Recently two advanced
models were published with some additional physiological factors incorporated
(Leonards, 1997, Smit et al., 1996b). PCB levels in scats showed good correspon-
dence with relative status of otter populations (Mason et al., 1992, Mason and
Macdonald, 1993a, b, 1994, Mason and Madsen, 1993). Estimating PCBs in otter

2. Materials and Methods
2.1. COLLECTION OF SAMPLES
All samples were collected in the Waldviertel, Austria and the adjacent area in the
Czech Republic (Figure 1). The otters in this area form the strongest population
in Middle Europe (Gutleb, 1992). Livers from nine otters found as road victims in
the Waldviertel and tissues of two animals found dead just over the border in the
Czech Republic between 1990 and the end of 1994 were included in this study. All
otters were deepfrozen as soon as possible. Prior to necropsy and for the collection
of tissue samples, otter carcasses were allowed to thaw for at least 24 hr to enable
tissue dissection. Collection of scats (n = 97) for the present study was carried out

between December 1990 and August 1991 in the study area. Scats were collected
in the ﬁeld in glass vials. Fish (n = 85) were obtained from local ﬁshermen between
spring 1990 end June 1992 and comprised six species (Rutilus rutilus, Esox lucius,
Tinca tinca, Carassius carassius, Perca ﬂuviatilis, and Salmo gairdneri). The six

Scats and ﬁsh were deep frozen on the day of collection.
2.2. CHEMICAL ANALYSIS
In the laboratory scats and ﬁsh muscle samples collected from the same site were
pooled, weighed, homogenized and mixed with anhydrous sodium sulfate in a mor-
tar. After the addition of an internal standard (PCB 53) the material was Soxhlet
extracted with hexane (150 mL) for six hours. The solvent mixture was evapo-
rated to 10 mL in a Kuderna-Danish evaporator. In an aliquot of the extract the
fat weight was determined gravimetrically. The extract was cleaned up on two
columns (25 cm × 11 mm i.d.). Columns were rinsed with 15 mL hexane prior of
use. The ﬁrst column was ﬁlled with 5% water-deactivated basic aluminium oxide
(2 g) and 1 cm anhydrous sodium sulfate on top. Extracts were eluted with 25 mL
hexane:diethylether (97:3) and evaporated to 1 mL. This eluate was quantitatively
transferred on a second column ﬁlled with 5% water-deactivated silica with 1 cm
anhydrous sodium sulfate on top. The extract was eluted with 20 mL hexane and
evaporated to 0.5 mL. 2 mL isooctane and 0.1 mL internal standard (PCB 103)
were added and the mixture was evaporated to 250 µL.
2.3. GC CONDITIONS
PCBs were determined with a Hewlett-Packard 5890 Series II GC (Walbronn,
Germany) with an ECD (electron capture detector) using a 50 m× 0.2 mm ID
(0.33µm) Ultra 2 Hewlett Packard column. Injection: 1 µL splitless. Purge time,
2 min. Injector temperature, 270 ◦C. Detector temperature, 300 ◦C. Temperature
program: 2 min at 90 ◦C, 15 ◦C min−1 to 190 ◦C, 15 min at 190 ◦C, 1 ◦C min−1
to 240 ◦C, 3 ◦C min−1 to 270 ◦C. Carrier gas, nitrogen, at 1.2 mL min−1. Makeup
gas, nitrogen, at 40 mL min−1.
An integrator C-R5A (Shimadzu, Kyoto, Japan) was used as an interface for a
PC with a Class Unipac software (Shimadzu, Kyoto, Japan) for calculation of data.
Each calibration curve contained 9 data points covering a concentration range of
three orders of magnitude. Every ten samples a calibration point was measured as
control standard.

2.5. STATISTICAL ANALYSES
We analysed PCB concentrations in otter livers either calculated from ﬁsh and
scat concentrations or analytically measured in the livers using Mann-Whitney U-
Test. Data are reported as geometric means. All statistical analyses were performed
using SPSS/PC+, version 6.0.1.
2.6. QUALITY ASSURANCE
With each series of samples a reference material (oil sample No. 458, Dr. Ehren-
storfer, Augsburg, Germany) was analysed. Recovery level of reference standard
samples and internal standard was always greater than 80%, and the coefﬁcient
of variation of ﬁve replicates was <10%. Concentrations were not corrected for
recovery. Limit of detection was 0.05–0.34 µg kg−1 lipid weight for single con-
geners. Total PCB concentrations were calculated by addition of the individual
concentrations of seven congeners (IUPAC No. 28, 52, 101, 118, 138, 153 and
180).

or measured in ﬁsh muscles in this study may be used to calculate PCB levels in
otter tissues by means of Equation (2).
2.8. STAGE 1: SCAT LEVELS CONVERTED TO FISH LEVELS

stated that otters excrete 8.4% of ingested PCBs (EPCB). The daily PCB intake

4. Discussion
The mathematical model used to predict otter liver concentrations from ﬁsh data
failed to explain the range as well as the mean value to be expected in otter livers
(4.2–130 mg kg−1, mean = 38 mg kg−1) giving much lower PCB concentrations
(1.4–27 mg kg−1, mean = 3.4 mg kg−1).
When scat levels are converted to ﬁsh levels using stage 1 of the model and
considering that the daily intake is equal to ﬁsh concentrations as otters eat one kg
ﬁsh per day the results (1.3–130 mg kg−1, mean = 12.7 mg kg−1) showed good
correspondence with the range of the tissue concentrations measured in otter livers
whereas the mean was somewhat lower.
Several considerations have to be taken into account when discussing the use-
fulness of such a mathematical model. Both methodological and biological factors
may partly explain the observed dicrepancies in our study between analysed and
and calculated results.
The crucial point of such a validation is the methodological approach. When
dealing with a rare and endangered species in most cases only a limited number of
samples will become available for chemical analysis. The observed discrepancies
may be attributed at least to some extent to the relatively small sample size.
The geographical distribution of sampling sites and the overall area of sample
collection with regard to ﬁnding places of dead otters seems to be another important
point for discussing the value of a given mathematical model. Whereas sampling
sites of ﬁsh, scats and otters were situated in an area of about 2500 km2 the total
area of the study still might have been too small and the fact that otters, especially
males are known to travel over long distances in only a few days (Green et al.,
1984; Kranz, 1995) may explain some of the observed differences. Otters might
accumulate PCBs in areas in the Czech Republic where ﬁsh are known to have

higher levels than in the area where the ﬁsh of this study were collected. There are
data for ﬁsh from the River Lusnice only some km downstream the Austrian border
where the mean PCB level was 0.069 mg kg−1 with the highest level of 0.4 mg kg−1
found in a roach (Rutilus rutilus) (Hlavac, 1995). These two levels in ﬁsh would
theoretically result in PCB concentrations in otter livers of 33 mg kg−1 as a mean
and 193 mg kg−1 as a maximum, which is closer to the actual levels in our sample.
Smit et al. (1996a) used a very rigid system for the selection of sampling locations
in their study with at least four otters found dead within a radius of ﬁve kilometers
from the lakes where ﬁsh were sampled. Compared to that study our sample area
may be too large.
The number of specimens in the pooled ﬁsh samples of our study may be too
small. Whereas we have pooled only 2–5 ﬁsh Smit et al. (1996a) pooled up to 25
ﬁsh which may overcome the problem of a possible high variability of PCB levels.
When using ﬁsh data for estimating PCB levels in otters detailed information
on the diets composition has to be present. Our ﬁsh sample corresponds to 50
up to 93% of the otters diet composition depending on the season and the only
ﬁsh species with a high percentage in the diet not included in our study is carp
(Cyprinus carpio). Scheidl (1992) reported that concentrations in 26 carp from our
study area were not exceeding 0.01 mg kg−1. Therefore we assume that our data
are representative for the exposition of otters in that area with PCBs and that the
discrepancies cannot be attributed to the sample composition.
Smit et al. (1996) developed a more detailed model with additional parameters
to calculate biomagniﬁcation of PCBs in otters, which showed good agreement
with the measured data in otter livers from Denmark. Beside other factors they
used a food ratio of 0.2 g g−1 d−1. If this factor is applied to the data for our ﬁsh
samples the expected mean is 5.4 mg kg−1 (2.3–43 mg kg−1) which is lower than
what was measured in otter livers from Austria.
The factor for lipid content of scats used in stage 1 of 1.3% might not be correct.
Elmeros (pers. comm.) reported a mean fat percentage for scats from Denmark
of 0.62%. If this percentage is used in stage 1 the calculated DIPCB by otters is
0.012 mg kg−1 (0.001–0.127 mg kg−1) corresponding to PCB concentrations in
ﬁsh of 0.008 mg kg−1 (0.001–0.085 mg kg−1) which is quite similar to the analysed
values.
For otters no differences between sexes are considered in the presented model.
The results of studies on mean PCB concentrations in different age classes and
sexes are not consistent between the different reported data. Whereas Kruuk and
Conroy (1996) found no correlation of total PCB concentrations and age in otters
from the Shetland Islands, Mason and Madsen (1993) and Leonards et al. (1996)
could show a tendency of increasing concentrations with age and a correlation with
sex for otters from Denmark. In the last study adult males had higher concentrations
than adult females and non-reproductive females had higher concentrations than
reproducing females. Excretion via milk is considered to be an important route of
excretion for females. Smit (1996a) reported on a female otter killed together with

her cub about three months of age (67PCBs = 0.81 mg kg−1 and 5.31 mg kg−1
respectively) thus providing evidence that transfer via milk has taken place. As nine
out of eleven otter samples were adult males this might explain the discrepancy of
data analysed in liver tissues and those derived from calculation. However, as the
excretion rate was calculated using only adult males as experimental animals we
have to expect that male concentrations should be calculated adequately by our
model whereas concentrations in females should be overestimated. In addition it
was shown that scats from males are ﬁve times more ﬁndable than scats from fe-
males (females 0.3 day−1; males 1.7 day−1) (Green et al., 1984). This will produce
another bias as the males cover and move over a larger area than the females.
Furthermore the condition of the otters found dead is not considered in the
model at all. Otters found dead may have starved or have been senile or diseased
before they die. Animals will have lower lipid contents in the liver under these
circumstances. Thus the same number of micrograms of PCB in the liver will result
in much higher concentrations when expressed as lipid concentrations in diseased
animals compared to that in healthy road casualties.
The elimination efﬁciency of 91.6% and its accurate measurement may be an-
other important point of both stages of the model especially as it is derived only
from a preliminary study (Smit, 1990). If the factor is not correct the consequences
for the ﬁnal outcome are far greater in the more uncertainty two-stage model (from
scats to ﬁsh to otter) than in the one-stage model (from ﬁsh to otter). For example
if a factor of 98% is assumed the difference for the ﬁnal outcome of stage 1 would
be 323%, while the results of stage 2 would change only about 7%.
5. Conclusions
The equations for calculating PCB levels in otter livers from data in ﬁsh resulted in
lower concentrations to be expected than what was actually measured. The equa-
tions which were used to estimate liver concentrations from scat levels failed to
predict the mean concentration whereas the range in otter tissues from a Middle
European area was explained by the model. Further investigation on the correla-
tions of PCBs in ﬁsh, scats and otters and the reliability of the factors used in
the mathematical models are therefore recommended as a model for the indirect
estimation of tissue concentrations would be of invaluable value for reintroduction
programmes and risk assessments with regard to the otter. The hunting legislation
in Austria should be changed to a mandatory collection of individuals from all
protected species to have more individuals available for scientiﬁc purposes. In
addition ﬁsh should be collected closer to the position of otters found dead.
