HABITAT MANAGEMENT AND THE RETURN OF THE EURASIAN OTTER,  LUTRA LUTRA, 
TO ANGLESEY 
 
Robert GREIG 
 
Anglesey Otter Project, Tegfan, Llanfaelog, Anglesey, Wales, LL63 5ST. UKemail: wildscape@gwyfyn.com  
 
 
1   INTRODUCTION 
 
Several otter surveys have been undertaken on the Isle of Anglesey (Figure 1) since 1996 
including a Breeding Havens Survey that included six large catchments across the island.   The 
objectives of the surveys were to determine the distribution of otters and identify strategic areas 
for habitat management.  This paper is based upon a poster presentation at the International Otter 
Survival Fund 2003 Otter Conference.

2   METHODS   
 
Breeding Havens are defined by habitat, food supply, levels of disturbance and current 
management practices (agricultural, environmental, urban) as having the best potential for otter 
conservation management within which resources should be targeted initially. 
Six catchments, the Alaw, Braint, Cadnant, Cefni, Crigyll and Marchogion, were 
surveyed in 1998, 2000 and 2002, each taking place within a four week period between May and 
June during daylight hours with normal to low water flow levels.  Full survey sites are 600 metre 
transects along one bank and approximately 15 kilometres apart.  Spot checks, approximately 
three kilometres apart, cover 30 metres up and downstream of a feature, such as a bridge, on both 
banks.  Otter signs are recorded at each site and spraint collected for dietary analysis.   
 
 
3   RESULTS   
 
All catchments have shown an increase in otter signs (Table 1), with the Alaw catchment 
recording the highest increase from 6.1% in 1998 to 16.3% in 2002, while the Crigyll increased 
from 18.2% in 1998 to 31% in 2002.   
The Braint (23.5% in 2002) and the Cefni (16.7% in 2002) showed little variation in 
numbers of recorded signs, though their distribution in 2002 moved up into the tributaries which 
were previously recorded negative.  Signs on the Cadnant have doubled since 1998, from 16.7% 
to 33.3% in 2002, although this must be seen in the context of a total of approximately 25 
kilometres of watercourse with just six survey sites.

4   DISCUSSION  
 
In 1996, signs of otters were recorded in Anglesey for the first time in 19 years.  Since then, eight 
of 42 catchments on the island have recorded otter activity.  A number of observations have been 
drawn from the results: 
• 
Apart from a slight increase in positive sites across the island, changes in 
distribution are also significant.  This can be seen on the Alaw with more positive signs in 2002 
than previously downstream of the reservoir towards the estuary along the main river and 
tributaries.  On the Cefni otter signs were found in 2002 along two tributaries, where no evidence 
of the species was recorded in the 2000 and 1998 surveys, the Ceint and Hirdrefaig. .   
• 
The Braint and Cadnant are the only rivers where small groups of positive signs 
have been constant in all surveys, though distribution for the Braint is uneven with long stretches 
devoid of visible activity.  Absence of signs, usually spraint, is often where the habitat is 


 
unsuitable but on the Braint the data shows that this is not always the case as there is an absence 
of signs along well-vegetated and undisturbed sections.  Thus, an increase in positive signs does 
not necessary reflect possibly more significant changes in distribution. 
• 
After only six years and three surveys it is too early to draw any firm conclusions 
from the upward trend in positive sites as factors such as natural fluctuations are unknown for 
this population. 
• 
A total of six confirmed sightings of otters was recorded during 2002, the highest 
number since the 1960s.  Two occurred in February, one in July and three in November.  The 
February otters were seen on the Afon (River) Goch which is the most polluted river on 
Anglesey, being affected by run-off from old copper and gold mine workings which colours the 
river orange and red.  This raises other issues such as what makes a clean river for otters. 
 
 
5   HABITAT MANAGEMENT  
 
A catchment strategy for otters on Anglesey has been  produced by identifying the status of land 
along a river, distribution of otter signs, food supply, percentage of habitat types and their 
distribution throughout the watercourse (e.g. woodland, scrub, marsh, rough grassland), existing 
available habitat for otters and threats to the species such as recreation, pollution and river 
engineering.   
Figure 2 shows how the habitat data are collated from Phase 1 Habitat Survey to provide a 
snapshot of the condition of a particular catchment or river.  Field surveys are crucial to 
interpreting these data regarding the distribution of habitat types along a watercourse.  The Afon 
Braint shown here includes approximately 79 kilometres of waterway of which an estimated 92% 
is improved/ semi-improved grassland providing little cover.  Further fieldwork found only 12% 
of the total length had tree cover along both banks, not all of which was adequately protected 
from livestock grazing.  Although tree cover in itself is not essential for otters, it can contribute 
favourably to the overall ecology of the habitat providing opportunities for holts in root systems, 
bank stabilisation against erosion and shading to reduce blanket weed growth and high water 
temperatures which may result in lowered oxygen levels.   
As a rough guide, each strategy objective is to establish and maintain suitable otter habitat 
for at least every linear kilometre.  Levels of recreational, agricultural, industrial and domestic 
disturbance are considered and measured in determining the most appropriate sites along with 
otter and habitat surveys to target effective management.  This includes fencing to exclude or 
strictly control grazing, encourage regeneration of rough grassland and scrub, and to protect new 
planting; construction of new and maintenance of existing ponds or lakes; artificial otter holt 


 
construction, for example log pile or plywood, along stretches of river with no natural holts. On 
Anglesey, artificial holts have been monitored and show that around 80% of those on rivers 
known to have otters were used within the first six months of construction, with up to 35% of 
these within the first three months. 
 
 
Overgrazing riverbanks commonly results in bank erosion, increased siltation, loss of fish 
spawning beds, reduction of bankside vegetation and natural regrowth, poor water temperature 
regulation with a possible effect of excessive algal and weed growth in the channels and a 
reduction in invertebrates. Bank erosion is often managed with a variety of methods, such as in-
situ hazel and willow hurdles (‘spiling’) and ‘in-river planting’ of willow to encourage organic 
matter accretion and reduce scour.     
Work on reed beds, marsh, and rush pasture and other wetlands includes reduced 
livestock grazing levels and the introduction of no-spray zones, where appropriate, to act as 
buffers.  These are negotiated with landowners in controlling the use of pesticides and fertiliser, 
such as lime and nitrates, within 30 metres or more of an area of valuable wetland.  Anglesey’s 


 
wetlands are relatively small and the ‘run-off’ effects from surrounding agriculture and 
associated practices can have a substantial impact on their ecology.    
Construction of new ponds and the maintenance of existing ones is undertaken on 
wetlands and other bankside habitat following site surveys to counteract the lack of existing 
suitable breeding sites on Anglesey.  Establishing off-stream ponds has proved successful with 
otters found to be using them regularly within a month of their establishment. 
Tributary mitigation schemes focus on heavily engineered and canalised floodplains and 
their smaller, less-disturbed tributaries and vestigial ponds from the original course of a river.  
This scheme can serve to avoid conflict with flood defence operations on the main rivers, assist 
farmers in protecting livestock from high-sided ditches and result in improved habitat for otters, 
spawning and feeding areas for fish and better water temperature regulation from increased 
bankside vegetation.  They can provide refuges off the main rivers for otters during severe 
flooding.
