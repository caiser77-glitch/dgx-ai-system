 Abstract Differences in habitat use by prey and predator
 may lead to a shift of occupied niches and affect dynamics
 of their populations. The weasel Mustela nivalis specializes
 in hunting rodents, therefore habitat preferences of this
 predator may have important consequences for the popula-
 tion dynamics of its prey. We investigated habitat selection
 by weasels in the Bialowieza Forest in different seasons at
 the landscape and local scales, and evaluated possible con-
 sequences for the population dynamics of their prey. At the
 landscape scale, weasels preferred open habitats (both dry
 and wet) and avoided forest. In open areas they selected
 habitats with higher prey abundance, except during the low-
 density phase of the vole cycle, when the distribution of
 these predators was more uniform. Also in winter, the dis-
 tribution of weasels at the landscape scale was proportional
 to available resources. In summer, within open dry and wet
 habitats, weasels preferred areas characterised by dense
 vegetation, but avoided poor plant cover. In winter, weasels
 used wet open areas proportionally to availability of habi-
 tats when hunting, but in contrast to summer, they rested
 only in habitats characterized by a lower water level, which
 offered better thermal conditions. At the local scale, the
 abundance of voles was a less important factor affecting the
 distribution of these predators. Although we were not able
 to provide direct evidence for the existence of refuges for
 voles, our results show that they may be located within hab-
 itat patches, where availability of dense plant cover and
 physiological constraints limit the activity of weasels. Our
 results indicate that in complex ecosystems of the temper-
 ate zone, characterized by a mosaic pattern of vegetation
 types and habitat specific dynamics of rodents, impact of
 weasels on prey populations might be limited.

 Introduction
 Predation is considered as a key factor responsible for
 changes of rodent density in cycling populations, and the
 impact of predators plays a crucial role during the low-den-
 sity phase of cycles (Hanski etal. 1991, 2001; Klemola
 etal. 1997). Studies trying to predict how voles survive
 through the population bottleneck invoke either random
 process or adaptive response, the latter assuming the exis-
 tence of physical or behavioural adaptations (Norrdahl and
 Korpimaki 2005).
 Theoretical models and some empirical data predict that
 sub-optimal habitats can provide refuges from predation
 during the bottleneck phase of the vole cycle and influence
 population dynamics of rodents (Huitu et al. 2003; Ylónen
 et al. 2003). This idea is mainly supported by data from
 boreal ecosystems, where field voles, the main prey of wea-
 sels, can go extinct due to predation in optimal habitats, but
 survive in wet habitats, where weasel predation is lower
 (Ylónen etal. 2003). However, Norrdahl and Korpimaki
 (2005) questioned this prediction, arguing that in a mosaic
 of forest and agricultural fields in Finland survival of
 rodents was determined by chance events.

 by reducing their activity, but also by choosing well-
 insulated shelters (J^drzejewski et al. 2000). Additionally, the
 smallest species, especially weasels, are vulnerable to attack
 by almost any other predator (Korpimaki and Norrdahl
 1989; Reichstein 1993). Thus, they are forced to find a com-
 promise between hunting to satisfy their energy demands
 and avoiding predators. This can be achieved by the selec-
 tion of habitats offering dense vegetation as protection

 tat preferences of weasels and rodents might differ in time
 and space, reducing the extent of mutual interaction and
 dynamics of both predator and prey populations.
 If the distribution of weasels is not proportional to the
 availability of rodents or the time lag between changes of
 rodent abundance and weasel density varies among habitats,
 we can expect the existence of refuges for rodents over time
 and/or space. Therefore, spatial and temporal variation of
 weasel and rodent distribution is very important for under-
 standing the influence of specialist mammalian predators on
 their prey. The aim of our study was not to provide support
 for either hypothesis concerning refuges, but rather indicate
 how decisions made by predators can affect the probability
 of prey survival in different habitats or within them. To
 examine possible consequences of physiological and behav-
 ioural constraints of weasels impact on populations of their
 prey, we investigated habitat preferences of these predators
 in the Bialowieza Forest in different seasons, at the land-
 scape and at the local scales. First, we identified factors
 responsible for weasel distribution within the whole study
 area (second-order selection, Johnson 1980), and next we
 assessed habitat selection within different types of vegeta-
 tion (third-order selection), analysing our data separately for
 spring, summer-autumn and winter. We predicted that: (1)
 within the study area weasels should prefer habitats with
 higher prey abundance, and/or (2) lower predation risk, (3)
 distribution and habitat preferences of weasels will differ
 between the low and peak phases of the vole cycle and/or
 between the reproductive and non-reproductive season.
 Even if we reject predictions concerning habitat selection at
 the landscape scale, there is still the possibility that refuges
 for voles exist within the habitat patches.
 Materials and methods
 Study area
 The study was carried out in the central part of the Bialowieza
 Forest (23.86°E, 52.70°N), north-east Poland, where we
 assumed that in habitats optimal for reproduction survival
 rates of rodents are lower, whereas less-preferred habitats
 ensure higher survival of rodents, but lower recruitment
 rates. Therefore, habitats which are less productive, but rel-
 atively safe from predation, are the "source" habitats at the
 lower densities of rodents. This assumption holds only if
 the hunting efficiency of predators differs among habitats or
 if predators selectively use different habitats. Unfortu-
 nately, Ylonen et al. (2003) did not provide any evidence
 for differences in hunting efficiency of small mustelids
 among habitats. Moreover, Norrdahl and Korpimaki (2005)
 demonstrated that activity of predators at low densities of
 rodents was comparable among habitats, which again did
 not give support for the refuges hypothesis.
 However, both studies by Ylonen et al. (2003) and Norr-
 dahl and Korpimaki (2005) emphasise the possible role of
 small mustelids in shaping rodent fluctuations, but pay less
 attention to the factors which may constrain the response of
 these predators. The main assumption of predator-induced
 cycles of small mammals is the time lag in the numerical
 response of small mustelids, which continues high-preda-
 tion pressure on declining population of prey (Hanski et al.
 1991; Korpimáki and Krebs 1995; Sundell 2003). Recently
 there have been a growing number of studies indicating that
 predation is not necessarily the main factor affecting popu-
 lation dynamics of voles (J^drzejewski and J^drzejewska
 1996; Lambin etal. 2000; Graham and Lambin 2002; Oli
 2003). This alternative hypothesis proposes that weasel
 populations follow rather than drive changes in prey densi-
 ties (King and Powell 2007). Moreover, if the densities of
 weasels and their prey fluctuate out of phase, then during
 the periods of lower availability of food (years of bottle-
 neck phase of the cycle and winter) these predators will
 demonstrate preferences for habitats characterised by
 higher rodent densities. Otherwise distribution of weasels
 and their predation pressure should be proportional to the
 availability and distribution of resources.
 Small mustelids are highly specialised in hunting rodents,
 thus prey availability seems to be the main predictor of their
 distribution and density (Erlinge 1974; Oksanen and Oksa-
 nen 1992; Klemola et al. 1999). Some authors also invoke
 the role of competitive interactions and landscape fragmen-
 tation in habitat use by these predators (Aunapuu and Oksa-
 nen 2003; Gehring and Swihart 2004; St-Pierre et al. 2006).
 Only Brandt and Lambin (2005), using data from radio-
 tracked animals, demonstrated a possible role of plant cover
 as an element of predation avoidance by weasels. Weasels
 specialise in hunting for rodents in their burrows, and this
 way of life strongly affects their morphology and physiol-
 ogy. The available data on metabolism support the conclu-
 sion that long and thin animals like weasels lose heat faster
 than normally shaped mammals of the same weight (Moors
 1977; Szafrañska et al. 2007). In consequence they have to

 Live trapping and radiotracking of weasels
 Between 2003 and 2006 weasels were live-trapped in each
 plot once in spring (April-June), 3 times in summer (July-
 September), once in autumn (October-November) and
 twice in winter (December-February). From 20 to 40 traps,
 spaced about 50 m apart, were located along transects (1.0-
 2.0 km of length), following fences, ditches and other linear
 features of the landscape, mostly preferred by weasels
 (King 1980; J^drzejewski et al. 1995). One trapping session
 lasted for 5-7 days. During winter we also performed
 snow-tracking (2-4 times, dependent on snow conditions)
 to estimate the number of weasels in each plot. The length
 selected three areas at the landscape scale, characterised by
 different types of vegetation. The first area (referred to
 henceforth as "forest") was located in the pristine forest of
 the Bialowieza National Park and encompassed an area of
 1.00 km2. This area was dominated by oak-lime-hornbeam
 forest, characterized by a high proportion of the old growth
 and a diverse composition of stands, composed mainly of
 oak Quercus robur, hornbeam Carpinus betulus, lime Tilia
 cordata, and spruce Picea abies (Faliñski 1986). The sec-
 ond area (referred to henceforth as "open dry") was located
 north of Bialowieza village on meadows and unmanaged
 grasslands surrounded by the forest, arable land and set-
 tlements (1.52 km2). Vegetation types occurring in the
 open dry area were fresh meadow and pasture communi-
 ties, including abandoned ones (Agropyro-Rumicion,
 Arrhenatherion), as well as dry meadows and unused
 grassland (Calluna-Nardetea, Sedo-Scleranthetea). The dry
 open area was directly next to the forest area. The third area
 (referred to henceforth as "open wet") stretched along the
 Narewka river valley (0.88 km2) and formed a mosaic of
 diverse plant communities: reeds (Phragmitetea), wet and
 moist meadows (Molinio-Arrhenatherethea), mires
 (Scheuchzerio-Caricetea), fresh meadows, dry meadows
 and unused grassland, willow and willow-alder brushwood
 (Faliñska 2003). In the past they were regularly mown, but
 from the end of 1960s were gradually abandoned. The third
 area was partly isolated from the two previous habitats by
 the village of Bialowieza.
 At the local scale (third-order selection), we did a more
 detailed study on habitat selection only within the open
 areas. In the open dry area we distinguished four type of
 vegetation characterised by different plant cover: occasion-
 ally managed open meadows with dense grass cover
 referred to henceforth as "grasslands", abandoned meadows
 sparsely covered by bushes with dense plant cover referred
 to henceforth as "bushes", cultivated arable land with mod-
 erate density plant cover referred to henceforth as "fields",
 and small woods and mature forest with poor ground plant
 cover referred to henceforth as "woods". In the open wet
 area we distinguished only three main types of vegetation,
 since there were no woods or forests there: sedge meadows
 and mires covered by vegetation types of moderate density
 and height (0.5-0.75 m), referred to henceforth as "wet";
 dense and high vegetation cover (over 1.0 m), dominated
 by Filipéndula ulmaria and Urtica dioica, referred to
 henceforth as "moist"; fresh meadows and unused grass-
 lands, referred to henceforth as "dry", characterized by the
 lowest (below 0.5 m), but most dense, vegetation cover.

 are of limited importance during the summer (J^drzejewska
 and J^drzejewski 1998). In the forest, weasels mainly cap-

 type of habitat in which the weasel was located (dry, moist

 Estimations of prey availability
 In order to estimate a rodent abundance index (n rodents
 captured per 100 trapnights), rodents were trapped in each
 area twice a year - in May and at the end of September or
 beginning of October. In open wet areas we also trapped
 rodents in summer (July-August). Every year the trapping
 session lasted 6 nights. Four trapping sites were located in
 each of the three areas (forest, open dry and open wet), and
 each trapping site consisted of six livetraps, baited with
 oats. Traps were checked twice daily, in the morning and in
 the evening, and all captured animals were taken to the lab-
 oratory for processing and released in the place of capture
 after the trapping session to avoid multiple counts. Addi-
 tionally, in 2004 (high density of rodents) and 2006 (low
 density of rodents) we performed more detailed surveys in
 the open wet area during summer, trapping rodents in six
 sites (two sites in each habitat category - wet, moist and
 dry). Despite a relatively low trapping effort we were able
 to obtain reliable results, allowing us compare abundance
 of rodents among seasons, years and habitats. Estimates of
 rodent abundance obtained by this method correspond very
 well with data acquired during more extensive trapping (for
 details see Pucek et al. 1993; Gliwicz and Jancewicz 2004;
 Zub 2004).
 Data analysis
 We used the program MARK (White and Burnham 1999)
 to estimate recapture rates of weasels in order to test our
 main assumption. If recapture rates did not differ among
 years, it means we were able to capture most of the animals
 inhabiting a given area or at least a similar part of the popu-
 lation every time, and compare data among years. We also
 related our density estimates based on trapping to the
 results obtained from snow-tracking. The latter method
 allowed us to estimate weasel density in the larger areas in
 winter.
 Habitat selection at the landscape scale
 To analyse habitat selection by weasels within the study
 area (second-order selection) we examined data for the
 entire study period and estimated the maximum number of
 animals present in a particular season in each of the three
 main habitats (forest, dry open and wet open area). This
 number was limited both by the size and the quality of the
 given habitat. We did not use the simple approach which
 assumes that differences among habitats are only a function
 of patch size, because the quality of the habitat might refl-
 ect differences in composition of rodent and plant commu-
 nities, as well as differences in prey species size. Next we
 compared the total number of weasels captured and
 observed in each season and plot with the distribution pro-
 portional to the estimated maximum recorded number.

 each season. If the observed number of animals fell within

 Results
 Selection of habitats at the landscape scale

 Habitat selection at the local scale
 To analyse habitat selection by weasels within the open
 areas (third-order selection) we applied a different
 approach. The study design enabled us to use a large num-
 ber of animals, but followed for a relatively short period.
 To analyse third-order habitat selection we modified the
 randomization procedure described by Heithaus etal.
 (2006). For each radiotracked weasel we selected the
 tracks, including all locations from 1 full day of observa-
 tions. All the points within one track were treated as loca-
 tions where a weasel was active. The end point of each
 track determined the "night resting site" of the animal. For
 the summer data we also determined the "daily resting
 sites", because the activity pattern of weasels in this season
 is characterised by two activity bouts separated by a short
 inactivity period (J^drzejewski et al. 2000). Next we ran-
 domly assigned "tracks" to the "night resting sites" (known
 from radiotracking), using night resting sites as the starting
 points. In some cases the number of night resting sites was
 higher than the number of tracks, because we did not have
 complete data for all days of radiotracking. The direction of
 movement was also randomly chosen from the range of 0°-
 360°. In this way we obtained 100 random tracks for each
 individual containing a variable number of locations where
 weasels were active depending on the length of the track.
 The tracks were accompanied by 100 randomly assigned
 day and night resting sites. The randomly assigned tracks
 and resting sites represented available resources, whereas
 tracks and resting sites recorded during radiotracking corre-
 sponded to used resources. The random tracks and resting
 sites as well as tracks and resting sites recorded during
 radiotracking were located on the habitat map using Map-
 Info software.

 Habitat selection by weasels in the local scale
 Open dry area
 Among factors which might affect daily movement dis-
 tances (DMD) of weasel males inhabiting open dry areas
 (season, body mass of weasel and density of rodents), only
 season was significant (P = 0.00001) and explained 23% of
 the total variation. DMD were significantly shorter in
 winter (on average 186 ± 223 m, n = 24 days) than in
 summer (on average 1,086 ± 895 m, n = 44 days, t = 5.2,
 P < 0.0001). Because rodent densities did not affect DMD
 we pooled data on habitat selection collected in different
 years.
 In summer, habitat use by weasels in open dry areas was
 characterized by similar patterns for all categories of move-
 ment (activity, day and night resting sites). Active weasels
 preferred grasslands (Gadj= 184.4, P< 0.0001; Fig. 2).
 Also day and night resting sites were located preferably in
 grasslands, but in meadows with bushes proportionally to
 availability of this habitat. Two remaining types of habitats
 (fields and forest) were avoided by active (Fig. 2) as well as
 resting weasels. When resting during the day, weasels
 never used forest habitats and only occasionally cultivated
 fields. All night resting sites were located either in grass-

 Low vegetation cover is avoided by weasels, because it
 cannot provide enough protection again raptors. Our results
 suggest that in open wet areas weasels selected environ-
 ments where prey was less numerous, but which provided
 better protection against predators. Habitat choice within
 dry open areas was also positively correlated with density
 of plant cover. Our observations that weasels avoid arable
 land and woods, characterized by sparsely distributed and
 lower ground vegetation, are in concordance with conclu-
 sions of Brandt and Lambin (2005). They emphasised that
 plant cover has a decisive role in habitat choice by weasels.
 In the dry environments, we did not detect any differ-
 ences in habitat choice during different activities and that
 probably resulted from specific hunting behaviour of wea-
 sels. According to existing observations, weasels finish
 their activity after making a kill (J^drzejewski et al. 1995;
 Sundell et al. 2003), thus selection of resting sites by wea-
 sels is mainly affected by the presence of rodents and their
 shelters.
 Habitats characterised by a high water level within open
 wet areas and low-vegetation habitats in dry environments
 may serve as refuges for voles during winter, but for differ-
 ent reasons. In the open wet area during winter, weasels did
 not demonstrate a preference for a particular habitat when
 active, but selected habitats with a low water level for rest-
 ing. Considering their high metabolic rates, use of better
 insulated shelters in winter might be the only way for wea-
 sels to save some extra energy during this demanding
 period (Szafranska etal. 2007). When resting, weasels
 occupy nests of voles, which are mainly located under-
 ground in dry areas and are much better insulated than nests
 in the wet sedge meadows, where they are located among
 vegetation above the ground (Chappell 1980). Thus, the
 need to minimize energy expenditure might partly explain
 the preference of weasels for dry habitats. Although open
 wet areas provide easily accessible prey for weasels,
 because on wet ground rodents do not build underground
 nests and runways, but this type of habitat does not offer
 optimal thermal conditions for resting. In contrast voles,
 despite their smaller body size, are better adapted to this
 harsh environment and do not leave sedge meadows unless
 they are flooded (Wang and Wang 2000). In dry environ-
 ments thermal conditions are equal, but habitats differ in

 It is usually assumed that the periods of low rodent den-
 sity are crucial for understanding their population dynamics
 (Hansen etal. 1999; Ergon etal. 2004; Norrdahl and
 Korpimáki 2005), thus we should pay more attention to the
 dynamics of weasels during this time, considering their role
 in shaping cycles of small mammals. According to the
 refuge hypothesis, at low densities of rodents we should
 observe more mustelids in optimal vole habitats than in less
 suitable habitats. By contrast, in our system the distribution
 of weasels at low densities of voles (bottleneck years and
 winter-spring season) was proportional to availability of
 resources. This pattern could be affected by at least two
 factors. The first factor is that in spring males search for mates
 and they cover much longer distances than in other seasons,
 which directly affects the distribution of individuals. This
 suggestion is supported by our observations from the breed-
 ing season, when we usually captured weasel females
 within the territory of males, occasionally in the same place
 (own unpublished data). The second factor is that the low
 level of resources may also cause higher intraspecific
 competition and produce more regular spacing of animals.
 On the other hand, far more important for the dynamics of
 prey population was the substantial decrease in weasel num-
 bers. In our study area the numerical response of weasel to the
 changes of vole abundance was very distinct, and mortality of
 these predators during 3-month periods exceeded 50%. We
 observed a high degree of correlation between weasel and vole
 dynamics, without any pronounced time delay. As a conse-
 quence, predation pressure of weasels was proportional to the
 prey abundance. This pattern may explain why the number of
 voles increased immediately after years of low abundance
 without a prolonged decline phase. This caused regular 3-year
 fluctuations of voles observed in open areas of the Bialowieza
 Forest (J^drzejewska and J^drzejewski 1998). The dynamics
 of voles in open wet area were characterised by a less regular
 pattern and extended phases of high abundance (Gliwicz and
 Jancewicz 2004), sharply contrasting with cyclical populations
 of voles in Fennoscandia, which are characterised by a pro-
 longed low density phase (Hanski et al. 1991, 2001).
