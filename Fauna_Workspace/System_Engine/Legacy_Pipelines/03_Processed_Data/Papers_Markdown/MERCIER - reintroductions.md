ASSESSMENT OF THE OTTER (LUTRA LUTRA) RE-INTRODUCTION EXPERIENCE IN 
FRANCE AND THE EEP STRATEGY 
 
Laurent MERCIER 
 
Centre de réintroduction des cigognes et des loutres, route de Ribeauvillé, 68 150 Hunawihr, France 
email:  loutremercier@hotmail.com 
 
 
Abstract. In December 1998, the first French re-introduction of the Eurasian otter began in Alsace.  Six 
individuals from the Breeding Centre of Hunawihr were released over a three-year period. The territorial 
evolution of four otters was followed daily as a result of the implantation of intra-peritoneal transmitters. From 
September 2000, only signs of presence made the monitoring of the population possible. The discovery of small 
prints in 2000 and the observation of two cubs with their mother in 2001confirmed the success of the experiment.  
A study of the diet between 2002 and 2003 showed the opportunism of otters even if they came from captivity in 
their ability to consume available prey in both quality or quantity throughout the different seasons. The otters 
were piscivorous, fish accounting for 98% of the diet.  Cyprinidae were the principal prey particularly in 
autumn and winter, being replaced by Cottidae, Cobitidae and Salmonidae in spring and summer. Today, 130 
kilometres of rivers are occupied by otters. Unfortunately, since the end of 2001, the experiment has been 
stopped because of genetic "incompatibility" between captive and wild populations.  What caused otters to 
disappear completely from the Alsatian rivers until ten years ago. 
 
Keywords: Eurasian otter, Lutra lutra, re-introduction, Alsace, distribution, diet 
 
 
1  INTRODUCTION 
 
On 29 December 1998, an otter (Lutra lutra) re-introduction programme began on the plain of 
Alsace, France, where the species disappeared at the end of the 1980s. Two individuals, fitted 
with intra-peritoneal transmitters, were released. Four others followed, including three which 
escaped from captivity during a storm in December 1999. Today, this shy, carnivorous 
mammal ranges over more than 130 kilometres of rivers and other aquatic environments. The 
small re-introduced population of six individuals was monitored regularly. At the beginning 
of the programme, radio-telemetry was used for individual location. When the signal died, 
tracks and spraints were monitored to determine the otter distribution and analyse its diet. The 
success of this operation was symbolised by the suspected birth of cubs in the wild. After four 
years, it was important to assess fully this first French re-introduction of the Eurasian otter. In 
this paper, we present the success of the release programme and describe the rapid adaptation 
of the released animals to their new natural environment. 
  
 
2   RE-INTRODUCTIONS BETWEEN 1998 AND 2001 
 
2.1 Captive otters from the European Breeding Programme (EEP) 
 
The Otter Breeding Centre of Hunawihr in France was created in 1991. Experience gained 
over the subsequent 12 years has resulted in cubs being born regularly and otters behaving 
‘naturally’, despite being in captivity. From a genetic point of view, all the individuals were 
born as part of the European Breeding Programme (EEP) and are from B-line. The original 
otters came from different countries and parks - the Otter Trust, Norfolk (Great Britain), 
Krefeld Zoo (Germany) and Zurich Zoo (Switzerland). From these, 44 descendants have been 
born with a ratio consisting at the time of two males per female. The risk of inbreeding is

avoided by the regular introduction of new individuals. Eight enclosures, measuring between 
300-1,000 m², were built with reconstituted environments, viz a large pool with running water, 
dense vegetation, holts. The largest is open to the public as part of an otter education 
programme. A special holt called "Spoutnik" makes it possible to film and follow the birth of 
cubs without disturbing them. The animals are fed twice daily, receiving 300-500 grams of 
fresh meat (giblets, minced meat, day old chicks, live fish).  Otters stay good hunters in 
captivity with natural prey which is frequently found eaten by them - Bubulcus ibis 
particularly in winter period, as well as Anas platyrhynchos, Turdus merula and Rattus rattus 
(personal observations). The hunting behaviour is instinctive and innate, and not apparently 
affected by captivity. When a pair is formed, and if the partners mate, cubs are usually born 
sixty days later. After more than ten years’ experience, it was regularly observed that, at the 
end of gestation, females become aggressive and chase off the males which go and live in 
another holt. Cubs stay with their mother until the end of weaning some 8-10 months. They 
are then transferred to a distinct enclosure where they receive different food irregularly during 
the days awaiting re-introduction. 
 
2.2   Intra-peritoneal transmitter implantation 
 
An implant is preferred to a harness, because the latter can cause wounds and also has a 
higher risk of fatal accidents.  The transmitter, IMP/300/L, is supplied by Telonics and is 
seven centimetres long, two centimetres in diameter and weighs 35 grams (Figure 1). Because 
of its lithium batteries, the transmitter has a theoretical operational life of 12 months. A 150 
MHz frequency allows for the location of each individual. The operation is undertaken at the 
Centre, about six weeks before release, by a veterinary surgeon well practised in the 
technique.

2.3   The release phase 
As mentioned in the ethical charter for otter re-introduction (BOUCHARD, LIBOIS AND 
ROSOUX, 1991), the animals were placed in a 150 m² enclosure before release. This was 
placed in the communal forest of Colmar to prevent too much human disturbance. During the 
last month of captivity, a pool allowed the daily delivery of various fish: trout, eels, carp etc. 
A holt was also constructed and hidden in vegetation. Moon, a two year old female, was the 
first individual placed in the enclosure on 25 November 1998. She was joined two days later 
by Mic, a two year old male. On 29 December, the trap door was opened and the young pair 
could escape. They waited until nightfall before leaving this protective place.

also escaped that day. Lastly, on 31 May 2000, Lundi, a five year old male, was released in

3  SCIENTIFIC FOLLOW-UP. 
  
3.1 Radio-tracking between December 1998 and September 2000  
 
The four individuals equipped with transmitters were followed daily until the last signal 
reception. The activity of "Mic" and "Moon" was followed over eight hour periods with fixes 
every fifteen minutes. Thus, it is relatively easy to monitor the territorial development of each 
individual during the initial period of freedom. A 4x4 vehicle, equipped with a rooftop 
directional antenna, easy to handle when driving, made it possible to locate the otters quickly. 
Two FT-290 receivers connected with portable antennas (TELONICS RA-14) refined the 
research thanks to two people operating along individual rivers. Measurements were 
sometimes difficult, in particular the perception of the signal when the vegetation was very 
dense, and whether the otter was under water or in a holt.  Nevertheless, the various results 
made it possible to draw up a precise map of territories (RENAUD, 2001) (Figure 2). 
 
3.2   The search for signs between September 2000 and March 2003  
 
From September 2000, the date when the transmitters ceased to signal, searching for tracks 
and spraints (faeces) became the only tool available to enable us to continue monitoring the 
released individuals. Thus, a first assessment of distribution published in September 2001 
(RENAUD, 2001) used data from telemetry and signs. The presence of many bridges within the 
occupied and adjacent areas in 2001 made it easier to understand the territorial evolution 
during 2002 and 2003.

3.3 Analysis of diet 
 
3.3.1 Material and Methods 
 
The typology of the environment is very varied, like fish and other prey species, and for this 
reason it is important to collect samples over a large area covering the different habitats. Fresh 
spraints (n = 330) were collected between 8 April 2002 and 31 March 2003. To determine 
seasonal variations, the spraints were analysed as monthly samples. They were initially frozen 
and stored before being thawed out, rinsed and filtered on 600 micrometre sieve to eliminate 
intestinal mucus and exogenic particles: sand, vegetable remains etc., before the prey was 
determined as described in LIBOIS, HALLET-LIBOIS AND ROSOUX (1987) and LIBOIS & 
HALLET-LIBOIS (1988). 
 
3.3.2 Results 
 
The results shown in Figure 3 are expressed in relative abundance because of a very weak 
difference between abundance and relative occurrence. The diet of Alsatian otters is primarily 
piscivorous (98%). Only batrachians represent other vertebrate prey, with only 2%. It is little 
diversified (H'<2) and slightly specialised (I<0.5). Cyprinidae are the main prey with a 
relative abundance of 54.7%.  Within this family, Gobio gobio (16.2%) and Phoxinus 
phoxinus (6.6%) are particularly favoured with their small size and ease of capture.  
Unspecified Cyprinidae account for a further 18.1% which confirms the otters’ preference for 
this family. Alburnus alburnus and Rutilus rutilus were also taken by otters on occasions. Fish 
greater than 30 centimetres in length, such as Tinca tinca, Barbus barbus, Blicca bjoerkna 
and Cyprinus carpio were less frequently consumed. Cobitidae represent 15.29% of

Percidae, Anguillidae and Batrachians were occasionally consumed and Perca 
fluviatilis (2.6%) and Lepomis gibborus (0.3%) were not abundant in the diet.  Anguilla 
anguilla (2.5%) was rarely taken. This differs from many other studies where this species is 
one of the main items in otter diet (LIBOIS, ROSOUX AND DELOOSE, 1991; ADRIAN AND 
DELIBES, 1987). Batrachians were found in small quantities in the diet (2%). The specific 
identification of Batrachians could not be done because of the absence of characteristic bones. 
Nevertheless, the presence of Rana temporaria and R. ridibunda and/or R. lessonae is 
suspected. 
There are significant differences of predation among seasons (anova F = 4.24; P < 
0.05) (Figure 4).  Indeed, a strong reduction of Cyprinidae consumption was measured, 
decreasing from 55.9% in spring to 43.8% in summer. This significant decline (chi-squared 
test, χ² = 17.13; P < 0.05) is compensated by an increase in the proportions of Salmonidae 
and Cobitidae consumed (relative abundances increase from 4.1-12.9% and from 8.6-32.0% 
respectively). Even if they are more occasional, other families present monthly variations 
(anova F = 3.22; P < 0.05). Thus, maximum relative abundances of Anguillidae 5.1%, 
Gasterosteidae 7.6%, Percidae 4.6%, Cottidae 10.7% and Batrachians 4.5% occurred in spring 
and drop significantly during other seasons. For the majority of these families, the 
reproduction takes place between April and June so they are more exposed to otter predation 
at this period.

4 DISCUSSION 
 
4.1 Success of the experience  
 
4.11 Evolution of distribution between December 1998 and March 2003 
Each otter had different territorial development. Nevertheless, in the first few days following 
release, it was noted that pairs remained together on a few occasions. Mic remained with 
Moon for four days, while Neptune and Nalla met daily (RENAUD, 2001). Their activity was 
measured preferentially for between 7-13 hours but could vary enormously according to 
individuals. Females appeared more active and mobile than males (RENAUD, 2001). Implanted 
individuals were followed for an average of 269 days. The six otters seemed to be well 
established in their own territories at the end of 2001 constituting 100 kilometres of rivers 
(Figure 2). The total dimensions of Mic and Moon territories were 30 kilometres. Monitoring 
continued in 2002 and 2003 by searching for otter signs (spraints and tracks) and highlighted 
the territorial expansion upstream. Otter signs showed an irregular presence on the high parts 
of Fecht Valley, on the Weiss River to Lapoutroie or on the Blind River to Riedwihr. Indeed, 
fresh deposits were periodically found, approximately every 15 days. This new colonisation 
of 30-50 kilometres of rivers could have been caused by recent births. So, during the four to 
five years following release, otters were very discrete because few people saw them in spite of 
their activity rhythm in the morning or twilight (RENAUD, 2001). Even biologists in charge of 
the tracking programme never saw them. This secretiveness was preserved instinctively in 
spite of their upbringing in captivity, suggesting that the breeding technique employed in the 
Otter Breeding Centre of Hunawihr achieved its objectives in that the otters could become 
completely wild. Environmentally, in spite of biotic and abiotic factors which were not 
completely suitable (DE LA GORCE, 1994), the otters have re-established themselves in their 
natural environment.  
On 29 September 2000, the tracks of a mother and her young were discovered. 
Moreover, in March 2001, a rural policeman observed a mother followed by her two cubs 
close to the Illwald Forest (RENAUD, 2001). These observations confirmed the evidence of the 
rapid extension of the species during 2002. Young erratic individuals could then have visited 


 
7
the zones recently colonised upstream of Fecht and Weiss. No evidence of actual births was 
found. The distribution of the species has remained stable since July 2002. 
 
4.1.2 The opportunism of captive otters and factors of the habitat 
The feral population exploited the more accessible and easiest to capture food resources. 
Thus, the diet was dominated by benthic living prey of small size which were found in groups 
or close to vegetation: the Cyprinidae, N. barbatulus, C. gobio, G. aculeatus etc.  The study of 
the diet in 2002-2003 showed no consumption of terrestrial vertebrates, as found in RENAUD 
(2001) when otters consumed a Myocastor coypus.  Nevertheless, little interest in Batrachians 
was observed which could be more important in quantity although they had never consumed it 
in captivity. 
It is very difficult to appreciate ex situ the health of an otter population. At the present 
time, we do not know the impact of toxic substances, such as PCBs, or their level of 
contamination. However, they are present in the food chain (GRANDJEAN AND DE 
ALENCASTRO, 1996). Their presence could affect fertility but the evidence of cubs in 2000 
and 2001 confirmed otter breeding.  The Alsatian aquatic environments chosen for re-
introduction are of tolerable quality for otters. It should be remembered that otters in captivity 
are unlikely to be heavily contaminated by PCBs with only 10.24 µg/kg of fresh weight found 
in spraints (MAZET, 2002).  
Roads split the territory and present mortal obstacles (LAFONTAINE, 1990) but otters in 
Alsace seem to pass always under bridges as confirmed by regular marking. With a weak 
anthropic disturbance on the plain, otters pass through three villages by Fecht River and one 
by Blind River. In the Vosgean Piedmont (mountains of 700 metres height), by night, they 
regularly pass through seven villages crossed by the Fecht and also the Weiss and Strengbach.  
 
 
5   CONCLUSION AND STRATEGY OF THE EEP BREEDING PROGRAMME 
 
Three years after the last release, the six otters and their cubs colonise 130 kilometres of 
streams in the Plain and in the Vosgian Piedmont. These individuals, which were born in 
captivity, became totally wild and opportunist in their diet. 
The viability of the little population is threatened because the experiment ceased in 
2001. New knowledge on otter genetics encouraged the Environment Ministry to stop the 
programme.  Actually, re-introduction is not a priority for the National Conservation Plan for 
the species.  
The six re-introduced otters are B-line otters, meaning that somewhere in the genetic 
ancestry of the animals, uncertainties exist as to their undisputed genetic origin. For most 
current B-line otters this is the discussion on Lutra lutra barang having been bred into Lutra 
lutra lutra. Otter geneticists are now arguing over this. Samples have been taken from several 
bloodlines within the studbook, and as an example Table 2 shows the proportions of B-line 
and A-line otters. Regarding this, Alfred Melissen commented: 
 
As there are some 60 institutions holding otters within the EEP, it is not possible 
to restock them all with A-line otters in one year, and as even otters do sometimes 
die, some breeding with B-line otters is necessary otherwise people would have 
empty enclosures for a longer time, which is unacceptable to any zoo, small or 
big. Then maybe they fill them with Asian short-clawed otters or another species, 
so the enclosures would be lost to the European Otter captive population breeding 
programme. It's chosen to advise not to euthanise or sterilise animals, as all B-
line otters are perfect display animals, and furthermore, there is still the chance 
that genetic research will find out that there is no difference between A-line and

B-line otters (only once every 2 or 3 years), and increased breeding at breeding 
centres with A-line otters. In time the B-line breeding pairs will all be replaced 
with A-line pairs.

So now, what is the interest in breeding otters in captivity if they are not suitable to be 
released? Would every institution have to look for "national" A-line otters so that they could 
be re-introduced in their country? How could countries like France restore their scarce and 
low populations as was done in Great Britain, Spain, Czech Republic, when it is not possible 
to have Centres for rehabilitation of orphaned and injured otters?
